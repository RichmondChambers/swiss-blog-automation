# Swiss Non-EU/EFTA Retirement Residence

# Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

For third-country nationals, Swiss "retirement residence" is not a distinct statutory "retirement visa". The substantive legal route is a discretionary admission ground for persons who are no longer economically active under Article 28 of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and Article 25 of the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. Any entry visa or entry authorisation is only the travel and pre-entry mechanismn; the status sought is a Swiss residence permit, in practice a purpose-bound residence permit for stay without gainful activity. Cantonal practice reflects this language: Zurich, for example, requires an application for an entry authorisation for retirees, not a free-standing retirement visa. 1

The black-letter federal conditions are cumulative. The applicant must have reached the minimum age fixed by the Federal Council,have particular personal ties to Switzerland, and possess the necessary financial means. Article 25 OASA then fixes the age at 55, prohibits gainful activity in Switzerland **and** **abroad** except for managing one's own assets, and defines sufficient means by reference to the amount above which a Swiss national and, where relevant, family members would not qualify for supplementary benefits under the EL regime. The SEM's 2026 directives add practical requirements: the authorities expect proof that the applicant has genuinely withdrawn from active working life, a formal undertaking not to work, and that the centre of life will be in Switzerland. 2

This route is exceptional and discretionary, not a right. Swiss higher-court case law repeatedly treats Article 28 AIG as a "can" provision that does not create an enforceable entitlement to residence even where the statutory conditions appear to be met. The SEM's directives make the same point and say the canton must exercise discretion with regard to public interests, including Switzerland's socio-demographic development, unlike any residence claim that might arise under free movement law for EU/EFTA nationals. Vaud's official page likewise characterises the route as a derogationfrom the ordinary admission rules and states that an accepted cantonal file is submitted to the SEM for approval. 3

The most sensitive substantive issue is usually "particular personal ties". The ordinance gives examples, especially earlier longer stays in Switzerland and close relations with parents, children, grandchildren or siblings in Switzerland. But Federal Administrative Court case law, now reflected in the SEM's gguidance, interprets Article 28 AIG more tightly: relatives in Switzerland may contribute to the assessment, but family ties alone will usually not suffice. The courts have insisted on direct ties **with** **Switzerland** **itself,** not merely indirect ties mediated through family members who happen to reside there. Independent socio-cultural or personal links, such as engagement with local communities, cultural participation,or direct contacts beyond the family circle, matter heavily. Property ownership and economic links alone are not enough. 4

Financial sufficiency is assessed practically rather than bya single national monetary figure. Article 25(4) OASA uses the supplementary-benefit threshold, which is household-sensitive and dynamic. The SEM says the funds must be available with a high degree of certainty for the rest of life so that the risk of

reliance on public funds is negligible; pensions, liquid capital and durable investment income are the usual evidence. Third-party support is scrutinised closely.The Federal Administrative Court has accepted in principle that third-party resources can be considered, but has also said that ordinary family promises will not usually provide the required certainty; if third-party support is relied upon, it must be secured comparably to the applicant's own means, for example through a bank guarantee. This matters because subsequent social-assistance dependence is itself a revocation risk under Article 62 AIG, and sustained dependence can jeopardise later settlement under Article 63 AIG. 5

No-gainful-activity compliance is broader than Swiss local employment. OASA expressly bars gainful activity in Switzerland **or** **abroad,** save for managing one's own assets. The ordinance also defines gainful activity broadly. Employment for an employer abroad still counts as employed activity, regardless of where the salary is paid, and self-employment includes income-oriented activity carried out under one's own organisation and risk. On that footing, remote salaried work for a foreign employer, paid consultancy, remunerated advisory roles and paid board mandates are difficult to reconcile with Article 25(3). By contrast, passive management of one's own portfolio remains expressly permitted. No official source located in this research run gives a retirement-specific safe harbour for remote work or remunerated directorships. 6

Main-home compliance also matters. The SEM states that retirees must have their centre of life in Switzerland and that renewal will be refused if the authorities later find that the centre of living circumstances is not in Switzerland. Separately, Article 61 AIG provides that a residence permit lapses after six months abroad if the person leaves Switzerland without deregistering; Article 79 OASA says temporary visits backto Switzerland do not interrupt that period. No primary or official source was found that makes "183 days" the decisive retirement-law test. The safer legal position is that the relevant concepts are centre of life, actual residence pattern, and the statutory lapse rules, not a single numerical shorthand. 7

Family members are not admitted under a special derivative retirement rule. The SEM says retirees who wish to settle with a spouse and minor children may also be admitted, but the ordinary family-reunification provisions apply. Where the principal retiree holds only a B permit, Article 44 AIGG is the key provision: cohabitation, adequate housing, no social-assistance dependence, language capacity, and limits linked to annual supplementary benefits all become relevant. For long-term planning, the retirement B permit does not automatically turn into a C permit. Settlement under Article 34 AIG requires ten years' residence overall, five continuous years on a residence permit, no grounds for revocation, and integration. Ordinary naturalisation requires a C permit and ten years' residence. Because the integration criteria in Article 58a AIG include language and participation in economic life or education, retirement-route applicants should plan well in advance for renewal, settlement and any later naturalisation. 8

## Structural overview of the legal framework

**Legal** **basis** **and** **permit** **nature.** The domestic legal basis is Article 28 AIG, read with Article 25 OASA. It concerns admission to stay without gainful activity for persons who have retired from economic life. It is not a separate visa category; rather, visa/entry issues are governed separately by Articles 5-6 AIG and Articles 5 and 10 OASA, while the residence permit framework sits principally in Article 33 AIG. 9

**Core** **statutory** **conditions.** The statutory minimum is age, special personal ties to Switzerland, and sufficient financial means. OASA fixes the age at 55, adds the prohibition on gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad except for managing one's owvn assets, and defines sufficient means by reference to the supplementary-benefit threshold. These are cumulative conditions. 10

<!-- 2 -->

**Particular** **personal** ties. Article 25(2) OASA gives examples rather than an exhaustive list. It mentions earlier longer stays and close relations with certain close relatives in Switzerland. However,the Federal Administrative Court and the SEM treat this criterion more narrowly than a straightforward reading might suggest: the ties must ordinarily include independent ties to Switzerland itself, not family presence alone. Property ownership or purely economic links are insufficient on their own. 11

**Financial self-sufficiency** **and** **public funds.** The official benchmark is not social assistance but the EL entitlement threshold under Article 25(4) OASA. The SEM nevertheless performs a practical public-funds analysis: resources must be secure enough for life that reliance on public assistanceis negligible. This explains why pensions, liquid assets and guaranteed income streams are stronger evidence than informal family undertakings, and why later social-assistance dependence remains relevant under the revocation rules. 12

**No** **gainful activity.** The ban extends beyond Swiss labour-market participation. OASA bars gainful activity both in Switzerland and abroad, while the ordinance's definitions of gainful activity expressly capture employment for overseas employers, foreign-paid work, and income-oriented self-employed or service activity. Managing one's own wealth is the sole express carve-out in Article 25(3). 13

**Health** **insurance** **and** **accommodation.** No freestanding health-insurance criterion was located in Article 28 AIG or Article 25 OASA themselves. The more defensible official position is therefore that health insurance is a general Swiss residence compliance requirement rather than an explicit retirement-route admission criterion: BAG states that anyone settling in Switzerland must obtain health insurance within three months, and Geneva explains that compulsory basic insurance covers illness and accident. Likewise, Article 28/25 do not contain a retirees-specific accommodation clause, but Article 44AIG does for accompanying family members, and cantonal procedure pages commonly require a concrete intended commune or housing-related evidence. 14

**Procedure** **and** **authority** **roles.** The cantonal migration authority handles the file first. If the applicant is abroad and the application is approved, the authority authorises the Swiss mission to issue the visa, or gives an assurance of the ppermit where no visa is required. On arrival, registration is required within 14 days for stays without gainful activityexceeding three months. Official cantonal pages for Zurich and Vaud treat the process as an application for entry authorisation from abroad, and Vaud says accepted retirement cases are transmitted to the SEM for approval because they are derogations from the ordinary admission conditions. 15

**Renewal and** **longg-term** **planning.** The permit granted is a residence permit for a specific purpose; initial validity is one year and extension can be for up to two years, subject to exceptions. The permit can lapse after prolonged absence, and renewal may be refused where the centre of life is no longer in Switzerland. A future C permit is possible only if Article 34 AIG is satisfied, and ordinary naturalisation requires both ten years' residence and a C permit. There is no official source supporting any automatic progression from a retirement B permit to settlemnent or citizenship. 16

## Primary legal texts

**Article** 2 **AIG.** The AIG applies to foreign nationals unless other federal provisions or international treaties apoply. For EU and EFTA nationals, the AIG applies only subsidiarily where free-movement law does not govern or where the AIG is more favourable. This matters because retirement residence for third-country nationals sits entirely within domestic AIG/OASA discretion, whereas EU/EFTA retirees may rely on treaty-based residence rights instead. 31

**Articles 5-6** **AIG** **and** **Articles** **5** **and** **10** **OASA.** These provisions governentry requirements, visa issuance and the mechanics of entry authorisation and post-entry registration. They matter because they show that the immigration architecture distinguishes between the entry document and the underlying residence entitlement: approval while the person is abroad leads to visa issuance or permit assurance, and registration follows after arrival. That is why "retirement visa" is an imprecise label for the Article 28 route. 32

**Article** 28 **AIG.** This is the core retirement provision. In close paraphrase, a foreign national who is no longer economically active may be admitted if they have reached the minimum age set by the Federal Council, have particular personal ties to Switzerland, and have the necessary financial means. The verb is discretionary, not mandatory. It matters because every non-EU/EFTA retirement case begins with these three cumulative statutory limbs. 33

**Article** **25** **OASA.** This is the implementing provision for Article 28 AIG. It fixes the minimum age at 55, gives examples of "particular personal ties", prohibits gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad except for managing one's own assets, and sets the financial test by reference to the EL threshold. It matters

<!-- 6 -->

because it both sharpens the black-letter test and expressly extends the no-work rule beyond Switzerland itself. 34

**Articles** **1a-4** **OASA.** These definitional provisions explain what counts as gainful activity. Employment for an employer abroad counts as employed work even if the salary is paid abroad; self-employment covers income-oriented activity under one's own organisation and risk; and doubt cases go to the competent authority/SEM. These provisions matter because they make it hard to argue that remote foreign work, consultancy or remunerated service activity falls outside the Article 25(3) prohibition. 35

**Article** 33 **AIG** **and** **Articles** **58-59** **OASA.** Article 33 says the residence permit is issued for stays of more than one year, for a specific purpose, may carry conditions, and is extendable if no revocation grounds exist. Article 58 OASA says first-time validity is one year and may be extended by up to two years, with exceptions; Article 59 sets the renewal timetable. These provisions matter because they explain the permit type and the basic renewal mechanicsfor retirees. 25

**Article** **44 AIG.** For spouses and unmnarried minor children of B-permit holders, a residence permit may be granted if they live together, have adequate housing, are not dependent on social assistance, meet language conditions, and the sponsoring person is not receiving annual supplementary benefits.This matters because the SEM says the ordinary family-reunification rules govern accompanying family members of Article 28 retirees. 36

**Articles** **58a,** 61, 62, 63 **and** **96** **AIG; Article** **79** **OASA.** Article 58a lays down the integration criteria used across the foreign-national law system. Article 61 provides for lapse of permits, including after six months abroad without deregistration. Article 62 makes social-assistance dependence, breach of conditions and other factors revocation grounds for residence permits. Article 63 does the same, at a higher thresholdd, for settlement permits and also allows downgrading where integration criteria are not met. Article 96 governs the exercise of discretion by reference to public interests, personal circumstances and integration. Article 79 OASA says return visits do not interrupt the lapse period. Together these provisions matter for renewal risk, centre-of-life analysis, later C-permit planning and the broad discretionary frame within which Article 28 cases are decided. 37

**Article** **34** **AIG.** Settlement is available after ten years' residence overall, including the last five years continuously on a residence permit, provided there are no revocation grounds and the person is integrated. This matters because the retirement route can be a staging ground for long-term residence, but only if the wider settlement conditions are later met. 38

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

**Official** **federal guidance.** The 2026 SEM directives are the single most useful administrative source located for Article 28 AIG. They say retirees must provide confirmation that they have actually withdrawn from active professional life; undertake not to work in Switzerland or abroad; have their centre of life in Switzerland; and show lifelong, highly reliable financial means. The directives also state that accompanying spouses and minor children are dealt with under ordinary family-reunification provisions, and that Article 28 admission is a discretionary cantonal decision taking socio-demographic development into account. On personal ties, the directives follow the restrictive court line and point to direct socio-cultural links beyond the family sphere. 39

**Canton** **Zurich.** Zurichn's official pages state expressly that retirees from third countries have **no** **entitlement** to a permit. The canton nevertheless provides a clear procedural checklist: the applicant must seek an entry authorisation; identify the commune where residence is planned; explain why

<!-- 7 -->

residence is sought in Zurich; document ties to the canton; confirm that no gainfuI activity will be pursued abroad; and evidence financial support. Zurich alsosays the examination can take several months. These are procedural and evidential points, not black-letter federal rules. 40

**Canton** **Vaud.** Vaud's page presents the route as residence "as a retiree, without activity" for persons aged 55 and over with important ties to Switzerland. Required documents include an explanatory letter on ties, a CV, a formal no-work undertaking covering Switzerland and abroad, proof of income and assets, and, where relevant, a lump-sum tax arrangement or a property deed. Vaud also states that if the canton accepts the file, it is referred to the SEM for approval because the case is a derogation from the admission conditions. These are particularly useful official practice notes on evidence and procedure rather than direct statements of federal law. 41

**Canton** **Schwyz.** Schwyz's official factsheet is one of the clearest sources on practice. It says retirees must have their centre of life in Switzerland; personal ties may arise from earlier stays or close relatives, but ties only to relatives in Switzerland are insufficient; independent socio-cultural or personal links are required; property ownership or economic links alone do not suffice; and resources must reach the EL benchmark with high certainty for life. It also says relatives' promises or guarantee letters are not enough and points instead to stronger security such as a bank guarantee. These are not federal statutory rules, but they closely track SEM guidance and Federal Administrative Court case law. 42

**Canton** **Geneva.** In a specialised context dealing with retired foreign-representation staff,Geneva states that a retiree B permit may be issued if the applicant is over 55, has sufficient financial means, does not work in Switzerland or abroad, and faces no revocation ground under Article 62 AIG. This page is narrower than a general Article 28 guide, but it remains a useful official confirmation that cantons treat the retirement route as a B-permit category and that the no-work condition includes foreign work. 43

## Practical legal analysis

The key architectural point is the difference between a treaty-based residence regime and a discretionary domestic regime. For EU/EFTA nationals, retirement residence is fundamentally linked to free-movement law. For non-EU/EFTA nationals, there is no equivalent treaty right: Article 28 AIG is a domestic permission framed by discretion, implemented by OASA, and administered first by the cantons and then, in practice, with SEM involvement. That single distinction explains why Article 28 cases are evidentially heavier, more variable between cantons, and more vulnerable to discretionary refusal than EU/EFTA self-sufficient residence cases. 44

The route is exceptional because Swiss migration law deliberately treats non-working third-country residence outside family, humanitarian or employment categories as a narrow derogation. The Federal Council's 2002 message described Article 28 as carrying forward the older retirement regime, leaving age to the ordinance and indicating that property ownership or economic ties alone were insufficient. The SEM's current directives and later case law then place the route within a restrictive migration policy and expressly connect its exercise of discretion to socio-demographic considerations. The result is that Article 28 is best understood less as a mainstream pathway and more as a controlled exception for genuinely retired,financially secure people with a real Swiss connection. 45

Financial sufficiency is not a one-number test because the formal federal benchmark is the EL threshold, and EL entitlement itself depends on househoId composition, relevant recognised expenses and income. The BSV describes EL as needs-based benefits with a legal entitlement, not social assistance in the ordinary sense, and Article 25(4) OASA uses that system as the admission benchmark. The SEM then adds a forward-looking risk analysis: the money must be highly reliable for life, and the possibility of future welfare dependence must be negligible. That is why pensions,investment income and liquid capital usually carry more weight than soft support arrangements, and why family support is examined for durability, legal security and actual affordability rather than simply accepted at face value. 46

<!-- 8 -->

The law on personal ties contains a genuine interpretive tension that future drafting should not flatten. The ordinance's examples are fairly broad and include close relatives in Switzerland. The 2002 message also suggested repeated stays, close relatives and even Swiss ancestry as possible illustrations. But the Federal Administrative Court later insisted that retirement residence is not a substitute for upward family reunification and therefore usually requires direct, personal ties to Switzerland independent of relatives. The SEM has adopted that stricter reading. The most defensible current drafting position is therefore: family relations remain relevant evidence, but in most contested third-country retirement cases they will not carry the file without broader Swiss-linked social, cultural or personal integration evidence. 47

No-gainful-activity issues are broader than many applicants assume. An applicant cannot safely argue that foreign remote work falls outside Article 25(3), because OASA defines employment by reference to the existence of an employer in Switzerland **or** **abroad** and says it does not matter where the salary is paid. Likewise, consultancy, advisory work or other income-oriented activity under one's own organisation will generally fit the self-employment definition. On the material located, the only clear positive safe harbour is the management of one's own assets. It followvs, as a professional inference from the statutory definitions, that paid cross-border consultancy, paid board roles and remunerated directorships are high-risk on a retiree permit, while ordinary passive investment management is allowed. No official retirement-specific carve-out for such activities was found. 6

Centre-of-life evidence matters on both grant and renewal. The SEM's directives expressly require retirees to have the centre of life in Switzerland and say that renewal is refused if this proves untrue. Separately, the statute and ordinance provide a hard lapse rule after six months abroad without deregistration, and return visits do not interrupt that period. This is why the familiar shorthand that one must spend "more than 183 days" in Switzerland is too crude. The legal questions are where the person's real life is centred, whether the permit purpose is still genuine, and whether statutory lapse has occurred. In later content generation, 183 days should therefore be described, if at all, only as a loose tax or practical heuristic, not as the decisive immigration-law rule for Article 28 retirees. 7

Cantonal practice materially affects outcomes because Article 28 is ddiscretionary and evidence-heavy. Zurich asks for reasons tied specifically to the canton and the intended commune. Vaud accepts proof of a lump-sum tax arrangement as one way of evidencing means and explicitly flags SEM approval. Schwyz foregrounds centre of life, independent social ties and lifelongfinancial certainty, and dismisses ordinary relative guarantees as insufficient. Even criminaI-record requirements vary in age cut-offs between cantons. None of thnat changes federal law, but it directly affects file preparation, timing, and the choice of where a case is likely to be presented most coherently. 48

Long-term planning has to start early because the retirement B permit is only the first layer. Settlement under Article 34AIG requires ten years' residence overall, five continuous years on a B or C permit, no revocation grounds and integration. The federal integration criteria in Article 58a AIG include language capacity and participation in economic life or education. Ordinary naturalisation then requires ten years' residence and a C permit. The official materiaIs do not create a retirement-specific exemption from those longer-term integration structures, although Article 58a(2) allows personal circumstances to be considered in some contexts. The prudent planning inference is that retirees who may eventually want

<!-- 9 -->

C status or citizenship should build a documented language and integration strategy from the beginning rather than assume that financial seIf-sufficiency alone will carry them through. 49

## Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

·Do **not** describe Article 28 AIG as a single Swiss "retirement visa" regime without clarifying that the legal status is a residence-permit route and that visa/entry authorisation is a separate procedural layer. 50

·Do **not** imply that non-EU/EFTA retirees have a legal entitlement to admission if they satisfy the criteria. Article 28 is discretionary. 26

·Do **not** omit the requirement of particular personal ties to Switzerland or present it as satisfied automatically by having children in Switzerland. Current SEM practice and Federal Administrative Court case law usually require broader, independent ties. 22

·Do **not** state a single national financial threshold unless you are quoting a current canton-specific evidential figure. The federal rule is the EL comparator, which is dynamic and household-sensitive. 20

·Do **not** say that relatives' guarantee letters are automatically sufficient. Official guidance and case law are much stricter. 51

·Do **not** suggest that remote work, foreign consultancy, or paid board/advisory roles are automatically permitted. The safer legal reading is the opposite, save for genuine management of one's own assets. 13

·Do **not** present "183 days" as the federal immigration-law rule. Centre of life and the six-month lapse rule are the stronger official concepts. 7

·Do **not** treat cantonal evidential practice as if it were federal law. Zurich, Vaud,Schwyz and Geneva each add their own procedural emphasis. 52

**·Do** **not** imply that a retirement B permit automatically leads to a C permit or ordinary naturalisation. Both require separate statutory conditions. 29

·Do **not** overstate health insurance as a freestanding Article 28/Article 25 admission criterion unless a canton expressly requires that evidence. The stronger official formulation located is that health insurance is a general residence requirement once settling in Switzerland. 53
