# Swiss Retirement Residence Housing Insurance and Practical Conditions

Suggested filename:

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate a single freestanding "retirement visa" regime. For residence purposes, the practical housing and insurance position differs sharply depending on whether the applicant is an EU/ EFTA national relying on treaty rights under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, or a non-EU/EFTA national seeking discretionary admission as a retiree under Article 28 FNIA and Article 25OASA. For EU/EFTA retirees, the core black-letter conditions are sufficient financial means and health and accident insurance covering all risks; if those conditions are met, residence has a treaty basis and is not merely discretionary. For non-EU/EFTA retirees, the black-letter immigration provisions focus on age, retirement from gainful activity, special personal ties to Switzerland, and financial means; the route remains discretionary, with cantonal handling and, where applicable, SEM involvement.1

On accommodation, the legal picture is narrower than many client-facing explanations suggest. For the principal retiree applicant, the core federal retirement provisions do **not** set a universal national room-count, square-metre, or ownership requirement. Instead, concrete accommodation evidence usually appears through cantonal practice: cantons commonly ask for a Swiss address, a copy lease, a landlord attestation, or, if already owned, title or land-registry material. Geneva expressly asks EU/EFTA applicants without activity to provide a lease copy or landlord attestation, and asks the same "if already available" for third-country annuitant applications; Vaud similarly requests arrival registration, and in retiree cases will accept land-registry or notarial evidence where property has been acquired. The clearest positive black-letter housing rule sits in **family** **reunification,** not in the principal retiree route: under the AFMMP, family reunification requires an "appropriate" family dwelling, and Zurich publishes a canton-specific administrative rule of thumb that the number of rooms should equal the number of persons minus one. 2

On insurance, there is a clear distinction between immigration-law conditions and general Swiss social-insurance obligations. EU/EFTA economically inactive residents must show comprehensive health and accident insurance as a condition of residence. Separately, under Swiss health-insurance law,every person who takes up residence in Switzerland must join compulsory health insurance within three months, each family member is insured individually, and insurers must accept persons into basic insurance regardless of age or health status. Accident cover is normally folded into compulsory health insurance unless the person is already fully covered for accidents under the Accident Insurance Act; BAG guidance states that persons who are not employed, are no longer employed, or work fewer than eight hours per week - expressly including retirees-must remain accident-insured through compulsory health insurance. 3

For non-EU/EFTA retirees, no equivalent express insurance condition appears in Article 28 FNIA or Article 25 OASA. The safer internal formulation is therefore that health insurance is a **general** **residence-law necessity and a practical filing issue,** rather than a separately worded Article 28admission criterion. Official cantonal retiree pages examined in Geneva and Vaud do not present proof of insurance as a standalone mandatory attachment in the same way they list passport,financial evidence, non-work undertaking, lease or property material, and ties to Switzerland. That does **not** reduce its practical importance: once the person is resident, KVG insurance is compulsory, and inability to meet ongoing insurance premiums and living costs will undermine the broader public-funds analysis relevant to both grant and renewal. 4

Cost planning is materially canton-sensitive. BAG states that basic-health premiums are gradded by canton and may also be regionally differentiated within a canton; Vaud's official guidance likewise notes that premiums vary from canton to canton and by region, even though benefits remain the same. Housing costs also vary substantially by region and canton; Zurich's official housing statistics show marked regional differences in rents. In retirement cases, these variations matter because the question is not merely whether the applicant can pay a nominal headline premium or rent, but whether the full household budget remains robust enough to avoid social assistance or, for retirees, fallinng below the supplementary-benefit benchmark used in federal retirement-residence rules. 5

Practically, address and housing are renewal-sensitive. A permit can expire on departure abroad, on moving permit status to another canton, on expiry, or on actual abandonment of Swiss residence. SEM guidance states that actual abandonment is assessed by whether the centre of life has moved abroad, and identifies the termination of a Swiss lease as one indicator. For retirees specifically, the AIG directives state that the centre of life must be in Switzerland and that renewal should be refused if that is not genuinely the case. Foreign nationals must generally report a change of commune or canton within 14 days; EU/EFTA nationals ordinarily retain Switzerland-wide geographical mobility and do not need a new EU/EFTA permit merely because they move canton, but their new address muust be recorded, whereas third-country nationals moving canton generally require advance permission from the new canton. 6

## Structural overview of housing and insurance requirements

**Legal nature** **of the** **requirement.** In retirement-residence cases, housing and insurance are partly black-letter law and partly evidential practice. Insurance is expressly part of the EU/EFTA economically inactive route and generally compulsory after Swiss residence begins; housing for the principal retiree is more often an issue of proof of real residence and family suitability than a fixed national threshold. 7

**EU/EFTA** **retirees.** The treaty route requires sufficient means and comprehensive health-and-accident coverage. The SEM's public materials and cantonal EU/EFTA pages treat the residence basis as a right if the treaty conditions are met. A B EU/EFTA permit without activity is ordinarily valid for five years, although VFP Article 17 allows the authorities to require earlier renewal for non-active stays if they consider it necessary. 8

**Non-EU/EFTA** **retirees.** Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA set the retiree route's core admission requirements: age 55, special personal ties, no gainful activity apart from managing one's own assets, and means above the supplementary-benefit threshold. Those provisions do not add a separate express immigration-law room or insurance formula for the principal applicant. In practice, however, cantons often ask for lease or property evidence and will examine whether the Swiss residence is genuine. 9

**Accommodation.** No universal federal room-count standard was located for a solitary retiree's own housing. For accompanying family members, the law is stricter: under the AFMP, family reunification requires an appropriate family dwelling, and Zurich's official practice translates this into a room-count formula. Cantonal paperwork for principal retiree filings often asks for a lease, landlord attestation, or property papers instead of applying a single national metric. This is best characterised as a **practice** **point,** not a single black&lt;-letter federal retiree rule. 10

<!-- 2 -->

**Insurance.** Once resident in Switzerland, compulsory basic health insurance under KVG must normally be taken out within three months, every family member is insured separately, and basic insurers must accept applicants without health-based exclusions. BAG guidance also makes clear that retirees who are not fully insured under the accident-insurance regime must keep accident cover within their health insurance. For EU/EFTA retirees, "coverage of all risks" is also an immigration condition. 11

**Household** **budgeting** **and** **public-funds** **risk.** Housing costs, health premiums, deductibles, co-payments, hospital contributions, and accident cover all sit inside the practical self-sufficiency analysis. The legal benchmark for retirees links to supplementary-benefit thresholds, but the lived assessment is budgetary and canton-sensitive. This is why official premiums and regional housing costs matter even though they are not themselves immigration statutes. 12

**Renewal and** **address** **changes.** Housing is not merely an entry-stage evidential item. Lease termination, residence abandonment, or a move showing that Swiss residence is not the real centre of life can affect renewal or lapse. Address changes must usually be reported promptly, and moving canton may trigger a renewed competence analysis, especially for third-country nationals. 13

## Primary legal texts

The principal housing, insurance and practical-residence texts are these.

**AFMP** **Annex** **I Article** **24** **and** **VFP** **Article** **16.** For economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals, the treaty architecture turns on sufficient resources and comprehensive insurance. The SEM's public factsheet states that retirees and other non-active EU/EFTA nationals enjoy a residence right if they have sufficient means to avoid social assistance and health insurance covering all risks, including accident; VFP Article 16 implements the financial-means test by reference to Swiss social-assistance levels and, for pensioners, the supplementary-benefit threshold. 14

**VFP** **Article** **17.** Although a B EU/EFTA permit is generally issued for five years, VFP Article 17 permits the authorities to demand an earlier renewal in non-active residence cases. For retirement content

<!-- 6 -->

generation, this matters because accommodation and insurance should not be described as "one-off" filing items only; they can reappear in interim compliance checks. 29

**FNIA** **Article** 28 **and** **OASA** **Article** **25.** Article 28 FNIA permits admission of retired foreign nationals if the Federal Council's minimum age is met, the applicant has special personal ties to Switzerland, and the required means exist. OASA Article 25 adds the specific age of 55, gives examples of the relevant ties, prohibits gainful activity in Switzerland and abroad except for management of one's own assets, and fixes the financial benchmark by reference to the supplementary-benefit threshold. Notably,these provisions do **not** themselves add a freestanding express clause requiring a lease, ownership of property, or a distinct insurance certificate for the principal retiree. 30

**KVG** **Articles** 3 **and** 8. Article 3 KVG requires every person domiciled in Switzerland to insure for medical care within three months of taking up residence. Article 8 KVG permits accident cover to be suspended only where the insured person demonstrates full compulsory accident insurance under the UVG. Read together with BAG guidance, these provisions explain why retirees ordinarily need accident cover inside their Swiss basic health insurance unless they still have full Swiss employment-based accident cover. 31

**AIG Articles 33, 37, 44 and 61, plus VZAE Articles 15, 58 and 59.** Article 33 AIG provides that the residence permit is issued for a particular purpose, is time-limited, and can be extended if no revocation grounds exist. VZAE Article 58 sets the general initial duration of a residence permit at one year, extendable by two years, and Article 59 requires extension applications in principle to be filed at least 14days before expiry. Article 37 AIG governs canton changes for third-country permit holders. Article 44AIG makes suitable accommodation a black-letter requirement for spouses and children of a B-permit holder. Article 61 AIG and the SEM directives are central to renewal analysis because they deal with expiry and actual abandonment of Swiss residence. VZAE Article 15 imposes the 14-day address-registration rule on moves. 32

**Civil-law** **residence** **concept.** ZGB Article 23 defines domicile by residence with the intention of permanent stay and states that a person cannot have multiple domiciles at once. That civil-law concept is not an immigration rule by itself, but it underpins the practical importance of a real Swiss home, a coherent address trail, and a centre of life genuinely located in Switzerland. 33

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

The federal guidance is relatively strong on insurance and renewal, and less prescriptive on a principal retiree's accommodation metrics.

SEM's EU/EFTA factsheet and FAQ say that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals, including retirees, must show sufficient means and an insurance policy covering illness and accident; the SEM permit page confirms that non-active EU/EFTA persons have access to a B permit if they can prove means and health/accident insurance, and the SEM FAQ adds that they must register with the commune within 14days after arrival for a stay over three months. The VFP directives add that AFMP family reunification requires an appropriate dwelling for the whole family, that cantonal authorities should verify this requirement both at filing and at entry, and that a dwelling that is too small can justify refusal. 34

For non-EUJ/EFTA retirees, the AIG directives are especially important. They state that retirees must show that they have genuinely withdrawn from economic life, that they must undertake not to work in Switzerland or abroad, that their centre of life must be in Switzerland, and that renewal should be refused if the centre of life is not in Switzerland. The same directives also say that spouses and minor

<!-- 7 -->

children can join under the ordinary family-reunification rules. Separate AIG directive material on expiry and abandonment of residence explains that permit lapse is tied to actual abandonment and that cancelling the Swiss home is one indicator that thecentre of life has shifted abroad. 35

BAG guidance supplies the strongest official material on insurance mechanics. It states that anyone taking up residence in Switzerland must take out basic health insurance within three months; each family member is insured separately; basic insurers must accept applicants without reservations or waiting periods; and people not or no longer employed, or working under eight hours weekly-including retirees - must insure accident risks through compulsory health insurance. BAG also explains that premiums are graded by canton and may be regionally differentiated, and it publishes the official premium-comparison tool through Priminfo. 36

## Cantonal practice notes

**Geneva.** Geneva's official pages are among the clearest and most useful practical sources. For EU/EFTA applicants without activity, Geneva states that there is a right of residence if sufficient means and health/accident insurance exist, and it requires a copy of the lease or a landlord attestation as an attachment. For third-country annuitants, Geneva states that the person must apply before entering Switzerland, lists the Article 28-type conditions, and asks for lease or landlord-attestation material "if already available." Geneva therefore treats accommodation evidence as a real filing issue even where the federal retiree texts do noot spell out a standalone housing criterion. 37

**Vaud.** Vaud's EU/EFTA page ties the route to AFMP Annex I Article 24 and lists arrival registration with the commune, identity document, explanatory letter, CV, and quantified financial evidence. Vaud's third-country retiree page requires a passport copy, explanation of ties, CV, formal undertaking not to work, financial evidence or lump-sum-tax material, and-if existing-notarial or land-registry evidence of Swiss real estate. Vaud's health-insurance pages additionally confirm compulsory health insurance on taking domicile in Switzerland and note that premiums vary by canton and region1. 38

**Zurich.** Zurich's public migration pages state that EU/EFTA nationals must register with the commune after arrival, that the move of residence within the canton or to another canton must be reported within 14 days, and that non-active EU/EFTA nationals must prove sufficient funds and adequate health and accident insurance. Zurich's family-reunification page applies a published rule of thumb for appropriate family housing: persons minus one equals rooms. That formula is useful evidentially, but it is a canton-specific practice aid rather than a national federal norm. 39

## Practical legal analysis

The principal doctrinal distinction is that **insurance is black-letter law for EU/EFTA retirees, while** **accommodation for the principal applicant is usually a matter of evidential credibility and real** **residence rather than a universal statutory formula.** AFMP-based residence expressly insists on comprehensive insurance and sufficient means. That makes health and accident coverage part of eligibility, not merely post-arrival administration. By contrast, the non-EU/EFTA retiree route does not contain an equally explicit article saying "a lease of X size must be shown" or "insurance proof must accompany the Article 28 request"; the safer legal position is that these matters arise through general residence law, cantonal filing practice, and the public-funds and centre-of-life analysis. 40

That difference matters in drafting. For EU/EFTA retirees, it is legitimate to say that health and accident cover is a substantive treaty condition. For non-EU/EFTA retirees, it is more accurate to say that the immigration route is built on age, ties, retirement from economic life, and financial means, but that

<!-- 8 -->

practical residence in Switzerland triggers compulsory healthinsurance and that cantons may want concrete housing evidence. Overstating a freestanding "accommodation requirement" as though it were textually identical across both routes would flatten an important legal distinction. 41

Accommodation becomes legally hard-edged when family members are involved. Under the AFMP, family reunification requires an appropriate family dwelling, and SEM guidance tells cantons to verify this at application and at entry. Zurich goes further and publishes a specific room-count formula. In other words, if the drafting topic moves from a single retiree to a retiree accompanied by spouse, children or dependent ascendants, the housing analysis becomes much stricter and more rule-like. The same is also true under the AIG family-reunification rules for B-permit holders, where suitable accommodation is written into Article 44 itself. 42

Insurance planning should also be drafted with more nuance than simply "obtain Swiss health insurance." The official position is that compulsory insurance usually starts for every Swiss resident within three months and each family member must be separately enrolled. Basic insurers cannot refuse admission because of age or health. But retirees also need to budget for the ongoing premium, franchise, co-payment and, where relevant, hospital contribution. BAG additionally explains that accident cover is not optional for retirees unless they remain fully covered under Swiss accident-insurance law. This means that an apparently modest underwriter quote can still understate the true residency budget if it ignores family enrolment, accident cover, and normal Swiss cost participation. 43

Cantonal difference is practicaI rather than merely cosmetic. The substantive immigrationtests are federal,but their evidential handling is not entirely uniform. Geneva asks for lease evidence up front on EU/EFTA files and, where available, on third-country annuitant files. Vaud highlights commune-arrival formalities and accepts property evidence where there is already Swiss real estate. Zurich gives a concrete rule for family housing and stresses prompt reporting of moves. Combined with BAG's premium variation by canton and region and Zurich's evidence of regional rent variation, this means that "adequate means for housing and insurance" must be budgeted and evidenced with the intended canton in mind. 44

Renewal risk is where housing and insurance become most operational. The retiree who cannot show a real Swiss home, who lets the lease lapse while spending most time abroad, or who falls into arrears on premiums risks a conclusion that Switzerland is not the genuine centre of life or that the permit conditions are no longer met. SEM guidance expressly treats termination of the Swiss home as a signal that the centre of life moved abroad, and says retirees must have their life centre in Switzerland. Address changes must be reported quickly, renewal applications are due before expiry, and third-country canton moves may require advance approval. On an internal drafting level, housing should therefore be framed not only as "proof of address" but as evidence of durable residence substance. 45

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss retirement residence as if there were one unitary national "retirement visa" with uniform housing and insurance rules. The EU/EFTA route is treaty-based; the third-country route is discretionary. 46

Do not state that Swiss federal law imposes a single national minimum room count or compulsory property ownership for a principal retiree applicant. The clearest accommodation metrics found were cantonal practice points and family-reunification rules, not a universal retiree rule. 47

<!-- 9 -->

Do not treat proof of health insurance and proof of accident insurance as interchangeable shorthand. EU/EFTA guidance expressly requires both, and BAG explains why retirees usually need accident cover inside compulsory health insurance unless fully covered under UVG. 48

Do not imply that third-country retiree admission under Article 28 FNIA turns on insurance in exactly the same textual way as the AFMP route. The more accurate formulation is that KVG insurance becomes compulsory on Swiss residence and is a practical necessity, but the Article 28 / Article 25 black-letter criteria are age, ties, retirement, and means. 49

Do not ignore family-member consequences. Where family accompanies the retiree,suitable accommodation becomes a sharper and more explicit legal issue, and every resident family member must have separate health-insurance enrolment. 50

Do not use a single Swiss-wide monthly budget figure for housing or insurance. BAG premium levels vary by canton and region, and housing costs vary materially as well. 51

Do not present lease evidence or Zurich's room-count formula as federal law. Those are practice tools or canton-specific handling points. 52

Do not reduce change-of-address issues to local registration only. For third-country nationals, a canton move may need advance permission; for everyone, residence abanddonment and centre-of-life drift can affect renewal or lapse. 53

### Open questions and limitations

No official federal source located in this research setting set a universal square-metre or room-count requirement for a **standalone** **principal retiree** applicant. The strongest housing rules found were AFMP family-reunification materials and canton-specific practice notes. That absence should be treated conservatively in future drafting. 69

For third-country retirees, official cantonal pages reviewed did not consistently list health-insurance proof as a standalone immigration attachment even though compulsory Swiss health insurance normally attaches on residence. Future canton-specific production should therefore check the target canton's current checklist before filing rather than assuming a uniform national documentary rule. 70
