## Executive legal summary

Swiss "retirement residence" is not a standalone visa categJory.For financial analysis, the law separates two very different routes. EU/EFTA retirees rely primarily on free-movement rights under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing ordinance. Non-EU/EFTA retirees rely on the discretionary retirement route in Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA. The financial question is therefore asked in two different legal settings: a treaty-based entitlement model for qualifying EU/EFTA applicants, and a restrictive discretionary admission model for third-country retirees. 1

For EU/EFTA retirees, the core rule is that they must have sufficient financial resources so that they do not need social assistance during their stay, together with comprehensive sickness and accident insurance. Swiss implementing law refines that rule in two ways. First, for economically inactive persons generally, sufficiency is measured against the social-assistance level that would apply to a comparable Swiss national under the SKOS guidelines. Secondly, for pension-entitled EU/EFTA nationals, resources are deemed sufficient if they exceed the amount that would entitle a comparable Swiss national to supplementary benefits under the federal supplementary-benefits legislation. In practice, SEM's 2026directives say that newly arriving pensioners must clear the supplementary-benefit benchmark, and that if they later seek social assistance or supplementary benefits, the permit may be revoked or not renewed. 2

For non-EU/EFTA retirees, Article 28 FNIA requires the "necessary financial means", but federal law does not set a single national CHF threshold. SEM's 2026 directives indicate that the benchmark is whether the person's means exceed the amount that would entitle a Swiss national,and any accompanying family members, to suppplementary benefits. Those means must be available with a high degree of certainty for the rest of the applicant's life, so that the risk of dependence on welfare is negligible. The directives treat pensions and assets as the paradigm cases. Guarantees from relatives are not ruled out, but they are treated more cautiously because enforceability is uncertain; third-party means need to be secured in a way comparable to the applicant's own means, for example by a bank guarantee,and the qualitative demands rise if the retiree's own means are weak. 3

A central drafting point is that Swiss supplementary benefits and Swiss social assistance are not interchangeable across all immigration contexts. In domestic immigration law, the Federal Supreme Court has repeatedly held that AHV/IV supplementary benefits are not the same as "social assistance" for purposes such as the revocation ground in Article 63 FNIA; that was reaffirmed in BGE 149 II 1. But in the specific AFMP context of economically inactive residence under Article 24 Annex I, the Federal Supreme Court has also held that supplementary benefits must be treated like social assistance for the purpose of deciding whether resources are sufficient and whether the residence right under that treaty route can continue. That distinction is legally important and should not be flattened in later drafting. 4

The practical means assessment is household-based and forward-looking. Supplementary-benefit law shows why: entitlement depends on recognised expenses exceeding countable income, with spouses' and certain children's figures aggregated. Recognised expenses include a general living allowance, capped rent and ancillary costs by cost region and household size, and an amount for compulsory health insurance. Countable income includes pensions and other periodic income,returns from movable and immovable assets, and for old-age pensioners a portion of net assets above statutory allowances. This explains why migration authorities look beyond gross pension figures and examine rent, insurance, family composition, liquid assets, asset income, and whether funds remain dependable over time. 5

Third-party support is treated differently in the two routes. Under the AFMP route, the Federal Supreme Court held in 2C891/2022 that proof of sufficient means can in principle come from a solvent third party; a maintenance undertaking can be an acceptable evidential vehicle if it is actually adequate for the purpose, and the Court accepted a six-month notice clause coupled with notification to the authorities as potentially sufficient. Under the non-EU/EFTA retirement route, SEM's directives, drawing on BVGer case law, apply a stricter standard because there is no treaty entitlement and because the authorities must be confident that long-term burden on public funds is highly unlikely. 6

Finally, public-funds risk is not assessed only at the initial grant stage. SEM's directives note that social-assistance authorities notify migration authorities of social-assistance receipt, and that organs administering federal supplementary benefits notify migration authorities of grants of supplementary benefits to foreign nationals living in Switzerland. That reporting architecture matters because financially borderline retirement files may be reopened on renewal or, depending on the legal route and status held, on review of existing permission. 7

## Structural overview of financial self-sufficiency

**For EU/EFTA** **retirees,** the financial requirement derives from Article 24 Annex I AFMP, read with Articles 16 and 17 VFP and SEM's free-movement directives. The legal test is whether the applicant has sufficient resources to avoid social assistance, with special treatment for pension-entitled applicants: their means must exceed the supplementary-benefit threshold applicable to an equivalent Swiss national. The default residence document is the B EU/EFTA permit for persons without gainful activity, usually issued for five years, although SEM's directives and Article 17 VFP allow a two-year revalidation or earlier review where authorities consider that necessary. 8

**For non-EU/EFTA** **retirees,** Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA require the minimum age, special personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means, and no gainful activity. Financially, the route is exceptional rather than rights-based. SEM's directives say the benchmark is above the level that would entitle a Swiss national and any family member to supplementary benefits, and the inflow must be dependable for life so that welfare dependence is negligible. 9

The **public-funds** **risk** **inquiry** is broader than a snapshot of current income. Both treaty and domestic guidance look to a realistic future budget. The Federal Supreme Court has said, in different contexts, that the likely future financial development matters, that supplementary benefits burden public finances even if they are not domestic "social assistance" in every legal sense, and that EU/EFTA inactive residence can fail where resources drop below the treaty threshold. 4

**The** **household-cost analysis** is not set by one universal immigration figure. Instead, Swiss law and guidance build it from legal benchmarks that vary with household composition, housing costs, insurance and regional price structures. Supplementary-benefit law expressly aggregates spouses' and certain children's finances, caps recognised rent by region and occupancy, and recognises compulsory health-insurance costs. That is why cantonal practice often asks for quantified proof of pensions, assets, rent, insurance and, where relevant, tax arrangements or guarantees. 10

## Primary legal texts

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 28.** Article 28 FNIA allows admission of foreign nationals who are no longer gainfully employed if they meet the minimum age set by the Federal Council, have special personal ties to Switzerland, and have the required financial means.The provision is enabling rather than rights-conferring, which is why financial adequacy for third-country retirees is assessed within a broader discretionary framework rather than as a rigid entitlement test. 19

**Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, Article 25.** Article 25 OASA operationalises Article 28 FNIA. SEM's 2026 directives summarise the current cumulative requirements as age 55+, special personal ties, necessary financial means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad except management of one's own assets. The financial limb matters boecause it interacts with the "no gainful activity" rule: the authorities expect the applicant's maintenance to come from existing pensions, assets or similarly secure non-work sources. 20

**AFMP, Article** **6** **and** **Annex** I, **Article** 24. Article 6 AFMP gives residence rights to persons who do not pursue economic activity according to Annex I. Annex I, Article 24 provides that a non-working national of a contracting party receives a residence permit of at least five years if he or she proves sufficient resources for self and family so as not to need social assistance, plus comprehensive sickness insurance; the right lasts only so long as those conditions remain fulfilled. This is the foundational treaty text for EU/EFTA retirees. 21

**Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons, Articles 16, 17 and 23.** Article 16 VFP states that means are sufficient if they exceed, for a comparable Swiss national, the social-assistance level under SKOS, and for pension-entitled EU/EFTA nationals if they exceed the amount giving entitlement to supplementary benefits. Article 17 allows the authorities to require revalidation after the first two years if needed. Article 23 allows revocation or non-renewal if the conditions are no longer met. Together, these provisions explain why financially borderline treaty cases may still receive shorter review cycles or later be revisited. 22

<!-- 5 -->

**Federal Act on Supplementary Benefits to Old-Age, Survivors' and Invalidity Insurance, Articles 2,** **9,9a,** 10 **and** **11.** ELG states that supplementary benefits are granted to cover essential needs where statutory conditions are met. The annual benefit equals recognised expenses minus countable income. Recognised expenses include, for home-dwelling persons, a general living allowance, rent and ancillary costs subject to caps that vary by cost region and household size, and a compulsory health-insurance amount capped at the actual premium. Countable income includes investment returns, pensions and other periodic income, and for old-age pensioners a fraction of net assets above statutory allowances. The Act also now includes net-asset ceilings. These provisions matter because the retirement-residence financial benchmarks for both EU/EFTA pensioners and third-country retirees are built by reference to the level at which a Swiss national would qualify for supplementary benefits. 18

**FNIA** **provisions** **on** **revocation** **and** **discretion.** SEM's guidance and the Supreme Court's case law show that public-funds risk remains relevant after grant. Under domestic law, social-assistance dependence can trigger permit consequences, bout AHV/IV supplementary benefits are not identical to social assistance for every purpose. Under AFMP Article 24 analysis, by contrast, claiming supplementary benefits can show that the treaty-level resource condition is no longer met. The source of the rule therefore matters. 4

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

**Federal guidance.** SEM's 2026 directives for the foreign-nationals field state that non-EU/EFTA retirees have the necessary means when they exceed the amount that would entitle a Swiss national and, where relevant, family members to supplementary benefits, andthat those means must be available with high certainty throughout life. The same directives caution that family promises may not provide enough security and expressly say that more robust third-party arrangements may be needed. 15

SEM's 2026 free-movement directives state that EU/EFTA economically inactive persons must prove sufficient means so as not to burden social assistance, that the general benchmark is the SKOS standard, and that for pension-entitled EU/EFTA nationals the relevant measure is the supplementary-benefit threshold. The directives also state that permits may be refused, revoked or not renewed if later social assistance or supplementary benefits are claimed, and that the initial permit is usually for five years but may exceptionally be reviewed after two years. 23

SEM's public-facing pages are consistent with that guidance. The SEM page for the B EU/EFTA permit states that persons without gainful activity have a right to the B EU/EFTA permit if they can prove sufficient financial means and adequate sickness and accident insurance. SEM's FAQ also repeats that inactive EU/EFTA nationals obtain B permits if they have enough means for themselves and dependants plus insurance. 24

SEM also publicly lists a joint SEM-BSV circular on data exchange concerning supplementary benefits, and its directives explain that both social-assistance and supplementary-benefit authorities report relevant payments to migration authorities. That is not merely theoretical guidance; it is part of the operating enforcement architecture for ongoing public-funds review. 25

**Cantonal practice** **notes.** Official cantonal materials confirm that documentary expectations are concrete and budget-led rather than formulaic. Vaud's page for EU/EFTA stays withoout gainful activity says that the principal condition is sufficient means under Article 24 Annex I AFMP and adds that means may be treated as secured by a signed undertaking from a guarantor; it asks for quantified evidence of income and assets, or a copy of a lump-sum tax arrangement if one exists. ## Practical legal analysis

The most important analytical distinction is not between "good finances" and "bad finances", but between **a treaty route and a discretionary domestic route.** EU/EFTA retirees benefit from a residence architecture in which financial sufficiency is the key gateway condition, but if it is met, the residence basis is comparatively strong. Non-EU/EFTA retirees, by contrast, are admitted only exceptionally and only within a restrictive policy framework that allows the authorities to demand greater confidence that the applicant will never become a long-term public-funds case.That difference explains why the same type of evidence may be treated more generously under AFMP than under Article 28 FNIA. 29

There is **no single national retirement-income threshold** that can safely be stated for Swiss retirement residence. The legal benchmarks are dynamic. AFMP pensioners are measured against the supplementary-benefit trigger for an equivalent Swiss national; non-pension inactive persons under the AFMP are mmeasured against the social-assistance standard; and third-country retirees are assessed by official guidance against the level that would entitle a Swiss national and family members to supplementary benefits, with heightened concern for future durability. Because the supplementary-benefit regime is built from recognised expenses, countable income, household aggregation and region-sensitive rent caps, the practical threshold will vary with family composition, location and actual cost structure. 30

That is also why **household-size** **analysis** is central. A married couple is not assessed in the same way as a single retiree. Under ELG, expenses and income of spouses are aggregated,children may be included, and rent caps rise by household size. A pension figure that looks comfortable in the abstract may not clear the benchmark once the authorities factor in Swiss rent, ancillary housing costs, compulsory insurance and the size of the household. Conversely, substantial liquid assets or investment returns can strengthen a file even where regular pension income alone is modest, because EL law counts both periodic income and portions of net wealth. 18

**Accommodation** **and** **health-insurance** **costs** matter legally, not just practically. ELG expressly recognises rent and ancillary costs only up to capped maxima that vary by cost region and number of people, and also recognises the compulsory health-insurance amount. **Third-party** **guarantees** need careful treatment. For EU/EFTA inactive residence, 2C891/2022 confirms that support from a solvent third party can in principle prove sufficient means; the authorities must not impose prohibitive evidential demands, and the Federal Supreme Court accepted that a maintenance undertaking with six months' notice plus notice to the authorities can be fit for purpose. But the same case also makes clear that the evidential question is one of sufficiency and usability, not a blanket approval of informal family support. For non-EU/EFTA retirement cases, SEM's directives are materially stricter:mere family promises may fail because of uncertain enforceability, and where own means are weak the third-party support must be secured to a higher standard. 6

A further drafting trap is the treatment of **supplementary** **benefits.** For AFMP Article 24, the Supreme Court has squarely held that supoplementary benefits must be treated like social assistance for assessing whether the person has sufficient means. That is why claiming EL can jeopardise EU/EFTA inactive residence. In domestic FNIA law, however, EL is not identical to "social assistance" for every revocation ground. BGE 149 II 1 confirms that AHV/IV supplementary benefits do not themselves constitute the Article 63 social-assistance revocation ground for a settlement permit, even though they still burden public finances and may matter in the proportionality analysis. Later legal content must keep those two frameworks apart. 32

Finally, **ongoing** **public-funds** **surveillance** **is real.** The official reporting mechanisms described by SEM mean that a retirement-residence file is not only about getting over the line at grant stage. If the resource model rests on volatile assets, an unstable guarantee, or optimistic assumptions about future living costs, the risk may materialise later through social-assistance or EL reporting. In practical terms, the strongest files are the ones that show a sustainable budget with margin above the legal benchmark, not just technical compliance on the day of filing. 33

## Drafting cautions

·Do not state a single Swiss national "minimum retirement income" unless an official source for the exact proposition is cited; the law uses comparative benchmarks, not one universal sum. 34

·Do not conflate EU/EFTA inactive residence with the non-EU/EFTA retirement route; the first is treaty-based, the second is discretionary. 35

·Do not say that supplementary benefits are always the same as social assistance. They are treated that way in the AFMP Article 24 context, but not as a blanket domestic-law rule. 36

·Do not present third-party guarantees as automatically sufficient. Their reliability, enforceability and duration matter. 6

·Do not focus oonly on pension income. Swiss law and guidance also look at assets, asset income, household composition, housing costs and insurance costs. 5

·Do not ignore the role of rent and compulsory health-insurance premiums in the benchmark. 18

·Do not treat cantonal online guidance about telework or forfait fiscal as if it were federal black-letter law. 37

·Do not suggest that a person who later claims EL or social assistance faces no immigration consequences; the position depends on the route and status held, and official reporting exists. 38

·Do not describe premium reductions simplistically. They are not themselves "social assistance" for every immigration purpose, but they may still affect the budget analysis. 39

·Do not assume that the applicant's actual personal EL entitlement is the relevant migration benchmark. For EU/EFTA pensioners, VFP Article 16(2) uses the amount that would entitle a comparable Swiss national. 12
