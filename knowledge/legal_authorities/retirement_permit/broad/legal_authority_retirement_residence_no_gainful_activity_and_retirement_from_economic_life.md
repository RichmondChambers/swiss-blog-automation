# Swiss Retirement Residence No Gainful Activity and Retirement from Economic Life

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate a single, freestanding "retirement visa" regime. For EU/EFTA nationals, the relevant legal position is residence as an economically inactive person under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing rules, supported by sufficient resources and full health insurance; in that treaty setting, "retirement" is descriptive rather than constitutive, and the decisive question is whether the person falls within the non-active category. For non-EU/EFTA nationals,by contrast, retirement residence is a discretionary residence-permit route under Article 28 AIG and Article 25 VZAE for persons who are no longer gainfully active, have reached the minimum age, have special personal ties to Switzerland, and have the necessary financial means. Swiss official sources therefore require a sharp distinction between treaty-based EU/EFTA residence without gainful activity and the discretionary third-country annuitant route. 1

On the non-EU/EFTA route, the activity restriction is strict. SEM guidance summarises the cumulative conditions for retirees as including "no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad" except management of one's own assets, and the Federal Administrative Court has likewise stated that Article 25 paragraph 3VZAE bars retirees from engaging in gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad, save for management of their own wealth. The same SEM guidance also requires evidence that the applicant has genuinely withdrawn from active professional life and says that retirees must provide a confirmation to that effect and undertake not to work either in Switzerland or abroad. In practice, official cantonal materials from Geneva, Zurich and Zug all reflect this by demanding a formal written declaration or commitment not to engage in gainful activity, usually expressly extending to foreign work performed from Switzerland. 2

The difficult modern questions are remote work, consulting, board roles, volunteering and intra-family support. Swiss migration law defines gainful activity broadly: work is gainful if it is of a kind normally carried out for remuneration, even if the individual case is unpaid, and SEM guidance adds that Swiss law generally treats volunteering as gainful activity in principle. That breadth makes it risky to characterise paid advisory roles, consulting mandates, directorships or regular unpaid functionsthat substitute for paid labour as compatible with retirement status, unless they truly remain within the narrow carve-out for management of one's own assets. For family care, SEM guidance on work concepts shows that even help by relatives may count as gainful activity where it substitutes for remunerated work, subject only to a narrow short-visit "social custom" exception in limited cases; and a Federal Administrative Court judgment has expressly said that caring for grandchildren is not the legislative rationale of the retirement route. 3

EU/EFTA applicants are in a different legal position. A 2022 SEM circular on home-office work states that EU/EFTA nationals physically present in Switzerland but working from home for a foreign employer may still be treated as economically inactive under Article 24 Annex I AFMP where their work has no impact on, and no link with, the Swiss labour market and they remain integrated into the foreign organisation; the circular extends the same logjic, in comparable cases, to self-employed persons operating from home without a Swiss-market nexus. The same circular stresses, however, that work linked to Swiss clients, Swiss customer acquisition, or other local labour-market effects is not treated the same way.

That treaty-specific flexibility does not transfer to third-country annuitants, whose route is expressly conditioned on not working in Switzerland or abroad at all, otherthan managing their own assets. 4

Breach matters both at grant and renewal stage. SEM guidance on permit non-renewal states that where the purpose of the permit is no longer respected, the stay purpose is in principle treated as fulfilled and the result is non-renewal and departure; Zurich's official practice note similarly treats the non-work requirement as a continuing condition of admission. Unauthorised work also has a sanctions dimension: officialSEM materials identify Article 115 AIG as covering unlawful stay and gainful activity without authorisation, and published case law confirms convictions for unauthorised gainful activity under that provision. For later drafting, the safest formulation is therefore that non-EU/EFTA retirement residence requires genuine withdrawal from economic life, documented by written undertakings and continuing compliance, while EU/EFTA "retirement" residence depends on remaining within the treaty category of economically inactive persons and not crossing into Swiss labour-market participation. 5

## Structural overview of no-gainful-activity requirements

### Non-EU/EFTA annuitants

Article 28 AIG covers third-country nationals who are no longer gainfully active, while Article 25 VZAE supplies the operative detail. Official federal guidance treats the requirements as cumulative: minimum age, specialpersonal ties to Switzerland, necessary financial means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad except management of one's own assets. The Federal Administrative Court has confirmed both the cumulative structure and the discretionarynature of the route, and has expressly stated that Article 25 paragraph 3 VZAE prohibits gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad apart from management of one's own wealth. 6

### EU/EFTA economically inactive persons

For EU/EFTA nationals, the governing category is not "retiree" as a separate immigration class but residence as a non-active person under Article 24 Annex I AFMP, implemented domestically by the VFP and explained in SEM's VFP directives. SEM's fact sheet on residence without gainful activity says the AFMP grants a residence right to economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals such as retirees, students and other non-active persons if they have sufficient resources and health insurance. Because this is a treaty-right framework, the analysis centres on whether the person remains "non-active", not on whether they satisfy a third-country annuitant concept of retirement from all economic life. 7

### The general Swiss concept of gainful activity

Across Swiss migration law, gainful activity is interpreted broadly. SEM's Chapter 4 guidance states that a self-employed or employed activity is treated as gainful activity whenever it is normally remunerated, even if in the concrete case it is unpaid or only minimally compensated, and that the labour-market effect is the key criterion. The same guidance says volunteer work is, in principle, treated as employed gainful activity, because it concerns tasks that are unpaid in fact but could theoretically be performed by a third person for pay. It also says that work relationships between family members or relatives generally count as gainful activity. 8

### Evidence of retirement from economic life

Statute and ordinance do not provide a separate, exhaustive definition of "retirement from economic life", but the official sources give the practical meaning. SEM guidance says retirees must provide a confirmation showing that they have actually withdrawn from active professional life and must

undertake not to pursue gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad. The Federal Administrative Courthas added that the "retiree" concept in Article 28 AIG is to be understood broadly as covering persons who no longer exercise professional activity. Cantonal materials operationalise this through mandatory written declarations and motivation letters. 9

### Consequences for grant and renewal

The no-work rule is not merely an entry condition. SEM's general guidance on residence permits states that, where the purpose of the permit is no longer observed, the purpose is treated as exhausted and this generally leads to non-renewal and departure. Zurich's internal guidance for retirees likewise treats the non-work requirement as a continuing substantive condition; Geneva and Zug expressly require formal undertakings not to work. At the sanctions level, Article 115 AIG addresses unlawful gainful activity without authorisation, and published decisions record convictions for work without permission. 10

## Primary legal texts

### Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, SR 142.20, Article 28.** The provision governs admission of foreign nationals who are no longer gainfully active. In close paraphrase, it allows admission of a person who is no longer economically active if the minimum age set by the Federal Council is met, special personal ties to Switzerland exist, and the necessary financial means are available. Official case law confirms that the provision is discretionary, that the conditions are cumulative, and that even full compliance does not create a legal entitlement. This is the anchor provision for non-EU/EFTA retirement residence and for any later analysis of what "retired" means in Swiss permit law. 25

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 11.** In close paraphrase,foreign nationals who wish to pursue gainful activity in Switzerland need authorisation, and gainful activity is understood broadly as any activity normally carried out for remuneration, whether employed or self-employed, even where the concrete case is unpaid. SEM's Chapter 4 guidance repeatedly applies that broad concept and uses labour-market impact as the operative test. This provision matters because it explains why unpaid directorships, volunteer roles or family support are not automatically outside immigration work rules. 26

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Articles 32 and 33, read with SEM guidance on** **permit purpose and** **conditions.** Residence permits may be linked to conditions, and the SEM directives explain that if the purpose of the permit is no longer respected, the permit purpose is in principle treated as exhausted, with non-renewal and departure following. This matters because "retirement without gainful activity" is not a one-off evidential fact but a continuing permit purpose. 27

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 115.** OfficiaI SEM materials identify Article 115 AIG as the criminal provision covering unlawful entry or stay, including gainful activity without the required authorisation, and published decisions refer to convictions under Article 115 paragraph 1

<!-- 6 -->

letter c AIG for unauthorised employment. This matters because activity breaches can have both administrative and penal consequences. 24

## Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment

**Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, SR 142.201, Article 25.** In close paraphrase, paragraph 1 sets the minimum admission age for retirees at 55; paragraph 2 gives examples of the "special personal ties" requirement; paragraph 3 provides that retirees may not carry out gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad, except management of their own assets; paragraph 4 links the "necessary financial means" requirement to a supplementary-benefits benchmark. Article 25 is the decisive operative text for non-EU/EFTA retirees and supplies the explicit own-asset-management carve-out. 28

**Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, Articles 1 to 3 and Article 1a as explained** **by** **SEM.** SEM's Chapter 4 materials interpret the ordinance as defining gainful activity broadly, including employed work, self-employment and cross-border service provision, whenever the activity is normally remunerated, regardless of modest or absent actual payment. The 2022 SEM home-office circular adds, for third-country nationals, that under Article 1a paragraph 1 VZAE employed activity includes work for an employer seated in Switzerland or abroad, regardless of whether salary is paid inside or outside Switzerland. These rules matter because they are the main official basis for analysing remote work, consultancy and foreign-employer activity from Swiss territory. 29

## Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and implementing ordinance

**Agreement between the Swiss Confederation and the European Community and its Member** **States on the Free Movement of Persons, SR 0.142.112.681, Annex I, Article 24.** In close paraphrase, Article 24 gives a residence right to EU/EFTA nationals who do not pursue economic activity, provided that they have sufficient financial means and comprehensive health insurance. SEM's VFP directives and fact sheet expressly include retirees within that non-active category. This matters because EU/EFTA retirement residence is not built on Article 28 AIG but on treaty rights for economically inactive persons. 30

**Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons, SR 142.203, Articles 16 and 17, as explained in SEM's** **VFP** **directives.** Article 16 governs sufficient resources; SEM explains that, for retirees drawing social-security pensions, resources must exceed the level that would entitle a Swiss national to supplementary benefits. Article 17 governs permit duration, which is generally five years for economically inactive persons but may in individual cases be limited to two years. These provisions matter because the economically inactive category depends on continuing resource and insurance compliance, not on a third-country concept of total retirement from all foreign work. 31

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

### Official federal guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal administrative source for non-EU/EFTA retirees is the SEM AIG Directive chapter onresidence without gainful activity. It states expressly that the Article 28 AIG and Article 25VZAE conditions are cumulative; lists "no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad (except own-asset management)" as one of those conditions; requires proof that the person has actually retired; requires an undertaking not to work in Switzerland or abroad; and treats the route as a cantonal discretionary decision subject to federal approval. That combination makes the activity restriction both substantive and evidential. 32

SEM's Chapter 4 guidance on gainful activity is equally important because the retirement texts themselves do not enumerate every modern working pattern. Chapter 4 says that gainful activity is interpreted broadly, that the question is whether the activity is generally performed for remuneration on the Swiss labour market, that actual lack of payment is not decisive, and that volunteer work is in principle treated as gainful activity. It also explains that family-member childcare or support can amount to gainful activity depending on whether it substitutes for paid labour, albeit with narrow exceptions in short-visit contexts. 8

For EU/EFTA nationals, the core federal sources are SEM's VFP directives, the SEM fact sheet on residence without gainful activity, and the 5 April 2022 SEM circular on home-office work. Those materials confirm that retirees are treated under the general treaty category of non-active persons,and the circular gives a materially important modern clarification: EU/EFTA nationals working from Switzerland for a foreign employer may still be treated as non-active under Article 24 Annex I AFMP where there is no Swiss labour-market nexus and no Swiss customer-facing activity. The same circular says the same can apply to self-employed persons in comparable circumstances. 33

The same 2022 circular also underscores why one must not generalise that EU/EFTA approach to third-country retirees. For third-country nationals, SEM emphasises the broader AIG/VZAE concept of gainful activity and the wider employer concept in Article 1a VZAE. That supports a conservative reading for annuitant cases: the specific Article 25 paragraph 3 ban on working in Switzerland or abroad is not displaced by the treaty-driven AFMP home-office practice. 22

## Practical legal analysis

### Why the third-country route is stricter

For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the legal structure is intentionally restrictive. The Federal Administrative Courtdescribes Article 28 AIG as a discretionary exception within Switzerland's restrictive admissions framework, and SEM's guidance makes actual retirement and non-engagement in work part of the cumulative admission criteria. The activity restriction is therefore not an incidental condition but part of the very legal identity of the route. That is why the safest reading is that the holder must have withdrawn from professional life in substance, not merely avoid formal Swiss employment contracts. 39

This has a direct impact on remote work and foreign work. Because Article 25 paragraph 3 VZAE expressly forbids gainful activity "in Switzerland or abroad", a paid foreign consultancy carried out from Switzerland remains problematic even if there is no Swiss client base. The statutory text removes the need to show a Swiss labour-market connection for third-country retirees; by design, the retirement route excludes gainful activity abroad as well. The federal and cantonal materials reviewed do not provide any retirement-specific carve-out for paid remote work beyond management of one's own assets. 40

### Why the EU/EFTA route is different

The EU/EFTA framework turns on economic inactivity under the AFMP rather than a domestic annuitant model. SEM's circular on home office shows that treaty law can classify some persons as "non-active" even while they continue to derive income from foreign work, provided that the work is for a foreign employer or comparable self-employed activity, they remain integrated into the foreign organisation, and there is no impact on or link with the Swiss labour market. SEM goes so far as to say that mere physical presence with a laptop in Switzerland does not by itself create worker status for AFMP purposes. That is a real and important exception, but it is treaty-specific. 21

<!-- 9 -->

The limiting principle is Swiss-market nexus. The same circular states that if there is customer acquisition or customer servicing in Switzerland, or another direct impact on the Swiss economy,the case moves away from Article 24 non-activity. In practical terms, this means that EU/EFTA retirees may in some cases preserve "without activity" status while drawing income from certain foreign home-office functions, but not where the activity is functionally directed at the Swiss labour or services market. Later drafting must therefore keep the AFMP position distinct from the stricter Article 28/25 annuitant route. 41

### Consultancy, advisory roles, board mandates and asset management

Swiss official retirement-specific materials do not list consultancy, advisory mandates or directorships one by one. The legal answer must therefore be built from the broad concept of gainful activity and the own-asset-management carve-out. Paid consultancy or paid advisory activity will ordinarily be of a kind normally remunerated and will therefore strongly tend to qualify as gainful activity; for third-country retirees, that makes it incompatible both in Switzerland and abroad. Even unpaid advisory or board roles are not automatically safe, because Swiss migration law looks to the ordinary remunerated nature of the activity rather than only to whether money was actually paid in the concrete case. 42

The only express safe harbour is management of one's own assets. That plainly covers passive holding and management of personal wealth. The difficult line is where a person says that a board seat, strategic advisory role or management function is merely part of "own wealth management". If the role is compensated, operational, client-facing, managerial, or resembles a function normally sold on the market, the better view is that authorities are likely to treat it as gainful activity rather than passive asset management. No official retirement-specific source reviewed here gives a broader safe harbour than the ordinance's narrow own-asset-management exception, so later drafting should present this boundary as fact-sensitive and risk-prone rather than settled in the applicant's favour. 43

### Volunteering and community activity

The instinctive assumption that volunteering is always safe is not supported by Swiss migration guidance. SEM's general work guidance says volunteer work is, in principle, employed gainful activity because the task may be one that a third person would usually perform for pay. That does not mean every civic, charitable or community activity will inevitably breach a retirement permit, but it does mean there is no blanket exemption for "volunteering" as such. The legal question remains whether the activity is of a type normally remunerated and whether it competes with or substitutes for paid labour. 18

For retirement cases, the absence of a dedicated official safe harbour is the key point. A retiree attending associations, cultural events or local community life should not in itself create difficulty; indeed, those matters may support personal-ties evidence for the third-country route. But structured roles with regular hours, operational responsibilities, replaceable labour content, or payments/benefits in kind may be viewed differently. The prudent internal position is therefore that occasional, genuinely civic participation is lower risk, while recurring or organisation-facing volunteer functions should be cleared with the competent canton before they are described as permitted. 44

### Family caregiving and household support

Family support raises a distinct risk. SEM's Chapter 4 guidance says employment relationships between family members are generally gainful activity, and discusses childcare by ascendants or descendants in terms showing that the decisive question is whether the foreign relative's assistance lets the Swiss resident work or increase their working hours. The guidance recognises a narrow "social custom" or

<!-- 10 -->

favour-based exception for short visitor stays of up to 90 days in some lineal-family situations, but that exception is tightly framed. It is a poor basis for normalising regular childcare or household functions under a long-term retirement residence permit. 19

The Federal Administrative Court's judgment in C-1156/2012 reinforces that caution from a different angle.There the applicant was described as being needed for childcare and domestic help with grandchildren, and the court accepted the administration's view that childcare of grandchildren was not the legislative purpose of the retirement permit route. That does not say every grandparental interaction is prohibited work, but it shows that applications framed around providing family labour are legally vulnerable. In internal drafting, "family support" should never be presented as an unproblematic side benefitof retirementresidence. 45

#### Grant, renewal and compliance planning

The official sources point to a two-layer compliance risk: immigration-purpose risk and sanctions risk. On the first, SEM states that where the purpose of the permit is no longer respected, the stay purpose is in principle treated as fulfilled and non-renewal follows. For a retiree permit, the non-work representation is part of the admission logic itself. On the second, unauthorised work can also carry penal consequences under Article 115 AIG. As a planning matter, that means no-gainful-activity analysis must be revisited whenever the person begins any regular paid or structured role, not only when taking up formal Swiss employment. 46

The practical drafting lesson is conservative classification. If an activity looks like paid work, advice, directorship, consulting, substitute childcare, or organised volunteering, it should not be assumed compatible with a third-country retirement permit unless it clearly falls within management of one's own assets. For EU/EFTA cases, the analysis should ask a different question: does the activity still leave the person within the AFMP category of economically inactive person because it has no Swiss-market nexus, or has the person crossed into Swiss worker/self-employed territory? Those are different legal tests and should not be collapsed into a single "retirement work ban" formula. 47

## Drafting cautions

·Do not describe Swiss retirement residence as one unified "retirement visa". Swiss law distinguishes an AFMP-based EU/EFTA route for non-active persons from the discretionary Article 28 AIG / Article 25 VZAE annuitant route for third-country nationals. 48

·Do not say that all retirees must cease every source of foreign income. That is too broad for EU/ EFTA cases, where SEM accepts certain foreign home-office activity with no Swiss labour-market nexus as compatible with Article 24 Annex I AFMP. 41

·Do not reverse that nuance for third-country annuitants. For them, the official rule is no gainful activity in Switzerland **or** **abroad,** except management of one's own assets. 49

·Do not state that unpaid work is automatically permissible. Swiss migration law treats activity normally carried out for remuneration as gainful even where no remuneration is actually paid. 17

·Do not describe volunteering as automatically safe. SEM's own guidance says volunteer work is in principle treated as gainful activity, and no retirement-specific exemption was identified. 18

·Do not assume that consultancy, paid advisory work or board mandates fall within "management of own assets". The ordinance creates only a narrow carve-out for own-asset management, and anything market-facing, remunerated or operational is likely to be scrutinised as gainful activity. 43

<!-- 11 -->

·Do not frame regular grandchild care or family household help as a benign ancillary activity. Official materials show that family labour can count as gainful activity, and case law says childcare of grandchildren is not the legislative rationale of Article 28 AIG. 50

·Do not omit the evidential point that applicants are commonly required to prove genuine retirement and sign a written undertaking not to work. 51

·Do not present cantonal checklists or online forms as if they were federal law. They are important procedural and evidential indicators of practice, but they are not themselves the primary legal rule. 15

·Do not understate renewal risk. If the non-work permit purpose is no longger observed, SEM guidance says non-renewal can follow, and unauthorised work may also trigger Article 115 AIG consequences. 46
