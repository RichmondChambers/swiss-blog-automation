# Comparison of Swiss Retirement Residence,

# Lump-Sum Taxation, and Other HNWI Routes

# Executive legal summary

Swiss law separates the **immigration basis for residence** from the **tax basis for residence.** For EU/ EFTA nationals, "retirement residence" is not a separate legal route at all: it sits within the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons regime for **persons** **not pursuing** **an** **economic activity,** which requires sufficient means and all-risks health insurance and ordinarily yields a five-year B permit. For non-EU/ EFTA nationals, by contrast, retirement residence is a **distinct** **discretionary** **immigration** **route** under Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA/VZAE, requiring a minimum age of 55, special personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland **or** **abroad** other than management of one's own assets. 1

"Lump-sum taxation" is legally different again. It is a **tax** **status** for foreign nationals resident in Switzerland without Swiss gainful activity, boased on living expenses rather than ordinary income taxation. It is not, on the materials reviewed, a standalone federal residence-permit category. In practice, it must be paired with an immigration route such as EU/EFTA non-active residence,non-EU retirement residence under Article 28 FNIA, or-where justified-an exceptional permit based on important public interests / considerable cantonal fiscal interests under Article 30 FNIA and Article 32OASA/VZAE. That public-interest route does not require the Article 28 "special personal ties" criterion, but it does require relocation of the centre of life to Switzerland and majority physical presence there. 2

For route selection, the decisive questions are usually these: whether the client is EU/EFTA or not; whether they can prove the **personal-ties** test for Article 28; whether they want to continue remunerated activity abroad; whether they are prepared to run a genuine Swiss business; and whether the canton is motivated primarily by **fiscal interest** rather than retirement logic. A lump-sum tax ruling may improve the overall package, but it cannot by itself cure a weak immigration case.3

## Cantonal practice notes

Published cantonal practice is much fuller on the **tax** side than on the **migration** side. The clearest official cantonal materials reviewed were from **Geneva, Valais** and **Vaud.** By contrast, public-facing cantonal migration guidance on Article 28 retirement residence and Article 30 / 32 fiscal-interest admissions is comparatively sparse, so federal guidance and direct pre-filing contact with the chosen canton remain especially important. 21

In **Geneva,** expenditure-based taxation is openly presented as a tax mode for foreign nationals resident in Switzerland without gainful activity. Geneva states that the taxation is based on maintenance expenditure, must generally be requested on arrival or before the first assessment becomes final, and that the agreement usually takes the form of a convention valid for five years. Geneva also states expressly that **a remunerated director's function is not accepted** under cantonal practice. 22

**In** **Valais,** the official material is unusually revealing because the canton publishes a form entitled **"information for the tax and migration authorities for the obtention of a residence permit".** The form asks for the intended commune of residence, prior tax domicile, whether the applicant has been taxed in Switzerland during the previous ten years, whether the applicant intends to exercise gainful activity in Switzerland, and a detailed "universal annual expenditure" budget. That is strong official evidence that, at least in Valais, forfait planning is operationally coordinated between the tax office and the migration office. 23

**In Vaud,** the reviewed official regulation confirms the familiar forfait mechanics at cantonal level: the tax is calculated by reference to annual living expenses, at least the sevenfold rent/ rental value or triple pension price, subjectto a higher control calculation if applicable; it also containsa modified-taxation provision. The public material located was the 2015 amending regulation entering into force on 1 January 2016, so the exact **current indexed** **minimum** should be checked directly with the canton before relying on any figure indrafting. 24

## Practical legal analysis

The most important legal distinction is this: for **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** "retirement residence" and "financially self-sufficient residence" are usually the **same** **route** **described** **from** **differennt** **factual** **angles.** The governing legal basis is AFMP residence for non-active persons. If the clientis simply retired and no longer working, nothing in the official material suggests a separate EUJ/EFTA retirement permit category. By contrast, for **non-EU/EFTA** **nationals,** Article 28 FNJIA is a genuinely separate retirement route and should be analysed on its own terms. 25

That distinction matters because **Article 28 is narrower than many HNWI clients assume.** **It** is not merely a wealth test. It requires evidence of retirement, special personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means that will reliably last, and a Swiss centre of life. SEM guidance states that ties to relatives in Switzerland are not enough by themselves; the applicant needs independent socio-cultural or personal links with Switzerland. It also states that the retiree must undertake not to pursue gainful activity in Swwitzerland or abroad, except for managing personal assets. For a client with active international board mandates, consulting work, or an intention to remain commercially busy, Article 28 may therefore be the wrong vehicle even if the client is affluent. 26

<!-- 5 -->

The **fiscal-interest route** under Article 30 FNIA and Article 32 OASA/VZAE serves a different function. On the official SEM guidance reviewed, it is an exceptional route based on important public interests, including considerable cantonal fiscal interests. Its logic is not "retirement plus ties to Switzerland"; its logic is "public interest plus relocation of the centre of life to Switzerland". That makes it potentially attractive where a non-EU HNWI cannot honestly prove the Article 28 personal-ties criterion. But it comes with its oown discipline: the centre of life must move to Switzerland, majority stay is required, and any gainful activity mmay only be pursued **abroad,** save for management of one's own assets. In substance, that route is closer to a **fiscal-interest residence** **case** than to a classic retirement case. 27

The relationship between **lump-sum** **taxation** and migration should therefore be drafted carefully.Tax law and migration law interact, but they do not collapse into each other. FTA and cantonal tax sources describe expenditure-based taxation as a substitute for ordinary income taxation for qualifying foreign nationals with no Swiss gainful activity. They also impose objective computation rules: worldwide living expenses, statutory minima, multiples of rent or pension price, and a control calculation tied to Swiss-source income and certain treaty-relieved foreign income. If the conditions fail-such as Swiss nationality being acquired, or Swiss gainful activity being undertaken-ordinary taxation resumes. Legally, that shows why a "forfait residence permit" label is misleading: the permit comes from immigration law; the forfait comes from tax law. 28

**For entrepreneur** **and** **business routes,** the divide is equally sharp. EU/EFTA nationals benefit from treaty rights for self-employment, whereas third-country nationals face a demanding labour-market and economic-interest review. Official SEM materials require proof of a genuine business, proper financial and operating conditions, and a lasting positive effect on the Swiss labour market. The directives emphasise job retention / creation, regional diversification, significant investment, new orders for the Swiss economy, and a detailed business plan, and they note that initial permits for business formation are generally limited to a maximum of two years. That is a long way from a passive investor model. A large capital injection is helpful only if it evidences a qualifying Swiss businesscase. 29

**For work-based** **admission,** official SEM guidance is even clearer that this is not an HNWI convenience route. Third-country workers mnust satisfy overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, salary and employment conditions, qualifications, and-in practice-employer sponsorship. The Federal Counncil left the 2026 quotas for third-country workers unchanged at **8,500** places. That route is therefore appropriate where the client will genuinely work in Switzerland in a senior or specialist role, but it does not solve the planning problem of a wealthy client who wants Swiss residence while remaining essentially retired or managing offshore wealth. 12

**On** **family** **and** **longer-term** **planning,** the routes also diverge. EU/EFTA non-active persons have treaty-based family reunification protection, and EU/EFTA B permits are ordinarily issued for five years. Settlement permits for many EU/EFTA nationals may follow after five years of regular and uninterrupted residence under settlement treaties or reciprocity arrangements, and otherwise after the longer period applicable under Swiss law. Non-EU retirees under Article 28 may bring spouse and minor children only under the ordinary family-reunification rules, and later settlement planning is governed by the normal permit-C and integration framework, not by the retirement label or any forfait convention.In other words, a tax ruling may optimise the fiscal position, but it does not change the later **C-permit/** **integration** analysis. 30

## Drafting cautions and source list

**H. Drafting** **cautions.** The safest drafting approach is to keep the immigration ground and the tax ground expressly separate. For EU/EFTA clients, avoid describing the case as a special "retirement visa" if the real legal basis is AFMP non-active residence. For non-EU clients, identify early whether the case is really an **Article 28 retirement file, an Article 30 / 32 fiscal-interest file, a self-employed / start-up** **file,** or a **work-permit file,** because the evidential burden changes materially. In particular, every paid board seat, consultancy mandate, advisory role, family-office function, and founder title should be audited for both **migration** and **tax** risk; Geneva's published tax practice is explicitly hostile to remunerated directorships in forfait cases, and SEM guidance under Article 28 is stricter still because it bars gainful activity abroad as well. Finally, current cantonal minimum figures and current local filing mechanics should be verified with the chosen canton before finalising advice, because some public cantonal materials are older or index-linked. 31
