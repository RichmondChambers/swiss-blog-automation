# Swiss Retirement Residence Main Home,

# Physical Presence and Centre of Life

# Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate with a standalone "retirement visa" that can be maintained merely by formal registration or occasional presence. For retirement cases, the relevant question is whether the person is genuinely residing in Switzerland under the applicable residence framework: for EU/EFTA nationals, under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the implementing free-movement regime for economically inactive persons; for non-EU/EFTA nationals, under the discretionary retirement route in Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA. In both settings, the legal analysis is about residence permits and factual residence, not lifestyle branding. 1

For EU/EFTA nationals, the legal position is materially stronger. Official SEM materials state that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals, including retirees, have a right to reside if they have sufficient financial resources and comprehensive health and accident insurance; the usual permit is a B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years, and it is normally renewed so long as the conditions continue to be met. SEM's free-movement directives also make clear that this residence status is tied to a specific purpose of stay and remains conditional on continued compliance. 2

For non-EU/EFTA retirees, the position is more exacting. Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA require at least age 55, special personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient resources and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad except management of one's own assets; even if thoose conditions are met, the route remains discretionary. Critically for the present topic, SEM's AIG directives expressly state that retirees "must have the centre of life in Switzerland", and that renewal is refused if it is later established that the centre of their life relations is not in Switzerland. That is the clearest federal administrative statement on the main-home requirement in the retirement context. 3

Swiss civil-law domicile concepts reinforce the same direction of travel. Article 23 of the Civil Code defines domicile as the place where a person resides with the intention of settling, and no person may have more than one domicile at a time. This does not mean residence law mechanically follows civil-law domicile in every case, but it does underline that Swiss law is concerned with real residence and the intention of permanent settlement, not a papoer address alone. Official cantonal practice materials then translate that general idea into residence-control criteria such as housing, the location of family and social life, the duration of stays in Switzerland and abroad, and the reasons for recurrent absences. 4

On physical presence, the clearest black-letter rule is not a "183-day rule" but the statutory lapse rule. Article 61(2) FNIA provides that, if the holder leaves Switzerland without giving notice of departure, a residence or settlement permit expires after six months abroad. Article 79 OASA adds that temporary return trips to Switzerland for visits, tourism or business do not interrupt that period. SEM's OLCP directives apply the same six-month lapse rule to EU/EFTA residence permits. In other words, a permit holder cannot preserve a Swiss residence merely by engineering brief return visits before each six-month point. 5

Beyond that hard rule, the official materials reviewed for this pack do not support a universal federal immigration requirement of "183 days" per year for retirement residence. The closest official migration-related proxy found is a Zurich cantonal instruction, based on federal case law, stating that with repeated long absences over several years, foreigners must as a **rule** be in Switzerland for the majority of the year, meaning at least six months, failing which there is a rebuttable presumption that the centre of life has been abandoned. That is a cantonal practice point in the permit-lapse context, not a national statutory retirement threshold. By contrast, "183 days" language appears in official Swiss tax guidance for principal-residence taxation questions, which is a different legal field. 6

The practical consequence is that retirement applicants and permit holders should plan on Switzerland being their reaI main home: housing should be genuine, family and daily life should be anchored in Switzerland if the family unit is relocating, and repeated or lengthy split-residence patterns should be documented and explained with care. Official canton pages and forms show that lease documents or host attestations, the commune or canton of intended residence, proof of resources, and departure/ renewal formalities are all operational points at which factual residence is tested. Registration matters, but official cantonal and statistical materials also show that register status and permit allocation are administrative constructs; they do not conclusively prove that Switzerland is in fact the centre of the person's life. 7

# Structural overview of the main-home and centre-of-life framework

**Legal nature** **of the** **requirement.** Swiss retirement residence is a residence-permit matter. There is no freestanding "retirement visa" code in Swiss law. The key legal question is whether the holder is genuinely residing in Switzerland under the relevant permit basis and still fulfils the permit purpose and conditions. 8

**EU/EFTA** **route.** EU/EFTA retirees fall under the AFMP framework for persons without economic activity. They benefit from a treaty-based right of residence if they have sufficient means and full health and accident insurance. The permit normally used is the five-year B EU/EFTA permit, though SEM guidance allows earlier review where means are uncertain; the permit remains linked to its purpose and conditions, and six months abroad causes expiry. 2

**Non-EU/EFTA** **route.** Third-country retirees rely on Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA. This route is discretionary and cumulative: age 55+, special personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means, and no gainful activity other than managing one's own assets. For this route, SEM expressly adds the substantive residence requirement that the retiree's centre of life must be in Switzerland, with renewal refused if it is not. 9

**Main** **home** **and** **centre** **of** **life.** Swiss Civil Code domicile is the place of residence with intention to settle, and only one domicile exists at a time. Cantonal practice built on federal case law assesses the centre of life by reference to the focus of family, social and private relations, living arrangements, the amount of time actually spent in Switzerland, the reasons for absences, and the balance of ties abroad and in Switzerland. 4

**Physical presence.** The clear national rule is the six-month lapse rule for residence permnits abroad without departure notice; temporary returns for visits, tourism or business do not stop the clock. There is no official federal migration rule found that prescribes a generic "183 days" for retirement residence. A majority-of-the-year idea appears only asa rebuttable cantonal practice point when repeated absences raise a centre-of-life issue. 10

**Formal registration** **versus** **factual residence.** Official forms and commune procedures require registration, a Swiss address, and often a lease or host declaration. But official statistical material itself shows that "principal residence" in the register for foreigners is tied to the commune for which the B or C permit is granted and does not itself resolve any foreign domicile issue. In practice, registration is necessary evidence, not conclusive proof of real residence. 11

**Renewal and** **monitoring.** Cantonal authorities issue and renew permits, receive departure notifications, and check compliance through renewal cycles and adddress-based administration. Geneva requires ddeparture notification for B/C holders leaving for more than six months and also when the person moves their main residence to another canton; Basel-Stadt sends renewal forms to the registered private address; Zurich instructions explicitly frame the enquiry around purpose of stay, canton of residence and centre-of-life evidence. 12

# Official guidance and cantonal practice

The most important federal administrative source is SEM's **AIG** **directives** as at 1 January 2026. In the retirement section, SEM states that the Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA conditions are cumulative; repeats the age, personal-ties, finances and no-work requirements; explains that ties to Switzerland must be independent of family ties alone; requires a written confirmation that the applicants have retired from professional life and will not work; and, most importantly for this pack, states that retirees must have their centre of life in Switzerland and that renewal is refused if the centre of life is elsewhere. The same section also notes that issuance of the permit is subject to SEM approval. 17

The corresponding **SEM** **OLCP** **directives** as at 1 January 2026 are the key federal guide for EU/EFTA retirees. They state that retirees and other economically inactive persons may reside in another contracting state with their family members if they have sufficient means and illness/accident insurance covering all risks; that the permit for persons without gainful activity is generally valid for five years but may be called back for review after two years; that it may be revoked or not renewed if means or

insurance cease to be sufficient; that the permit is linked to a specific purpose of stay; and that EU/EFTA residence permits also lapse after six months abroad. 29

SEM's shorter **factsheet on residing in Switzerland without gainful activity** confirms the same architecture in simpler form: EU/EFTA economically inactive persons may stay if they have sufficient means and full health/accident insurance; a five-year B EU/EFTA permit is issued for stays beyond three months; and the authorities may shorten or withdraw the permit if the person no longer has the necessary resources. ch.ch's official permit pages complement that by emphasising that permits are required for stays over three months, are dealt with through the communeand canton of residence, and differ according to nationality and purpose. 30

**On** **cantonalpractice,** Geneva is useful for procedure and evidential handling. Its official "without gainful activity" pages distinguish clearly between EU/EFTA and third-country applicants; require third-country applications in principle to be filed before entry; and require lease documents or a host attestation, proof of resources and, for third-country retirees, a motivation letter and formal undertaking not to work. Geneva's departure pages also require B and C permit holders to notify departure for absences of more than six months and, even for shorter absences, where the main residence moves to another canton; permanent departure terminates the residence rights and a new authorisation is needed on return. These are procedural and administrative points, not separate federal legal tests, but they are highly relevant to monitoring of residence substance. 31

Zurich's official migration instructions are particularly valuable for **residence-substance** **analysis.** In its guidance on non-working residence, Zurich asks third-country retirees to explain why residence is being taken in the canton, identify the intended commune, detail their ties to the canton, and provide written confirmation that no work will be undertaken in Switzerland or abroad. Separately, Zurich's instruction on permit expiry states that repeated long stays abroad make the centre of life decisive; that, as a rule, the person should spend more than half of the year in Switzerland; and that the enquiry should examine housing, family, duration of presence, reasons for absences, and social/private ties. Again, this is cantonal practice rather than black-letter national law, but it is a rare official articulation of how centre-of-life issues are actually assessed. 32

Basel-Stadt's official EU/EFTA form for persons without gainful activity is also useful evidentially. It asks for present and future residential address, duration of desired stay, monthly housing costs and supporting financial documents, and explains that the permit for economically inactive EU/EFTA persons is generally valid for five years but may be limited or withdrawn if means or insurance are inadequate. As with Geneva and Zurich, this is proocedural rather than normative authority, but it shoows how the "real residence" question is operationalised through address, housing and cost evidence. 33

# Practical legal analysis

The main-home issue is legally different for EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA retirees, but factually important for both. EU/EFTA retirees stand on a treaty-based residence right if they remain financially self-sufficient and insured. That gives them a stronger legal footing at the initial grant stage. Third-country retirees, by contrast, are asking to be admitted under a narrow and discretionary retirement exception. For them, any sign that Switzerland will be only a secondary base, seasonal residence or family-visit platform is more likely to undermine the application, because SEM already insists that their centre of life must be in Switzerland. 34

The official sources do not present "main home" as a single mechanical test. Swiss law instead combines several layers. First, the civil-law concept of domicile requires residence with intention to settle. Secondly, residence permits are tied to a specific purpose of stay and can lapse by operation of law after sustained absence. Thirdly, administrative guidance and cantonal practice examine where the person's life is actually centred. For retirement cases, that means the enquiry is not limited to where the person is registered; it is about where they in fact live, where their home is, where their closest daily relations are concentrated, and whether their conduct is consistent with a genuine relocation to Switzerland. 35

<!-- 8 -->

That is why the common shorthand oof "183 days" should be handled very cautiously. In the federal migration materials reviewed for this pack, no general retirement-residence rule requiring 183 days in Switzerland was located. The hard national rule is the six-month lapse rule for residence permits abroad, together with the rule that temporary visits do not interrupt that period. Zurich's official instruction then adds a practical, rebuttable majority-of-year presumption in repeated-absence cases. By contrast, the "183 days" formulation appeared in official tax guidance about principal residence and foreign work. As a drafting matter, it is therefore safer to say that Swiss immigration law looks to real residence, permit purpose and the centre of life, with six months abroad being the decisive lapse threshold and majority-year presence only a cantonal practice marker in some contexts. 36

The evidence point is equally important. Official materials directly ask for a Swiss lease or host attestation, the commune or canton where residence will be taken, proof of resources, and in some cases a motivation letter and a no-work undertaking. Zurich's instruction shows what lies behind those documents: officials want to know why the person is choosing that canton, what ties they really have there, how much time they actually spend in Switzerland, what their living arrangements are abroad and in Switzerland, where their family and social circle are, and whether their social life is embedded locally. The reviewed official materials do **not** provide a closed statutory checklist mentioning banking or healthcare by name. Those matters should therefore be treated as evidentially helpful, not as separate legal tests: local doctors, health-insurance administration, ordinary day-to-day banking,and similar indicators may strengthen the inference that Swiss residence is genuine, but the formal legal enquiry remains one of actual settled life and the centre of personal and private relations. 37

Split residence and frequent travel are therefore not prohibited in the abstract, but they are risky. Zurich's official instruction recognises that genuine "frequent travellers" can still keep their centre of life in Switzerland where stronger family, social and private ties remain here. But the same instruction treats repeated long absences as a centre-of-life problem and requires a holistic assessment.That is entirely consistent with federal case law on permits lapsing despite intermittent returns for visits or business and with SEM's specific stance that retirees must genuinely have their centre of life in Switzerland. In practice, someone who keeps a comfortable Swiss apartment but spends most of the year abroad, keeps their real family and social base abroad, and returns to Switzerland only episodically may find that registration and property ownership do not save the permit. 38

Finally, formal registration and factual residence should never be conflated. Official statistical guidance shows that, for foreigners, "principal residence" in the residents' register corresponds administratively to the commune for which the B or C permit was granted, while a possible foreign domicile is not captured by Swiss residents' registers. That is a strong indication that registration is an administrative starting point, not a complete legal answer. Geneva's departure rules reinforce the same point by requiring notification when the main residence moves elsewhere, evenbefore any formal lapse dispute arises. For retirement drafting, the correct formulation is that registration, a lease, and a permit card are necessary indicators of Swiss residence, but the decisive question remains whether Switzerland is truly the person's principal home in substance. 39

# Drafting cautions

·Do not describe Swiss retirement residence as a single national "retirement visa" scheme; distinguish residence permits, AFMP free-movement residence, and the discretionary FNIA/OASA retirement route. 8

·Do **not** imply that EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA retirees face the same legal test. EU/EFTA retirees have a treaty-based residence right if the conditions are met; third-country retirees rely on a discretionary admission power. 40

·Do **not** state or imply that formal commune registration alone proves compliance with retirement residence conditions. Swiss law and official practice look to factual residence and the centre of life. 41

·Do **not** present "183 days" as a universal Swiss immigration rule for retirees unless a route-specific official source actually says so. The reviewed migration materials did not reveal such a federal rule. 42

·Do **not** omit the six-month lapse rule, or suggest that short return visits automatically preserve a permit. Article 79 OASA says temporary visit, tourism or business stays do not interrupt the period. 43

·Do **not** understate the centre-of-life requirement for third-country retirees. SEM expressly says retirees must have their centre of life in Switzerland and that renewal is refused if they do not. 17

·Do **not** turn Zurich's majority-of-the-year formulation into black-letter federal law. It is a cantonal practice point, albeit one grounded in federal case law. 21

·Do **not** treat owning Swiss property as sufficient, by itself, either for personal ties or for proving the main home. Official federal guidance says property ownership alone is not enough for the personal-ties requirement. 17

·Do **not** over-claim on "banking" or "healthcare" as named legal tests. Official materials more directly cite housing, family, social ties, duration of presence and local integration; banking and healthcare are better treated as supporting evidence by inference. 44

·Do **not** ignore cantonal procedure. Official Geneva, Zurich and Basel materials show that lease papers, host attestations, registered residential address, departure notifications and renewal administration are practical control points for residence-substance issues. 45
