# Executive legal summary

A. **Executive** **legal summary.** Swiss law does not operate a single autonomous "retirement visa" category. For retirement residence planning, the decisive distinction is between, first, EU/EFTA nationals who rely on treaty-based residence rights under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and, secondly, non-EU/EFTA nationals whno seek discretionary admission as retired persons under Article 28FNIA and Article 25 OASA. Those two routes differ not only in eligibility, but also in legal strength, permit duration logic, renewal risk, and long-term settlement planning. 1

For EU/EFTA retirees, the underlying residence right is treaty-based. Annex I, Article 24 AFMP provides that a person of a contracting state wvho is not pursuing an economic activity in the host state is to receive a residence permit of at least five years if they can show sufficient financial means for themselves and their family and comprehensive sickness insurance. The permit is automatically renewed for at least five years so long as the admission conditions remain satisfied. However, the treaty and the implementing ordinance also allow the authorities to require an earlier revalidation after the first two years, and the current SEM guidance states that if means or insurance cease to be sufficient, revocation or refusal of renewal is possible. EU/EFTA retirees therefore have a comparatively strong route, but it remains conditional throughout its life. 2

For non-EU/EFTA retirees, Article 28 FNIA is materially narrower and structurally weaker. The statute says retired foreign nationals "may beadmitted" if they meet the minimum age fixed by the Federal Council, have special personal relations to Switzerland, and have the required financial means. Article 25 OASA then fixes the minimum age at 55, requires particular personal ties to Switzerland, prohibits gainful activity in Switzerland and abroad except management of one's own assets, and links financial sufficiency to the complementary-benefits benchmark. This is not an entitlement route. The SEM's LEI directives treat retired-person admission as subject to cumulative conditions and state that grant of the residence permit is subject to prior SEM approval. In practical terms, the status granted is a residence permit B, typically issued first for one year and afterwards extendable,commonly in two-year increments where the circumstances justify it. 3

Renewal, for both routes, is not simply a question of whether the card has expired. The decisive issue is whether the holder still meets the substantive basis of stay. For EU/EFTA retirees, that means continuing self-sufficiency and full insurance under Annex I, Article 24 AFMP. For non-EU/EFTA retirees, the current federal materials are stricter: renewal may be refused if admission conditions are no longer met, if the person's centre of interests is no longer in Switzerland, or if the person no longer respects the approved purpose of stay. This means that residence planning must be built around ongoing compliance, not just initial approval. 4

<!-- 1 -->

Absence and relocation are central risk areas. Under Article 61 FNIA, residence and settlement permits lapse, among other cases, where the person leaves Switzerland without notifying the authorities and remains abroad beyond the statutory period; the implementing ordinance adds that temporary stays abroad for visits, tourism or business do not interrupt that clock. For EU/EFTA non-active residents, Annex I, Article 24 AFMP expressly says that interruptions not exceeding six consecutive months do not affect permit validity. The federal guidance on settlement permits further stresses that short return visits do not preserve a permit once the centre of interests has genuinely shifted abroad. The sources reviewed forthis pack do not support reducing the immigration analysis to a simple "183 days" formula. 5

Long-term planning must distinguish sharply between a renewable B permit and subsequent permanent-status objectives. A C permit is not the automatic end-point of retirement residence. For EU/ EFTA nationals, the AFMP does not regulate settlement permits; the SEM states that C-permit eligibility instead follows the FNIA and establishment agreements, with some nationalities eligible after five years and others after ten. For non-EU/EFTA retirees, Article 28 FNIA creates no special shoortcut to settlement: the ordinary Article 34 FNIA framework applies, including the general residence periods and integration requirements. Ordinary naturalisation is a further, separate step and requires a C permit plus ten years' residence; stays on B and C count, but L-permit stays do not. Importantly for retirees, OASA Article 77e states that participation in economic life can be satisfied where a person can maintain themselves and their family through income, assets, or third-party benefits to which they are entitled, so retirement itself does not necessarily block later C-permit planning. 6

# Structural overview of permit duration and renewal

## B. StructuraI overview of permit duration and renewal.

**EU/EFTA** **retirees.** The legal basis is Annex I, Article 24 AFMP, read with Articles 16 and 17 OLCP and current SEM guidance. The status is a B EU/EFTA permit for economically inactive persons. Its ordinary validity is at least five years, with automatic renewal for at least five years while the conditions continue to be met. Authorities may require revalidation after two years where they consider that necessary, especially if financial sufficiency appears uncertain.7

**Non-EU/EFTA** **retirees.** The legal basis is Article 28 FNIA together with Article 25 OASA. The resulting status is a residence permit B under the ordinary residence-permit framework, not an independent special permit type. Under Article 58 OASA and the SEM LEI directives, the first residence permit is normally issued for one year and can then be prolonged, usually in two-year increments, subject to motivated exceptions. The initial grant is treated in SEM guidance as requiring prior approval by the SEM. 8

**Renewal tests.** EU/EFTA renewal turns on continued sufficient means and health/accident insurance under the treaty regime. Non-EU/EFTA renewal turns on continued satisfaction oof the retired-person conditions and, under OASA approval rules, continued presence of the centre of interests in Switzerland and continued adherence to the authorised purpose of stay. The sources reviewed do not identify a single fixed nationwide "retirement amount" or a retirement-specific annual review formula; rather, the authorities reassess whether the legal basis for the existing permit remains intact. 9

**Insurance, accommodation and household continuity.The** federal sources are explicit on continuing insurance; they are less explicit on housing as a standalone renewal ground in retirement cases, but Swiss residence permits remain tied to actual residence in Switzerland and registration at the place of residence. In practice, continued Swiss residence, declared address, and the reality that Switzerland

<!-- 2 -->

remains the main home are therefore part of renewal preparation, even where the black-letter renewal rules speak more directly in terms of permit conditions and centre of interests. 10

**Absence** **and** **lapse.** For EU/EFTA non-active residents, interruptions below six consecutive months do not of themselves invalidate the permit. UJnder the FNIA/OASA framework more generally, notice of departure or an unnotified stay abroad beyond the statutory period triggers lapse, and temporary visits back to Switzerland do not interrupt that period. Residence-substance therefore matters both legally and evidentially. 5

**Long-term** **planning.** A C permit and ordinary naturalisation are separate legal frameworks. EU/EFTA nationals may, depending on nationality and establishment-agreement position, reach C after five or ten years; the AFMP itself does not regulate C. Third-country retirees have no retirement-specific C route and must satisfy the ordinary settlement-permit framework. Ordinary naturalisation requires a C permit and ten years' residence in Switzerland, with B- and C-permit years counting and L-permit years excluded. 11

**E. Official guidance and administrative materials.** The most useful federal guidance for this topic is current and relatively consistent. The SEM factsheet on residence in Switzerland without gainful activity states that EU/EFTA non-active persons, including retirees, have a right of residence if they and their

<!-- 8 -->

family have sufficient financial resources and full health/accident cover; it adds that a five-year B EU/ EFTA permit is issued, automatically renewed while conditions continue, and may be revalidated after two years if means look insecure. The OLCP directives repeat the same structure in more detail and explicitly say that if the authorities find insufficient financial means or insufficient insurance cover, the permit may be revoked or the extension refused. 35

For third-country retirees, the SEM LEI directives are especially important because they go beyond the bare text of Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA. They restate the cumulative conditions, confirm that no gainful activity is permitted in Switzerland or abroad apart from management of one's own assets, explain that property ownership or business links alone are not decisive, stress that personal ties must be with Switzerland itself and not merely with relatives in Switzerland, and state that grant of a residence permit is subject to SEM prior approval. The same directives also state, in the general chapter on residence permits, that the initial permit is in principle issued for no more than one year and may thereafter be prolonged in two-year increments where the concrete circumstances justify it. 36

The SEM's public pages on the B EU/EFTA permit and C EU/EFTA permit are also operationally important. The B-permit page confirms that EU/EFTA nationals without gainful activity are entitled to a B permit if they prove sufficient means and full insurance. The C-permit page states expressly that the AFMP does not regulate C-permit acquisition; instead, settlement follows the FNIA and establishment agreements, and certain EU/EFTA nationalities benefit from a five-year route while others do not. 37

For long-term planning beyond the B permit, the SEM's ordinary naturalisation pages make two points that matter directly in retirement-roadmap drafting. First, ordinary naturalisation requires ten years' residence and a C permit. Secondly, time spent on B and C permits counts, while time spent on an L permit does not. These pages therefore support the planning distinction between "renewable residence" and "settlement/naturalisation-ready residence". 25

Finally, two federal circulars matter on the public-funds side. The joint SEM-OFAS circular on complementary benefits confirms that the bodies paying comnplementary benefits must report those payments to migration authorities because they can affect a foreign national's right to stay. The separate SEM circular on social assistance, as updated to April 2025, records that the former mandatory SEM-approval procedure for extending certain third-country B permits with substantial social-assistance dependency has been abolished from 1 April 2025, while leaving cantonal authorities free to seek SEM approval where needed under OASA Article 85(3). 38

G. **Practical** **legal** **analysis.** The route architecture explains most duration and renewal outcomes. EU/ EFTA retirees start from a position of stronger legal security because, if they meet the AFMP conditions, the state must recognise the residence right and issue the corresponding permit.That is why the default duration is five years and renewal is framed as automatic while the conditions continue. By contrast, third-country retirees are in a discretionary admission regime from the outset; there is no comparable treaty entitlement, which is why the permit is generally shorter, more reviewable, and more sensitive to purpose drift. 45

For third-country retirees, the most important renewal insight is that the permit is not simply "for retirees" in the abstract; it is a residence permit granted on a very specific factual basis. OASA Article 86makes that clear by linking apoproval of extension to continued satisfaction of admission conditions, continued Swiss centre of interests, and continued respect for the approved purpose of stay. In a retirement case, this can capture several different problems at once: resumption of paid roles, a return to living mainly abroad, or financial deterioration that undermines self-sufficiency. 46

Public-funds analysis is an especially delicate renewal issue. For EU/EFTA retirees, the AFMP/OLCP regime measures sufficiency of means against Swiss welfare benchmarks, and the SEM guidance expressly says that calling on complementary benefits after the permit has been issued can lead to revocation or non-renewal. At the same time, one should not flatten all benefits into "social assistance". The joint SEM-OFAS circular shows that complementary benefits are separately reportable because they can affect residence rights, while the Federal Supreme Court has held in ATF 149 II 1 that complementary benefits are not the same thing as social assistance for the specific revocation ground in Article 63(1)(c) FNIA. Drafting on long-term renewal must keep that distinction intact. 47

<!-- 10 -->

Residence-substance is the other major planning point. The sources reviewed do not support a stand-alone immigration rule that a retiree must simply spend "183 days" in Switzerland to keep status. What the federal rules do show is more nuanced: EU/EFTA treaty law protects interruptions of up to six consecutive months; the FNIA/OASA framework causes lapse after longer absence abroad; short visitor or business trips back to Switzerland do not stop the lapse clock; and, in third-country approval cases, extension can fail where the centre of interests is no longer in Switzerland. In practice,the real question is whether Switzerland remains the person's actual principal place of life and authorised purpose of stay. 48

Long-term planning therefore needs a staged approach. The first stage is a durable, renewable B permit with clean compliance history. The second is settlement planning: for EU/EFTA nationals, whether and when C becomes available depends on nationality and establishment-agreement position, not on the AFMP alone; for third-country retirees, Article 34 FNIA and Article 60 OASA govern, and there is no retiree-specific shortcut. The third stage is citizenship planning: ordinary naturalisation requires a C permit and ten years' residence, although years on a B permit count. Retirees are not necessarily blocked by the "economic participation" limb of integration law because OASA Article 77e expressly recognises self-support through income, assets, or third-party benefits. 49

Changes in family composition also matter more than is sometimes acknowledged in retirement files. Marriagge, separation, divorce, or changes affecting accompanying family members can feed directly into the migration file because OASA requires civil-status reporting to the migration authorities. Even where the principal retiree retains an independent basis of stay, such changes may alter the household means assessment, derivative permits, or the evidential picture at renewal. 24

# Drafting cautions and source list

## H. Drafting cautions.

·Do not describe Swiss retirement residence as a single "retirement visa" framework.Swiss law distinguishes between treaty-based EU/EFTA residence and discretionary third-country admission under general residence-permit law. 50

·Do not imply that third-country retirees have a right to admission or renewal if they meet headline criteria. Article 28 FNIA is discretionary. 51

·Do not overstate the security of the EU/EFTA route. Even a five-year B EU/EFTA permit can be revalidated after two years and may be revoked or not renewed if means or insurannce fail. 14

·Do not state that third-country retirement B permits are always renewed annually.Federal law sets one year initially and usually two-year prolongations thereafter, with exceptions. 19

·Do not treat complementary benefits and social assistance as interchangeable. They overlap in practical risk, but they are not identical in every legal context. 52

·Do not reduce residence-substance analysis to a "183 days" shorthand. The stronger federal anchors are continued compliance, six-month absence rules, and centre-of-interests analysis. 53

·Do not assume that beginning work merely "sits alongside" retirement status for third-country retirees. OASA Article 25 prohibits gainful activity in Switzerland and abroad except management of one's own assets, and OASA Article 86 ties extension to the continued purpose of stay. 46

<!-- 11 -->

· Do not say that a retirement B permit automatically leads to a C permit. C is a separate status governed by separate rules. 54

·Do not state a universal five-year C-permit route for all EU/EFTA retirees. The SEM states that settlement timing depends on nationality and establishment-agreement coverage. 22

·Do not imply that naturalisation planning can start from B status alone. Ordinary naturalisation requires a C permit and ten years'residence. 25

·Do not present cantonal filing deadlines, forms, or fee schedules as federal law. They are practice points only. 44
