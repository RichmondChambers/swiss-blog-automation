## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not create a standalone "retirement visa" category. For third-country nationals,the relevant route is admnission as a retired person under Article 28 of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and Article 25 of the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. The statutory conditions are cumulative: the applicant must have reached the minimum age fixed by the Federal Council, must have particular personal ties to Switzerland, and must have the necessary financial means; the ordinance fixes the minimum age at 55, requires no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad apart from management of one's own assets, and uses the supplementary-benefits benchmark for assessing means. 1

The personal-ties requirement is the central gatekeeping feature of the non-EU/EFTA retirement route. Article 25 paragraph 2 OASA/VZAE gives two express examples of such ties: sufficiently long previous stays in Switzerland, and close ties to close relatives in Switzerland. But the accessible case law and current SEM guidance treat those examples as illustrative, not exhaustive, and reject the view that family proximity alone is enough. The Federal Administrative Court's line, repeated in the 2026 SEM instructions, is that the statute requires ties **to** **Switzerland** **itself,** not merely ties **to** **relatives** **located** **in** **Switzerland.** The Court therefore distinguishes between direct Swiss links and indirect links mediated only through family members living in Switzerland. 2

On the evidence, official guidance is more concrete than the bare statutory text. The 2026 SEM instructions state that close links may be shown by longer or repeated previous stays such as regular holiday stays, by close relatives in Switzerland, or by Swiss ancestry/origins; they also state expressly that ownership of real estate or economic ties to Switzerland does not by itself satisfy the requirement. The same instructions, relying on FAC case law, explain that the necessary independent ties may take socio-cultural or personal forms, such as links to local communities, participation in cultural events, or direct contacts with the local population. 3

The case law is consistent with a restrictive reading. In C-6349/2010 the Federal Administrative Court explained, by reference to legislative history, that the retirement route is not a substitute for upward family reunification and that direct ties with Switzerland are required. In C-1156/2012 the Court again treated the Article 25 paragraph 2 examples as non-exhaustive but stressed that close relatives in Switzerland are not automatically equivalent to the statutory requirement in Article 28 AIG. In 2C124/2023 the Federal Supreme Court held that, on the facts found below, three prior three-month stays plus reliance on step-granddaughters in Switzerland did not establish the necessary particular personal ties. 4

In practice, personal-ties cases are evidentially demanding and canton-sensitive. Zurich's official materials require a detailed explanation of the reasons for choosing Zurich, the intended commune of residence, and a detailed account of ties to the canton, with proof of earlier stays from residents' registration offices where relevant. Zurich's internal guidance accepts that prior stays, documented regular holidays, and some intensive private or business relationships may be relevant, and says that cantonal fiscal interests or extraordinary cultural or economic merits may also be considered, but it still insists that family ties alone, economic links alone, and property ownership alone are insufficient. Fribourg likewise requires proof of the special personal relationship to Switzerland, information about family circumstances, a reasoned application, and translated and authenticated supporting materials. 5

The route remains discretionary even if all threshold elements appear to be met. Article 28 AIG uses permissive language; Article 3 paragraph 3 AIG requires regard to Switzerland's demographic, social and societal development; and Article 96 AIG directs the authorities to weigh public interests, the foreign national's personal circumstances, and integration. SEM guidance further states that grants of residence permits for retirees are to be submitted to SEM for approval, and that renewal may be refused if the retiree's centre of life is not in Switzerland. A retirement permit therefore requires not only a historically persuasive personal-ties case at grant stage, but also a credible and continuing Swiss centre of life thereafter.6

## Structural overview of the personal-ties requirement

### Legal basis

For non-EU/EFTA retirees, the governing federal law is Article 28 AIG, which allows admission of foreign nationals who are no longer economically active if they meet the minimum age, personal-ties and financial-means conditions. Article 25 VZAE/OASA implements Article 28 by fixing the minimum age at 55, specifying examples of "particular personal ties to Switzerland", prohibiting gainful activity in Switzerland and abroadd apart from management of one's own assets, and defining sufficient means by reference to the supplementary-benefits threshold. 1

### Personal ties as a distinct admissibility criterion

The statutory examples in Article 25 paragraph 2 are not exhaustive. Long previous stays and close relationships with close relatives in Switzerland are examples, but the Federal Administrative Court and SEM guidance read the underlying requirement more narrowly: the applicant must show particular personal ties **with** **Switzerland,** not only with persons resident there. That is why the retirement route cannot simply be treated as a family-co-location mechanism for ascendants. 7

### Family ties and their limits

Family ties remain relevant. Article 25 paragraph 2 expressly names parents, children, grandchildren and siblings as close relatives whose presence in Switzerland may be relevant. But SEM guidance and FAC case law say that such family ties do not, without more, satisfy Article 28 AIG, because independent socio-cultural or personal ties must also exist. The Supreme Court's 2C124/2023 decision illustrates the same restrictive approach on recent facts. 8

### Typical positive indicators

The official materials most clearly recognise long or repeated previous stays in Switzerland, regular holiday stays, and close but not standalone family links. SEM guidance adds Swiss ancestry/origins as a possible indicator. It also gives concrete examples of independent links: ties to local communities, attendance at cultural events, and direct contact with the local population. These examples sit naturally with evidence such as long-term recurrent presence, stable participation in Swiss local life, and credible non-family social embeddedness. 9

<!-- 2 -->

### Indicators with limited weight

Property ownership and economic relationships are expressly treated as insufficient on their own in SEM guidance and in the case-law line reflected in FAC materials. Zurich's official guidance adopts the same position, even while accepting that intensive private or business relations, cantonal fiscal interests, or extraordinary cultural or economic merit may be considered in the cantonal assessment. The legal weight of those latter considerations is discretionary and canton-specific, not black-letter federal law. 10

### Procedure, evidence and renewal

A stay without gainful activity for more than three months requires a permit, and the application is normally to be made before entry; if someone lawfully enters for a temporary stay and then seeks a permanent stay, the decision must generally be awaited abroad unless the conditions are obviously met. In practice, cantons ask for a detailed narrative of ties, proof of previous stays, and explanatory evidence showing why residence will be taken in that canton. SEM guidance also states that the permit must be submitted to SEM for approval and that the retiree's centre of life must be in Switzerland, failing which extension may be refused. ⑪

## Primary legal texts

## Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration

**Article** 28 **AIG-retired** **persons.** The provision states that foreign nationals who are no longer economically active may be admitted if they have reached the minimum age fixed by the Federal Council, possess particular personal ties to Switzerland, and have the necessary financial mmeans. This is the core statutory basis for the non-EU/EFTA retirement route and the source of the personal-ties requirement itself. 30

**Article 10 AIG - permit requirement for longer non-working stays.** Article 10 provides that **a** non-working stay longer than three months requires a permit and that the application must generally be made before entry at the authority for the intended place of residence, subject to Article 17 paragraph 2. This matters because retirement cases are not simply "arrival and register" cases;they are permission-based residence cases for third-country nationals. 31

<!-- 5 -->

**Article 17 AIG - waiting abroad pending decision.** Article 17 states that **a** person who lawfully entered for a temporary stay and later applies for a permanent stay must in principle wait abroad for the decision, unless the canton considers the admission conditions obviously fulfilled and permits the stay during processing. This is relevant to sequencing strategy and to incorrect attempts to convert a visitor stay into residence onshore. 32

**Article 3 paragraph 3 AIG - demographic, social and societal development.** The Act requires that, when admitting foreign nationals, account be taken of Switzerland's demographic, social and societal development. In retiree cases, this is frequently cited as part of the public-interest backdrop supporting restrictive discretion. 33

**Article** **96** **AIG** **-** **exercise** **of discretion.** Article 96 requires the competent authorities, when exercising discretion, to consider public interests, the foreign national's personal circumstances, and integration.It is important because even a well-documented personal-ties file does not convert the route into an entitlement. 34

**Articles 33, 34 and 61 AIG - permit type, long-term planning and lapse.** Article 33 provides that an Aufenthaltsbewilligung/residence permit is for stays of more than one year, is purpose-bound, time-limited and renewable if no revocation grounds apply. Article 34 provides, at a high level, for settlement permit eligibility after ten years' stay, including the last five years continuously on a residence permit, plus absence of revocation grounds and integration. Article 61 provides that permits lapse on deregistration abroad and, if the person leaves Switzerland without deregistering, after six months for residence permits. These provisions matter because personal-ties cases must be planned as maintainable residence, not merely as an entry success. 35

## Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment

**Article** **25** **VZAE/OASA** **-** **retired** **persons.** Article 25 fixes the minimum age at 55; says particular personal ties exist in particular where sufficiently long previous stays in Switzerland are proven or where there are close ties to close relatives in Switzerland; prohibits gainful activity in Switzerland and abroad except managing one's own assets; and defines sufficient means by reference to the supplementary-benefits threshold. This is the implementing text that operationalises the personal-ties inquiry. 36

The practical importance of Article 25 paragraph 2 is twofold. First, it confirms that earlier stays and close relatives are statutory examples. Secondly, because the provision says "in particular", the list is open-ended; that open texture is what allows SEM and the courts to evaluate broader socio-cultural and personal evidence, but also what makes the route evidentially uncertain and discretion-sensitive. 17

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

## Official guidance and addministrative materials

The most important official administrative source is the SEM's 2026 Instructions in the Foreign Nationals field, section 5.3 on retirees. SEM states that the Article 28 AIG and Article 25 VZAE conditions are cumulative; that grants of residence permits for retirees must be submitted to SEM for approval; that strong ties to Switzerland mmay be shown by longer or repeated earlier stays, close relatives in Switzerland, or Swiss ancestors/origins; and that ownership of real estate or economic links to Switzerland do not by themselves fulfil the requirement. SEM then adopts the FAC's case-law line that particular personal ties to Switzerland mean ties to Switzerland and not merely to relatives in

Switzerland, and says independent socio-cultural or personal ties are therefore required. It gives as examples connections to local communities, participation in cultural events, and direct contacts with the local population. 37

SEM's guidance also adds two planning points that matter for drafting. First, retirees must produce a declaration showing that they have actually withdrawn from active professional life and undertake not to work in Switzerland or abroad. Secondly, they must have their centre of life in Switzerland; SEM says renewal is refused if it is later established that the centre of life is not in Switzerland. These points show how the personal-ties requirement interacts with genuine residential settlement and later extension. 27

The SEM's public UK FAQ, although not a full legal analysis, is useful as a concise official confirmation that third-country retirees are assessed under Article 28 AIG and Article 25 VZAE and must apply through the migration authority of their intended canton of residence. It is therefore a reliable procedural cross-check, but not a substitute for the Instructions or the legislation. 38

## Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich.** Zurich's public application page requires a notably canton-specific evidential package: the applicant must explain why residence is sought in canton Zurich, identify the intended commune,and provide a detailed account of ties tothe canton; previous stays in the canton are to be evidenced by municipal registration certificates where applicable. Zurich's internal 2021 guidance is more revealing stiIl. It says that earlier stays in Switzerland with immigration permission, intensive private or business relations, and regularly documented holiday and tourist stays may be relevant; that cantonal fiscal interests and extraordinary cultural or economic merits may also be considered; but that close relatives in Switzerland do not by themselves establish the Article 28 criterion, because independent socio-cultural or personal ties are required, such as connections to the local community, cultural participation or direct contact with the local population. The same Zurich guidance states expressly that family ties, economic ties and property ownership in Switzerland are insufficient on their own. These are cantonal practice points, not federal black-letter law. 39

**Fribourg.** Fribourg's official page is less analytical but useful on evidential handling. It requires proof of the special personal relationship to Switzerland, a written declaration that the applicant has retired from economic life both abroad and in Switzerland, information on family circumstances, a reasoned application, proof of sufficient means, a copy of the lease, and proof of comprehensive Swiss health and accident insurance. It also states that all documents must be translated into an official language and certified by the Swiss representation, and that on arrival the person must register within 14 days.It adds that, if the conditions are satisfied, an L permnit may be issued for stays under one year and a B permit for stays over one year. 40

**Zurich** **cantonal** **decisions.** Zurich's published administrative decisions also provide cautious practice guidance. Decision 0168 states that the old practice of refusing retiree residence merely because descendants remained in the home country no longer survives under the newer legislation, and that the main criteria are age, close relations to Switzerland and financial independence, with discretion focusing largely on the intensity of the Swiss connection. Decision 0193 makes the same point in a different way: children remaining in the home country do not automatically bar admission, but the lack of any entitlement means the circumstance can still matter in discretionary balancing. Decision 0312shows that even where personal ties may be arguable, insufficient means can still defeat the application. These are canton-level practice materials only. 41

## Practical legal analysis

The legal functionof the personal-ties requirement is to stop Article 28 AIG from becoming a general wealth-based route for retired third-country nationals who simply prefer to live near family in Switzerland. The Federal Administrative Court's reasoning in C-6349/2010 is especially important here: it treats the retirement route as historically distinct from ascendant family reunification and therefore insists on **direct** Swiss ties. Put operationally, an applicant must show why Switzerland itself is already a meaningful place of personal life, not merely why Switzerland is where the applicant's relatives happen to live. 18

That distinction also explains why Article 25 paragraph 2 cannot be read literally in isolation. On its face, subparagraph (b) could suggest that close relatives in Switzerland are enough. The courts and SEM do not read it that way. Instead, they read the ordinance as giving examples of potentially relevant evidence, while preserving the statute's stronger requirement of particular personal ties to Switzerland. The Federal Administrative Court in C-1156/2012 explicitly described the Article 25 examples as illustrative rather than exhaustive, and again refused to equate family proximity with the statutory requirement. 42

For evidence planning, the strongest positive material is usually a combination of three things: a substantial history of prior Swiss presence, a credible non-family social or cultural relationship with Switzerland, and a coherent explanation of why the chosen canton is the applicant's genuine future place of life. The official sources accept long or repeated earlier stays, including regular holiday stays, and SEM expressly points to community links, cultural participation and direct contacts with the local population as examples of the independent ties that the case law demands. Zurich's materials show how this is operationalised in practice by asking for a detailed canton-specific narrative and documentary proof of previous stays. 43

By contrast, the authorities draw a clear line around what is insufficient. SEM says that ownnership of real estate and economic ties do not, by themselves, satisfy the requirement; a 2012 FAC extract says the same; and Zurich reproduces the same approach in its practice instructions. Property, business activity, or investment links may therefore bolster a broader narrative of long-term engagement, but they cannot replace direct personal ties. Zurich's additional references to cantonal fiscal interests and extraordinary cultural or economic merits should be treated cautiously: they show local discretionary openness, not a federal legal test. 44

On family evidence, the safest position is that relatives in Switzerland are relevant but never self-sufficient. That is true even for close blood relatives listed in Article 25 paragraph 2, and a fortiori for more attenuated or step-family relationships. The Supreme Court's 2C124/2023 decision is useful as a current warning example: the Court upheld the view that three prior three-month stays and reliance on step-granddaughters in Switzerland did not estabolish the necessary particular personal ties, and it also rejected the attempt to recast the case as one of protected family life under Article 8 ECHR on the facts found below. 45

Language learning, language competence, volunteering and community service need careful handling in future drafting. In the accessible primary legislation and official guidance reviewed here, none of those matters appears as a standalone statutory element of Article 25 VZAE. The more defensible position is that they may help only insofar as they concretely evidence the sort of independent socio-cultural ties identified by SEM and the courts, such as genuine links with local community life and direct contact with the resident population. That is an inference from the official examples, not an express

<!-- 8 -->

federal rule. No official source was found in the accessible materials making language ability or volunteering, by themselves, sufficient for Article 28 personal ties. 46

Finally, personal-ties analysis should not be divorced from residence maintenance. SEM says the retiree must place the centre of life in Switzerland and that renewval should be refused if that is later shown not to be the case. The permnit itself is purpose-bound and time-limited under Article 33AIG, and longer-term progression to settlemnent under Article 34 AIG remains conditional rather than automatic.For internal drafting, this means that "personal ties" should be presented not merely as an entry-stage dossier exercise, but as part of a broader residence narrative in which Switzerland is credibly the applicant's actual home. 47

## Drafting cautions

·Do not describe the route as a Swiss "retirement visa" without explaining that the operative legal framework is a residence-permit route under Article 28 AIG and Article 25 VZAE. 48

·Do **not** imply that meeting age, ties and means creates an entitlement. The route remains discretionary. 13

·Do **not** say that having children, grandchildren or siblings in Switzerland automatically satisfies the personal-ties requirement. 49

·Do **not** collapse the retirement route into upward family reunification. FAC case law treats them as distinct concepts. 19

·Do **not** state that Article 25 paragraph 2 is exhaustive. The examples are illustrative only. 17

·Do **not** present property ownership, business links or investment activity as sufficient evidence of personal ties. They are expressly inadequate on their own. 44

·Do **not** elevate Swiss ancestry/origins into a freestanding statutory criterion. It appears in guidance and legislative/case references, not in the current text of Article 25 paragraph 2 itself. 50

·Do **not** state that language competence or volunteering is an express legal requirement for Article 28 retiree admission. No such official standalone rule was identified in the accessible materials. 46

·Do **not** ignore cantonal evidential handling. Zurich and Fribourg both demand detailed documentary narratives, and Zurich's practice is materially more developed than the bare federal text. 51

·Do **not** overlook procedure: longer non-working stays require a permit and the application is ordinarily made before entry. 28

·Do **not** omit the centre-of-life issue at renewal stage. SEM treats it as a condition of continued residence. 27

·Do **not** treat Zurich practice points such as fiscal interest or extraordinary cultural/economic merit as if they were national federal law. 52

## Open questions and limitations

The highest-confidence propositions in this pack are well supported by the legislation, SEM's 2026guidance, Zurich and Fribourg official mnaterials, and one recent Federal Supreme Court decision. Three limitations remain. First, no accessible official source was found that treats language competence or volunteering as a standalone Article 25 criterion; in this pack they are therefore treated only as possible inferential evidence of broader socio-cultural ties. Secondly, the full official texts of some older FAC decisions cited in SEM guidance were not stably retrievable through the web tool, so their holdings are stated conservatively and cross-checked against SEM's current guidance and official cantonal practice.
