# Swiss Retirement Residence Family Members and Derivative Residence Authority Pack

## A. Executive legal summary

Swiss "retirement residence" does not create a single family-migration code. The family position depends on the sponsor's legal route. If the sponsor is an EU/EFTA national residing in Switzerland as an economically inactive person under the Agreement on the FreeMovement of Persons, the relevant family rights are treaty-based and derivative. If the sponsor is a non-EU/EFTA retiree admitted under Article 28 of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, the family position normally falls under the ordinary family-reunification rules for holders of Swiss residence permits, especially Article 44FNIA/AIG for holders of B permits. SEM's own guidance on retired persons expressly says that retirees who want to live in Switzerland with a spouse and minor children may be admitted, but that the ordinary family-reunification provisions apply for them. 1

For EU/EFTA retirees, the family regime is markedly broader and stronger. SEM's AFMP family-reunification materials state that EU/EFTA nationals with a Swiss L or B EU/EFTA permit, including economically inactive persons, may be accompanied by a spouse, descendants under 21 or otherwise dependent, and dependent ascendants of the principal or of the spouse, regardless of thoserelatives' nationality. The family right is derivative: it depends on the EU/EFTA sponsor having an original residence right. Swiss practice, following Federal Supreme Court case law, no longer requires the third-country family member to prove prior lawful residence elsewhere in the EU/EFTA before relying on Article 3 Annex I AFMP. 2

The EU/EFTA route is nevertheless conditional. The sponsor must have adequate accommodation for the family. Where the sponsor is self-employed or economically inactive rather than a worker, SEM's fact sheet states that family members may only be brought if the sponsor has the financial means necessary for their maintenance; SEM's FAQ on economically inactive residence further states that sufficient means exist where a similarly situated Swiss national would not qualify for social assistance, and that the applicant must also show insurance against illness and accident covering all risks. The AFMP family permit follows the principal status in duration, and SEM's guidance adds that a family member's permit should correspond to the validity shown on the principal's own EU/EFTA permit. Spouses and children admitted under this route have access to work throughout Switzerland, but ascendants admitted as dependent relatives do not have an automatic right to take employment. 3

For non-EU/EFTA retirees, the family route is much narrower. Article 44 FNIA/AIG covers the spouse and unmarried children under 18 of a person holding a Swiss B permit. The Act uses discretionary language, and SEM's AIG guidance says expressly that there is no legal entitlement and that cantons may apply stricter requirements. The statutory conditions include living together, adequate accommodation, no social-assistance dependence, and no receipt of annual supplementary benefits under the EL system by the sponsor or likely entitlement to them because of the family reunification. For the spouse, the law also requires competence in the language spoken at the place of residence, with enrolment in a language course being sufficient for initial grant; SEM's guidance states that by renewal the spouse must prove oral A1. Registered partners are equated with spouses by Article 52 FNIA/AIG.4

That narrower statutory family circle matters in retirement cases. Ordinary family reunification for a non-EU/EFTA retiree does not extend to parents, grandparents, adult children, or an unmarried partner. Those persons would need some other legal basis, typically a distinct hardship or human-rights argument rather than ordinary derivative residence. SEM's AIG guidance states that addult children normally fall outside Article 8 ECHR family-life protection absent a particular dependency relationship, and that unmarried partners only exceptionally derive a claim where there is a long-standing, stable relationship and marriage is imminent. Geneva's official cantonal guidance for non-EU/EFTA cohabiting partners is equally explicit that there is no right to such a permit and that the route is discretionary, requiring proof of a stable relationship, common household, and sufficient means. 5

Work rights also diverge. Under Article 46FNIA/AIG, spouses and children admitted through family reunification to Swiss, C-permit, or B-permit holders may work anywhere in Switzerland.Geneva's official guidance reflects this approach operationally, while also making clear that certain L-permit family situations remain more restricted. By contrast, AFMP work rights are treaty-based and attach independently to spouses and children of EU/EFTA sponsors, even where the spoonsor is not themselves admitted for gainful activity. 6

Procedurally, ordinary third-country family reunification is handled through the cantons, often with front-end involvement by the Swiss representation abroad; the 2025 SEM directive explains that the representation performs a preliminary review and forwards the dossier to the competent cantonal migration authority, with SEM approval reserved in certain cases. Official cantonal pages in Basel-Stadt and Zurich confirm that parallel foreign-side and Swiss-side filings may be needed for visa-required relatives and that applicants generally must wait abroad for the outcome. For AFMP family members, the process is lighter, but visa-required third-country relatives may still need an entry application and must prove identity, family relationship, and in dependent-relative cases the fact of dependency.7

Long-term planning requires care because "derivative" really means dependence on the principal route, at least initially. Under the AFMP, the family member's position is tied to the existence of the sponsor's original residence right. Under the FNIA/AIG route, the family permit is initially time-limited and discretionary, although Articles 50 and 51 FNIA/AIG can preserve residence in defined post-breakdown situations. SEM's AIG guidance states that family-reunification residence permits are initially granted for one year and may then be extended for two years. In short, EU/EFTA retirees usually have the firmer family platform; non-EU/EFTA retirees do not. 8

## B. Structural overview of family/-member rules

### Legal nature of derivative residence

The central structural point is that Swiss retirement residence does not produce a self-contained "retiree family permit". Instead, the family analysis follows the sponsor's legal status. For EU/EFTA sponsors,the AFMP and its implementing framework are primary, with the FNIA/AIG applying only subsidiarily where the treaty and implementing rules do not regulate the point or where Swiss domestic lawis more favourable. For non-EU/EFTA retirees admitted under Article 28 FNIA/AIG and Article 25 OASA/VZAE, the sponsor's spouse and children fall back into ordinary Swiss family-reunification law, usually Article 44FNIA/AIG because the principal normally holds a B permit rather than a C permit. 1

### EU/EFTA family members of retirees

SEM's official AFMP family-reunification materials treat economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals, including retirees, as capable of sponsoring spouses, descendants under 21 or dependent,and dependent ascendants of the principal or spouse, without nationality restrictions. The right is derivative of the EU/EFTA sponsor's original residence right, so it lasts only so long as that original right continues, subject to limited retention situations. Current Swiss practice also treats registered partners in the same way as spouses, although the presently operative treaty text is not yet formally updated to codify all of the broader "updated FZA" family categories discussed in SEM's 2026 comparative fact sheet. 9

<!-- 2 -->

### Non-EU/EFTA family members of retirees

For a non-EU/EFTA retiree with a Swiss B permit, Article 44 FNIA/AIG is the ordinary family-reunification provision. It covers only the spouse and unmarried children under 18. SEM's AIG guidance states expressly that there is no entitlement, and that the cantons may make admission subject to stricter conditions. Registered partners are brought into the same regime by Article 52 FNIA/AIG. Parents, grandparents, adult children, and unmarried partners do not fall within Article 44's ordinary derivative regime. 10

### Household conditions

Under the AFMP, adequate housing is required, and for non-working sponsors thne financial-means assessment must cover family maintenance. SEM's fact sheet and FAQ also tie economically inactive residence to sickness and accident insurance covering all risks. Under Article 44 FNNIA/AIG, the listed federal conditions are cohabitation, adequate housing, no social assistance, no annual supplementary benefits or likely eligibility for them because of the family reunification, and spouse language ability or at least language-course enrolment at the initial stage. The difference is important: under the AFMP, insurance is an express residence condition for the economically inactive sponsor; under Article 44, insurance is not listed as a separate statutory family-reunification criterion, although household insurance costs obviously matter to the financial analysis. 11

### Work rights and permit duration

The AFMP is more generous on work rights. SEM states that spouses and children admitted through AFMP family reunification may work or become self-employed anywhere in Switzerland irrespective of nationality, whereas dependent ascendants do not have an automatic labour-market right. The family member's permit should mirror the principal's permit duration. By contrast, Article 46 FNIA/AIG gives spouses and children of Swiss, C-permit and B-permit holders access to work throughout Switzerland, but this protection does not automatically extend to every L-permit scenario; Geneva's official guidance still requires a separate work authorisation for some non-European family members of non-European L holders. On duration, SEM's AIG guidance says fammily-reunification B permits are initially valid for one year and may then be extended for two years; that is a different structure from the AFMP's explicit permit-mirroring rule. 12

### Procedure and evidence

For AFMP family members, the documentary burden usually includes identity documents, proof of relationship, visa materials where applicable, and for dependent relatives proof of actual support or co-residence from the homne or state of origin. For Article 42-45 FNIA/AIG cases, SEM's 2025 directive provides the core procedural map: the Swiss representation abroad pre-screens the dossier and transmits it to the competent canton; the canton then checks its competence and performs the substantive assessment, with SEM approval reserved in certain cases. Official cantonal pages in Zurich and Basel-Stadt illustrate how this translates into practice through rent contracts, recent salary slips or other proof of livelihood, relationship documents, and explicit instructions to wait abroad while the application is processed. 13

## D. Primary legal texts

### Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and Annex I

**Annex** I, **Article** 3. This is the core AFMP family-reunification provision. As reflected in SEM's official fact sheet and VFP guidelines, it requires adequate family housing and defines the family circle as the spouse, descendants under 21 or otherwise dependent, and dependent ascendants of the sponsor or spouse; it also underpins the rule that family permits mirror the principal permit and that spouse and children have labour-market access. The current SEM guidance further states that "other family members" may receive favoured admission but have no treaty claim on that ground. (31

**Annex** I, **Article** **24.** This is the economically inactive residence provision relevant to EU/EFTA retirees. SEM's VFP guidance and FAQ explain it as requiring sufficient financial means and sickness insurance covering all risks for residence without gainful activity; the family-reunification fact sheet adds that self-employed and non-working sponsors may only bring family if they have the necessary financial means to maintain them. SEM's VFP guidance also records that the permit for the economically inactive is ordinarily issued for five years, but exceptionally only for two years if authorities consider that necessary. 32

### Federal Act on Foreign NNationals and Integration

**Article** **2** **FNIA/AIG.** SEM's federal guidance states that the FNIA/AIG is only subsidiarily applicable to free-movement beneficiaries, namely where the AFMP and the Federal Council's implementing rules do not regulate the matter or domestic law is more favourable. This provision is what prevents the EU/EFTA retiree route from being collapsed into the third-country route. 33

**Article** **28** **FNIA/AIG.** Article 28 is the non-EU/EFTA retirement admission rule. The Act says foreigners who are no longer gainfully employed may be admitted if they have reached the minimum age set by the Federal Council, have particular personal ties to Switzerland, and possess the necessary financial means. SEM's retirement guidance then states that if such retirees want to settle with a spouse and minor children, the ordinary family-reunification provisions apply. 34

**Article** 44 **FNIA/AIG.** This is the central family provision for non-EU/EFTA retirees who hold a B permit. The Act provides that the foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18 of B-permit holders **may** be granted and renewed a residence permit if they live together, adequate accommodation exists, they are not dependent on social assistance, they can communicate in the local language, and the sponsor neither receives annual supplementary benefits nor would become entitled to them because of the family reunification; enrolment in a language course is enough for the initial grant, and children are exempt from the language requirement. 35

<!-- 7 -->

**Article** **45** **FNIA/AIG.** If, exceptionally, the principal retiree only has an L permit, Article 45 governs family reunification. It covers only the spouse and unmarried children under 18, and requires cohabitation, adequate accommodation, no social assistance, and no annual supplementary benefits or likely entitlement to them because of the family reunification. The statutory architecture is even more fragile than under Article **44.** 36

**Article** **46** **FNIA/AIG.** This provision gives spouses and children of Swiss citizens and holders of settlement or residence permits admitted under Articles 42-44 the right to engage in employed or self-employed activity throughout Switzerland. For retirement-family cases, this means that the sponsor's own "no gainful activity" condition does not automatically prevent the spouse or child from working once admitted under Article 44. 36

**Article** **49** **FNIA/AIG.** This is the exception to the cohabitation requirement. It provides that the living-together requirement under Articles 42-44 does not apply where important reasons justify separate residences and the family community continues. That point matters for temporary split-location arrangements, although it should be used cautiously in retirement cases because the family community must remain real. 37

**Articles** **50** **and** **51** **FNIA/AIG.** Article 50 preserves residence after dissolution of the family community in certain cases, notably after at least three years of family life with integration or where important personal reasons require continued residence. Article 51 extinguishes family-reunification claims where they are asserted abusively or where revocation grounds exist. For derivative-retirement cases, these two provisions are central at the renewal and breakdown stages. 38

**Article** **52** **FNIA/AIG.** This provision states that the chapter's rules on foreign spouses apply equally, mutatis mutandis, to registered partnerships of same-sex couples. In domestic-law terms, this places registered partners squarely inside the ordinary family-reunification regime. 39

### Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment

**Article** **58** **paragraph** **1 OASA/VZAE.** SEM's AIG guidance explains this provision as meaning that, where a residence permit is issued through family reunification, the initial validity is one year and renewal may be granted for two years. Thnis is the official federal reference point for third-country family-permit duration. 40

**Article** 73 **OASA/VZAE.** SEM's guidance treats Article 73 as extending the five-yearand twelve-month filing deadlines to family reunification by holders of B permits. The deadline begins when the B permit is granted or when the family relationship comes into being. 41

**Article** **73a** **OASA/VZAE.** SEM's AIG guidance explains that, for spouses admitted under Article 44 FNIA/ AIG, proof of oral A1 in the language spoken at the place of residence is required by the renewal stage, and the initial grant may rely on enrolment in an appropriate language course instead. 42

**Article** **76** **OASA/VZAE.** SEM cites Article 76 together with Article 49 FNIA/AIG in relation to justified separate residences where the family community continues. This is part of the machinery that limits the strictness of the cohabitation requirement. 40

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal administrative source for the **EU/EFTA** **route** is SEM's Weisungen und Erläuterungen zur Verordnung über den freien Personenverkehr (VFP Guidelines, version 1/2026). Chapter 7states that AFMP family reunification always presupposes an original residence right, defines the protected family circle, explains dependent-relative evidence, confirms the permit-duration mirroring rule, records access to the labour market for spouse and children, and clarifies that "other family members" only benefit from favoured admission rather than a claim1. 43

SEM's short **AFMP** **family-reunification** **fact sheet** is also load-bearing. It confirms that workers, the self-employed and the economically inactive can be accompanied by family members; that the family circle is nationality-neutral; that adequate housing is required; that self-employed and non-working sponsors must have sufficient financial means for family maintenance; that third-country relatives may need a visa unless they already hold a valid Schengen residence document; and that spouse and children have labour-market access, whereas ascendants do not. 44

SEM's **FAQ** **on** **free** **movement,** available in Italian and other languages, is usefulas an official synthesis. It confirms the economically inactive sponsor's sufficient-means and insurance requirements, identifies the AFMP family categories, notes that ascendants' dependency must be shown, and restates spouse/children work rights. The FAQ is not a primary legal text, but it is a reliable federal expolanatory source for operational drafting. 45

For the **non-EU/EFTA** **route,** the indispensable source is SEM's Weisungen und Erläuterungen I. Ausländerbereich (AIG Guidelines, state of 1 January 2026). The retirement section says that retirees who want to reside with a spouse and minor children fall under the ordinary family rules. The family-reunification chapter then explains the defining features of Article 44: no entitlement, possible stricter cantonal requirements, adequate accommodation, minimum financial means, supplementary-benefits control,language requirements, permit duration, deadlines, and the possibility of work by spouse and child after admission. 46

SEM's **Directive on family-reunification procedure for third-country nationals** dated 4 December 2025 is the principal current federal procedural note. The directive makes clear that ordinary family reunification under Articles 42-45 FNIA/AIG is handled by the cantons, that a Swiss representation abroad pre-checks the dossier when the application is lodged there, that the canton undertakes the substantive review, and that SEM approvalI remains reserved in certain cases. It also emphasises that applicants relying on family ties must prove them and cooperate in the establishment of the relevant facts. 47

One further federal source deserves a cautionary note: SEM's **13** **March** **2026** **comparative** **fact** **sheet** **on the current and "updated" AFMP family-reunification rules.** It is valuable only as an explanatory and future-facing document. It says expressly that the updated AFMP would extend the codified claim to registered partners and widen the facilitated category to life partners and certain dependent relatives, while also noting that registered partners are already treated like spouses in current Swiss practice. It should therefore be used only to frame current practice carefully, not as operative law for present-day drafting. 48

#### G. Practical legal analysis

The first drafting point is route separation. Family members of an EU/EFTA retiree are not analysed under the same scheme as family members of a non-EU/EFTA retiree. In the AFMP route, the family right is treaty-derived and nationality-neutral in scope. In the FNIA/AIG route, the sponsor's retired status under Article 28 does not itself create a family entitlement; it simply leaves the sponsor to use the ordinary family provisions applicable to the permit they hold, normally Article 44 for a B permit. That is why EU/EFTA retirement families are usually on firmer legal ground than third-country retirement families. 53

<!-- 10 -->

The second point is that the AFMP family circle is materially wider. Spouses, younger descendants, dependent older descendants, and dependent ascendants of the sponsor or spouse are all within the official federal definition. That immediately makes the AFMP family route more flexible for retirement planning, because older parents and certain adult children can in principle qualify if real dependency is shown. Swiss official guidance and Federal Supreme Court case law treat dependency as a factual matter, not a merely formal one: it must be genuine, and support before entry is important, but the authorities look at the real circumstances of need and care. 54

That flexibility does not carry over into the third-country route. Article 44 FNIA/AIG deliberately limits ordinary family reunification to the spouse and unmarried children under 18 of a B-permit holder. For a third-country retiree, that means parents, grandparents, and adult children sit outside the ordinary derivative structure. If such persons are to be admitted at all, the argument must be on another basis, such as a severe dependency relationship under Article 8 ECHR logic or a hardship category. The same caution applies to unmarried partners: ordinary statutory family-reunification provisions do not cover them. Geneva's official cohabitant route is explicitly discretionary and does not create a right. 29

The third point is that household sufficiency must be assessed at the level of the family unit, but the legal technique differs by route. Under the AFMP, the non-working sponsor must have the funds to maintain the family and the insurance needed for economically inactive residence. Under Article 44FNIA/AIG, the Act and SEM guidance focus on avoiding social-assistance dependence and annual supplementary-benefits exposure, with SEM using the SKOS social-assistance guidelines as the minimum financialI baseline and warning that future income is usually disregarded unless it is concretely and durably secured. In retirement cases this matters acutely, because the sponsor ordinarily has no employment income and the family budget must be demonstrated through pensions, assets, and predictable household outgoings. 55

Work rights are another area where retirement cases are often misstated. The sponsor's retired or non-working status does **not** automatically prohibit all family work. Under the AFMP, spouse and child work rights derive directly from Article 3 Annex I and exist even where the EU/EFTA sponsor is not admitted for gainful activity. Under domestic third-country law, Article 46 FNIA/AIG gives spouses and children admitted under the B-permit family route access to work throughout Switzerland. The real cautioon is narrower: dependent ascendants under the AFMP do not have the same work entitlement, and L-permit family scenarios can be more restrictive than B-permit ones. 56

Permit duration needs careful wording. For AFMP-based retirees, the family permit mirrors the principal's permit. If the principal's economically inactive permit is issued for five years, the family's derivative EU/EFTA permit should generally follow that duration; if the principal's permit is shortened, the family's derivative permit also shortens. In the FNIA/AIG route, by contrast, SEM's federal guidance for family reunification points to an initial one-year family residence permit, with later extensions possible, rather than to a clean treaty-style mirroring rule. A content system should therefore not say, without qualification, that Swiss retirement-family permits always "match the principal applicant". That proposition is clear for AFMP families and only partially true for Article 44 families. 57

Procedure is also more route-sensitive than generic guidance often suggests. AFMP family members still need identity and relationship documents, and dependent relatives need proof of maintenance. But the ordinary third-country route is more documentary and more intensely canton-led. The SEM's 2025directive places the cantonal migration authority at the centre of substantive review and uses the Swiss representation abroad as a preliminary filter where the application is lodged there. Zurich and Basel-Stadt show that cantons may ask for recent payslips, tax material, rent contracts, room-count evidence, and other practical proofs beyond the bare statute. Those requirements are not always black-letter federal law,buit they can determine outcomes in practice. 58

<!-- 11 -->

Finally, derivative residence in retirement cases remains vulnerable to the sponsor's continuing position. AFMP family rights depend on the sponsor's original treaty right. Third-country family rights under Article 44 remain discretionary and can be lost or not renewed if revocation grounds arise; although Article 50 can later stabilise the spouse's position after family breakdown, that is a separate statutory safety valve rather than part of the original derivative status. In retirement cases, that interacts directly with the principal sponsor's continued financial self-sufficiency and genuine Swiss residence. A content system should therefore treat initial grant, renewal, and later permit independence as three distinct legal stages. 59

## H. Drafting cautions

·Do **not** describe Swiss retirement family migration as a single "retirement family visa" regime. Swiss law distinguishes between treaty-based AFMP family rights and domestic FNIA/AIG family reunification. 14

·Do **not** flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA positions. The AFMP routeis broader and rights-based; Article 44 FNIA/AIG is discretionary and narrower. 60

·Do **not** suggest that all relatives of a non-EU/EFTA retiree can obtain derivative residence. Ordinary Article 44 family reunification covers only the spouse and unmarried children under 18. 61

·Do **not** omit the wider AFMP family circle for EU/EFTA retirees, including dependent ascendants and dependent older descendants. 62

·Do **not** say that unmarried partners currently have an AFMP entitlement. Under current SEM guidance they fall, at most, within favoured admission, not a treaty claim. 18

·Do **not** confuse current law with SEM's March 2026 comparison of the current and "updated" AFMP. That document is explanatory and future-facing; it should not be treated as the operative source of present entitlements. 48

·Do **not** ignore the Article 44 spouse-language requirement, including the distinction between initial course enrolment and proof of oral A1 by renewal. 63

**·Do** **not** state that family members of retirees may never work. Under the AFMP, spousesand children may work; underArticles 44 and 46 FNIA/AIG, spouses and children of B-permit holders may also work. 64

·Do **not** state that all family members may work. AFMP ascendants do not have an automatic labour-market right, and some L-permit family scenarios remain restricted.65

·Do **not** assume permit duration always matches the principal sponsor. That is clear for AFMP family permits, but AIG family B permits follow a different validity structure. 28

·Do not present cantonal document requests, room formulas, or processing workflows as if they were federal black-letter laww. They are practice points only. 66

·Do **not** overlook post-breakdown and loss-of-status rules. Article 50 FNIA/AIG can preserve residence in defined cases, whereas AFMP family residence usually needs an independent or retained basis once the principal right falls away. 30
