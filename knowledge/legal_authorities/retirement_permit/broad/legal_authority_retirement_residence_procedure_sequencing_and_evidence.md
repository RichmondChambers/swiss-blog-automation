# Swiss Retirement Residence Procedure,

# Sequencing and Evidence Pack

# Executive legal summary

Swiss "retirement residence" is not a single visa category in Swiss law. For long stays, the operative legal question is whether the person can obtain or keep a **residence** **permit;** where a person is visa-required, **a** **national** **D** **visa** is usually only the entry mechanism for a residence purpose that itself depends on cantonal annd, in some cases, federal approval. Official Swiss materials draw this distinction clearly: EU/ EFTA nationals may often complete the residence steps after arrival, whereas non-EU/EFTA nationals seeking residence without gainful activity generally have to apply **before** **entry,** and visa-required applicants must also engage the competent Swiss representation abroad. 1

For **EU/EFTA** **retirees,** the legal basis is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its Swiss implementing rules. Official SEM guidance treats retirees as part of the broader class of economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals who have a right of residence if they have sufficient financial means and comprehensive health and accident insurance covering all risks in Switzerland. Official SEM factsheets further state that, for stays over three months, the person must register with the competent cantonal authority or migration office and, on production of a valid passport or identity card, receives a B EU/ EFTA permit ordinarily valid for five years. Federal guidance also recognises that the permit can be scrutinised earlier where means are uncertain, and that the permit can be revoked or not renewed if the conditions cease to be met. 2

**For non-EU/EFTA** **retirees,** Swiss retirement residence sits within the discretionary admission rules of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and the implementing ordinance. SEM guidance states that the relevant conditions are cumulative: minimum age 55, particular personal ties to Switzerland, necessary financial means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad other than management of one's own assets. The same guidance stresses that Article 28 FNIA does **not** create an entitlement even where the conditions are met; admission remains an exercise of cantonal discretion within federal law, with SEM consent reserved where required by the approval regime. The evidence burden is therefore materially higher than in the EU/EFTA route. 3

**The** **sequencing** differs sharply between the two routes. EU/EFTA applicants usually regularise their stay through commune or cantonal registration after arrival for a stay exceeding three months. Federal FAQ material says that the necessary steps for obtaining the permit can be taken after arrival. By contrast, non-EU/EFTA applicants seeking residence without gainful activity must, in principle, file **before** **travelling.** Official federal guidance on entry for a stay without gainful activity says that a permit is required before entry for stays exceeding three months, and that a visa-required applicant needs a national D visa. Geneva's official page for third-country no-activity residence says the entry-and-residence application must be lodged before entry; it can be made through the Swiss representation abroad or, in Geneva's own system, through the canton's online process, but if approved the applicant receives an entry authorisation to collect from the competent Swiss representation. 4

The **authorities** are layered. SEM's AIG directives state that cantons are generally competent to grant, renew or extend residence permits unless federal rules provide otherwise. The samme directives explain that SEM cannot force a canton to grant a permit; rather, in cases coveredby the federal approval regime, the canton must submit a favourable intended decision to SEM, which may approve, refuse, time-limit or attach conditions. Geneva, Vaud and Zürich materials all confirm the central role of cantonal migration authorities in examining and deciding residence files, while Swiss representations abroad act as intake and visa-issuing authorities for applicants abroad. 5

As to **evidence,** the reviewed official materials converge on a core set. These include identity documentation; proof of financial means; proof or commitment showing retirement and no gainful activity for non-EU/EFTA retirees; evidence of personal ties to Switzerland for the Article 28 route; housing information or lease evidence; and civil-status documents for family members. SEM's AIG directives also state that a valid travel document must accompany the permit application, and that for longer intended stays a criminal record extract from the state of origin may be requested, subject to special rules for persons benefiting from free movement law. Cantonal practice adds documentary specificity: Genève asks EU/EFTA applicants for ID, lease/landlord attestation and resource evidence; its third-country retirement page asks for passport copy, photograph, proof of means, accommodation if already available, and a motivation letter plus formal undertaking not to work. Zürich asks third-country retirees for the entry application, passport copy, reasons for choosing Zürich, municipality of intended residence, detailed proof of ties, written confirmation of no gainful activity, proof of financing, and-if under 75-a home-state criminal-record extract with certified German translation. 6

The procedural lesson for drafting and case preparation is straightforward. **EU/EFTA** **files** are registration-driven and condition-based. **Non-EU/EFTA** **files** are pre-entry, evidence-heavy, discretionary, and canton-sensitive. In both routes, weak document control, poorly evidenced finances, unclear housing arrangements, or a muddled explanation of the applicant's intended Swiss residence pattern can create avoidable delay or refusal risk. That last point is partly a professional inference from the documentary structure of the official processes, but it is strongly supported by the level of documentary detail demanded in cantonal practice. 7

# Structural overview of procedure and evidence

## Legal nature of the process

Swiss retirement residence analysis should begin with the residence-permit framework, not with the colloquial phrase "retirement visa". For long stays, visa law is only part of the entry sequence. SEM and FDFA materials distinguish between the **national** D **visa** for entry for a stay of more than 90 days and the **residence** **permit** itself, which is issued or refused under the residence rules. Official federal material also states that anyone staying in Switzerland for more thanthree months needs a permit. 8

## EU/EFTA sequencing

For economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals, the AFMP route is normally post-arrival registration rather than prior discretionary admission. Official ch.ch and SEM materials say that a person mayenter and then register as a person without gainfulI employment if the stay will exceed three months; the federal FAQ also says the steps necessary for obtaining the permit may be completed after arrival.The core documents at federal level are a valid passport or identity card and proof that the AFMP conditions are met. Cantonal practice may add local forms, lease evidence and online filing steps.(9

### Non-EU/EFTA seqquencing

For third-country retirees, official federal and cantonal material is significantly stricter. The stay must in principle be authorised **before** **entry.** Applicants who are visa-required need a **national** D **visa,** and the visa is issued subject to approval by the competent cantonal migration authority. Geneva's official guidance says the third-country no-activity application must be filed before entry; if approved,the applicant receives an entry authorisation for collection via the competent Swiss representation. Zürich's practice page describes the process as an **entry-authorisation** application for retirees. 10

### Authorities and institutional roles

SEM's AIG directives state that cantons are generally responsible for granting, renewing and extending residence permits, subject to the federal distribution of competences. Those directives also explain that SEM cannot compel a canton to grant a permit, but in approval cases it has a supervisory and veto role. FDFA pages confirm that Swiss representations abroad receive visa applications, and Vaud's commune guidance explains that communal foreign-national desks act as reception and transmission points for files that are then analysed and decided by the cantonal population service. 11

## Core evidence categories

Across the reviewed federal and cantonal materials, seven evidence categories recur.

Identity and travel documents are fundamental. SEM's directives state that home-state identity papers must accompany the permit application and that, in principle, a valid passport is required for permit grant or extension. 12

Proof of retirement and no gainful activity is central in third-country retirement files. SEM's retirement guidance requires a confirmation that the person has actually withdrawn from active professional life and an undertaking not to pursue gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad. Geneva and Zürich both expressly require written no-work commitments. 13

Financial evidence is required in both routes, but the legal benchmark differs. EU/EFTA materials point to sufficient mneans and insurance; third-country materials require proof of the necessary means under Article 28 FNIA / Article 25 OASA. Official cantonal document lists commonly ask for pension evidence, bankable wealth or other proof of resources. 14

Evidence of personal ties to Switzerland is required only for the third-country retirement route. Zürich asks for a detailed account of the ties and proof of prior stays; Geneva asks for a motivation letter, and SEM's guidance describes theunderlying legal standard. 13

Housing evidence is procedurally important. Geneva asks EU/EFTA applicants for a lease or landlord attestation and asks third-country retirees to provide accommodation evidence if alreadly available; Zürich wants the intended municipality of residence. 15

Insurance evidence is a substantive condition in both routes, even where the precise documentary format varied in the canton pages reviewed. Federal materials uniformly require comprehensive health and accident insurance. 16

Criminal-record and civil-status material can also be required. SEM's AIG directives say a criminal-record extract from the state of origin may be requested before grant of a permit for a likely longer stay,

## Timing and file management

Processing times are not uniform. FDFA warns that national-visa processing can take several weeks or even months. Zürich states that review of a retirement entry-authorisation request may take several months. Geneva publishes processing-time monitoring for OCPM applications, and its follow-up page offers personalised follow-up only for applications submitted more than two months earlier. These are procedural practice points rather than black-letter national rules. 18

# Source-grounded legal materials

## Primary legal texts

# Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration

## Article 10 paragraph 2 FNIA

Official significance: for a stay without gainful activity that is intended to last more than three months, the permit must in principle be sought before entry from the authority competent for the intended place of residence. SEM's AIG directives reproduce this point expressly in the section on "entry for a stay without gainful activity". 32

## Article 13 paragraphs 1 and 2 FNIA

Official significance: the permit application must be supported by home-state identity papers, and for a likely longer stay a criminal-record extract from the state of origin may be required. SEM's AIG directives tie these points to Article 13 and explain that AFMP beneficiaries are subject to special rules on the criminal-record issue. 33

## Article 17 FNIA

Official significance: if a person enters without securing the appropriate authorisation first and then applies for a permit, cantonal authorities may require the applicant to wait abroad for the decision. SEM's AIG directives invoke Article 17 in recommending that a person intending a long stay without gainful activity oobtain an assurance of the permit before entry. 32

## Article 28 FNIA

Official significance: the substantive federal basis for third-country retirement residence. SEM's AIG directives paraphrase it as requiring that the person no longer be economically active, have reached the minimum age set by the Federal Council, possess particular personal ties to Switzerland and have the

<!-- 7 -->

necessary financial means. SEM also states that the rule does not itself generate a right to the permit. 24

## Article 99 FNIA

Official significance: the SEM approval regime. SEM's AIG directives explain that SEM may refuse, time-limit or condition a cantonal decision in approval cases, but can only do so once a cantonal authority has taken a favourable position. 34

## Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment

## Article 25 OASA

Official significance: the key implementing provision for third-country retirees. SEM's retirement guidance and Zürich's official practice note both paraphrase it as fixing the retirement age at 55, requiring particular personal ties, requiring necessary means, and prohibiting gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad apart from managing one's own assets. Article 25 paragraph 4 is also the bridge to the supplementary-benefits benchmark for necessary means. 35

## Articles 83 to 86 OASA

Official significance: the competence and approval framework. SEM's AIG directives use these articles together with Article 99 FNIA and the EJPD approval ordinance to explain when cantonal decisions must be submitted for federal consent and when the residence card may or may not be issued. 34

# Article 8 OASA

Official significance: accompanies the passport/identity requirement in permit files. SEM's AIG directives cite Article 8 OASA together with Article 13 FNIA when reqquiring home-state identity documents to be attached to the application. 26

# Ordinance on Free Movement of Persons

## Articles 16 and 17 OLCP

Official significance: these are the main Swiss implementing provisions used by SEM for economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals. SEM's OLCP directives say Article 16 links financial sufficiency to the question whether a similarly situated Swiss citizen would need social assistance, with CSIAS guidelines as the reference; for retirees with pension income, the benchmark is whether means exceed the threshold for Swiss supplementary benefits. Article 17 is used by SEM to explain the general five-year duration of the initial permit and the possibility of an earlier revalidation where necessary. 36

## AFMP and Annex I

# Article 24 Annex I AFMP

Official significance: the treaty basis for EU/EFTA economically inactive residence. SEM's OLCP directives and factsheets say it covers retirees and other persons without gainful activity, together with family members,provided they have sufficient means and health/accident insurance. The same official materials connect Article 24 to revocation or non-renewal if the person later falls into public dependency or loses the insurance/means conditions. 37

# Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's **factsheet on residing in Switzerland without gainful activity** is the clearest federal operational source for EU/EFTA retirees. It sets out the two indispensable conditions-sufficient means and insurance-states that the B EU/EFTA permit is issued for more than three months on presentation of

<!-- 8 -->

valid identity documents, and notes that the permit is ordinarily valid for five years, though authorities may shorten that horizon if means are not secure. 38

The SEM **AIG** **directives** are the strongest federal administrative source for the third-country route and for procedural architecture more broadly. They contain separate sections on the division of competences between cantons and SEM, on entry for a stay without gainful activity, on passport and criminal-record evidence, and on retirees under Article 28 FNIA / Article 25 OASA. They also make clear that retirees must provide a confirmation that they have actually retired and must commit not to pursue gainful activity. 39

The SEM **OLCP** **directives** supplement the treaty route. Their key procedural contribution is that they operationalise the financial benchmark, emphasise that economically inactive EU/EFTA residence is not subject to the restrictive non-EU admission rules, and explain permit duration, review and revocation logic for this class of permit holder. 40

The federal portal ch.ch is useful for public-authority procedure summaries. Its no-gainful-employment page states that EU/EFTA nationals can registerfor no-work residence for stays over 90 days, and that non-EU/EFTA nationals seeking such residence must apply to the cantonal authority before arrival and obtain a visa from the relevant Swiss representation where required. Its permits page also gives a general renewal window of no earlier than three months and no later than two weeks before permit expiry. 41

The **FDFA/EDA** **visa** **pages** are the key official sources for the role of Swiss representations abroad. They explain that national visa applications are filed with a Swiss embassy or consulate where applicable, that requirements differ by country of residence, and that long-stay visa processing may take several weeks or months. The generic FDFA residence page also distinguishes between EU/EFTA and third-country residence, and directs applicants to canton-specific migration authorities. 42

# Cantonal practice notes

## Geneva

Geneva's OCPM pages are particularly useful because they break the procedure out by nationality and by no-activity category. For **EU/EFTA** no-activity residence, Geneva states that the person has a right to reside if sufficient resources and insurance exist, and asks for a colour copy of ID,lease or landlord attestation, resource evidence, and civil-status evidence for accompanying family members; official translations are required where needed. For **third-country** retirement files, Geneva says the application must be lodged before entry, either via the competent Swiss representation or through Geneva's online process; if approved, the applicant receives entry authorisation to collect at the Swiss representation. Geneva requires a passport copy, a passport photograph, proof of means, accommodation if already available, and a motivation letter plus formal undertaking not to work in Switzerland or abroad except for management of one's own assets. These are cantonal evidential and procedural rules, not federal black-letter law. 43

## Zürich

Zürich publishes a dedicated officiaI pathway for a third-country retiree's **entry** **authorisation.It** sets out the substantive threshold and gives one of the clearest official document lists: completed entry application, passport copy, reasons for choosing Zürich, intended municipality, detailed evidence of links to Zürich, written confirmation of no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad, proof of financing, and-for applicants under 75-a criminal-record extract from the home country with certified German translation. Zürich also tells applicants that review of the request may take several months and points to a separate canton-issued practice note explaining its approach to economically inactive third-country

<!-- 9 -->

residence, including personal ties and financial scrutiny. These are highly useful evidence-handling and sequencing sources, but they remain cantonal practice. 44

## Ticino

Ticino's official page for a new B permit for EU/EFTA nationals states that a no-activity applicant must submit the request to the migration office within **14** **days** **of entry** and warns that a late application may lead to a fine. This is a useful illustration that filing windows can be expressed concretely at canton level. It should not be overstated as a universal federal rule for all cantons. 45

## Vaud

Vaud's commune guidance explains the procedural role of the communal **Bureau** **des étrangers** as reception and transmission desk for permit files to the cantonal population service (SPOP),and says that foreigners taking residence in Switzerland must announce themselves to the residents' control office of the commune within **eight days.** It also explains that the communes constitute and transmit the residence file to SPOP, which then analyses and decidesit. This is a strong official source on procedural routing in one canton, but again it is cantonal practice material. 46

# Practical legal analysis

The **single** **biggest** **procedural divide** is between treaty-based residence and discretionary admission. For EU/EFTA retirees, the filing structure is essentially evidential registration: prove sufficient means, prove comprehensive health/accident cover, present identity documents, and register the stay. Future drafting should reflect that the legal burden is to prove satisfaction of defined AFMP conditions, not to persuade the canton that admission is desirable in the abstract. That is why EU/EFTA procedure is relatively standardised, whny the permit is ordinarily five years, and why federal materials speak in the language of entitlement rather than discretion. 47

For third-country retirees, the reverse is true. The route is controlled by a discretionary admission framework in which both **substance** **and** **sequencing** matter. The applicant should ordinarily remain abroad, open the file before entry, satisfy the canton on the Article 28/Article 25 requirements, and-where the approval regime applies-await the federal overlay that follows a favourable cantonal position. Procedurally, this means that non-EU/EFTA retirement cases are far lessforgiving of under-prepared applications. Unlike the EU/EFTA route, there is no safe drafting shortcut that treats the process as a near-automatic administrative registration. 48

The reviewed materials also show that **evidence** is **route-specific.** In an EU/EFTA case, the centre of gravity is financial and insurance compliance, with housing and identity as standard administrative proof points. In a third-country retirement case, those core items remain necessary but are no longer sufficient. The file must also evidence real retirement, no gainful activity, and particular personal ties to Switzerland. Zürich's and Geneva's official lists demonstrate how cantons operationalise that evidential burden. A professionally prepared Article 28 file should therefore bestructured in modules: identity and travel, retirement/no-work, finances, ties to Switzerland, accommodation, family civil status, and public-order documents. That modular structure is an inference from the reviewed official document lists, but it is a high-confidence one. 49

The federal and cantonal materials further show why **preliminary** **canton** **assessment** matters. SEM's AIG directives expressly recommend obtaining an assurance of the permit before entering Switzerland for a longer no-work stay; Geneva's process shows how a positive cantonal outcome can be converted into entry authorisation for collection through the Swiss representation. In practice, that makes the pre-entry stage more than a formality. It is the stage at which the canton tests whether the file is complete enough, coherent enough and strong enough to move into the visa/entry phase. 50

<!-- 10 -->

On **document quality** **control,** the safest view is conservative. The official sources reviewed here do not provide a single all-Switzerland rule on translations or legalisations for every document type in every canton. What they do show is that cantons often require **official** **translations** where needed, and sometimes specify the translation language and the document type precisely. Geneva says this expressly for marriage annd birth records submitted in family-linked files; Zürich requires a certified German translation for the foreign criminal-record extract and for powers of attorney. A broader point -that inconsistent names, dates or civil-status data across passports, leases, bank letters and family records will predictably damage a file-is best treated as professional inference rather than as a stated federal rule, even though it is strongly supported by the structure of the reviewed forms and checklists. 51

Finally, **timing** must be drafted conservatively. Federal FDFA material warns that national visa files can take several weeks or months, and Zürich says retirement entry-authorisation review may take several months. There is therefore no safe generic representation that a retirement application can be filed shortly before an intended move. The prudent internal drafting position is that a third-country retirement matter should be prepared well in advance, with time allowed for cantonal scrutiny, SEM approval where needed, visa issuance, and then the post-arrival registration steps. EU/EFTA matters are usually faster but still require local registration and file assembly. 52

# Open questions and limitations

No single official federal source was located in the retrieved materials that sets out a universal Switzerland-wide checklist for **translations and** **legalisations** in retirement-residence cases. The evidence instead came from canton-specific pages and from general federal doctrine on document filing. Any future drafting on apostilles, legalisation chains or sworn-translation practice should therefore be framed as **canton- and** **document-specific,** unless a more direct federal source is added to the pack. 51

The same caution applies to **local** **filing** **deadlines** such as "within 14 days" or "within eight days": official canton pages support those statements for specific jurisdictions, but they should not be written as universal federal black-letter rules without canton qualification. 30

# Drafting cautions

·Do not describe Swiss retirement residence as a single "retirement visa" route. For stays over 90days,the decisive legal act is the grant of a residence permit; where required, a national D visa is only the entry instrument. 53

·Do not flatten the distinction between **EU/EFTA treaty residence and non-EU/EFTA** **discretionary** **admission.** The former is a rights-based AFMP route; the latter is an Article 28FNIA / Article 25 OASA route with no entitlement even if the conditions are met. 54

·Do not imply that a third-country retiree can ordinarily arrive first annd then regularise the stay. Official federal and cantonal materials point the other way. 22

·Do not omnit the **SEM** **approval layer** in positive third-country retirement cases. The canton is central, but it is not always the final approving body. 55

·Do not treat "proof of retirement" as satisfied merely by age or self-description. Official guidance expects evidence of withdrawal from professional life and a formal no-work undertaking. 13

<!-- 11 -->

·Do not state that a criminal-record extract is universally required in all retirement files. Federal guidance says it may be requested for intended longer stays, with special rules for AFMP beneficiaries; Zürich makes it an express requirement in its third-country retiree path, but that is cantonal practice. 56

·Do not present a single national rule on translations, legalisations or local filing windows unless the source is specific. The safer formulation is that these matters are often handled by canton-specific procedural requirements. 57

·Do not present commune or canton practice as if it were federal law. Geneva, Zürich, Ticino and Vaud materials are procedurally useful, but they remain local practice documents. 57

·Do not promise short processing times. Official sources support a "weeks to months" range for long-stay/third-country files. 31
