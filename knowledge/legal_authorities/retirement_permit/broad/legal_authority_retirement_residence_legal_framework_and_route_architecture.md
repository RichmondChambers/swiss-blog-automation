# Swiss retirement residence legal framework and route architecture

## A Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate a single statutory "retirement visa". What practitioners and clients often call Swiss retirement residence is, legally, two different residence architectures. For EU/EFTA nationals, the relevant route is the treaty-based right of residence for persons who are not pursuing economic activity in the host state under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its Annex I. For third-country nationals, the relevant route is the domestic admission ground for retired persons in Article 28of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, implemented by Article 25 of the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. A visa, where one is required, is an entry instrument;the residence status itself is the permit. 1

That distinction matters because the EU/EFTA route is rights-based and the third-country route is discretionary. The treaty text gives economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals a residence right if they can show sufficient financial resources and comprehensive sickness insurance, and SEM's own public guidance states that non-working EU/EFTA nationals are entitled to a B EU/EFTA permit if they prove adequate resources and health/accident cover. By contrast, Article 28 AIG states that retired third-country nationals "may" be admitted, and SEM guidance and Federal Administrative Court case law both confirm that Article 28 is a potestative provision, not an entitlement even when its conditions are met. 2

For EU/EFTA retirees, the core legal conditions are that the applicant is not pursuing economic activity in Switzerland, has sufficient means not to rely on social assistance, and holds sickness insurance covering all risks. SEM's January 2026 VFP directives add that there are no quotas for stays without gainful activity and that the restrictive admission conditions in Articles 23-25 VZAE are not applicable to this treaty route. The same federal materials explain that the sufficiency benchmark is tied to Swiss social-assistance standards, with a specific supplementary-benefits benchmark for retirees receiving pensions, and that the normal B EU/EFTA permit is granted for five years, though it may exceptionally be limited to two years where means appear insecure. 3

For non-EU/EFTA retirees, Article 28 AIG and Article 25 VZAE/OASA require a narrower and more demanding case. The official materials locate four cumulative conditions: minimum age 55, particular personal ties to Switzerland, the necessary financial means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad other than management of one's own assets. SEM's AIG directives add two further points of practical importance: the retiree must genuinely have their centre of life in Switzerland, and the permit is refused renewal if it later appears that the centre of life is elsewhere. Those directives also state that the grant of the residence permit must be submitted to SEM for approval. 4

The most litigated third-country issue is "particular personal ties". Article 25 VZAE gives examples such as sufficiently long earlier stays in Switzerland and close relationships with close relatives in Switzerland. But the Federal Administrative Court has interpreted the statutory phrase more narrowly than a simple family-link test: the required ties must be direct ties to Switzerland itself, and later published case law confirms that the mere presence of a close relative in Switzerland is not enough on its own. The official SEM directives therefore treat family relationships as relevant evidence, but not as automatically sufficient legal fulfilment of Article 28 AIG. 5

Financial self-sufficiency is also handled differently across the two routes. Under the EU/EFTA route, SEM guidance ties sufficiency to the social-assistance threshold, and for retirees specifically to the level above which a person would qualify for supplementary benefits. Under the third-country retirement route, the AIG directives and case law require resources that are available with a high degree of certainty for life, so that future reliance on public funds is practically negligible; family promises are often treated as insufficient unless they provide security comparable to the applicant's own funds, such as a bank guarantee. 6

Family position is likewise route-sensitive. EU/EFTA retirees benefit from Annex I, Article 3 AFMP, which grants derivative residence rights to defined family members regardless of nationality,gives them permits of the same duration, and allows the spouse and qualifying children access to employment. Third-country retirees do not receive a bespoke Article 28 family code; SEM guidance sends them into the ordinary family-reunification provisions of the AIG. If the sponsor holds a B permit, Article 44 AIG makes family admission discretionary and subject to cohabitation, adequate accommodation, no social assistance, no supplementary benefits caused by the reunion, and language/integration conditions. 7

At a high level, then, Swiss retirement residence should be drafted as two legally distinct systems. EU/ EFTA retirement residence is best understood as non-economic residence under treaty free movement. Third-country retirement residence is best understood as an exceptional, individually assessed B-permit route under Article 28 AIG. Any later drafting that flattens these into one "Swiss retirement visa" framework will misstate the legal architecture. 8

## B Structural overview of the legal framework

**Legal** **nature** **of** **retirement** **residence.** Swiss law separates entry control fromm residence status. The AIG regulates entry and visas in Articles 5-6, while short-stay and residence permits are regulated separately in Articles 32-33. On the substance of "retirement residence", Swiss law splits between a treaty route for EU/EFTA nationals without economic activity and a domestic admission route for retired third-country nationals. 9

**EU/EFTA** **retirement** **residence.** The AFMP gives a right of residence to persons who do not pursue economic activity in the receiving state, provided they have sufficient resources and health insurance. SEM's public permit page states expressly that non-working EU/EFTA nationals have a claim to the B EU/ EFTA permit if those conditions are shown. SEM's 2026 VFP directives add that there are no quotas for this category and thnat the restrictive third-country criteria in Articles 23-25 VZAE do not apply. 10

**Non-EU/EFTA** **retirement** **residence.** Article 28 AIG allows admission of foreign nationals who are no longer economically active if they meet the age, personal-ties and financial-means conditions. SEM guidance and published Federal Administrative Court decisions treat these conditions as cumulative and the whole route as discretionary. The official materials currently identify the minimum age as 55. 11

**Financial self-sufficiency** **and** **insurance.** For EU/EFTA applicants, SEM's fact sheet and VFP directives link sufficient resources to the Swiss social-assistance standard and,for retirees, to the supplementary-benefits threshold; all risks must be covered by sickness/accident insurance. For third-country retirees, SEM's AIG directives say the means must exceed the level that would entitle a Swiss comparator to supplementary benefits and must be available with a high degree of certainty over time. 12

<!-- 2 -->

**No** **gainful activity.** The EU/EFTA treaty route is framed as residence for a person who does not pursue economic activity in the host state. The third-country retirement route is stricter in the published federal materials: SEM states that there must be no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad, other than management of one's own assets. 13

**Particular** **personal** ties **to** **Switzerland.** Article 25 VZAE gives examples of qualifying ties, notably longer previous stays and close relations with close relatives in Switzerland. However, Federal Administrative Court case law and SEM's own directives treat those examples as illustrative only and insist on direct, independent ties to Switzerland itself rather than famnily links alone.5

**Accommodation.** In the official materials reviewed, accommodation is not expressed as a stand-alone AFMP eligibility criterion for the principal EU/EFTA economically inactive applicant; the treaty text and SEM fact sheet focus instead on sufficient means and insurance. Under the third-country framework, accommodation features expressly in ordinary family-reunification provisions and in cantonal evidential practice, for example in Zurich's published retirement application guidance. This is best treated as an evidential and family-reunification issue rather than the core legal test for the main Article 28 retiree. 14

**Authorities, procedure, permit type and duration.** Article 98 AIG allocates residual federal competence to SEM and leaves cantons to designate their responsible authorities; Article 99 AIG allows the Federal Council to define cases requiring SEM approval. SEM's AIG directives say retirement permits fall within that approval practice. EU/EFTA non-workers ordinarily receive a B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years, renewableif conditions remain met. Third-country retirees fall within the general AIG residence-permit framework of Article 33: a time-limited residence permit tied to the purpose of stay and renewable only if revocation grounds do not arise. 15

## E Official guidance and administrative materials

The main official federal guidance located for this pack is unusually useful and should be treated as essential context for later drafting.

SEM's **Weisungen** **VFP** dated January 2026 are the key federal administrative source for theEU/EFTA route. They frame retirees as part of the broader category of "nichterwerbstätige" persons, confirm that treaty law applies directly, state that there are no quotas for non-working EU/EFTA stays, and explain the financial benchmark, the supplementary-benefits benchmark for retirees, and the normal five-year permit period with exceptional two-year limitation. They also state that the restrictive third-country conditions in Articles 23-25 VZAE are inapplicable to this route. 43

SEM's two-page fact sheet **"Aufenthalt in der Schweiz ohne Erwerbstätigkeit"** is a concise official statement of the EU/EFTA architecture. It says that non-working EU/EFTA nationals such as retirees have a residence right if they have sufficient means and insurance covering all risks, that means are measured against Swiss social-assistance levels, that pension income counts, that a B EU/EFTA permit is required for residence beyondd three months, and that the permit is normally five years, valid throughout Switzerland and automatically renewed if conditions remain satisfied. It also states, specifically for retirees, that means are generally adequate when they exceed the amount entitling a Swiss national to supplementary benefits. 44

SEM's public page on **B** **EU/EFTA** **permits** is less detailed but highly useful because it states the position in plain language: persons without gainful activity from all EU/EFTA states have a claim to the B EU/EFTA permit if they prove sufficient financial means and adequate health/accident insurance. This page is helpful for capturing the rights-based quality of the EU/EFTA route. 45

SEM's **AIG** **directives** dated 1 January 2026 are the key addministrative source for third-country retired persons. They state that the Article 28 AIG and Article 25 VZAE conditions are cumulative, identify the current mninimum age as 55, require particular personal tiesto Switzerland and necessary financial means, and add the published administrative rule that retirees may not work in Switzerland or abroad except in relation to their own asset management. They also explain that family ties alone are not enough after the Federal Administrative Court's interpretation, that the retiree must have their centre of life in Switzerland, that renewal is refused if that centre is elsewhere, that financial means must be available with lifelong reliability, and that family guarantees can be inadequate if not practically enforceable. 26

SEM's **legal-grounds** **page** is primarily an index, but it is useful for confirming the official instrument set for this topic: AIG, VZAE and the SEM directives themselves. 46

## G Practical legal analysis

The decisive legal divide is between treaty residence and discretionary domestic admission. The EU/ EFTA route is structurally stronger because the AFMP and its Annex I create a residence right once the applicant satisfies the treaty conditions. SEM's own public wording reflects that by speaking in terms of entitlement to a B EU/EFTA permit. The third-country route is structurally weaker because Article 28 AIG is framed as a "may be admitted" provision, and the Federal Administrative Court has repeatedly said that no entitlement arises even where the conditions are otherwise met. That is the central architectural point for any later legal-content generation. 51

A seconnd difference lies in how the law conceptualises retirement itself. Under the AFMP route, the principal question is whether the EU/EFTA national is not pursuing economic activity in the host state and is self-sufficient and insured. Under the third-country route, the published federal guidance is materially tighter: retirement is treated as withdrawal from economic life, and the no-work condition is stated to cover both Switzerland and abroad, save for management of the applicant's own assets. As a matter of drafting prudence, that means remote consultancy, paid advisory work and remunerated board roles should not be presented as obviously compatible with Article 28 merely because the client would be based outside Switzerland; the official wording is broader than that, and later drafting should flag those scenarios as risk-sensitive. This last sentence is a professional inference from the breadth of the official wording, not a direct quotation from a retirement-specific federal list. 13

<!-- 10 -->

Financial sufficiency is also not a single universal number. On the EU/EFTA side, the law is benchmark-driven: the AFMP itself ties sufficiency to the level below which nationals could claim assistance, while SEM translates that into Swiss social-assistance standards and, for pensioners, into the supplementary-benefits line. On the third-country side, the statute simply requires the "necessary" means, but SEM and the Federal Administrative Court interpret that in functional terms: the authorities must be able to treat public-funds dependence as practically negligible for the future. That is why family undertakings are scrutinised so closely. A signed promise from children in Switzerland is not automatically worthless, but the official sources insist that third-party money must offer reliability comparable to the applicant's own funds, with bank-style security as the benchmark example. 52

The "particular personal ties" condition is where Article 28 cases often fail, and it must be handled conservatively. Article 25 VZAE gives examples that might encourage overly expansive drafting, especially the reference to close relationships with close relatives in Switzerland. But the Federal Administrative Court's line of authority, endorsed again in the published 2025 decision BVGE 2025 VII/4, makes clear that Article 28 retirement residence is not a disguised ascending-line family-reunion route. What matters is a direct relationship with Switzerland itself: previous stays, social or cultural connectedness, local involvement, Swiss origins, or other ties not reducible to "my children live there". That is why SEM's AIG directives expressly cite the case law and refer to links with local communities, cultural participation and direct contact with the resident population. 5

Accommodation requires careful drafting because its legal role differs by route. For the principal EU/ EFTA economically inactive applicant, the treaty text and SEM's fact sheet focus on means and insurance, not on a separate stand-alone housing threshold. For third-country retirees, Article 28 AIG itself likewise does not list accommodation as a stand-alone condition. Housing becomes explicit in the ordinary family-reunification provisions and in cantonal evidence requests. The safest internal position is therefore to describe accommnodation as an important evidential and family-linked issue rather than inflating it into the core statutory test for the principal applicant unless a canton's own published practice goes further. That is an inference from the federal text structure and the cantonal materials reviewed. 14

Cantonal practice matters far more on the third-country route than on the EU/EFTA route. Article 96 AIG requires the authority exercising discretion to consider public interests, personal circumstances and integration; Articles 98-99 then combine cantonal first-instance competence with a federal approval layer where prescribed. SEM's own directives say grants under the retiree route must be submitted for approval, and the Zurich materials show how a canton may ask for detailed evidence about locality,past stays, municipality of residence and the applicant's specific reasons for settling in that canton.By contrast, the EU/EFTA route is more standardised because the treaty conditions are narrower, rights-based and nationwide, and SEM's VFP directives emphasise the absence of quotas and the inapplicability of the stricter third-country conditions. 53

Permit form and time horizon must also be described carefully. EU/EFTA retirees normally receive a B EU/EFTA permit with five-year validity, valid throughout Switzerland, renewable while the treaty

<!-- 11 -->

conditions remain satisfied. Third-country retirees, by contrast, sit within the ordinary Article 33 AIG residence-permit framework: a purpose-bound, time-limited residence permit that can be renewed if no revocation issue arises, but that is not itself a settlement right. Settlement is governed separately by Article 34 AIG and its integration rules. Later content should therefore avoid implying that an Article 28retirement permit automatically or mechanically matures into a C permit. 54

## H Drafting cautions

·Do not describe Swiss retirement residence as one unified "retirement visa".Distinguish entry visas from residence permits, and distinguish the AFMP route from the Article 28 AIG route. 9

·Do not merge EU/EFTA and third-country positions. EU/EFTA non-wvorkers have a treaty-based rights route; Article 28AIG is a discretionary domestic admission ground. 55

·Do not imply that Article 28 AIG creates an entitlement. The official and judicial materials treat it as a "Kann" provision. 23

·Do not omit the Article 28/VZAE conditions for third-country retirees: age 55, particular personal ties, necessary means, and no gainful activity apart from own asset management. 4

·Do not treat family ties alone as automatically satisfying "particular personal ties to Switzerland". The published case law points the other way. 56

·Do not state a single nationwide minimum capital figure unless an official canton source is being quoted for that canton. Federal sources use functional benchmarks, not a universal article-28cash amount. 57

·Do not say that financial support from adult children is automatically acceptable. Official materials and case law scrutinise third-party support for reliability and enforceability. 25

·Do not say that non-EU retirees may freely undertake paid work abroad. The published federal wording is broader than "no Swiss work only". 22

·Do not overstate accommodation as a stand-alone statutory test for the principal applicant.Treat it carefully as family-reunification, evidential and cantonal-practice material unless a source says more. 14

·Do not present cantonal checklists as if they were federal law. They are useful for procedure and evidence, but they do not replace the statute, treaty or SEM directives. 58

·Do not imply that an Article 28 retirement permit automatically leads to a C permit. Settlement is separately governed by Article 34 AIG and integration criteria. 42

·Do not assume that the family rights are symmetrical across routes. EU/EFTA family members benefit from Annex I, Article 3 AFMP; third-country retirees rely on ordinary AIG family-reunification provisions, often discretionary where the sponsor only has a B permit. 59
