# Core Swiss Retirement Residence Framework Pack

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not create a standalone "retirement visa" as a separate legal category. In practice, retirement residence is dealt with through residence-permit law for persons without gainful activity. Two distinct legal architectures apply. First, EU/EFTA nationals rely principally on the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its Swiss implementing ordinance for economically inactive persons. Secondly, non-EU/EFTA nationals rely on Article 28 of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, read with Article 25 of the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. The distinction matters because the EU/EFTA route is treaty-based and rights-oriented once conditions are met, whereas the third-country route is expressly discretionary. <sup>1</sup>

For EU/EFTA nationals, retirement is only one practical example of "residing in Switzerland without gainful activity". There is no separate retirement-age threshold in the treaty route. The key conditions are sufficient financial means and all-risks sickness insurance, with Swiss implementing rules distinguishing between ordinary economically inactive persons and pensioners: for pensioners, the benchmark is whether means exceed the level that would entitle a Swiss national to supplementary benefits; for other non-working persons, the benchmark is the welfare/SKOS threshold. Residence permits for non-working EU/EFTA nationals are generally issued as five-year B permits, though authorities may limit the initial permit to two years where they consider closer review necessary. If sufficient means or adequate insurance later fall away, the permit may be revoked or not renewed. <sup>2</sup>

For non-EU/EFTA nationals, Article 28 AIG is an exceptional admission route for retirees who are no longer gainfully active. The statutory elements are a minimum age set by the Federal Council, special personal ties to Switzerland, and the necessary financial means; Article 25 VZAE sets the minimum age at 55, requires no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad other than management of one's own assets, and uses the supplementary-benefit threshold as the federal benchmark for required means. Current SEM guidance adds that these conditions are cumulative, that the applicant must prove genuine withdrawal from active professional life, and that the centre of life must be in Switzerland. The same guidance also treats the route as discretionary and expressly contrasts it with any stronger position that may arise under the free-movement regime. <sup>3</sup>

The most difficult issue in third-country retirement cases is usually "special personal ties to Switzerland". Although Article 25 VZAE mentions longer prior stays and close relationships to near relatives in Switzerland as examples, SEM guidance and Federal Administrative Court case law require more than family links alone. The authorities look for independent ties to Switzerland of a socio-cultural or personal nature, such as contact with the local community, cultural participation, or direct links with the resident population. The official materials also state that property ownership and economic links alone are not enough. This is a central drafting caution: family in Switzerland may support the case, but it does not by itself satisfy Article 28 AIG. <sup>4</sup>

Financial self-sufficiency is not a single national lump sum. The federal rules use legal benchmarks tied to welfare or supplementary-benefit eligibility, but the actual assessment is evidential and forward-looking. SEM's AIG instructions say that retirees' funds must be available with "great certainty" until the end of life so that the risk of welfare dependence is negligible, and warn that promises or even written guarantees from relatives in Switzerland may be insufficient unless the third-party funds are secured comparably to own assets, for example through a bank guarantee. By contrast, in the EU/EFTA context the Federal Supreme Court has recently confirmed that third-party undertakings can in principle prove sufficient means under AFMP Article 24, provided the funds are actually available, the third party is solvent, and the arrangement is credible over time; over-strict evidential requirements are not permitted. <sup>5</sup>

Maintenance of the status over time depends less on simplistic "183 days" shorthand and more on residence reality, centre of life, and continued compliance. SEM's AIG instructions say renewal may be refused if the retiree's centre of life is not in Switzerland; the Civil Code defines domicile by actual presence with the intention of remaining and says a person cannot have several domiciles at once; and AIG Article 61 provides that L permits lapse after three months and B/C permits after six months abroad if the person leaves without deregistering, subject to the special maintenance rule for C permits. Long-term planning therefore requires early attention to actual residence practice, integration, and the eventual move from B to C. A C permit is not automatic: under federal law it generally requires ten years' lawful stay and the last five years continuously on a residence permit, while SEM confirms that EU-15/EFTA nationals may in some cases obtain a C permit after five years under separate settlement agreements and that the AFMP itself does not govern permanent residence. Ordinary naturalisation then requires a C permit and, in principle, ten years' residence in Switzerland. <sup>6</sup>

## Official guidance, cantonal practice and practical analysis

### Official federal guidance / administrative materials

The most important federal administrative source for third-country retirees is the SEM AIG Instructions, current in the retrieved extract to 1 January 2026. Section 5.3 states that all retirement conditions are cumulative, lists the four operational criteria as age 55, special personal ties, necessary means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad save for own asset management, and adds several practically decisive points: applicants must prove withdrawal from active professional life, must keep their centre of life in Switzerland, and may bring spouse and minor children only through the ordinary family-reunification provisions. The same section also states that funds must be available with high certainty for life and that family guarantees may be insufficient unless secured comparably to own assets. <sup>50</sup>

For EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM OLCP Instructions and the SEM English factsheet on non-working residence are the key official guidance materials. They confirm that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals have a right to stay if sufficiently financed and adequately insured, that non-working stays are not quota-bound, that pensioners are measured against the supplementary-benefit level, and that initial permits are normally five years but may be reduced to two years in exceptional cases. The SEM FAQ adds procedural detail: non-working EU/EFTA residents register with the local authorities and apply for the relevant permit after arrival, generally within 14 days. <sup>51</sup>

Health insurance is handled partly through immigration law and partly through Swiss social-insurance law. On the immigration side, AFMP Article 24 requires all-risks sickness insurance, and the SEM factsheet repeats that accident and health risks must be covered. More generally, the Federal Office of Public Health states that anyone settling in Switzerland must take out health insurance within three months after taking up residence, while noting that under the European coordination rules some residents remain insured in another EU/EFTA or UK system. For retirement drafting, the safe formulation is therefore that adequate health coverage is mandatory, and that the exact insurer/system depends on whether the person is subject to Swiss mandatory insurance or a recognised coordination exception. <sup>52</sup>

Long-term planning should also use official settlement and citizenship guidance. SEM's AFMP FAQ states that the AFMP does not cover permanent residence and that EU-15/EFTA nationals may obtain a C permit after five continuous years under separate establishment agreements, while others obtain it after the regular ten-year period. SEM's naturalisation guidance states that ordinary naturalisation generally requires ten years' residence and a C permit, with B and C time counting and L time not counting. <sup>53</sup>

## Practical legal analysis

The EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA retirement routes differ fundamentally in legal strength. The AFMP route is built on a treaty right for non-economically active persons who are adequately financed and insured. Swiss implementing law specifies what "sufficient means" means, but the underlying logic is rights-based: if the treaty conditions are met, the authorities issue the corresponding permit. The third-country route under Article 28 AIG is different. It is not a treaty right, not a general migration channel for affluent parents, and not a family-reunification shortcut. It is an exceptional discretionary admission category for genuine retirees who can stay without becoming a public burden and who already have a sufficiently independent Swiss connection. <sup>58</sup>

That difference explains why EU/EFTA retirees usually have the stronger residence basis. Their route does not require a minimum age, does not demand proof of "special personal ties to Switzerland", and accepts a broader principle that support may come from third parties if it is actually available and credible. The third-country route, by contrast, requires both Article 28 AIG and Article 25 VZAE to be satisfied and still leaves the authorities with discretion. SEM's own instructions underline that this is an Ermessensentscheid and that demographic, social and public-interest considerations remain relevant. <sup>59</sup>

Financial self-sufficiency is assessed practically rather than by one universal public number. The federal rules use legal thresholds, but they do not eliminate the need for a file-by-file evidence assessment. For third-country retirees, the official guidance is particularly demanding: means should flow with great certainty for life, and the public-assistance risk should be negligible. This is why family undertakings are scrutinised closely. In the EU/EFTA context, the line is more liberal: third-party support can work, but it must be genuine, solvent and durable. For drafting, that means "resources sufficient under Swiss law" is the right high-level phrase, followed by the route-specific benchmark and the evidential rider, rather than a single, national minimum-income figure. <sup>60</sup>

The "special personal ties" criterion is the decisive doctrinal filter for third-country retirees. VZAE lists prior stays and close relatives as examples, but SEM and the Federal Administrative Court insist that the relevant ties must be ties to Switzerland and not merely to family members living there. That is why evidence of language, local participation, repeated and meaningful stays, community links, and personal familiarity with Swiss life can matter disproportionately. The same logic also explains why property ownership and purely economic ties are weak evidence on their own. A case built only on adult children or grandchildren in Switzerland is exposed to the objection that Article 28 would otherwise become a simplified ascendant family-reunification route, which the courts have rejected. <sup>61</sup>

"No gainful activity" is also broader than many clients assume. For third-country retirees the official rule extends to work in Switzerland and abroad, with only the management of one's own assets carved out. The official materials retrieved do not offer special carve-outs for remote consulting, paid advisory retainers, remunerated board positions or similar modern arrangements. The conservative legal position is therefore that such activity is risky and should not be presented as automatically permissible. If there is any continuing remunerated activity, the lawyer should test whether the factual pattern is really passive asset management or whether a different immigration route is needed. <sup>22</sup>

Centre-of-life evidence matters both at grant and on renewal. SEM says the retiree's centre of life must be in Switzerland and that renewal may be refused if that ceases to be true. The Civil Code's domicile concept reinforces that by focusing on actual residence plus the intention of permanent stay, while AIG Article 61 gives hard lapse rules tied to time abroad. For this reason, "183 days in Switzerland" is not a safe legal summary of the immigration position. It may sometimes be a practical shorthand used outside immigration, but the controlling immigration questions are where the person is actually living, whether the Swiss residence is their real home, whether the permit conditions continue to be satisfied, and whether any long absences trigger lapse or call the centre of life into question. <sup>13</sup>

Cantonal practice can materially affect outcomes. Federal law provides the framework, but the operational file is built and screened at cantonal level, and the official canton sources retrieved show meaningful variation in documentation, sequencing and evidential emphasis. Vaud openly requests an explanatory ties narrative and accepts a lump-sum-tax agreement as part of the evidence package; Geneva stresses the need, in principle, to apply from abroad; Zurich publishes an internal practice note that is notably restrictive on third-party support and emphasises the absence of any entitlement. In consent cases, however, cantonal support is not the end of the matter: the Federal Administrative Court confirms that SEM may refuse approval without being bound by the canton's view. <sup>62</sup>

Retirement residence also needs to be planned against the client's long-term objective. If the goal is eventual settlement or citizenship, the route matters. For many third-country retirees, the first step will be a purpose-bound B permit under Article 28 AIG, not an assured path to C. EU/EFTA retirees may in some cases reach C earlier under separate settlement agreements, but SEM is explicit that permanent residence is outside the AFMP itself. Ordinary naturalisation then sits another step beyond that and requires, at federal level, a C permit plus ten years' residence. Retirement clients who expect frequent long absences, minimal integration activity or continuing professional engagement may therefore qualify for an initial residence status yet still be poor candidates for later C-permit or naturalisation planning. <sup>63</sup>

Lump-sum taxation and other high-net-worth options should be handled as distinct from retirement residence. Official cantonal materials show that a concluded forfait fiscal arrangement may sometimes be used as evidence of financial capacity, and Zurich separately describes admission based on significant cantonal fiscal interests under the broader derogation framework rather than under Article 28 AIG itself. The right drafting approach is therefore to treat tax residence and immigration residence as related but separate questions: a tax ruling can support an immigration file, but it is not itself the retirement-permit entitlement. <sup>64</sup>

## Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

- Do not describe Swiss retirement residence simply as a "retirement visa" without clarifying that Swiss law works through residence permits and, for many third-country cases, an entry-authorisation procedure as well. <sup>65</sup>
- Do not flatten the EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA routes into one test. The AFMP route and Article 28 AIG route are structurally different. <sup>66</sup>
- Do not imply that non-EU/EFTA retirees have an entitlement to residence if they meet the paper criteria. Article 28 AIG remains discretionary. <sup>67</sup>
- Do not omit the Article 28 AIG / Article 25 VZAE "special personal ties to Switzerland" requirement for third-country retirees. <sup>21</sup>
- Do not treat family ties alone as automatically sufficient for Article 28 AIG. The official guidance and case law reject that simplification. <sup>68</sup>
- Do not state one national minimum wealth or income figure unless an official source for that exact amount exists. The legal framework uses benefit thresholds and case-specific evidence, not one universal retirement number. <sup>69</sup>
- Do not ignore health insurance. AFMP cases require all-risks sickness insurance, and Swiss residence usually triggers mandatory health insurance within three months unless a coordination exception applies. <sup>70</sup>
- Do not assume that remote work, foreign consulting or paid advisory/board roles are compatible with the third-country retirement route. The official carve-out is only own asset management. <sup>22</sup>
- Do not present "183 days" as the sole or decisive immigration test. The official sources focus on domicile, centre of life and lapse rules. <sup>13</sup>
- Do not present cantonal evidence requests or Zurich's internal financial caps on third-party support as federal law. They are canton practice points. <sup>71</sup>
- Do not imply that a retirement B permit automatically leads to a C permit or citizenship. Separate residence-duration, integration and status rules apply. <sup>63</sup>
- Do not confuse Article 28 retirement residence with separate admissions based on substantial fiscal interests or with lump-sum taxation arrangements. <sup>72</sup>

## Open questions and limitations

The retrieved official Fedlex PDF for the AIG was the consolidated text as at 15 October 2023, and the directly retrieved VZAE PDF extracts were from older but apparently unchanged consolidations. The current SEM instructions as at 1 January 2026 continue to apply the same retirement criteria. Before publication or external reliance, a final clerk-level check against the newest Fedlex consolidated versions would still be prudent. <sup>86</sup>

No official source was retrieved that expressly resolves all edge cases around unpaid directorships, paid foreign consultancy, hybrid remote work, or a nationwide non-EU rule for shortening permit validity where finances are marginal. Those points should be treated as legally risky and canton-sensitive rather than as settled black-letter law. <sup>87</sup>
