# Swiss legal authority pack on salary and local employment conditions in Swiss non-EU local hire work permit cases

Suggested filename:

legalauthoritynoneulocalhiresalaryandlocalconditionspack.pdf

This pack is drafted in English for internal use, but the legally authoritative Swiss federal texts are the German,French and Italian consolidated versions. Available English federal translations are expressly informational only. 1

## Executive legal summary

For a third-country national being locally hired by a Swiss employer, salary and local employment conditions are not a side issue: they are a distinct statutory admission requirement. At federal level, salaried admission depends on the overall economic interest of the employment, an employer-filed application, and compliance with the linked admission conditions in Articles 20 to 25 FNIA, including resident-worker priority, customary salary and employment conditions, personal qualifications, and suitable accommodation. The competent cantonal labour-market authority decides first whether those admission conditions are met before the first work-related short-stay or residence authorisation is granted, and SEM approval may still be required in some cases. 2

The salary test is not a single Swiss threshold and should never be described that way in automated content. Article 22 FNIA requires salary and employment conditions that are customary for the place, profession and sector. Article 22 OASA then tells authorities what "customary" means in practice:they must consider legal rules, collective employment agreements, standard employment contracts,pay and conditions for comparable work in the same enterprise and sector, and salary statistics. SEM's own guidance adds that statistics are averages rather than automatic legal floors, although they may often function as practical minima in cities, while slightly lower levels may in some cases be accepted elsewhere. 3

In practice, contract quality is central. OASA requires the employer to provide an employment contract or mnandate confirmation showing the duration of the work, the employment conditions and the salary. SEM goes further and expects written information on the workplace, function, duration, working time, base salary, any supplements, social benefits and deductions; a binding written contract signed at least by the employer; permnit-reservation wording; and, as a matter of guidance, a contract using the foreign worker's language. Zurich's official third-country guidance also expects a current, complete contract signed by both parties. 4

Salary compliance does not collapse into broader permit eligibility. A salary can be locally compliant yet the application still fail because the role is not in the economic interest of Switzerland, the resident-worker search is not adequately evidenced, quotas are unavailable, or the candidate does not meet the qualification threshold for managers, specialists or other qualified workers. Conversely, a very strong skills-shortage narrative does not waive Article 22. Zurich's official notes illustrate this separation:

shortage professions may justify lighter search evidence, but the salary and employment-conditions requirement still remains. 5

Variable remuneration needs cautious handling. SEM states that, in principle, only salary elements agreed by contract may be taken into account. Employee participation plans may be counted, but only if the worker has sufficient direct means of support and the participation remains in a reasonable proportion to the basic salary. For postings, travel, board and lodging reimbursements are expressly not salary. In sector-specific SEM guidance for professional sport, certain in-kind items such as health-insurance premiums, rent, maintenance costs and public-transport costs may count, but performance-related bonuses, expense reimbursements and sponsor benefits do not. That sector-specific sports guidance should not be generalised automatically to ordinary local-hire cases. 6

Part-time, fixed-term and probationary contracts are not automatically barred by Swiss employment law, but they create permit-framing risks. Swiss contract law expressly recognises part-time work as employment. Fixed-termn contracts end without notice on expiry, and probation defaults to one month with a seven-day notice period unless otherwise agreed, subject to a three-month cap. In immigration law, the type of permit follows the intended duration of stay: a short-stay permit is for a stay of up to one year, while a residence permit is for a stay of more than a year. That said, Geneva's current local-hire guidance for third-country nationals states, as a cantonal practice point, that the employment should be full-time. 7

## Structural overview of salary and local conditions

The requested structural overview is best understood as a layered compliance model. First, **the** immigration file must satisfy the core admission stack for salaried work: economic interest, employer sponsorship, quotas where relevant, resident-worker priority, salary and local employment conditions, personal qualifications, and accommodation. Salary compliancetherefore sits inside, not above, the general admission framework. 8

**Secondly,** the salary and local-conditions inquiry is deliberately contextual. The legal touchstone is not what the employer wishes to pay, nor a Swiss-wide average, but what is customary for the place of work, the profession and the sector. OASA makes that concrete by directing the authority to legal norms, generally applicable collective agreements, standard employment contracts, same-firm and same-sector comparators, and salary statistics. The official posting platform reaches the same destination from the labour-market side: some sectors are governed by legally binding collective agreements or standard contracts with mandatory minimum wages, while others use a customary wage range, and the simplified web presentationis only an aid because the original collective agreement text remains legally binding. 9

**Thirdly,** evidence is document-led. The employer must submit a contract that allows the authority to test salary, hours, duration, benefits and deductions against the asserted benchmark. SEM guidance expressly says the authority must have written information on those matters and must examine a binding written contract before granting the permit. This is one reason salary review interacts strongly with employer credibility: the permit system uses the contract and supporting payroll narrative as proof that the role is real, locally benchmarked, and lawfully structured. 10

**Fourthly,** "local conditions" are broader than cash salary. Ordinary Swiss employment law supplies baseline conditions on working time, rest, overtime/overwork, continued pay during incapacity and holiday pay. The Labour Act sets maximum weekly hours of 45 or 50 depending on the category of staff, a daily rest period of at least 11 consecutive hours, and a mandatory 25% supplement for statutory

overwork unless compensated by time off. The Code of Obligations separately regulates contractual overtime, continuation of salary during illness and similar incapacity, paid holiday, fixed-term expiry and probation. When Article 22 FNIA speaks of local employment conditions, these labour-law baselinesand any applicable collective or standard-contract conditions form part of the relevant context. 11

Fifthly,official boenchmarking tools are aids, not guarantees. FSO's Salariumn is a statistical calculator based on observed salary data; the SECO national wage calculator produces a median and interquartile range for a profile and expressly says the results are not wage recommendations. SECO also states that a canton-level wage range is only shown when minimum statistical coverage and reliability thresholds are met. These tools are useful evidence, but they do not replace the legal analysis of sector, porofession, canton, collective agreement, working time and contract structure. 12

### Primary legal texts and official administrative materials

This section combines the requested primary legal texts and official administrative materials. The legal core for local-hire third-country work cases is FNIA Articles 18, 21, 22, 23, 24,32,33 and 38, together with OASA Articles 22, 22a, 83 and 84. Those provisions establish the admission structure,the customary-salary test, the supporting-document requirement, the permnit-duration framework and the cantonal pre-decision. The available English FNIA text used here is expressly informational only, so article references should be checked against the official German, French or Italian consolidated text when drafting any publication-facing legal proposition. 28

SEM's Directives LEI chapter on gainful employment is the most important administrative explanation of Article 22 for present purposes. It explains the protective purpose of the salary rule, identifies the benchmark sources, states that only contractually agreed salaryelements are normally countable, discusses employee participation plans, requires written detail on key remuneration and working-condition elements, and expects a binding contract with permit-reservation wording. It also reminds authorities to check social-insurance contributions and adequate health and accident cover, and notes that for engagements exceeding three months the worker must be affiliated to the occupational-pension system. 29

For wage evidence, the main official tools are FSO's Salarium and SECO's national wwage calculator. Salarium estimates a gross monthly salary and salary bracket for a defined profile using observed data from the Swiss Earnings Structure Survey, while SECO's calculator gives a median and interquartile range for a profile and expressly says the result is not a wage recommendation. Both tools are statistical rather than dispositive. Salarium's current public page also notes a data lag: at the time of the source used for this pack, the calculator used data up to 2022, with 2024 data expected to be released during the second quarter of 2026. 30

The official labour-market materials matter because salary compliance is often sector-driven. The Confederation's posting platform states that Switzerland distinguishes between sectors covered by legally binding collective agreements or standard contracts with mandatory minimum wages and sectors in which a customary wage range applies. The same platform provides simplified summaries of collective agreements but explicitly says that only the original collective text is legally binding. For automated content, that warning matters: the summary page is helpful for triage, but the operative wage and condition clauses must be taken from the original agreement or standard contract. 31

Ordinary Swiss labour law supplies the rest of the "local conditions" baseline. The Code of Obligations defines the employment contract, including part-time work, and regulates overtime, salary continuation during incapacity, paid holiday, fixed-term expiry and probation. The Labour Act and its ordinances then supply maximum weekly hours, daily rest and the supplement for overwork beyond the statutory maxima. Together, these texts explain why a permit file that shows a headline salary but omits hours, leave, overtime treatment, benefits or deductions is incomplete as a matter of local-conditions analysis. 32

## Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich.** Zurich's official explanatory note for third-country nationals is one of the clearest cantonal practice documents. It mirrors the federal sequence closely: economic interest, vacancy-notification duty where applicable, resident-worker priority, salary and employment conditions, and personal requirements. For salary review, Zurich says the purpose is equal treatment of foreign and domestic workers, and requires the employer to inform the labour-market authority about the duration of employment, employment conditions and salary. The contract must be current, complete in content and consistent with conditions customary for the location and sector; a dated contract signed by both parties is listed as mandatory. Zurich also expressly says that in occupations with a pronounced shortage of skilled labour the search-evidence burden may be reduced, but the applicant must justify the shortage. 33

Geneva.Geneva's current third-country local-hire guidance is stricter and highly operational. OCPM grants the permit only if the OCIRT foreign-labour directorate gives a favourable prior view. Geneva states that the employer must have explored the Swiss and EU/EFTA labour markets first, must prove the recruitment search, and must notify the cantonal employment office as quickly as possible, irrespective of the unemployment rate in the sector. Geneva also states that customary salary and working conditions for the sector must be respected, refers employers to an online salary calculator, and says the job should be full-time. The same page notes quotas, the possibility of conditions or a different authorisation type, and the need for a motivation letter from the employer. Separately, Geneva's labour-law pages emphasise that all businesses must respect the salary and working conditions applicable in the canton, and Geneva's statutory canton-wide minimum wage page confirms that workers habitually employed in Geneva are subject to a cantonal minimum wage, which from 1January 2026 is CHF 24.59 gross per hour, subject to stated exceptions andd sector-specific rules such as agriculture and floriculture. This is canton-specific and must not be generalised to Switzerland as a whole. 34

**Bern.** Bern's official page on employing workers from third countries is concise but procedurally useful. It requires the application to be filed at least eight weeks before the intended start date andd lists the expected supporting package: a justification letter, CV, training evidence or diplomas, employment contract, passport copy, and evidence of recruitment efforts in Switzerland and the EU-27/EFTA. For internal drafting, Bern is a good reminder that a salary benchmark is only one part of a file that must still tell a coherent story about necessity, profile and lawful sponsorship. 35

**Basel-Stadt.** Basel-Stadt's official third-country employment page is shorter, but it is useful because it expressly points applicants to Salarium to calculate the "owed" wage and links directly to the relevant federal legal bases. That is not a legal rule of exclusive use, but it is a useful indication that official salary calculators are accepted practice material in cantonal review. 36

**Sector-specific** **cantonal conditions.** Cantonal practice can also be sector-specific rather than route-wide.Vaud, for example, publishes current agricultural occupation conditions under its agricultural

## Practical legal analysis

The key analytical point is that "salary compliance" in Swiss third-country local-hire cases is a comparison exercise, not a checklist item. The authority is asking whether the proposed package fits the labour-market reality of the specific role in the specific place of work. Under OASA, that means starting with any binding legal mninima, CBA clauses or standard-contract terms, then testing the proposed package against same-firm and same-sector comparators and current statistics. Because SEM requires the contract to disclose working time, supplements, social benefits and deductions, the realistic comparison unit is not simply annual cash pay; it is the proposed job package for the stated hours under the stated Swiss law or sector rules. 38

Role, profession, seniority and candidate profile all matter, but they matter in different ways. Profession and sector drive the benchmark. Candidate experience and qualifications matter under Article 23 FNIA, which separately asks whether the individual is a manager, specialist or other qualified worker, and whether the qualifications fit the job. Zurich's guidance and the SEM directives both show that serious skills shortages can sometimes soften the burden of proving an exhausted local labour pool for some functions. What they do not show is any relaxation of Article 22 itself. In practice, therefore, a salary level that looks too low for the level of specialism claimed can damage the case twice: once under Article 22because it is not locally customary, and again as a credibility problem becauuse it does not match the asserted seniority or scarcity of the candidate. That second point is an inference from the structure of the sources, but it is a strong one. 39

The relationship between salary compliance and resident-worker priority is equally important. Article 21FNIA requires proof that no suitable domestic or EU/EFTA worker could be found, and SEM says Article 22 exists not just to protect the foreign worker but also to prevent the domestic workforce being undercut by cheaper labour. Salary and priority therefore work together conceptually: an application that offers a below-market salary creates a natural suspicion that the employer is solving the recruitment problem by wage undercutting rather than by showing a genuine shortage of suitable candidates. Geneva's third-country practice, which requires extensive proof of prior recruitment and respects local salary conditions, shows this interaction clearly. 40

Working time should be treated as a first-order issue in internal content. Swiss employment law recognises part-time employment, so there is no federal labour-law assumption that only full-time contracts are real employment contracts. But the immigration file still has to compare like with like.A 60% role cannot safely be benchmarked against a full-time salary without normalisation. Nor can a headline monthly figure be assessed sensibly if the contract omits hours, because SEM expects the authority to examine working time expressly. The Labour Act then adds hard outer limits of 45 or 50hours per week depending on the worker category, a daily rest period of at least 11 consecutive hours, and a 25% supplement for statutory overwork unless compensatory time off is granted. In short, local conditions analysis is inseparable from the hours model. 41

Benefits, allowances and deductions also require separation. SEM's general rule is that only contractually agreed salary elements are normally countable. That is narrow enough that automated content should not assume that commissions, discretionary bonuses, future upside or informal allowances can close a salary gap. The strongest official positive statement for non-cash elements that this research located is sector-specific: in professional-sport guidance, SEM accepts certain material

<!-- 9 -->

items such as rent or public-transport costs as part of salary, while excluding performnance bonuses, sponsor benefits and expense reimbursements. That is too specialised to be generalised into a broad proposition for ordinary corporate or professional local hires. For postings, the law is clearer: travel, board and lodging reimbursements are not salary. A conservative content system should therefore treat expenses and benefits as separate unless an applicable official source for the exact sector says otherwise. 42

Mandatory and ordinary incidents of employment should likewise be kept distinct from the benchmarked wage. The Code of Obligations requires continued salary for a limited time if the employee is prevented from working without fault because of illness, accident, legal obligations or public duties, provided the employment relationship has lasted or was concluded for more than three months. Holiday is paid and may not ordinarily be replaced by money while the employment is ongoing. Family allowances, meanwhile, are social-insurance benefits intended to offset child-related costs; the official BSV and AHV/IV materials describe them as allowances, and employers generally pay them together with wages through the allowance system. These are parts of the Swiss employment package, but they should not be presented as simple substitutes for an otherwise insufficient base salary. 43

Fixed-term and probationary contracts are legally possible, but they need careful permit framing.A fixed-term employment contract is not invalid or suspect merely because it ends automatically on a specified date; that is the ordinary Swiss rule. Immigration law then overlays the duration framework: short-stay permits are for stays up to one year and residence permits for stays over one year. Probation is also ordinary Swiss law, with a defaulIt of one month, a seven-day notice period and a maximum of three months. The drafting risk is not that fixed-term or probationary clauses are unlawful, but that very short, highly conditional or loosely drafted contracts can make the role appear unstable or speculative. Where an application is already asking the canton to accept a specialist shortage or a high-value role, instability in the contract can weaken the overall credibility of the sponsorship narrative. That last proposition is again an inference, but it is strongly supported by the document-heavy cantonal practice in Zurich, Geneva and Bern. 44

The common weak points in salary evidence follow from the sources. Unsigned contracts, missing hours, vague bonus language, missing deductions, no reference to applicable CBA or standard-contract terms, failure to show the sector benchmark, unexplained divergence from calculators, and role descriptions that do not match the candidate's CV all make it harder for the canton to verify Article 22. From a system-design perspective, the safest internal practice is to make the salary benchmark traceable: identify the exact work canton, sector, occupational function, weekly hours, applicable agreement or standard contract, tool output used, and any non-cash component, then explain why the proposed package is still locally customary. 45

### Drafting cautions and source list

For salary-related blog or explainer content generated by an internal immigration content system, the following cautions should be treated as hard editorial constraints.

·Do not write that Switzerland has a single minimum salary for third-country work permits. The federal rule is "customary for the place, profession and sector", and binding minima may instead come from a sectoral collective agreement, a standard contract, or a canton-specific norm. 46

·Do not collapse salary compliance into the whole permit test. Articles 18, 21, 22, 23 and 24 FNIA are cumulative, and cantonal pre-approval remains essential. 2

<!-- 10 -->

·Do not imply that calculator output guarantees approval. Salarium and SECO's calculator are statistical tools and expressly not wage recommendations; SECO also limits display of a "usual" wage range to statistically robust cases. 47

·Do not say that bonus, commission, equity, housing or future upside automatically cure a low fixed salary. The safe official rule is that only contractually agreed salary elements generally count; equity is only conditionally countable; expense reimbursements are not salary; and sector-specific acceptance of certain benefits in professional sport should not be generalised. 42

·Do not universalise Geneva practice to the whole of Switzerland. Geneva's full-time expectation and cantonal minimum wage are local rules or practice points, not federal rules. 48

·Do not present simplified collective-agreement summaries as legally binding text. Use the original CBA or standard-contract wording where operative terms matter. 20

· Do not ignore working time, overtime treatment, leave, deductions, social-insurance contributions and ordinary employment protections. SEM expects those matters to be apparent from the contract, and Swiss labour law supplies real minima on hours, rest and paid leave. 49

·Do not omit the permit-contingency clause or rely on an unsigned or incomplete draft contract. SEM guidance expects a binding written contract reviewed before permit grant, and Zurich explicitly lists a current, complete contract signed by both parties asmandatory evidence. 50
