# Swiss authority pack on employer justification and business need for non-EU local hires

**Suggested filename:**

legalauthoritynoneulocalhireemployerjustificationandbusinessneedpack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

In Swiss non-EU local-hirecases, the employer's case is not a free-standing plea for apreferred recruit. It is a structured evidential burden built around the statutory admission criteria: overaIl Swiss economic interest, resident-worker priority, vacancy-notification where applicable, compliance with local salary and working conditions, candidate qualification, and quota availability. Official federal and cantonal materials show that the employer must substantiate these points with concrete documents and role-specific explanations, not generic statements that the candidate is valuable or that the business "needs talent".1

The business rationale matters, but only to the extent that it helps prove legally relevant matters. Commercial growth, client demand, project urgency, technical need, organisational gaps, and Swiss-market activity can support the required showing of overall economic interest and role necessity.They do not, by themselves, displace resident-worker priority, salary compliance, qualification requirements, or quotas. Official SEM and cantonal materials are explicit that employers must also prove unsuccessful recruitment of priority workers and provide evidence on the role, the company, the candidate, and the employment terms. 2

Official materials are relatively thin on a standalone legal formula framed as "why this role must be in Switzerland". The more precise position is that authorities consistently ask for the **reason** **the vacancy** **must be filled, the job description, company information,** and, where relevant, **project contracts,** **project descriptions, implementation plans, or start-up business plans.** The practical consequence is that an employer should show a clear Swiss nexus for the role: Swiss operations, Swiss clients, Swiss projects, Swiss contractual obligations, Swiss organisational need, or Swiss market development. That is best understood as a well-supported practice point rather than a separately formulated statutory test. 3

A genuine vacancy must be distinguishable from a role designed around a preferred non-EU candidate. SEM guidance states that the search for priority workers must be real in time, geographical scope, and recruitment method; contact with third-country nationals should only occur after the Swiss and EU/EFTA search has actually failed; and candidates with priority must not be filtered out by irrelevant criteria such as unnecessary language demands or marginally relevant experience requirements. This is the clearest official basis for treating "candidate-shaped" roles with caution. 4

For start-ups, scale-ups, recently formed businesses, and smaller employers, the federal legal tests are the same, but the evidential focus becomes heavier. Official materials ask for a three-year business plan, intended organisation, staff development, financing, market and competition information, incorporation documents, annual accounts, and explanations of losses or financial sustainability. In

practice, that means weaker operating history must be offset by stronger documentary proof of market reality and business viability. 5

No official material supports the proposition that employer support makes approval routine. Zurich's published guidance states that there is no legal entitlement to a permit,that applications are examined individually, and that authorities may require additional documents beyond the basic list. Quotas remain a separate control, and the 2026 federal circular confirms that cantons may only draw on the federal reserve with a justified and documented need. ⁶

## Structural overview of employer justification and business need

Employer justification in a Swiss non-EU local-hire case is strongest when it is built in the following order.

**Swiss** **economic** **and** **business** **nexus.** The employer should first show why filling the role serves Switzerland's overall economic interest, rather than merely the employer's private preference. Official materials connect this to labour-market conditions, sustainable economic development, integration prospects, and the need to avoid supporting low-wage structures or mere particular interests. 7

**A** **real Swiss** **vacancy.** The employer should then identify the actual Swiss role: itsduties, place in the organisation, business function, and why it must be staffed now. SEM asks for the reason the vacancy must be filled, the job description, and company information; for project work, it additionally asks for the work contract or order, project description, and implementation plan.8

**Unsuccessful** **recruitment** of **priority** **workers.** Only after defining the real role should the employer prove that the Swiss and EU/EFTA labour markets were genuinely tested and that suitable priority workers were not available. Official guidance expects RAV and EURES steps, sector-appropriate advertising, agency searches where relevant, and evidence on candidates considered and rejected. 9

**Suitability** **of the** **named** **candidate.** The named non-EU candidate must then be shown to fit the role, not merely to be impressive in the abstract. Official materials focus on CV, diplomas, references,and the match between qualifications and the tasks of the new role. 10

**Compliance** **of** **employment** **terms.** Salary and other conditions must be shown to match local, occupational, and sectoral norms. A signed contract is a core document, and some cantonal guidance recommends adding SECO wage-calculator material to demonstrate compliance. 11

**Quota** **and** **procedural viability.** Even a strong substantive case can still fail if the relevant quota is unavailable or if the file is incomplete. The employer therefore has to align the business-need case with timing, permit type, and quota management. 12

### Primary legal texts, official guidance and cantonal practice

**Primary** **legal texts.** The core federal framework is the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, as summarised by SEM under the heads of overall economic interest, quotas, precedence,vacancy-notification, salary and employment conditions, and personal requirements. For this topic, the operative provisions are the employer-application rule, the employment-admission rule for employees, quota control, resident-worker priority, vacancy-notification where applicable, local salary and working conditions, and qualification requirements. SEM's procedure and application-document pages then show howv those statutory criteria are operationalised in practice. 25

**Official federal guidance and administrative materials.** SEM Chapter 4 of the foreign-national directives is the most important administrative source. It gives the most precise official explanation of what counts as overall economic interest, how priority is proved, when shortage occupations can ease proof burdens, how vacancy-notification interacts with admission, how local salary norms are checked, and what qualifies as personal suitability. It also incorporates case-law references from the Federal Administrative Court. On documentation, the SEM application-documents page is especially useful because it sets out, in one place, the expected evidence for precedence, role-necessity, project files, start-up files, and salary documentation. SECO materials are important for the vacancy-notification system, the labour-shortage indicators, and the national wage calculator. 26

**Cantonal practice** **notes.** Zurich's January 2024 note on third-country employment is the clearest official cantonal source for ordinary local-hire cases. It expressly organises local-hire files around economic interest, vacancy-notification, priority, salary/work conditions, and personal requirements, and it gives a concrete but non-exhaustive document list. Geneva's published page for salaried third-country workers and its 2023 gJuide on drafting the employer's motivation letter are particularly valuable for understanding how cantonal authorities expect employers to articulate the company narrative, role importance, recruitment chronology, candidate selection, salary benchmarking, and start-up evidence. Ticino's official materials are thinner on narrative evidence, but they confirm the same examination headings: quotas, resident-worker priority, salary/work conditions, and qualifications. 27

### Practical legal analysis

**The** **legal basis** **for** **employer justification.** Swiss law does not create a free-standing statutory concept called "employer justification letter", but the federal and cantonal materials make clear that the employer must justify and evidence the case across each admission criterion. SEM expressly says applicants must explain and document why admission is in the overall economic interest; Zurich makes

<!-- 6 -->

a "specific application justification" mandatory for local hires; and SEM's application-document page requires the reasons the vacancy must be filled together with the role description and company information. The practical lesson is that the employer letter is best understood as the narrative container for legally required evidence, not as an independent source of legal sufficiency. 28

**What must be shown about the role and the business need.** The official sources repeatedly focus on **the** **actual Swiss** **role** and its **commercial function.** SEM requires the reasons the vacancy must be filled and the duties of the position; for project work it asks for a work contract or order, a project description, and an immplementation plan; and for business start-ups it asks for incorporation documents and a three-year business plan showing intended organisation, staff development, financing, and market and competition analysis. Zurich goes further in local-hire practice by listing a business plan, market prospects and comparisons, orders, balance sheet, and profit-and-loss material as evidence that may support the economic-interest showing. Geneva's guide likewise asks the employer to explain the importance of the post, the role's contribution to business development,and the elements showing economic interest for the canton. 3

**Organisation, headcount, sector, growth, turnover, projects, client obligations, and Swiss-market** **activity.** These are best treated as evidential indicators, not statutory criteria in their own right. Geneva's official guide asks for the company's history, recent developments, activities, services, clientele, staff numbers, staff nationalities and permit status, annual accounts, and, where losses exist, an explanation of how they will be absorbed; for new or forming businesses, it asks for informnation on shareholders, financial means, and a three-year plan. SEM's start-up materials ask for intended organisation, staff development, financing, market data, and competition analysis. Zurich's note asks for business-plan material, orders, and financial statements. Taken together, official practice supports using headcount, growth, turnover/revenue material, projects, and client obligations as proof that the role sits in a real Swiss commercial setting. But none of those items replaces the nneed to prove priority, salary conformity, and candidate qualification. 29

**Why** **the** **role** **must** **be** **filled** in **Switzerland.** Official texts do not formulatethis as a separate black-letter rule. What they do require is evidence that the vacancy must be filled, that the role has defined duties within the employer's organisation, and, where relevant, that there is a Swiss-linked project, order, implementation plan, or business development reason. The most defensible internal-content formulation is therefore that the employer should show a **Swiss** **operational nexus** for the role: for example, Swiss customer delivery, Swiss project execution, Swiss regulatory or client-facing responsibilities, Swiss organisational oversight, or Swiss market development. That formulation is an inference from official document requirements and cantonal practice, not a standalone statutory phrase. 5

**Why the role cannot be filled by available resident labour.** This is one of the hardest legal burdens and should not be diluted. SEM states that precedence applies in principle in all cases; employers must show genuine efforts as to timing, geographical breadth and recruitment measures; and the search via Swiss, EU, and EURES channels must have failed before turning to a third-country candidate. Both SEM and Geneva expect evidence not only of advertising, but also of what the employer did with the applications received. Zurich asks for an applicant list naming nationality, qualifications, date of application, and reason for rejection. SEM also says employers are expected to make efforts in the form of specific training and further training of workers already available on the labour market, and Geneva expressly asks the employer to show that a worker available in Switzerland cannot be trained within a reasonable period. These materials together mean that a bare statement such as "we could not find anyone suitable" is legally weak unless backed by chronology, channels, candidate-comparison material, and a training analysis where relevant. 30

<!-- 7 -->

**Genuine vacancy versus role designed around a preferred candidate.** Swiss official sources do not use that phrase as a formal test, but the logic is clear. SEM says contact with third-country nationals should occur only after unsuccessful searches for priority workers, that search efforts must not be a mere formality, and that priority candidates must not be excluded by non-relevant filters such as unnecessary language skills or marginal requirements. Zurich and SEM both require dated advertisements and detailed applicant lists. The administrative risk therefore increases where the role description seems tailored to one pre-selected candidate, where the search started too late, where criteria shifted after the candidate was identified, or where rejection reasons are generic and unparticularised. In internal drafting, this should be treated as a strong practice risk arising from the application of resident-worker priority, rather than as a separate statutory doctrine. 31

**Why** **the** **named** **non-EU** **candidate** is **suitable.** Official sources require a direct match between the candidate's qualifications and the role actually to beperformed. SEM's basis page and directives focus on CV, diplomas, certificates and references, with the broader rule that admission is limited to managers, specialists, and other qualified workers. Zurich's local-hire note states that the professional qualifications must correspond to the new activity. Geneva's guide asks the employer to set out the candidate's profile and career, explain why that person was selected, and attach the CV, diplomas, and signed contract. The safest drafting therefore links each core duty of the Swiss role to specific evidence in the candidate's background. Generic claims of rarity, global profile, or cultural fit are much weaker than role-to-qualification matching. 32

**Shortage** **occupations.** Shortage helps, but only in a disciplined way. SEM allows the proof burden on resident-worker priority to be eased where the occupation is demonstrably affected by a pronounced structural skilled-labour shortage, and Zurich similarly states that detailed advertisements and candidate lists may be dispensed with in such occupations. At the same time, both SEM and Zurich make clear that the shortage must be shown plausibly and that temporary cyclical fluctuations are not enough. SEM ties the analysis to SECO indicators and experience from labour-force admissions. Shortage therefore works best as an evidential explanation for why local and EU/EFTA recruitment failed; it does not remove the need to prove the role, the candidate's fit, or salary compliance. 19

**Salary** **and** **local conditions.** Salary is legally load-bearing because it protects both the foreign employee and the domestic labour market. SEM says wages, social-insurance contributions and conditions must match local, occupational and sectoral norms, with checks against legal rules, comparable work in the same business and sector, and current statistical data. Zurich requires a current,complete, signed contract. Geneva's guide recommends attaching an extract from the SECO national wage calculator, and SEM's own salary guidance points to official statistical wage tools. Crucially, official guidance does not treat high remuneration as a substitute for other legal criteria.A well-paid role can still fail on priority or qualification, while an otherwise strong case can fail if the contract or wage terms do not match local norms. For start-up packages, SEM adds that only contractually owed salary elements are normally counted, though employee participation may count if there are still sufficient direct means of support and the participation is proportionate to the base salary. 20

**Quotas and** **timing.** Quotas are not merely background policy; they are an additional gate. Zurich states that initial short-stay and residence permits are quota-controlled and can be issued only if a quota is available. SEM's 2026 circular confirms 8,500 third-country quotas, split into 4,500 B permits and 4,000 L permits, with part retained in the federal reserve. The same circular says cantons may ask for supplementary reserve quotas only on a justified basis and that SEM asks for early notice and information such as planned corporate relocations to Switzerland or projects requiring qualified foreign staff.Geneva's public page advises employers to file roughly six to eight weeks before entry. The

<!-- 8 -->

conservative view is that timing should be built into the employer case from the outset, especially where business need depends on project start dates or market-launch windows. 33

**Start-ups, scale-ups, and** **small employers.** Federal law does not create a distinct admission test for start-ups in local-hire cases, but the evidence demanded in official materials is notably heavier. SEM asks for deed of foundation, commercial-register entry, and a three-year plan covering organisation, staffing, financing, market and competition. Zurich lists business plans, budgets, orders, balance sheet and profit-and-loss material. Geneva asks for annual accounts, explanations of losses, and, if the business is recent or in formation, information on shareholders, financial means, and a year-by-year three-year business plan. The practical effect is that newer or smaller businesses should expect closer scrutiny of whether the Swiss role is real, funded, and commercially anchored. Official materials are relatively thin on any softer or more permissive start-up doctrine; caution is therefore warranted. 3

**Multinationals and** **group** **companies.** The key distinction is between an ordinary Swiss local hire and a true intra-group transfer. SEM expressly says that precedence does not apply to operational transfers of senior managers and essential specialists in international companies, but that is presented under the exception framework. If the worker is being directly engaged into a Swiss role rather than transferred under the recognised intra-group model, the ordinary local-hire burden should still be assumed to apply. Group status, by itself, does not replace the need to show the Swiss role, the business need, and -unless an exception is properly available-the unsuccessful recruitment of priority workers. 34

### Drafting cautions

A support letter or internal content entry on this topic should be drafted with the following cautions in mind.

·Do not say or imply that strong employer support makes approval likely as a matter of course. Official materials describe individual assessment, no automatic entitlement, and separate quota control. 6

·Do **not** present "business need" as a free-standing legal test detached from priority, salary, and qualification. The legal structure remains cumulative. 35

· Do **not** equate employer preference for a known candidate with immigration necessity. SEM guidance expects the search for Swiss/EU/EFTA candidates to come first and to be genuine. 4

·Do **not** use generic formulations such as "global talent shortage", "international mindset", or "strategic importance" without role-specific evidence, market context, and documentary back-up. Official materials focus on duties, company information, projects, orders, and business plans. 3

·Do not overclaim shortage occupations. The official standard is structural shortage, notshort-term hiring difficulty or cyclical pressure. 36

·Do **not** omit the candidate-comparison record. Applicant lists, rejection reasons, and a chronology of recruitment remain central where priority applies. 37

·Do **not** rely on salary level alone. Local salary compliance is necessary, but itdoes not repair weak evidence on priority or candidate fit. 38

·Do not universalise canton-specific points as if they were immutable federal rules. Geneva's published guidance includes, for example, a full-time-employment condition and a canton-focused economic-interest framing; these should be labelled as canton practice, not universal black-letter law. 39

·Do **not** treat start-up cases as easier because they are innovative. Official evidence requirements for company formation are often more demanding, not less. 5

·Do not conflate a genuine local hire with an intra-group transfer. The exception framework for transfers is separate and narrower. 24
