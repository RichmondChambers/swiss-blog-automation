# Swiss legal authority pack for non-EU local hire changes renewals and continuity

## A. Executive legal summary

For a Swiss **non-EU** **local** hire, the legal basis is not a free-standing "work permit" in the abstract. It is a **purpose-specific admission and residence authorisation** under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the implementing ordinance, issued after an employer-led application, a cantonal labour-market assessment, and, where required, SEM approval. The residence card then **confirms** the permit status; it is not a separate source of immmigration status in its own right. The official English FNIA translation is expressly stated to be for information only and to have no legal force; the binding texts are the German, French and Italian versions published in Fedlex. 1

After approval, Swiss law distinguishes sharply between **short-stay** **permit holders (Permit** L) **and** **residence** **permit** **holders** **(Permit** **B).** For **Permit** L, the permit is tied to a specific stay purpose and job changes are tightly restricted: a change is only possible for good cause, ordinarily only within the same branch and profession, and only where the inability to continue with the existing employer is not attributable to the worker. For **Permit** B, the default statutory position is more mobile: a third-country national admitted for employment may ordinarily work anywhere in Switzerland and change job without additional authorisation. However, that default changes if the B permit was issued **with** **an** **express labour-market condition or condition linked to a specific post.** In that **case,** **a** fresh cantonal application is required before the change. 2

The most important drafting point is therefore this: do **not oversimplify** **B-permit** **mobility.** It is wrong to say that all non-EU local-hire permits remain tied to the original employer forever; it is equally wrong to say that all B-permit holders can freely alter their employment circumstances without immigration consequences. The correct position is that a normal B permit has significant professional mobility, but **express conditions, a change of purpose, or a material change in the factual basis of admission** **can** **reactivate** **a** **need** **for** **immigration** **review.** Official materials are much thinner on internal promotions, title changes, reductions in hours, variable remuneration changes,and garden leave than they are on simple employer changes. Where those changes alter the approved function, salary basis, or permit condition, the safer authority-grounded view is that a fresh cantonal assessment may be needed. 3

Renewal is **not automatic.** A B permit is time-limited and may be extended only if the legal requirements remain met and there are no revocation grounds. A first B is generally issued for up to one year, then extended for up to two years at a time depending on the case. The renewal application must normally be filed at least 14 days before expiry, and if a renewal application has been filed the foreign national may remain in Switzerland during the procedure unless a different order is made. Geneva's official guidance goes further for durable B and C permit holders and states expressly that,if the expired permit authorised gainful employment, the person may continue working while awaiting the renewal decision. 4

For unemployment and continuity, Switzerland does **not** give third-country local hires the same codified residence-retention regime that EU/EFTA nationals have under Article 61a FNIA. Instead, the official SEM directives state that a residence permit **may** **be** **extended** as long as unemployment-insurance entitlement continues, or where the person is in an unemployment programme; on re-employment, the general rules apply again. The same directives also state that unemployed B-permit holders can in principle search across Switzerland for a new job, although a legal claim to move canton depends on being able actually to take up the new position. No equivalent general federal right to remain as an ordinary third-country jobseeker after loss of employment was identified in the reviewed official sources. 5

Long-term continuity also turns on absence rules and C-permnit planning. A permit expires on departure abroad, on grant of a permit in another canton, on expiry, or on certain expulsion events. If the person leaves without formally deregistering, an L permit lapses after three months abroad and a B or C permit after six months abroad; short visits back to Switzerland do not interrupt those periods if the centre of life has moved abroad. Settlement permit planning usually requires **ten** **years' total stay** **on** **L and/or** B **with** **the** **last** **five** **years** **uninterrupted** **on** B, plus no revocation grounds and adequate integration. That means a period spent only on Permit L does not satisfy the "last five years on B" requirement, even though L time can count toward the overall total. 6

## B. Structural overview of employment changes renewals and continuity

The Swiss legal structure for non-EU local hires is built around **admission** **for** **a** **defined** **purpose.** In a local-hire case, the Swiss employer files the application; the cantonal labour-market authority assesses the labour-market criteria; SEM reviews the admission question where federal approval is required; and the cantonal migration authority issues the residence permit card. Zurich's public guidance reflects the same architecture: third-country nationals need a cantonal labour-market preliminary decision before the migration authority issues the residence permit. 7

That structure matters because later employment changes are analysed not primarily through Swiss employment law, but through the **continuity of the authorised immigration purpose.Federal** law says that L and B permits are granted for a specific purpose and may be subject to conditions. The ordinance adds that, where the permit was granted under an admission rule for a specific stay purpose, **a** **change** **of purpoose requires** a **new** **permit.** SEM's directives then state that,if the stay purpose changes or a condition is no longer met, a new application must be made and the admnission requirements are reassessed. 8

For day-to-day drafting, the practical sequence is therefore: first identify the permit category; then ask whether the permit is **plain** B, **conditioned** B, or L; then ask whether the proposed change affects the approved employer, the branch/profession, the salary and conditions under Article 22 FNIA, the permit condition, or the stay purpose. That sequence is far more accurate than treating all non-EU local-hire approvals as uniformly employer-locked. 9

A separate continuity layer concerns the difference between **status** **and** **document.** SEM's directives describe the foreigner's card as a confirmation of the residence title. The card's physical validity therefore matters administratively, but the underlying status is governed by the permit decision itself, the statutory expiry rules, and any pending extension process. This distinction is especially important when drafting about expired cards, pending renewals, change-of-employerfilings, and time spent abroad. 10

## D.Primary legal texts

The primary federal legal texts for this topic are the **Federal Act** **on** **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** (FNIA/AIG/LEI/LStrI; SR 142.20) and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Gainful** **Employment** (VZAE/OASA; SR 142.201). The binding versions are theofficial consolidated texts published in Fedlex in German, French and Italian; the officially published English FNIA translation states on its first page that it is for information only and has no legal force. 28

The FNIA provisions doing most of the work here are Articles 11, 18, 21, 22, 23, **32,33,34,37,38,40,41,** **61 and** **62.** Taken together, they establish the employer-led application route, the labour-market admission criteria, the purpose-specific nature of L and B permits, B-permit job mobility,permit expiry rules, and revocation grounds. Articles 32 and 33 are especially important because they confirm that both L and B permits are granted for a specific purpose and may be made subject to conditions. 29

The VZAE provisions most directly relevant are Articles 4, **54,** 55, **56, 57,** **58,** 59 **and** **79.** Article 54requires a new permit when the authorised stay purpose changes. Article 55 restricts L-permit job changes. Articles 56 and 57 distinguish a true re-granting or chaining of L permits from a simple extension. Article 58 sets the usual initial B validity at one year, extendable by up to two years. Article 59sets the general renewal filing rule and confirms continued lawful stay during the pending procedure. Article 79 explains that short visits do not interrupt the statutory absence periods. 30

A further primary source, although annual rather than structural, is the Federal Council's yearly quota setting for third-country admissions under the VZAE annexes. For 2026, SEM's official circular records that the Federal Council kept the third-country quota at **4,500** **B** **permits** **and 4,000** **L** **permits,** with a split between cantonal allocations and federal reserve. Because these numbers change by year, drafting should describe quotas as **annual and** **variable,** not as fixed baseline law. 24

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important administrative source is the **SEM** **AIG** **Directives,** especially **Chapter** **4** **on** **gainful** **employment** and the main directives on **residence** **regulation.** These directives are the clearest federal explanations of how the authorities analyse L-permit job changes, B-permit job mobility, conditioned B permits, purpose changes, unemployment extensions, permit expiry through absence, and the distinction between extension and renewal. They are also where the clearest official elaboration of

"important reasons", "same branch and profession", and "new permit where purpose/conditions change" appears. 31

SEM's public **FAQ** **on** **working** is also unusually useful for this topic. It restates, in a concise public-facing way, that L holders need cantonal approval for a job change; B holders can normally change job without a permit; and an expressly conditioned B requires anew application. The same FAQ also distinguishes family members of L and B holders for work purposes. 32

SEM's **non-EU/EFTA** **procedure** **pages** and the general overview pages confirm the practical architecture of employer filing, cantonal review, SEM approval and permit issuance. They also confirm the limited and quota-based nature of non-EU labour-market access, which is why employer changes and material post-approval changes cannot be analysed as if the person had an unrestricted labour-market status. 33

What the official materials do **not** do particularly well is address every post-approval fact pattern. The reviewed federal sources are comparatively thin on: internal promotions wvithout employer change; demotions that leave the employer and branch unchanged; variable remuneration revisions; part-time conversions; and garden leave. The best federal method is therefore to reason fromthe controlling concepts of **purpose, condition,** and **Article 22 salary/working-condition compliance,** rather than to claim the existence of detailed federal bright-line rules that the official materials do not actually contain. 18

## F. Cantonal practice notes

### Geneva

Geneva's official guidance is the clearest cantonal source on post-approval job changes. It states that **holders of a durable B permit do not need to announce job changes,** but that **a third-country L** **holder** **must** **apply** **for** **a** **change** **of employer.** Geneva's dedicated L-permit change page further states that the **new employer must file the application,** ordinarily **six to eight weeks before the intended** **start date,** and must **wait for OCPM/OCIRT confirmation before the personstarts work.** Geneva's renewal guidance also separates **durable** **B/C** renewals from **L** **or conditioned** **B** renewals, and states that for third-country L or conditioned B cases the **employer** must make theextension request; it also says that third-country L holders **cannot** **in** **principle** **changge jobs.** 34

Geneva is also useful on continuity during renewal. Its durable B/C renewal page says that,once the renewal request has been filed, a person whose expired permit authorised gainful employment **may** **continue** **to** **work** **pending** **the** **decision.** That is a cantonal practice statement, not a broad federal clause found in the reviewed statutes, so it should be described as Geneva practice unless equivalent cantonal confirmation is available elsewhere. 35

### Zurich

Zurich's official materials are valuable for framing the **local-hire** **category** **itself.** The canton's public guidance distinguishes **local employment under Article** **18** **FNIA** from secondments and other categories, states that third-country nationals need a permit **from** **the** **first** **day,** and makes clear that **the** **employer** **must** **apply.** Zurich's January 2024 explainer also states plainly that there is **no** **legal** **entitlement to a specific permit and no entitlement to extension,** and that **first-time B and L** **permits** **are** **quota-limited.** Zurich's general residence page further confirms the cantonal labour-market preliminary decision model for third-country workers. 36

### Basel-Stadt

Basel-Stadt's official site confirms that, **for third-country nationals resident in Switzerland,** **employers** are the ones who submit requests to **extend** **work** **permits.** That is procedurally important for drafting content about renewals and change management, because it supports an employer-driven rather than employee-driven description of the route. 37

#### Vaud

Vaud's official Form 1350 is useful as a practice indicator because it expressly includes fields for **change** **of employer, extension of stay, renewal, B permit,** **and L permit** within the same official work-authorisation workflow. It is not a legal rule on its own, but it is good evidence that the canton operationalises third-country employment changes and renewals through the formnal authorisation route rather than treating them as informally self-effecting. 38

### Ticino

Ticino's official services describe **"modifying** **a** **foreigner's** **permit"** as a recognised administrative process and state that the migration office **issues, renews** **and** **modifies** permits concerning foreign nationals present in the canton. Ticino's B-permit detail page also routes third-country cases to employer-facing guidance. As with Vaud, these pages are better used as practice notes than as primary legal authority. 39

## G. Practical legal analysis

For **employer** **changes,** the legal answer depends primarily on whether the person holds L, **plain** B, or **conditioned** B. On the authorities reviewed, a third-country L holder should be treated as needing fresh employer sponsorship and prior cantonal approval, with the ordinary federal criteria remaining restrictive and Geneva expressly requiring the new employer to file before the start date. A third-country B holder should ordinarily be treated as having post-admissionlabour-market mobility,but only so long as the permit was not expressly linked to a particular post and the change does not alter the authorised purpose or the conditions on which the permit was granted. 40

**For promotions, demotions and internal duty changes,** the law is less explicit than for employer changes. The best official reading is functional: if the change is only cosmetic and does not alter the authorised purpose, the employer, the branch/profession, or the salary and contractual conditions that were originally assessed, the federal materials do not identify a universal mandatory refiling duty. By contrast, if the change moves the person into a materially different function, changes the approved salary basis, converts the post into a different category, or breaches an express condition, the official logic points toward a fresh cantonal review. That conclusion is an inference from the federal purpose-and-condition rules, because the reviewed official sources do not provide a detailed closed list of "role changes requiring notification". 18

**For salary reductions, reduced hours, part-time conversions and variable-pay changes,** the same structured caution applies. Article 22 FNIA and SEM's implementing guidance show that salary and working conditions are not incidental; they are part of the admission decision and are checked against a written contract. It follows that a material reduction that would take the arrangement below the locally and sectorally customary level, or that significantly changes the employment configuration reviewed by the authorities, creates immigration risk. However, the reviewed official materials do not state that every ordinary bonus change or every modest hours variation triggers a fresh filing. The careful ddrafting

<!-- 9 -->

position is therefore to say that **material contract changes can require reassessment,** especially where the permit was conditioned or where Article 22 compliance is put in doubt. 41

**For termination, unemployment and jobs-gap continuity,** the strongest official authority found is the SEM residence directive stating that the residence permit may be extended while unemployment-insurance entitlement continues or where the person is in an unemployment programme. That is helpful, but it should not be overstated into a free-standing statutory right of residence comparable to the EU/EFTA regime in Article 61a FNIA. The very existence of Article 61a as a special EU/EFTA residence-retention rule is a drafting warning against importing EUJ-style unemployment continuity language into third-country content. The safest formulation is that a third-country B holder may in some cases maintain or extend residence during insured unemployment, subject to the ordinary permit framework and cantonal handling, but no general EU-style codified right for ordinary non-EU local hires was identified. 5

**Garden** **leave** is not specifically addressed in the reviewed official sources. The most defensible authority-based approach is to tie the analysis to the continuing existenceof the employment relationship, continued salary/payment under the approved employment terms, and whether the authorised stay purpose remains intact through the relevant period. Because the federal materials do not speak directly to garden leave, content should avoid categorical statements that garden leave is always immigration-neutral. That would go beyond the sources. 18

**For renewals,** **it** is essential to distinguish **extension** from **renewal in the SEM sense. A** B permit is time-limited and extendable; an L permit can be extended up to two years in total in the ordinary course. By contrast, a true L "Erneuerung" under the SEM directives means a fresh, separate stay after interruption, generally requiring a one-year break and counted against a new quota. This is why drafting should never use "renewal" looselyfor all post-expiry or follow-on L scenarios. In internal content, it is better to speak of **extension of the same permit** versus **fresh re-admission/new permit** **after interruption.** 42

**On** **quotas,** the best authority-grounded rule is that **first-time** L and B permits for third-country workers are subject to annual quotas, and that an L re-grant after interruption may consume a new quota. The Zurich and SEM materials reviewed do not say that every ordinary continuing extension of an existing B consumes quota. Drafting should therefore say that quotas remainrelevant **for** **initial** **admission and for any scenario legally treated as a fresh permit,** not that quotas automaticallyre-open at every routine continuation stage. 43

**For residence continuity and document validity,** the main point is that the person's lawful status depends on the underlying permit decision and the statute, not on possession of an unexpired plastic card alone. The card confirms the residence title; the permit can expire by statute on departure,expiry, or prolonged absence abroad. If a renewal application is timely filed,federal law preserves lawful stay during the pending procedure unless the authorities decide otherwise. That is why content should distinguish between "the card has expired", "the permit has expired", and "the person remains lawfully present while the renewal is pending". Those are not the same thing. 44

**For** **family** **members,** the safest route-specific statement is a derivative one. Family members of an L holder can receive L permits of the same duration, and their work authorisation requires the Article 26VZAE route. By contrast, spouses and children of a B holder have a right to work without an additional employment-authorisation procedure, but the SEM FAQ states expressly that the **duration** **of that** **work** **authorisation is limited to the validity of the principal B permit.** If the principal worker's permit is not extended, family continuity can become an immigration issue too. 45

<!-- 10 -->

**For** **long-term** **C** **planning,** local-hire content should explain that the path is usually ten years in total on L and/or B, with the **last** **five** **uninterrupted** **on** B, no revocation grounds, and adequate integration. That means a person who spends too long on an L permit may be accruing total lawful stay,but not the decisive five-year uninterrupted B period needed immediately before the C application. Unemployment, social-assistance dependence and other revocation issues can also interfere with that pathway. 46

## H. Drafting cautions

Use **"work** **authorisation"** and **"residence** **permit"** as separate concepts. The permit card evidences the permit; it is not the legal source of the status. 47

For third-country **B-permit** content, use language suuch as **"as** **a** **rule", "normally",** or **"unless** **the** **permit** wvas **expressly** **conditioned"** when discussing job changes. A blanket statement that a B holder can always change employer without immigration consequences is too broad. 48

For third-country **L-permit** content, do not use mobility language borrowed from EU/EFTA materials. Draft instead that job changes are exceptional, require important reasons, ordinarily must remain within the same branch and profession, and require cantonal approval. 14

Do not say that **renewal** is **automatic.** The safer language is that a permit may be extended if the statutory conditions remain met, there are no revocation grounds, and the competent canton processes the request positively. 21

Do noot imply that **every** **minor contract** **amendment** requires anew permit. The official materials do not go that far. Say instead that **material changes** to employer, authorised purpose, express conditions, salary basis, working hours or the job actually approved may require fresh cantonal review. 18

Do not import **EU/EFTA unemployment and residence-retention rules** into third-country drafting. Article 61a FNIA is an EU/EFTA rule, and official EU guidance on involuntary unemployment should not be transposed into non-EU route content. 49

Where discussing family, say that the immigration consequences are often **derivative** of the principal holder's category and continuity. In particular, B-family work rights last no longer than the sponsor's B validity. 50

## Open questions and limitations

The reviewed official materials are strong on **employer changes, conditioned B permits, L-permit** **limits, renewal timing, absence-based expiry, and family work rights.** They are materially thinner on **garden leave, purely internal promotions, title-only changes,** and **fine-grained rules for variable** **remuneration** **or part-time** **conversions** after approval. Any future content on those scenarios should therefore stay close to the purpose/condition/Article 22 framework and avoid categorical claims unless a canton or court has supplied more specific authority. 18
