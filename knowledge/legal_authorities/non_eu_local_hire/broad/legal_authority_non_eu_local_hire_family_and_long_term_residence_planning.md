# Swiss Legal Authority Pack for Non-EU Local Hire Family and Long-Term Residence Planning

## Executive legal summary

**A.Executive** **legal summary.** For a non-EU/EFTA national locally hired into Switzerland, the worker's own admission for employment and the family's later admission are legally related but distinct. The principal worker is admitted through the foreign-national employment regime, which requiresan employer-led application and is limited in practice to well-qualified third-country nationals; family members are not automatically approved as part of that employment decision and must satisfy a separate family-reunification framework. In Swiss law, the most important distinction is between a **residence** **permit** holder under Article 44 FNIA and a **short-stay** **permit** holder under Article 45 FNIA: family members of a B-permit holder **may** **be** **granted** residence if the statutory conditions are met, whereas family members of an L-permit holder **may** **be** **granted** short-stay status only, with a materially weaker long-term position. This is why family-relocation content for non-EU local hires must always ask first whether the principal is expected to hold L or B, and must never describe family admission as automatic. 1

For a principal on a B **permit,** the core federal conditions for family reunification are cohabitation, suitable accommodation, no social-assistance dependency, language capability in the local national language for the joining spouse or registration for language support, and no disqualifying receipt of federal supplementary benefits; unmarried children under 18 can qualify without a language requirement. The consequence of admission under Articles 42 to 44 FNIA is strong on labour-market access for the accompanying spouse and children: Article 46 FNIA gives them the right to work anywhere in Switzerland on a salaried or self-employed basis. By contrast, for family members of an L **permit** holder, federal law gives no equivalent automatic work right; the OASA requires a separate employer-led work authorisation, and Geneva's current official guidance states the same. 2

Long-term residence planning is structurally different from family-admission planning. A family member who has lawfully joined a non-EU local hire does not thereby obtain settlement security. For settlement, the general rule is Article 34 FNIA: settlement may be granted after **ten** **years** **in** **total** on short-stay or residence permits, provided the person has held a residence permit without interruption for the last five years and meets integration requirements. Earlier settlement is possibole after five uninterrupted years on a residence permit where the higher integration and language threshold is met. For ordinary naturalisation, the decisive planning point is stricter still: the applicant must hold a C **permit at the time of filing,** must usually show **ten years' residence in Swvitzerland,** and time spent **on** **a** **B** **or** **C** **permit** **counts,** while time spent on an L **permit** **does** **not.** Accordingly, an L-based family strategy may preserve family unity but may delay or disrupt both settlement and naturalisation planning. 3

Employment loss requires careful drafting. The federal statute contains a specific post-employment residence rule for **EU/EFTA** nationals in Article 61a FNIA, but not for third-country local hires. For non-EU local hires, the safer legal description is therefore that permit risk usually materialises through **permit** **expiry,** **non-renewal,** or **revocation,** rather than through an express third-country statutory grace period. That makes dependant family status vulnerable where the sponsor's status weakens, although the position is not one of immediate automatic cancellation on the date employment ends. This is a

conservative structural inference from the statute and official guidance, not a separate express federal rule. 4

## Structural overview of family and long-term planning

**B. Structural overview of family and long-term planning.** **A** Swiss non-EU local-hire case should be analysed in four layers. First comes **principal-worker** **admission**: the Swiss employer applies for the work/residence approval, and the expected permit type determines almost all downstream family consequences. Secondly comes **family** **reunification**: spouse/registered partner and unmarried children under 18 may qualify, but only if the family-reunification requirements are separately met and the application is lodged within the statutory deadline. Thirdly comes **continuity** **and** **renewal**: the principal's status must remain renewable, and the family remains structurally exposed to non-renewal or revocation of the sponsor's status. Fourthly comes **long-term** **residence** **planning**: C-permit timing, absences, and naturalisation counts require a separate residence-clock analysis for each family member, especially where any person has spent time on an L permit. 5

The legal system therefore draws a sharp line between **eligibility** **to join** **and** **security** **of stay** **once** **joined.** Eligibility to join is governed mainly by Articles 44 to 47 FNIA and implementing OASA provisions. Security of stay depends on renewal practice, revocation rules, the integration framework, and later qualification for C or citizenship. A well-drafted internal content system should therefore treat "Can the family come?" and "How secure is the family's long-term position?" as separate questions, even if they are often asked together by clients. 6

A further planning distinction is between **B-linked** and **L-linked** family residence. Official cantonal guidance in Zurich, Geneva and Bern aligns with the federal framework: B/C holders can seek family admission for spouse/registered partner and children under 18; L holders can also seek family admission, but the family's own status is short-stay and the spouse's work is not automatic. For internal drafting, this is the single most important "route architecture" point after nationality. 7

## Primary legal texts and official administrative materials

**D. Primary legal texts.** The core federal statute is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and** **Integration.** For this topic, the critical provisions are Article 34 on settlement permmits; Article 38 on work mobility for B-permit holders admitted for employment; Articles 43 to 47 on family reunification, spouse/child employment, and time limits; Articles 49 to 51 on exceptions to cohabitation, retained rights after family breakdown, and expiry of family-reunification rights; Article 58a on integration criteria; and Articles 61 to 63 on permit expiry and revocation. These are the primary legal anchors for any content concerning family accompaniment, renewals, and long-term planning for non-EU local-hire workers. 25

<!-- 6 -->

The principal implementing ordinance is the **OASA.** For family planning, the most relevant provisions are Article 26 on employment for family members of short-stay permit holders; Article 58 on the validity of residence permits and Article 59 on extension timing; Articles 60 to 62 on settlement-permit language/integration requirements and early settlement; Articles 73 to 76 on family-reunification deadlines, language levels and late-child reunification; and Article 77 on language evidence where residence is retained after family breakdown. These provisions matter especially because they convert the broad statutory tests into operational thresholds used by cantonal migration offices. 26

For naturalisation-adjacent planning, the decisive official materials are SEM's **ordinary** **naturalisation** page and the SEM **citizenship** **handbook** for naturalisation applications. Together they establish that ordinary naturalisation requires a **C** **permit,** ten years' residence in Switzerland, three years in the last five before filing, double counting between ages 8 and 18, and the non-counting of L-permit time; the handbook also explains how absence and "centre of interests" are assessed for continuity. 21

**E. Official guidance and administrative materials.** The most useful federal guidance for this topic is the SEM **FAQ** **on** **residence** **and** **integration** **criteria,** which sets out family-reunification deadlines, the practical housing rule of thumb, spouse language expectations, and the principle that social assistance does not automatically trigger migration-law consequences bout is assessed case by case by cantonal authorities. SEM's I. **Ausländerbereich** page confirms that the underlying migration directives were updated as from **1 January** **2026,** even where the more accessible public FAQ is older. 27

On the education side, the most authoritative public coordination sources are **EDK** and **ch.ch.** They show that public compulsory schooling is free of charge for all children and that the school-attendance obligation applies even to children without legal residency status; they also confirm that school systems provide language support and integration support for children with foreign-language backgrounds. This is relevant to family-accompaniment content because children's school integration is often a practical stabiliser of centre-of-life arguments and later residence continuity, even though schooling itself does not create permit entitlements. 28

## Cantonal practice notes

**F. Cantonal practice notes.** Cantonal material is best read as **administrative practice,** not as **a** substitute for federal law. Zurich's official family-reunification page closely tracks the federal structure and is particularly helpful in separating the three sponsor categories: B/C holders, L holders, and other status groups. For B/C sponsors it confirms the expected conditions of suitable housing, no social assistance, no annual supplementary benefits, spouse language proof or course registration, and family-reunification deadlines. For L sponsors it confirms that family reunification exists, but on the weaker short-stay basis. Zurich also expressly says that if a third-country national later naturalises as Swiss, the family-reunification deadline does not start again; that is a useful drafting warning against "reset" language. 29

Geneva's English-language guidance is valuable for operational drafting because it is unusually explicit **on work rights, education, documents, and entry procedure.** It states that non-EU/EFTA B/C-family members have the right to work or become self-employed throughout Switzerland, whereas eligible L-permit family members who want to work must obtain a work permit. It also states that children admitted through family reunification have access to general education, apprenticeships and vocational training on the same terms as Swiss children, and that visa-required family members must normally apply through the Swiss representation abroad and wait abroad for the decision. 30

<!-- 7 -->

Bern's official guidance is especially useful for **evidential practice.** It adopts the room-count formula "number of persons minus one equals number of rooms", states expressly that no social assistance or supplementary benefits may be involved, sets out the five-year and twelve-month statutory famnily deadlines, and makes the important point that the A1 language/course requirement does **not** apply where the sponsor only has a short-stay permit. Bern also requires extensive financial supporting documents such as a work contract, the last four payslips, debt-enforcement extract, and social-services confirmations. Those document requests are not federal-law conditions in themselves, but they are highly relevant for internal content on proof burdens. 31

Basel-Stadt and Ticino add useful procedural nuance. Basel-Stadt states that the family-reunification application does not authorise entry or stay by itself and that processing must generally be awaited abroad; it also describes suitable accommodation by reference to local Swiss standards. Ticinomakes the same abroad-filing point for third-country family members and provides a dedicated online initiation process. These cantonal sources support a conservative process statement: for visa-required non-EU family members, the family-reunification procedure is ordinarily an **out-of-country** process that must be separately completed. 32

## Practical legal analysis

G. **Practical legal analysis.** The correct substantive starting point is that Swiss family reunification for non-EU local hires is **status-dependent and** **permit-type** **dependent.** If the principal is admitted on aB permit, the spouse or registered partner and unmarried children under 18 may qualify under Article 44FNIA. If the principal is admitted on an L permit, Article 45 FNIA applies instead, and the family's own status is also short-stay. Because many local-hire cases begin with the principal's employment authorisation, content systems often over-assume that family approval "follows" the worker's admission. The law does not do that. It creates a separate, conditional family route that must still be applied for and evidenced. 33

The sponsor-side conditions must be stated precisely. Federal law requires **cohabitation, suitable** **accommodation, lack of social-assistance dependency,** spouse **language ability** in the local national language or at least **registration for language support,and** no disqualifying **federal supplementary** **benefits** in the sponsor household. Official guidance then adds practical evidential content: SEM and Bern both use the housing rule of thumb "number of family members minus one equals number of rooms", Geneva asks for lease evidence and proof of finances, and Bern asks for detailed salary, debt, and social-services records. Those evidential specifics are not the statute itself, but they are the safest practical description of what a cantoon will normally want to see. 34

The spouse's labour-market position depends sharply on whether the sponsor holds B/C or L. Under Article 46 FNIA, the spouse and children of Swiss, C-permit and B-permit sponsors may work anywhere in Switzerland, on a salaried or self-employed basis, without a separate labour-market clearance. This is a major stabilising feature of B-linked family migration. By contrast, OASA Article 26 provides only a **possible** work authorisation for family members of short-stay permit holders, and it depends on a separate employer application, compliance with wage and employment conditions, and fulfilment of personal admission conditions. Geneva's current guidance mirrors that structure. Internal drafting should therefore never use an undifferentiated phrase such as "accompanying spouses can work in Switzerland"; that is generally true for B-based reunification but not for L-based reunification. 35

<!-- 8 -->

Children's residence is simpler on admission but more nuanced for long-term planning. On admission, the federal route covers only **unmarried** **children** **under** 18. There is no federal spouse-language requirement for children, and official guidance says children admitted through family reunification can access schooling and training. Public compulsory education is free of charge and includes language and integration support. That matters legally because schools and family residence will often anchor the factual "centre of life" in Switzerland, but it should not be overstated: schooling supports integration and continuity; it does not create an autonomous right to residence. 36

The greatest practical vulnerability is the link between the principal's permit continuity and the family's continued status. For non-EU local hires, the statute does not contain a stand-alone third-country rule equivalent to the EU/EFTA post-employment regime in Article 61a FNIA. Instead, the legislation addresses expiry of permits, revocation grounds, and renewal conditions. The safest statement is therefore this: **termination of employment does not appear in the statute as an immediate** **automatic cancellation event for third-country B/L holders, but it can undermine the very basis** **on which renewal or continued residence is assessed.** Family members whose permits are anchored to the sponsor's B/L route are correspondingly exposed if the principal's permit is not renewed or is revoked. This is a conservative inference from the legislative structure and public SEM guidance, and it should be drafted as such. 24

Where family life later breaks down, Article 50 FNIA becomes relevant, but only in its own defined field. Continued residence after dissolution of the marriage or family household depends either on a family life that lasted at least three years with the integration criteria met, or on important personal reasons making continued residence necessary. OASA Article 77 adds the language threshold for the three-year/ integration route. This is important for internal systems because the existence of Article 50 can easily be misused in drafting as a general "fallback" after any status problem. It is not. It is a post-breakdown rule, not a general remedy for job loss or quota problems. 23

For settlement planning, the core distinction is between the sponsor's work-based B/L history and the dependant's own residence history. Under the **general** settlement rule in Article 34 FNIA, time on L and B can count towards the ten-year total, but the person must have held a residence permit without interruption for the last five years. This creates a familiar planning risk: time spent on an L permit may help for the overall total, but it cannot satisfy the last-five-years-on-B requirement. Early C after five uninterrupted years on B is possible, but only with higher language and integration performance, and OASA says that, for early C, the integration of family members over 12 is taken into account as well. If and when the principal later secures C, the family's legal position becomes materially stronger, including the special five-year spouse route under Article 43(5) and the directC entitlement for children under 12.37

For naturalisation-adjacent planning, the decisive caution is that **L** **time** **does not** **count** for ordinary naturalisation and a **C** **permit** is **mandatory** at **filing.** SEM's public guidance also states that B and C residence counts, that the residence total is ten years, and that years between 8 and 18 count double, subject to a minimum actual residence period. The SEM citizenship handbook adds the continuity analysis: declared departure or actual residence abroad for more than six months ordinarily breaks the naturalisation residence period, but up to one year abroad for work or education can still be treated as a short absence if the centre of interests remains in Switzerland and return is intended. For mobile international families, this means that frequent or extended overseas postings may be manageable for permit purposes in some cases, yet still damage later naturalisation planning. 21

A final point on spouse independence. Official sources clearly support the spouse's right to work in B-linked cases. They do not, however, provide a simple, general federal mechanism saying that a family-based spouse can automatically "convert" to an independent work-based B permit whenever desired. In practice, an independent-work route would normally require satisfaction of the ordinary employment-admission framework, which is employer-led for third-country nationals. For internal content, that means the better formulation is that spouse employment may build **factual** **independence** **and later** **legal options,** but a clean legal "conversion" route is **not** automatic on the materials reviewed. 38

## Drafting cautions and source list

**H. Drafting cautions.** Internal content should avoid the following formulations. Do not say that family members "automatically accompany" a non-EU local hire; describe family admission as a **separate** **family-reunification** **application.** Do **not** collapse B and L treatment; for L-based reunification, spouse work is not automatic and long-term residence is much less secure. Do **not** treat the cantonal housing room formula as if it were the text of federal law; label it as administrative guidance. Do **not** imply that loss of employment automatically cancels a third-country permit on the same day, but also do **not** imply any guaranteed statutory grace period after job loss. Do **not** imply that family reunification creates a direct path to C or nationality. Do not say that all lawful time in Switzerland counts for naturalisation; official SEM guidance states that **L-permit** **time** **does** **not** **count.** Do **not** present schooling, apprenticeship access or spouse employment as independent residence rights; they are important for integration and stability, but they do not replace the permit basis. 39

**Open** **questions** **and** **limitations.** The official federal materials reviewed are comparatively thin on one point that matters in practice: there is no clearly expressed general federal rule on how a third-country local-hire permit and linked family permits should be treated immediately after employment termination, beyond the broader expiry, non-renewal and revocation framework. The conclusions above are therefore conservative and structural. The materials reviewed are also thin on the specific administrative mechanics for moving a spouse from a family-based status to an independently employer-sponsored work-based status, and on whether time accumulated before the principal obtains C is always counted in the same way fora spouse later relying on Article 43(5). Those issues should be treated as canton-sensitive unless a route-specific authority is identified for the exact fact pattern. 40
