# Swiss legal authority pack on labour market testing in non-EU local hire work permits

## Executive legal summary

For a non-EU or non-EFTA national who is hired directly into a Swiss role by a Swiss employer, the labour-market-test question sits inside a broader statutory admission framework. At federal level, salaried admission requires: an employer-filed application; overall economic interest; compliance with quota rules where applicable; satisfaction of the resident-worker / free-movement priority rule; compliance with Swiss local salary and working conditions; and a suitably qualified worker. In statutory terms, Article 18 FNIA ties salaried admission to the requirements of Articles 20 to 25; Article 21contains the priority rule; Article 22 contains the salary and working-conditions rule; and Article 23contains the personal-qualification rule.1

The priority rule is not framed as a light-touch formality. Article 21 FNIA requires proof that no suitable domestic employee, and no suitable national of a state benefiting from a free-movement agreement with Switzerland, can be found for the post. "Domestic employees" are defined in the statute and include Swiss nationals, settlement-permit holders, residence-permit holders authorised to work, temporarily admitted persons, and persons granted temporary protection with work authorisation. Swiss university graduates are an express statutory derogation: they may be admitted despite Article 21(1)where the work is of high academic or economic interest. 2

In practice, the employer bears the evidential burden. The current SEM directives explain that, outside shortage occupations, the employer must be able to make it credibole that it searched in **good** **time** and **appropriately** for Swiss and EU/EFTA candidates; that third-country candidates should only be approached once those efforts have failed; that the exercise must not be undertaken merely to tick a box; and that priority candidates must not be screened out by irrelevant criteria, such as foreign stays or language / technical requirements that are not genuinely indispensable for the role. The same directives also state that employers are expected to take the necessary steps to find an available worker, including advertisements in daily papers and specialist press, electronic media, and private placement agencies, and they cite Federal Administrative Court case law in support of the expectation that employers consider job-specific continuing training for available workers. 3

There is, however, an important legal-evidential distinction. Federal law states the priority requirement as a substantive admission condition. The **shape** **and intensity** **of evidence** are then developed by SEM guidance and, in day-to-day handling, by cantonal labour-market authorities. The SEM directives allow a more flexible evidential approach where the role falls within a profession suffering clear structural skilled-labour shortage, but that is not a blanket statutory exemption. The directives say cantonal authorities may dispense with detailed proof of search where the occupation is manifestly shortage-affected,yet they may still ask for ad hoc proof in difficult cases, including ORP / public employment service advertising or wider EU advertising. 4

A further distinction is essential for drafting andanalysis. Article 21 FNIA priority is **not** **the** **same thing** as Article 21a FNIA vacancy-notification rules. Article 21a creates a separate public-employment-agency notification mechanism for professions, fields or regions with above-average unemployment, including shortlisting / interview obligations for suitable registered jobseekers and periodic lists drawn up by the Federal Council. SEM and cantonal materials make clear that a third-country hire can therefore involve both: the substantive Article 21 priority assessment and, where triggered, the separate Article 21a vacancy-notification regime. They should never be collapsed into one concept. 5

The most useful practical proposition for future drafting is therefore this: in Swiss third-country local-hire cases, labour market testing should be described as a **source-backed, evidence-heavy** **burden** **on** **the** **Swiss employer,** assessed under federal law but often decided through canton-specific evidential expectations. Weak recruitment evidence does not merely create a minor documentary issue; it can undermine the core admission condition that priority was given to the Swiss and EU/EFTA labour market. 6

## Structural overview of labour market testing in non-EU local hire cases

The federaI structure is straightforward in outline. For a local hire by a Swiss employer, the usual statutory route is Article 18 FNIA on salaried employment. Article 18 requires that the employer file the application and that the requirements of Articles 20 to 25 be met. Those linked provisions include quotas, priority, salary / employment connditions, personal qualifications, and accommodation. In other words, labour market testing is one component of a cumulative admission test, not a free-standing inquiry. 7

The persons who must be considered before a third-country national can be admitted are broader than "Swiss nationals". Article 21(1) requires that no suitable domestic workers or nationals of states with which Switzerland has concluded a free-movement agreement can be found. Article 21(2) then defines "domestic employees" by immigration status. SEMM's public guidance to employers states the same point in plainer terms: the future employer must show that the necessary staff cannot be recruited from the Swiss labour market potential or from the labour markets of EU / EFTA states. 8

The official materials reviewed do **not** provide auniversal federal checklist saying, for example, "advertise for X days on Y platform". Instead, federal guidance uses standards of timneliness, appropriateness, seriousness and relevance. That is why cantonal public materials become important in practice. Zurich publishes a concrete document list; Geneva pubolishes explicit proof expectations and required attachments; Lucerne sets out the high-level requirements without the same level of documentary granularity; and publicly accessible Ticino material is comparatively procedural and refers users back to SEM materials rather than publishing a detailed public checklist. 9

Shortage occupations qualify the evidential picture rather than displacing the legal rule. The current SEM directives say that, in professional fields affected by strong structural shortage of skilled labour, cantonal authorities may apply the proof requirement more flexibly and may even refrain from demanding proof of completed searches, provided the employer plausibly shows that the request concerns a shortage occupation. Zurich's official guidance states the same point at cantonal level and adds that cyclical fluctuations do not by themselves establish a shortage occupation. 10

### Primary legal texts and official guidance

### Primary legal texts

The key federal statute is the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** **Act.** For this topic, the central provisions are Articles 18, 21, 21a, 22 and 23. Article 18 puts third-country salaried employment inside the overall economic-interest and employer-application framework; Article 21 provides the priority rule; Article 21a creates the separate vacancy-notification regime; Article 22 governs local salary and working conditions; and Article 23 defines the required worker profile. Articles 24 and 25 then continue the linked admission conditions. 22

The implementing ordinance is the **Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment.** In the public materials reviewed, the ordinance is consistently presented by federal and cantonal authorities as the main implementing instrument for admissions to employment. However, for the specific labour-market-test issue, the publicoperational detail is found much more clearly in SEM directives and canton-level materials than in a publicly accessible federal ordinance checklist. That isan important drafting point: one should not overstate the ordinance as if it contained a complete evidential code for Article 21 searches. 23

The statute itself already shows why labour-market testing must be separated from related questions. Article 21 asks whether suitable priority workers are available. Article 22 asks whether salary and conditions are locally customary. Article 23 asks whether the third-country candidate is a manager, specialist or otherwise qualified worker, with additional integration-oriented factors for residence permits and specified derogations for certain categories. Each question is legally distinct even though the same facts may affect more than one limb. 24

### OfficiaI guidance and administrative materials

For current administrative practice, the most important federal sourceis the SEM's **Directives and** **Comments, Chapter 4, Stay with gainful employment,** in the version showing status **1 January 2026.** That chapter expressly addresses proof of compliance with the priority rule, distinguishes shortage occupations from other professions, states that recruitment must be timely and appropriate, warns against purely formal searches, and says that irrelevant language or technical criteria should not be used to eliminate priority candidates. It also notes that cantonal labour-market authorities may request ad hoc special proof in difficult cases. 25

<!-- 6 -->

The same SEM directives also explain how evidential flexibility works in shortage occupations. They say that in professions manifestly affected by strong structural skilled-labour shortage, cantonal authorities may refrain from requiring proof of search and may accept a plausible demonstration that the request concerns a shortage occupation. The list is periodically revised by SEM in collaboration with SECO and currently includes numerous management, engineering, ICT, health and higher-education occupations. 26

SEM's public "basis for admission" pages provide the concise federal summary that employers must show necessary personnel cannot be recruited either from the Swiss labour-market potential or from EU / EFTA labour markets. Those same pages place priority, salary compliance and qualification requirements side by side, which is useful for drafting because it prevents the common mnistake of treating labour-market testing as the only meaningful hurdle in third-country admissions. 27

Article 21a vacancy notification is best understood through both the statute and the related SEM / SECO materials. Article 21a FNIA creates the public-employment-agency mechanism. SEM's circular of 28 June 2018 addresses the immigration-law consequences of vacancy notification, and SECO's vacancy-notification materials explain that the regime is aimed at giving registered jobseekers in Switzerland a head start and that the annual scope is determined by unemployment thresholds and lists. The current SEM directives likewise note that, in 2026, the unemployment threshold is 5% and cross-refer to the Employment Service Ordinance. 28

Finally, SECO's 2023 indicator-system publication matters because the SEM directives expressly rely on SECO indicators and empirical admission practice to identify the occupational fields in which proof requirements may be applied more flexibly. That pubolication therefore does not replace the legal rule, but it does help explain why certain professional groups receive a lighter evidential treatment in practice. 29

### Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** publishes the clearest public evidential checklist found in the materials reviewed. Its January 2024 guidance for third-country employment states that, for local hires, employers should provide: proof of vacancy notification where Article 21a applies; RAV and EURES advertising; dated print or online advertisements; and a candidate list containing nationality, education / qualifications, application date and rejection reason. Zurich also states that, in occupations with pronounced skilled-labour shortage, ads and candidate lists may be dispensed with, but the applicant must explain why the occupation is a shortage occupation and temporary cyclical fluctuations do not suffice. 30

**Geneva** also publishes unusually detailed public expectations. Its current official page states that a Swiss employer in Geneva may apply for a third-country work authorisation only after first exploring the Swiss and European labour markets without success. Geneva says the employer must prove: prompt notification of the vacancy to the cantonal employment office; timely and thorough searches in Switzerland and EU / EFTA states, including websites and placement agencies; and that no available worker on the Swiss labour market can be trained in reasonable time for the role. For documentary attachments, Geneva requires proof of searches and **detailed** **results** of those searches for both longer contracts and shorter permits. 18

**Lucerne** publishes a shorter, high-level information sheet. It confirms the need to show that the vacancy cannot be filledd by suitable domestic or EU / EFTA workers, and places that requirement alongside overall economic interest, quotas, salary compliance and qualifications. It is less prescriptive than Zurich or Geneva on the exact documentary form of recruitment evidence, which itself is a useful reminder that canton-level published granularity varies. 31

<!-- 7 -->

**Ticino,** at least in the public Italian-language material surfaced in this research, is comparatively procedural. The openly accessible pages reliably confirm that the employer must file the application while the worker remains abroad, and they direct users to SEM guidance on third-country admissions, but they do not publish a Zurich-style or Geneva-style recruitment-evidence checklist on the pages reviewed. For internal drafting, that means Ticino should be treated as a canton where public-facing practice notes are thinner and direct authority contact or internal precedent may matter more. 32

The cross-cantonal lesson is not that federal law changes by canton. It is that **federal admission** **conditions are constant, while the published evidential expectations can be much more or much** **less** **explicit depending** **on** **the** **canton.** Future drafting should always distinguish the federal legal rule from canton-specific public documentary practice. 33

### Practical legal analysis

For internal content work, the safest analytical model is to treat Swiss third-country labour-market testing as having three layers. The first layer is the **legal rule**: Article 21 FNIA priority. Thesecond is the **federal administrative** **interpretation**: SEM directives on timeliness, appropriateness, shortage occupations, irrelevant criteria and the need for genuine efforts. The third is **canton-specific** **proof** **practice**: what Zurich, Geneva or another canton actually expects to see in a file. A good application narrative should satisfy all three layers at once. 34

In practical terms, the employer should be able to reconstruct the recruitment story chronologically.A defensible file usually needs dated advertisements; a clear explanation of where the post was advertised and why those channels were chosen; evidence that Swiss and EU / EFTA priority pools were genuinely tested; a candidate log showing the applications received; interview steps taken; and concrete rejection reasons tied to genuine role requirements. The Zurich and Geneva materials show how authorities think about this: they ask for dated ads, candidate lists, detailed results, and, in Geneva, an explanation why an available local worker cannot be trained within reasonable time. 35

The official materials strongly suggest that **search methodology matters as much as search volume.** SEM refers to daily papers, specialist press, electronic media and private agencies. Zurich specifically mentions RAV and EURES. Geneva expressly refers to Swiss and EU / EFTA internet platforms and agencies. It is therefore reasonable to infer that professional networks, targeted outreach and specialist recruiters can legitimately supplement the search, especially for niche roles, but they should not stand alone unless the employer can explain whny the role genuinely required that approach and why outward-facing Swiss / EU-EFTA market testing would have been ineffective. That final point is an inference from the structure of the official materials rather than an express statutory rule. 36

Shortage occupations need careful treatment. The SEM directives and Zurich guidance support a **relaxation** **of** **proof,** not a full legal exemption. A shortage-role narrative should therefore not stop at "this is an ICT/ engineering/ health role". It should identify the shortage logic, link the role to the relevant occupation field, and still present whatever recruitment evidence exists. The more the employer relies on shortage status, the more important it becomes not to undermine that position through obvious evidential weaknesses such as undated adverts, implausibly narrow job criteria, or a very poor salary offer. The SEM directives' definition of shortage itself is conditioned by the terms offered for the post. 37

<!-- 8 -->

Candidate suitability must also be handled conservatively. If Swiss or EU / EFTA applicants were rejected, the reasons should map to essential job requirements rather than preferences that appear tailored to the chosen third-country worker. SEM expressly warns against excluding priority candidates through non-essential foreign experience, language or technical requirements. The Federal Administrative Court case law cited in SEM guidance, and the BVGE 2011-1 summary, reinforce that recruitment efforts and selection criteria are judicially reviewable matters rather than purely commercial choices beyond scrutiny. 38

The interaction with **Article** 22 **salary** **and** **working** **conditions** is also practically significant. Legally, Article 22 is separate. But evidentially, an unattractive or below-market package can weaken the employer's claim that no suitable Swiss or EU / EFTA candidate was available, because the SEM shortage framework itself assumes analysis of supply and demand under the conditions actually offered. Similarly, a persuasive **business** **need** narrative under Article 18 will not cure poor priority evidence: overall economic interest and resident-worker priority remain separate limbs. 39

The main weak points in recruitment narratives are therefore predictable. They include searches that begin only after the preferred third-country candidate has already been identified; evidence created late and apparently for litigation rather than real recruitment; advertisements that are too narrow, too short-lived or too poorly targeted; rejection reasons that are formulaic or unsupported; failure to document interviews or candidate numbers; reliance on a single channel when a broader market test was realistic; failure to explain why a local candidate could not be trained; and job profiles that seem written around the non-EU national's biography. Each of those weaknesses cuts against the SEM directives' requirement of timely, appropriate and genuine search efforts. 40

### Drafting cautions

Future internal or external drafting on Swiss sponsorship of non-EU workers should not say, or imply, that sponsorship is routine once the candidate is senior or technically skilled. Official Swiss materials state the opposite structure: each case is assessed individually, admission remains restricted, and multiple cumulative conditions must be met. That point is especially important because public English-language summaries can easily slide into over-simplified "if highly skilled, then eligible" phrasing. 41

It is also unsafe to state that a Swiss employer merely needs to "advertise the role locally" or "prove no Swiss candidate was available". The protected pool is wider than Swiss nationals; it includes defined resident workers and the separate pool of EU / EFTA nationals. Equally, posting the vacancy only with the pubolic employment service should not be described as universally sufficient. Official federal guidance points to a broader search effort, while cantons such as Zurich and Geneva require broader evidence in publicly available materials. 42

Do not present Article 21a vacancy notification as if it were the entire labour-market test. It is a distinct regime with its own threshold logic, lists, and candidate-handling duties. In third-country local-hire cases, a role may fall outside Article 21a yet still fail Article 21 because the employer's recruitment evidence is weak; conversely, a role may satisfy Article 21a formalities yet still require a convincing broader narrative under Article 21. 43

Avoid rules of thumb that are not anchored in official sources. In the materials reviewed, there is no universal officially published federal minimum number of advertising days for Article 21 searches, no generalI official exemption for urgent or confidential roles, and no official rule that seniority alone removes the need for labour-market testing. Where a role is said to be niche, confidential or urgent,the safer drafting line is that those features may affect **how** the search is carried out and evidenced, but not whether the employer needs a credible priority-compliance narrative. 21

When writing about shortage occupations, use restrained language. The sound formulation is that shortage can justify **a** **more** **flexible** **evidential approach,** depending on the profession and canton, not that shortage occupations are automatically exempt from the labour market test. If discussing public Italian-language or other canton-specific practice, it is prudent to say explicitly where the public official material is thin. 44

### Open questions and limitations

The official materials reviewed do not provide a single public federal checklist fixing a minimum advertising period, a mandatory number of interviews, or an exhaustive list of acceptable recruitment channels for Article 21 FNIA. Public federal guidance instead uses standards such as timeliness, appropriateness and genuineness, leaving significant practical texture to cantonal handling. 45

Public cantonal transparency varies. Zurich and Geneva publish comparatively concrete evidential expectations; Lucerne publishes a briefer overview; and the Ticino material surfaced here is more procedural and referential. That means canton-specific public practice should be treated as illustrative, not exhaustive, unless supplemented by direct authority material or internal precedent. 46

Direct full-text retrieval from Fedlex was inconsistent in the web environment used for this research. Statutory wording for the FNIA provisions was therefore taken from a Fedlex-file-named mirror that surfaced in search, and cross-checked against SEM's current official guidance and official canton pages. Because the operative propositions in this pack are also confirmed by SEM and cantonal materials, the core conclusions above remain high confidence; but for formal citation in later drafting, direct Fedlex wording should still be preferred wherever available. 47
