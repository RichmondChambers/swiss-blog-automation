# Swiss legal authority pack on candidate suitability and qualifications in non-EU local-hire work permit cases

Suggested filename: legalauthoritynoneulocalhirecandidatesuitabilityandqualificationspack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

Swiss non-EUJ local-hire work permit cases are legally restrictive and structurally cumulative. The candidate's qualifications and suitability matter, but they are only one part of a broader admission matrix that also includes overall economic interest, an employer-filed application, quota availability, resident-worker priority, vacancy-notification compliance where applicable, salary and local employment conditions, and suitable accommodation. A strong CV does not cure failure on labour-market testing, pay compliance or the general economic-interest requirement. 1

The core federal statutory concept is not "the employer's preferred candidate", but a **manager,** **specialist** or **other** **qualified** **worker** within the meaning of Art. 23 FNIA/AIG/LEI/LStrI. For residence permits, the statute also requires that the person's professional qualifications, professional and social adaptability, language skills and age indicate likely lasting integration into the Swiss labour market and social environment. SEM's current guidance operationalises this by looking at degrees, higher-professional diplomas, specialised training with several years of experience, additional training, or exceptional indispensable specialist knowledge. 2

Formal qualifications are highly important, but they are not always the only route. SEM guidance accepts that, in professions with a proven structural skilled-labour shortage, permits may also be granted for some non-academic roles if the candidate can prove at least a vocational qualification or, usually,at least five years of documented relevant experience. The same guidance is explicit that unqualified workers and helpers are not admitted under Art. 23. This is a controlled exception, not a relaxation of the general rule. 3

Candidate suitability is assessed **against** **the concrete** **Swiss** **role,** not in the abstract. The authorities expect a close match between the candidate's training and experience and the proposed duties. The employer must also show genuine prior effortsto recruit in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area, and SEM guidance warns that priority candidates must not be excluded through criteria that are not genuinely required for the job, such as unnecessary language requirements, foreign stays or weakly related niche skills. That is the principal legal reason over-tailored job descriptions are risky. 4

The evidence burden is substantial. Current SEM and cantonal materials call for a reasoned application explaining the business, project and foreign candidate; a written binding contract; CV; diplomas and employment certificates; translations where needed; and proof of Swiss/EU/EFTA recruitment efforts. In regulated professions, recognition and practice-licence evidence may be indispensable; immigration approval and professional authorisation are separate requirements. 5

A narrow but important exception concerns prior Swiss study. Swiss university graduates may benefit from Art. 21(3) FNIA, and SEM now also treats certain Swiss tertiary higher-vocational graduates as "qualified" under Art. 23 where the job is closely related to the training; but even then the activity must still be of high scientific or economic interest, and other admission conditions continue to apply. SEM's 2018 circular further confirms that vacancy-notification duties can still apply even where proof of priority is waived. 6

## Structural overview of candidate suitability and qualifications

For this route, "local hire" means employment **with** **a** **Swiss** **employer** or a **Swiss establishment,** rather than a temporary posting from abroad. SEM guidance distinguishes employment with a Swiss employer from service provision or secondment by a foreign employer, and the local-hire file is normally submitted by the Swiss employer to the cantonal labour-market authority. 7

Within that local-hire framework, the candidate-suitability limb sits inside a sequence of linked questions. The authorities first ask whether the admission serves Switzerland's overall economic interest; then whether quotas are available; then whether Swiss and EU/EFTA priority workers were genuinely given precedence; then whether any vacancy-notification obligation has been met;then whether the salary and conditions are locally and sectorally compliant; and only then, alongside those points, whether the person qualifies under Art. 23 and is a credible fit for the role. The legal and practical effect is that candidate strength is necessary but not self-sufficient. 8

The federal legislation does **not** provide an exhaustive general definition of "manager", "specialist" and "other qualified worker". Instead, the statute names those categories, and SEM guidance gives them content by reference to qualification level and role necessity. In the core federal materials reviewed, "senior employee" is not treated as a separate general admission class; it is safer in drafting to use the statutory categories and then explain the level of responsibility, specialist expertise or managerial function on the facts. 9

The suitability enquiry is therefore both **legal** and **evidential.** Legally, the person must fall within an admissible category and, for longer stays, show likely integration prospects. Evidentially, the application must persuade the authorities that the claimed level of qualification is real, documented, role-specific and commercially necessary. The same idea explains why Swiss practice attaches such importance to the CV, diplomas, employment certificates, job description, recruitment history and contract terms. 10

### Cantonal practice notes

Swiss work-permit practice is federal in legal basis but strongly cantonal in administration. The cantonal materials reviewed are therefore best treated as **practice** **notes,** not as independent law.

**Zurich.** Zurich's official materials state the cumulative federal conditions in straightforward form: overall economic interest, qualified-worker status, resident-worker priority and compliance with local salary and working conditions. Zurich's practice note for third-country workers also says there is no legal entitlement to a permit or renewal, requires the qualifications to correspond to the new activity, and lists the core documents expected in the file. It also accepts that, in proven shortage occupations, some recruitment evidence may be dispensed with, but places the burden of showing a real shortage on the applicant. 45

**Geneva.** Geneva's official page is useful because it is comparatively explicit about labour-market testing. It says the cantonal labour authority checks whether the employer has notified the vacancy to the cantonal employment office as early as possible, whether substantial searches were made in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area, and whether the employer could reasonably train or have trained an available worker instead. Geneva also states that usual salary and working conditions must be respected and, as a cantonal practice point, says the role should be full-time. 46

**Ticino.** Ticino's official materials confirm the standard route mechanics: the employer applies while the foreign national waits abroad for entry authorisation; the canton also flags regulated professions and directs applicants to the SBFI recognition framework. That is useful for internal drafting because it shows how cantons integrate federal qualification rules wvith profession-specific recognition systems. 47

## Practical legal analysis

### Candidate suitability is an admissibility limb, not a standalone gateway.

For non-EU local hire, the candidate's profile is assessed under Art. 23, but always in its relationship to the rest of the admission structure. In practice, the application must tell one coherent story: the business need is real; the role is economically relevant; no priority worker could be hired; the salary is credible forthe role; and the candidate is objectively qualified for that role. A candidate can fail if any one of those parts is weak, even where the person is commercially attractive to the employer. 48

### What "qualified" means in practice.

The safest internal formulation is that Swiss federal law targets managers, specialists and other qualified workers, and SEM interprets those categories through education level, specialist training and role-specific experience rather than through a rigid universal definition. In ordinary cases, the strongest profiles remain degree-holders with several years of relevant experience. Where there is no degree, the file needs a clear alternative basis in formal vocational training, specialist certifications, substantial

post-training experience, or demonstrably indispensable know-how. Generic "strong experience" language is too vague for this route. 49

### Experience can substitute only in controlled ways.

SEM's shortage-occupation guidance is important because it prevents overstatement in both directions. It shows that formal university education is noot always mandatory, but it also makes clear that experience-only cases must still be **documented, substantial and** **role-specific.** The usual benchmark in the guidance is at least a vocational qualification or, typically, five years of relevant experience. That is not a licence to present a generally experienced worker as "specialist" without proving an actual shortage occupation and a real fit with the offered duties. 3

#### Role fit is central, and over-tailoring is a real refusal risk.

Swiss practice distinguishes sharply between a candidate who happens to be the employer's preferred choice and a candidate whose profile is objectively justified by the role. That is why SEM insists on genuine recruitment efforts and warns against selection criteria that exclude priority workers for reasons not genuinely required by the job. The risk is highest where the role is described with unusual combinations of language, overseas-market, client-network or niche-system experience that appear designed around a preselected third-country national rather than the real operational needs of the business. Internal drafting should therefore justify every unusual requirement by reference to the actual duties, not by reference to the candidate's biography. 50

#### Technical, sector-specific and language experience are relevant only if the role genuinely requires them.

SEM accepts specialist knowledge and sector-specific experience as valid qualification markers, and in highly specialised international contexts it accepts that good English may be sufficient for long-term integration. But the same guidance says that in sectors with stronger day-to-day contact with the local environment, including healthcare and gastronomy, local-language knowledge may be decisive. The legal lesson is that language, client and market experience are not automatically probative one way or the other: they are persuasive only insofar as they are tied to the operational reality of the Swiss role. 51

#### The evidence package must be documentary, not narrative.

Current SEMand Zurich materials show that persuasive files are built from documents, not assertions. At minimum, the employer should expect to provide a passport copy, cantonal form, application justification, a binding written contract, CV, diplomas, employment certificates and proof of recruitment efforts; translations are required where the documents are not in a Swiss official language or English. Sector-specific routes can require more, including business plans, project descriptions, realisation plans, training programmes, regulatory approvals or professional-recognition documents. Internal drafting should therefore separate what is **legally** **required** from what is **needed** **to** **persuade**: both matter, but they are not the same thing. 32

#### The labour-market test and vacancy notification directly shape how the candidate profile is judged.

A third-country candidate is not assessed in isolation from the recruitment history. SEM's public pages and directives require credible, timely and appropriately broad searches in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area. Geneva adds that employers should also show why a locally available worker could not be trained in reasonable time. For occupations subject to Art. 21a vacancy notification, compliance is a separate admission requirement and SEM's circular says it can remain apoplicable even in categories otherwise exempt from ordinary priority proof. In practical terms, a candidate can look "qualified" yet still be unsuitable in immigration-law terms if the recruitment file suggests the employer started with the third-country national and worked backwards. 52

<!-- 9 -->

**Salary and business need must reinforce, not contradict, the claimed specialist profile.** Art. 22 and OASA art. 22 require review against legal rules, collective agreements, same-employer comparators and statistical wage sources. SEM also requires a written contract with the essential terms. As an analytical matter, that means the job level, the business case and the salary package must be mutually consistent. If the employer describes the role as highly specialised, managerial or commercially critical, but offers a package that looks ordinary for a junior or administrative role,the file becomes less credible. The same is true if the business-need explanation is thin, generic or disconnected from the candidate's actual qualifications. That conclusion is an inference from the way the authorities are directed to test salary, contract terms and overall economic interest together. 53

#### Regulated professions require special caution.

In regulated occupoations, immigration suitability and professional licensure are separate hurdles. SBFI explains that regulated professions may only be practised when the required regulated qualifications are held, and OASA art. 7 saysprofessional authorisations do not replace immigration approval. SEM's sectoral guidance shows the practical consequences in healthcare: doctors outside the EU/EFTA face MedReg and recognition constraints; for nurses and other regulated health professions, a positive pre-check may support a short permit in some circumstances, but a residence permit generally requires actual recognition or the competent authority's confirmation. Internal drafting should therefore never treat a regulated-profession candidate as "qualified" without first checking the recognition and practice-authorisation path. 54

#### Prior Swiss study, internship, assignment or residence history has limited but sometimes important relevance.

The clearest federal shortcut tied to prior Swiss history is the Swiss university-graduate rule in Art. 21(3), as explained by SEM, together with SEM's newer treatment of recognised Swiss tertiary higher-vocational graduates under Art. 23. Both are narrow: the work must still be of high scientific or economic interest, and the role must be closely related to the training. Outside those specific channels, the reviewed federal materials do not identify prior Swiss residence or earlier Swiss employment as a standalone substitute for qualification evidence; at most, such history may help on adaptability, integration or credibility. That final point is an inference from the current materials reviewed and should be drafted cautiously. 55

### Common weak points in candidate-profile evidence.

The most recurrent weaknesses suggested by the official materials are uncomplicated: insufficiently documented professional history; missing translations; qualifications that do not truly match the duties; recruitment evidence created too late or too narrowly; reliance on criteria that are not job-essential; absence of recognition or registration evidence in regulated professions; and inconsistency between the role description, the economic-interest justification and the offered salary. Those are partly legal defects and partly evidential ones, but in Swiss permit practice the distinction is often outcome-determinative. 56

## Drafting cautions

·Do not write that a non-EU worker is admissible merely because they are "skilled" or "highly skilled". Swiss law requires cumulative compliance with multiple admission conditions, and Art. 23 is only one of them1. 57

·Do not collapse **employer preference** into **immigration-law suitability.** The legal question is not whether the employer wants this specificperson, but whether the role and market test objectively justify third-country recruitment and whether the candidate objectively fits the role. 58

<!-- 10 -->

·Avoid loose labels such as "senior employee" unless they are immediately anchored to the statutory categories of manager, specialist or other qualified worker. 34

·Do not imply that degrees are always mandatory, but do not understate their importance either. Experience-only cases should be described as exceptional and document-heavy, especially outside shortage occupations. 59

·When discussing shortage occupations, make clear that SEM's facilitation is still conditional: the shortage must be evidenced, sector-specific rules may still apply, and unqualified labour remains excluded. 60

·Treat language, market and client-experience requirements with care. They are valid only if genuinely tied to the duties; otherwise they risk looking like impermissible screening devices against priority workers. 61

·Do not present Swiss-study cases as a general "study-to-work" pathway. The Swiss university and higher-vocational rules are narrow and still require high scientific or economic interest plus close role-to-qualification alignment. 62

·Always separate **legal eligibility** from **evidential persuasiveness.** A proposition may be legally sound yet fail in practice if the file lacks the documents called for by SEM and the canton. 32

·In regulated professions, never describe a person as fully suitable without checking diploma recognition, compulsory registration and any cantonal practice licence. 63

·Avoid canton-blind drafting. Geneva, Zurich and other cantons can intensify the evidence they expect, even though the legal basis remains federal. 64
