# Swiss legal authority pack on ICT corporate link and group structure

Suggested filename: legalauthorityictcorporatelinkandgroupstructurepack.pdf

# Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate a single, self-contained statutory "ICT permit" regime in the way some other jurisdictions do. The operative framework is instead built from the general admission rules in the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, the implementing Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment, SEM's binding administrative directives, and SECO guidance on posted workers and labour-market access. The official sources therefore require the analyst first to classify the factual pattern correctly: Swiss local hire, firm-internal posting, external cross-border service provision, or a narrow business-transfer/ GATS-type intra-corporate transfer. 1

For genuine intra-company cases, the clearest official descriptions focus on a real relationship between the foreign employer and a Swiss establishment of the same business or corporate group. SEM's guidance on firm-internal transfer describes employees of a foreign business or company working temporarily in a Swiss branch, subsidiary or branch office. In the narrower GATS-related category, SEM states that the assignee must have worked for at least one year in the foreign group company before the Swiss application and that the transfer is to a Swiss establishment of that group. Art. 46 VZAE, as explained by SEM, is designed to simplify the transfer of senior staff, highly qualified specialists and indispensable specialists in internationally active companies, and to justify an exception from the ordinary domestic-priority rule. 2

The federal legislation and public guidance do not set out a closed, exhaustive checklist of corporate ownership documents that must always be filed. Instead, the public materials show an evidential method: the file must make the foreign employer, Swiss host, assignment period, function, work location, remuneration, expense treatment, project or host context, and (where relevant) the service-contract chain clear; official cantonal and SEM forms then allow the authorities to request more. In practice, that means the weaker or less obvious the corporate link, the more the dossier should prove ownership, control and operational reality through corporate extracts, a group chart, branch or subsidiary registrations, and intercompany assignment documents. That last point is an inference from the official dossier requirements and forms, not an express statutory checklist. 3

The most important analytical distinction is between a true intra-company transfer and other lookalike fact patterns. A Swiss local hire means a Swiss employer is hiring into Switzerland and the ordinary labour-market tests apply unless a specific exemption exists. A posting means the foreign employer remains the employer and the worker stays on a temporary assignment, normally project-bound. An external service case may be lawful even without a group relationship if there is a real service or project contract with a Swiss customer; however, if the arrangement is really just the supply of labour to a Swiss user undertaking, it moves toward prohibited labour leasing from abroad. None of the official sources supports treating intra-group approval as automatic. 4

## Structural overview of corporate link and group structure

The Swiss materials point to a classification-first approach. The same mobile employee can fall into very different legal boxes depending on who employs them, who files the application, whether the Swiss entity is a group company or an end client, and whether the work is temporary project performance or entry into the Swiss labour market. Corporate-link analysis is therefore strongest in firm-internal postings and business-transfer cases, weaker in external cross-border service cases, and usually irrelevant in a standard Swiss local-hire case except as a possible basis for a narrowly framed exemption from recruitment proof. 5

**Primary** **legal texts.** The controlling federal texts are the AIG and the VZAE, which SEM lists on its official legal-basis page. Within the public guidance reviewed, the most operationally relevant provisions are AIG arts. 18, 21, 21a, 22, 23, 24, 26 and 30, together with VZAE art. 46 and the expense-reimbursement rules applied through AIG art. 22 and VZAE art. 22a. SEM's labour-market overview and directives repeatedly tie the practice back tothose provisions. 37

For ICT fact patterns that become client-service postings or labour-supply questions, two further federal regimes matter. First, posted-worker law and SECO's official posting guidance define the temporary foreign-employer posting model and its wage/minimum-condition logic.Secondly, the Employment Service Act framework on private placement and labour leasing becomes relevant where the real arrangement is staff supply rather than a genuine intra-group transfer or deliverable-based service contract. 38

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

At federal level, the most useful materials are SEM's labour-market admission overview, SEM's AIG directives, SEM's application-document page, and the SEM assignment-confirmation annex. Taken together, these materials show how the authorities expect a file to be built: complete dossier, employer-led application, written contract or secondment confirmation, proof of qualifications, explanation of the business need,and-where the case is project-based-service-contract and project documents. In ICT-style assignments, the official template itself asks for the foreign employer, the Swiss host company, the date since which the worker has been employed abroad, the duration and task of the assignment, salary components, and who bears travel, accommodation and meal costs. 39

SECO's materials do the classification work around postings and prohibited labour-supply structures. SECO states that a posting exists where the foreign employer sends workers temporarily to another country while the employment relationship continues, and that the worker normally remains subject to the social-security system of the origin state. Its foreign-law pages distinguish Swiss local employment from detachment in Switzerland, exclude labour leasing and private placement from the free-service regime, and the KMU / SECO guidance expressly gives the standard example that labour leasing from abroad into Switzerland is prohibited. Those are key sources when a purported ICT starts looking like a Swiss customer simply receiving manpower. 40

Zurich provides the clearest cantonal public breakdown. Its official notes divide third-country cases into Swiss local hire, firmn-internal posting and external posting. For firm-internal posting, Zurich states that the foreign employer applies, that the posting is temporary, and that domestic priority is waived because the service provider returns home afterwards. It then specifies the required content of an assignment contract for postings over four months: foreign "home company" and Swiss "host company", role, task area, place of work, start and duration, base salary abroad, assignment allowance, confirmation of full payment of travel/accommodation/food expenses, insurance and social-security arrangements, working-time and holiday terms, and signatures. Zurich also says that complete files may require additional documents. 41

Geneva's official pages are particularly useful on evidence handling. For ordinary third-country employment, Geneva requires a detailed employer motivation letter, company information, signed contract, and labour-market search evidence, but expressly carves out "transferts de cadre intra-groupe" from the proof-of-search requirement in the over-four-month checklist. For third-country postings or foreign service providers, Geneva requires a signed service contract, a signed employmnent contract, a signed detachment letter setting out base pay, detachment allowance, travel, accommodation, food, duration and weekly working hours, plus remuneration and service-detail forms. This is strong public authority for separating genuine intra-group management transfer from local hire, and for requiring a contract chain in service or posting cases. 42

Vaud's official detachment form is narrower but practically important: it asks for the foreign employer, the employee, function, date of engagement in the company, salary paid abroad, assignment allowances, separate expenses for travel, accommodation and meals, assignment dates, working hours and exact workplace, and reserves the right to request further documents on the posted employee's working conditions. Ticino's public material is thinner on corporate-link proof, but it confirms that for third-country L-permit applications from abroad the employer must file the application and that labour-market examination fees apply. For internal drafting, Vaud and Ticino therefore support the proposition that cantons may require route-specific posting evidence even when federal law does not publish a single ICT checklist. 43

## Practical legal analysis

**How** **the** **corporate** **link** is **actually** **analysed.** The Swiss public materials do not treat "corporate link" as an abstract company-law question divorced from the employment facts. They ask whether the case is truly intra-group and temporary, whether the Swiss recipient is a recognised group establishment, whether the worker remains tied to the foreign employer or instead enters the Swiss labour market, and whether the contract chain matches the narrative. In other words, the authority is testing the substance of the assignment: foreign employer, Swiss host, temporary deployment, matching role, and a plausible business reason. That is why official forms and guidance concentrate on who employs,who hosts, what work is done, where, for how long, and on what pay and expense terms. 44

**What evridence** **should** **a** **prudent ICT** **file** **contain.** Public sources expressly require the employment or assignment documentation, remuneration breakdown, project or service documents where relevant, host details, qualifications, and company / business justification. They do not publish a universal federal checklist of shareholding documents. Nevertheless, because the public file must prove a real foreign-employer/ Swiss-host relationship and the authorities may ask for more, a prudent internal evidence pack for a corporate-link case should usually add: foreign and Swiss register extracts; a current group organogram; documents shoowing shareholding or control; branch registration or proof of Swiss establishment where the host is not itself the Swiss employer; an assignment letter or secondment agreement; any intercompany host or cost-allocation agreement; and, if the case is project-based, the Swiss service / works / project contract. This is a conservative practice inference from the official forms and completeness requirements, not an express federal checklist. 34

**How to distinguish genuine ICT from local hire.** If the Swiss entity is the employer, signs **a** Swiss employment contract as employer, and is filling a position in the Swiss labour market, the case is a local hire even if the candidate comes from the same multinational group. Zurich and Geneva both frame those files as Swiss-employer cases. In that box, the ordinary labour-market evidence normally matters, although Geneva publicly recognises an exception from search-proof for intra-group management transfers in its local-hire checklist. Internal content should therefore avoid calling every group-to-group move an "ICT" without first checking whether the worker is actually being hired into Switzerland. 6

**How to distinguish ICT from posted-worker / service-provision cases.** **A** worker can lawfully come to Switzerland while remaining employed abroad for either an internal group posting or an external client-service mission. Zurich's official notes separate those categories, and GGeneva requires the complete service-contract chain for third-country foreign service providers. That means a weak corporate link does not necessarily destroy the whole case; it may instead show that the wrong route label has been chosen and that the case is really external service provision. But if the filing is presented as an intra-company transfer, the file must prove a genuine intra-group host structure rather than just a Swiss end client. 45

**How to distinguish ICT or posting from third-party contracting and labour supply.** The critical danger sign is when the foreign enntity is not really delivering a defined service or project and the Swiss recipient is in substance justtaking labour on hire. Official SECO / KMU guidance says labour leasing

from abroad into Switzerlandis prohibited, and the SEM GATS material expressly excludes personnel lessors in the relevant service-provider category. In practice, the risk rises where the Swiss side controls the worker like ordinary staff, there is no real deliverable, the contract chain is incomplete, or the worker is simply inserted into the Swiss client's workforce. Internal drafting should therefore screen "contractor", "vendor", "body shop", "payrolling", and "outsourced staff" fact patterns before calling them ICT. 46

**Payroll, allowances, expenses, insurance and social security.** The Swiss authorities do not only look at headline pay. SEM's directives and forms insist on separating base remuneration, assignment allowances and reimbursable expenses. Travel, accommodation and meal costs are not salary components and must not be deducted from the protected wage figure. The SEM template also captures Swiss health-insurance payments and Swiss social deductions where applicable, while SECO and BSV explain that a genuine posting normally leaves the worker tied to the foreign employment relationship and, if the social-security conditions are met, to the origin-state social-security regime. For longer uninterrupted postings, SEM's directives state that the special employer duty to reimburse detachment expenses is generally time-limited after twelve months, subject to important exceptions. 47

**Prior** **employment** **and** **weak** **or** **recent** links. Prior foreign employment is explicit and strict in the narrow GATS-type intra-corporate transfer category, where SEM requires at least one year in the foreign group company. Zurich's and Vaud's forms also ask for the employee's date of engagement with the foreign employer, and SEM's labour-market basics note a six-month same-company threshold in relation to a posting-related exception under the job-notification regime. What the official sources do **not** provide is a published safe harbour for newly created Swiss entities, recently acqquired companies, bare SPVs, or lightly documented "affiliate"structures. The cautious conclusion is that such cases are evidence-heavy: the file should prove not only formal ownership or control but also operational reality, a real Swiss host function, and a credible business reason for the transfer. That conclusion is an inference from the official materials rather than an express statutory rule. 48

## Drafting cautions

**Do not describe Switzerland as having a single statutory ICT route.** The safer formula is that Swiss ICT-style cases are handled through the general AIG / VZAE framework, with Art. 46 VZAE and SEM / cantonal guidance doing much of the practical work. 25

**Do not collapse local hire and intra-group posting into one category.** The decisive question is whether the worker remains employed by the foreign employer or enters employment with a Swiss employer. Zurich and Geneva publicly separate those scenarios. 49

**Do not use "affiliate", "group company" or "representative office" loosely.** The reviewed public official materials expressly name branches, subsidiaries and branch offices; they do not publish a developed rule for representative offices or weakly evidenced affiliates. Future content should therefore say that non-named structures require case-specific proof of legal and operational linkage. 36

**Do not imply that intra-groupcases are exempt from all labour-market controls.** Even where domestic priority is waived, the sources still maintain quotas, wage and working-condition controls, qualification review and housing requirements. 50

**Do not present service-provider or contractor cases as ICT unless the corporate link and host** **structure** **are** **real.** If the Swiss recipient is an end client and the worker is there to performm a service contract, the case mnay be external service provision instead; if the arrangement is pure manpower supply, cross-border labour leasing risks arise. 51

**Do** **not** **blur** **salary** **with** **allowances** **and** **expenses.** Swiss sources require separate treatment of basic pay, assignment allowances, tax or insurance elements, and reimbursable travel, accommodation and meal costs. 52

**Do not universalise the one-year prior-employment rule.** It is clear in the narrow GATS-type corporate-transfer category, but public Swiss materials do not frame it as an across-the-board requirement for every group mnove. Where prior service matters, the drafting should say wvhy it matters in that specific route. 53

**Do not suggest approval is automatic because the move is internal.** Zurich explicitly states there is no right to a permit or extension in third-country cases, and Geneva public materials emphasise quotas and the need for prior approval before work begins. 54
