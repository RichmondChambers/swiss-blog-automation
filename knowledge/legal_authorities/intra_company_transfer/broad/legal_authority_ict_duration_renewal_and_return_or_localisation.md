# Swiss legal authority pack on ICT duration, renewal, return and localisation

## A. Executive legal summary

Swiss law does **not** create a self-standing residence category called an "ICT permit" for third-country intra-company transferees. In practice, Swiss ICT cases are built from the ordinary permit architecture for work-based stays-especially the short-stay permit L and the residence permit **B** - combined, where relevant, with the statutory **exception** for intra-corporate transfers in **Article** **30** **paragraph** **1letter** **h** **FNIA/AIG** and **Article** **46** **OASA/VZAE.** SEM's 2026 employment guidance treats these cases as **purpose-specific** and, in the project/posting context, fundamentally **temporary,** not as a natural route into permanent Swiss employment.

Duration is assessed first by the **real operational need** shown by the project plan or transfer rationale, not by an open-ended business preference. SEM states expressly that the **actually** **necessary** **period** **of** **presence** is decisive; many project or extraordinary-task cases can be covered by permits for up to four months, foreseeably longer projects are handled primarily through **L** **permits,** and demonstrably delayed or prolonged projects may, after an L permnit, be examined for a **B** **permit** with conditions. Where the case falls within Switzerland's **GATS** commitments for intra-corporate transfer, SEMstates a specific time frame of **three** **years, extendable** **to** **four,** for executives and highly qualified specialists who have worked for the foreign group company for at least one year before the application. 2

**Renewal** and **localisation** must be kept analytically separate. Renewal means that the **same** **temporary** **assignment** **logic** still exists and is still evidenced. By contrast, if the individual is **newly** **hired by the Swiss entity,** SEM treats that as a **change of purpose** under **Article 54 OASA/VZAE,** and says that a **new** **admission** **procedure** under the FNIA/OASA framework will generally apply, explicitly including the ordinary Swiss labour-market test in **Article** **21** **FNIA/AIG.** That is the key legal dividing line between a continued posting and a move into ordinary Swiss employment. 3

The sources reviewed do **not** reveal a universal, free-standing statutory rule saying that every ICT transferee must return to the foreign employer at the end of the assignment. However, the entire posting structure assumes that the person remains under the **foreign** **employment** **relationship** and is only **temporarily** **deployed** in Switzerland. SEM's project/service-provider annex states this directly and uses it to distinguish lawful assignment from localisation. For internal drafting, the safer formulation is therefore that **return to the foreign employer is not an independent statutory condition uniformly** **stated in the legislation, but is embedded in the temporary posting structure that justifies the** **admission.** 4

Settlement planning must be handled conservatively. Dependants of an **L-permit** holder may themselves receive only **L-family** **status,** and the work authorisation available to such family members is tied to the principal's L-permit duration. For permanent residence, **temporary** **stays** do not count towards the continuous-residence calculation for a **C** **permit** under **Article** **34** **paragraph** **5** **FNIA/AIG.** The official ICT/GATS materials are framed around **timne-limited** **market access,** not integration into permanent Swiss residence. This pack therefore does **not** treat ICT residence as a built-in route to settlement or naturalisation. 5

## B. Structural overview of duration, renewal and localisation

At the federal level, the architecture is layered. **Articles** **18** **to** **24** **FNIA/AIG** contain the ordinary admission requirements for work-based stays: overall economic interest, employer application, quotas where applicable, local salary and work conditions, qualifications, and accommodation. **Article** **30paragraph** **1 letter** **h** **FNIA/AIG** then allows derogation from the ordinary admission scheme in order to **facilitate** **intra-corporate** **transfers** of senior management and indispensable specialists. **Article** **46OASA/VZAE** operationalises that exception for multinational companies by allowing L and B permits for senior staff and indispensable specialists, while still requiring economic interest, an employer application, compliance with quotas, Swiss salary/work conditions, and adequate accommodation. ⁶

The permit logic then splits by time and by factual structure. A **short-stay** **permit** L is theordinary temporary permit for limited work stays and is issued for up **to** **one** **year,** extendable up to **two** **years** in total under **Article 32 FNIA/AIG. A residence permit B** is for stays of **more than one year;** under **Article** **58** **OASA/VZAE,** a first B permit is generally valid for **one** **year** and can be extended by **two** **years,** subject to exceptions in specific cases. SEM overlays that general framework with operational guidance for projects: it says many projects can be covered under the up-to-four-month route; foreseeably longer assignments should generally be structured as L permits; and only where there are **special circumstances,** such as extraordinary tasks or documented delay, should a longer B-permit solution be considered in this project/posting context. 7

The **GATS** layer matters only for a subset of cases. SEM states that Switzerland's special commitments cover intra-corporate transfer of indispensable executives and highly qualified specialists from foreign service companies with a Swiss branch, subsidiary or office. For this sub-category, SEM gives a maximum stay of **three years, extendable to four,** and requires at least **one year of prior** **employment** with the foreign group company. SEM also makes clear that GATS does not displace all Swiss controls: quotas still apply, salary/work conditions still apply,and geographic/professional mobility restrictions remain. 8

The central legal fault line is between **continuation of a temporary foreign assignment and entry** **into** **ordinary** **Swiss** **employment.** Zurich's official guidance reflects the same distinction: a Swiss **Stellenantritt** means a normal employment relationship with a Swiss employer; a true **Entsendung** means the worker remains bound by the foreign employment contract, the work in Switzerland is temporary, and it is generally project-bound. SEM's annex then adds the decisive localisation rule: once there is a new direct Swiss employment contract with the Swiss branch, the case is treated as a **purpose** **change** and generally falls into a new admission process. 9

Continuity is also regulated procedurally. For **B** **permits,** the renewal application must be filed **no** **later** **than** **14** **days** **before** **expiry,** and the person may remain in Switzerland while the renewal request is pending unless a contrary decision is issued. By contrast, serial oor chained L **permits** are restricted: **Article** **56** **OASA/VZAE** requires a one-year interruption before a new L permit is issued, subject to exceptions, and **Article** **57** **OASA/VZAE** bars immediate chaining of several short-stay categories and requires at least **two** **months abroad** between them. Those anti-chaining rules are important in ICT drafting because they stop temporary assignment permits from being described as freely renewable rolling status. 10

## D-E. Primary legal texts and official guidance / administrative materials

**D. Primary legal texts.** The core statutory framework is the **Foreign Nationals and Integration Act.** For ICT duration/localisation work, the most important provisions are the ordinary work-admission rules in **Articles** **18** **to** **24,** the exception power in **Article** **30** **paragraph** **1** **letter** h, the permit structure in **Articles** **32** **and** **33,** employment mobility in **Article** **38,** family provisions in **Articles** 44 **to** **46,** and the expiry rules in **Article** 61.Together, those provisions show that Swiss ICT cases are admitted through the ordinary L/B system, subject to purpose-specific conditions and later expiry rules. 24

The core ordinance is the **OASA/VZAE.** The most relevant provisions are **Article** **46** on intra-corporate transfer in international companies, **Article** 54 on change of purpose, **Articles** **55** **to** **57** on L-permit job changes, reissue and chaining, **Articles** 58 **and** **59** on B-permit duration and renewal timing, **Article** **79**on expiry during time abroad, and **Article** **26** on work for family members of L-permit holders.These ordinance rules are what give the duration and continuity questions concrete legal shape. 25

**E. Official guidance and administrative materials.** The most important federal administrative authority is **SEM's Chapter 4 "Aufenthalt mit Erwerbstätigkeit",** current to **1 January 2026.** WVithin that chapter, the load-bearing sections are: 4.3.4.1-4.3.4.2 on posted-worker salary/expense rules; 4.4.9on intra-corporate transfer in international companies; 4.7.1.2 on project staff; 4.8.1.4.2-4.8.1.5.2 on GATS-based intra-corporate transferees; and **4.8.2.3-4.8.2.4** on non-EU/EFTA service providers and posting logic. SEM's short annex on **project staff and** **service** **providers** is especially important because it directly addresses **project change, project duration, and posting versus localisation.** SEM's **confirmation** **of assignment** form is also highly useful because it states, in documentary form,the fields the authorities expect to see evidenced in a posting case. 26

## F. Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** is valuable because its public guidance cleanly distinguishes a Swiss local hire from an assignment. Its third-country page says a **Stellenantritt** is a work relationship with a Swiss employer, whereas an **Entsendung** means the employee remains under the foreign employment contract and the Swiss work is temporary and usually project-bound. Zurich's forms for both ordinary posting and purely intra-group posting also require a detailed documentary package: assignment duration, role, location, base foreign salary, Swiss allowance, expense coverage for at least twvelve months from entry, insurance and social-security arrangements, qualifications, and identity documents. That is strong cantonal confirmation of the federal temporary-assignment logic and of the evidential burden that will matter again on extension. 27

**Geneva** openly states that third-country businesses posting staff into the canton must obtain prior permission, that the file should be lodged **6** to **8** **weeks** before the mission, that the worker must reside in Switzerland during the mission, and that the assignment file must include the service contract, employment contract, motivation letter, qualifications, a signed posting letter, and remuneration details including travel, accommodation and food costs. Geneva is also useful on employer change: for a third-country national who already holds an **L** **permit,** a change of employer requires a **new** **work** **permit,** whereas holders of a durable **B** **permit** do not generally need to report ordinary job changes. For ICT localisation analysis, Geneva therefore supports the proposition that L-permit assignment cases are not freely portable into new Swiss employment. 28

**Basel-Stadt** is thinner on substance but helpful on workflow. Its public renewal page expressly groups **third-country** **nationals and** **posted** **workers** together and routes renewal through an employer-submitted work-permit form. That is not a substantive localisation rule, but it is a useful official cantonal indicator that assignment extensions remain employer-driven and should be regularised through a formal extension process rather than informally rolled forward. 29

## G. Practical legal analysis

The safest federal reading is that **assignment duration** is **assessed** **functionally.** SEM says the authorities must match the permit to the **actually** **necessary** **presence** under the project plan. That means Swiss ICT content should never describe duration as something the group may choose freely once the person meets the skill threshold. The decisive inputs are the documented task, implementation plan, timeframe, resource need, and whether the assignment remains genuinely temporary. In the GATS sub-category, SEM provides a hard maximum of : $3+1$ **years;** outside GATS,the general L/B statutory framework applies, but project practice remains short-term and conditional.A further practical timing constraint arises from the social-security side: SEM notes that a posting is not unlimited in time and is usually bounded, as a social-insurance matter, at around **five** **years.** That is not itself an immigration duration rule, but it can become relevant when a client asks whether a long-running "temporary" transfer can simply continue indefinitely. 30

**On** **renewal,** the legal question is not "can an ICT be renewed?" in the abstract; it is whether the original legal basis still exists. The federal legislation gives the broad renewal architecture-L may be extended within its statutory ceiling; B may be extended if there are no revocation grounds; B-renewal applications must be filed at least 14 days before expiry-but the practical proof burden comes from SEM's project and posting materials. Those materials require, or strongly imply, a current project description, the service or work contract, a reasoned explanation of the assignment need, a realistic implementation plan, confirmation of the task, location and duration, salary structure,posting allowances, expense coverage, insurance and social-security arrangements, and evidence of qualifications. The official materials are comparatively **thin** on a dedicated "ICT renewal checklist", so the conservative internal rule should be**: refresh the original posting evidence and prove that the** **same temporary need still exists.** 31

**On** **return** **to** **the** **foreign** **employer,** the materials are more structural than explicit. The legislation reviewed does not contain a universal article that simply commands return to the foreign employer after every intra-company assignment. But SEM's localisation note supplies the practical answer: in a lawful posting, the employee remains under the **foreign** **contract;** if there is a "new" direct fixed-term employment with the Swviss branch, that is already a **change** **of purpose** and generally triggers a fresh admission process. So the best internal formulation is not "Swiss law always requires an express return clause", but rather**: the legal logic of the assignment presupposes continued foreign employment** **and temporary Swiss deployment; if that ceases, the posting rationale is gone.** Zurich's public guidance is consistent with that view, because it defines assignment by continued foreign contractual subordination and contrasts it with Swiss local hire. 32

On **localisation** **into** **Swiss** **employment,** the key issue is a shift in legal basis, not merely/a change in payroll mechanics. SEM expressly says that a new direct hire by the Swiss branch is a **purpose** **change** under **Article** **54** **OASA/VZAE,** and generally requires a **new** **admission** **procedure,** "in particular" including **Article** **21 FNIA/AIG.** That means localisation is not a subset of renewal. It is a sepoarate admission question. This is also why federal **B-permit job** **mobility** in **Article** **38(2) FNIA/AIG** must be handled carefully in ICT content: although B holders may ordinarily change jobs freely, that general rule does **not** neutralise **Article** **54** **OASA/VZAE** where the underlying purpose of stay changes from

<!-- 8 -->

temporary foreign assignment to ordinary local Swiss employment. For **L** **permits,** the position is even stricter: job change is allowed only for **important** **reasons,** and Geneva's public practice requires a new work permit for a third-country L-holder changing employer. 33

On **changes of assignment purpose, role, salary, host entity or project,** the safest view is that none of these should be treated as automatically immaterial. SEM's annex says permits are issued for a **specific documented project** and that **project change** is equivalent to **job change,** even where the canton and end user remain the same. Salary and conditions matter because Swiss local wages and work conditions remain a standing admission requirement, and the posting letter/contract must transparently show the foreign base salary, Swiss allowances and expense treatment. Zurich and Geneva both require this kind of detail. If the host entity, client, task or contractual structure changes enough that the original purpose statement is no longer true, the case should be reviewed as at least a permit amendment issue and, in many cases, as a new application. 34

**On** **project** **extension** **or** **delay,** the sources are helpful. SEM says that, for delayed or prolonged projects or extraordinary tasks, **a B permit under Article 20 paragraph 1 OASA/VZAE** may be examined after an initial L **permit,** with conditions and obligations attached. That is the cleanest official authority for moving from shorter to longer duration while preserving a still-temporary project rationale. What the sources do not provide is a general licence to convert every long-running assignment into renewable B status. The official materials continue to frame the case as exceptional, documented, and still tied to the temporary project logic. 15

On **assignment completion** **before** **permit** **expiry,** the official materials reviewed are comparatively sparse. No dedicated federal ICT rule was located explaining the exact legal consequence of the project ending before the card end-date. The cautious answer is therefore twofold. First, the permit remains a **purpose-specific** authorisation under **Articles 32-33 FNIA/AIG** and **Article 54 OASA/VZAE,** so the remaining validity period should **not** be assumed to authorise redeployment to a different Swiss role, client or employer. Secondly, if the transferee leaves Switzerland, the ordinary expiry rules apply: without deregistration, the L **permit** lapses after **three** **months abroad** and the **B** **permit** after **six** **months** **abroad;** short visits to Switzerland do not interrupt those periods. 35

**On family residence and long-term continuity,** the safest drafting line is narrow. **Article 45 FNIA/AIG** allows family reunification for spouses and unmarried children under 18 of an L-holder, but only by way **of** **L-family** **status; Article** **26** **OASA/VZAE** ties anny work authorisation for those family members to the principal's L-permit duration. That means family continuity must be assessed every time the principal permit is renewed, lapses, or is localised. For settlement planning, **Article** **34** **paragraph** **5 FNIA/AIG** is the key brake: **temporary** **stays** do not count towards the continuous-residence requirement for a **C** **permit.** The materials gathered therefore support a conservative drafting rule: do **not describe** **temporary ICT residence as carrying a reliable route to C-permit eligibility. As to naturalisation,** this pack does **not** make an ICT-specific claim; a separate nationality-law review wvould be needed for citizenship timing. 36

**Open** **questions** **and** **limitations.** The official sources are strong on permit structure, project duration, localisation and employer/project changes, but they are **thinner** on three points: a dedicated federal **ICT renewal checklist,** an express universal statutory **return-to-foreigJn-employer clause,** and **a** detailed federal rule on **early assignment completion before permit expiry.** The conclusions above on those points are therefore intentionnally conservative and are framed as structural inferences from purpose-specific admission, posting documentation, and change-of-purpose rules rather than as bright-line statutory commands. 37

## H. Drafting cautions

·Do not describe Swiss ICTs as a separate "ICT permit" route. Say instead that Switzerland deals with intra-company transfer cases through the ordinary **L/B** **permit framework,** with a specific exception for intra-corporate transfer under **Article** **30(1)(h)** **FNIA/AIG** **and** **Article** **46** **OASA/** **VZAE.** 38

·Always distinguish **renewal of the same temporary assignment** from **localisation into Swiss** **employment.** They are not the same legal event. 17

·State explicitly whether the transferee remains under the **foreign** **employment contract** and which entity is the **home** **company** and which is the **host company.** If that foreign-employment premise disappears, the assignment logic becomes unstable. 39

·If relying on **GATS,** say so expressly and only where the facts fit: Swiss branch/subsidiary/office, executive or highly qualified specialist,and **at** **least one** **year's** **prior** **employment abroad.** Also state the **3-year** **maximum, extendable** to **4** **years,** rather than implying indefinite renewability. 8

·Do not imply that a project extension automatically allows a rolling series of assignment renewals. SEM allows longer handling only where delay or prolongation is **demonstrated,** and its project materials remain temporary in tone. 15

·Do not assume that a change in **project, host entity, role, salary structure, client or employer** is immaterial. SEM treats a **project** **change** as authnorisation-relevant, and Swiss salary/work conditions remain an ongoing admission condition. 40

·Do not write that ICT residence naturally leads to **permanent Swiss employment, C-permit** **eligibility,** or **citizenship.** The official materials support the opposite drafting instinct: this is a **temporary** **market-access** **mechanism,** and temporary stays do not count straightforwardly towards the continuous-residence requirement for C. 41

· If the assignment ends early, do not assume the remaining validity on the card may simply be reused for another Swiss role. Treat further Swiss work as requiring fresh legal analysis under the permit's **purpose-specific** basis and, where relevant, the departure/expiry rules. 35

## Cantonal practice materials

**·Kanton** **Zürich,** page on **third-country** **nationals,** distinguishing Swiss local hire from foreign assignment and stating that postings remain under the foreign contract and are temporary/ project-bound. 47

**·Kanton Zürich,** forms on **posting of employees and intra-group posting,** identifying required evidence on duration, salary, expenses, insurance and qualifications.48

**·Canton of Geneva,** page on **posting staff or providing services in Geneva from a non-EU/** **EFTA state,** including timing, required documents and mission conditions. 49

**·Canton** **of** **Geneva,** pages on **change** **of employer** for permit holders, including the rule that a third-country **L-permit** holder must obtain a new work permit to change employer, while durable B-permit holders do not generally need to report ordinary job changes. 50

**·Basel-Stadt,** page on **renewing a work permit,** which expressly includes **third-country** **nationals and posted workers** in the extension workflow. 29
