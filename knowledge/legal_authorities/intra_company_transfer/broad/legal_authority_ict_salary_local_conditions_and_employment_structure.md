# Swiss legal authority pack on salary, local conditions and employment structure in intra-company transfer cases

Suggested internal filename:

legalauthorityictsalarylocalconditionsandemploymentstructurepack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

Swiss official materials do nnot present a separate, self-contained "ICT permit" equivalent to the EU ICT Directive. Instead, intra-group transfers appear in the ordinary Swiss third-country admission framework under the FNIA/AIG and VZAE/OASA, alongside specific facilitation for operational transfers in international companies and, where the facts fit, cross-border service provision or secondment.For compensation questions, the decisive point is not the label attached to the assignment, but whether the arrangement is a Swiss local hire, a secondment for a foreign employer, or a cross-border service-provider case. In each of those structures, salary and working-condition compliance remains central. 1

The core Swiss rule is that foreign workers must receive salary, social-security treatment and employment conditions that correspond to local, occupational and sectoral standards. SEM's guidance states that the cantons must check this systematically, with reference first to binding legal rules and actual pay and conditions for comparable work in the same business and branch, and then also to current statistical wage material where collective minima sit below real market pay. This applies not only to local hires, but also to posted or seconded workers. 2

Foreign payroll, a home-country employment contract, or a split-pay arrangement does not displace Swiss compliance. Official Swiss guidance and cantonal forms expressly contemplate assignments in which salary is paid abroad and toppoed up or supplemented for Switzerland, but they require transparent disclosure of the home salary, Swiss assignment allowances, travel, lodging and meal treatment, insurance, social deductions, and the roles of the home and host entities.The reviewed sources therefore support a qualified proposition: foreign payroll is possible in Swiss ICT-type cases, but only if the package still satisfies Swiss local standards and the structure is fully documented. 3

Swiss official sources draw a hard line between salary and expense reimbursement in secondment cases. Under SEM guidance and SECO's international wage-comparison instruction, travel, accommodation and meal costs connected with the posting are reimbursable expenses, not wage, and must not be deducted from gross pay. By contrast, assignment allowances that compensate the gap between home-country and Swiss remuneration can count as wage components in the posting comparison. This distinction is one of the most important compliance points in Swiss ICT files. 4

Documentation quality matters. SEM requires a binding written employment contract for local hires and recognises secondment confirmations or assignment contracts for posted cases. Zurich and Geneva practice materials show that the authorities want the assignment structure spelled out in unusual detail,including the home and host entities, function, Swiss worksite, duration, base salary abroad,

Swiss allowances, expenses, weekly hours, insurance, social charges and signatures. Weak drafting on these points creates both approval risk and later compliance risk. 5

A separate but closely related risk is structural misclassification. SEM's directives distinguish lawful secondment for performance of a contract or project from prohibited cross-border labour leasing. If the Swiss host effectively directs the worker's day-to-day work, bears the operational result risk, integrates the worker into its organisation and simply pays for labour time, the arrangement can move away from a real secondment and towards unlawful personnel leasing from abroad. In parallel, the AIG uses a factual concept of employer, so the Swiss host can still be treated as an employer for permit-compliance purposes even where the paper contract sits abroad. 6

## Structural overview of salary, local conditions and employment structure

The reviewed Swiss materials point to three recurring structures for ICT-type cases. The first is a **Swiss** **local** **hire** or "work with Stellenantritt", namely work for an employer established in Switzerland or a Swiss establishment of a foreign company. In that model, the Swiss employer ordinarily files the application, a binding employment contract is required, and the employer must handle Swiss labour-market, social-security and tax registration conseqquences identified in the official guidance. 7

The second is an **intra-group** **secondment** or internal posting. Zurich's official materials describe this as a case where an employee of a foreign company works temporarily in a Swiss branch, subsidiary or group establishment while remaining attached to the foreign employer. Zurich expressly statesthat posted workers remain subject to the home-country employment contract, that the foreign employer is responsible for the work-permit filing in such cases, and that compoliance with local salary and local conditions still has to be shown. 8

The third is an **external cross-border** **service-provider** **posting,** where the foreign employer keeps the employment relationship but sends staff to perform a service contract for a Swiss client. SEM's guidance places these cases under the rulesfor service provision without local "employment start", and Zurich's guidance treats them separately from internal secondment while requiring a contract or project description, secondment evidence and a complete remuneration breakdown. In these cases the Swiss client often has an immigration role, but the foreign employer remains central to salary and compliance evidence. 9

Across all three structures, Swiss authorities are concerned with the reality of the arrangement rather than labels alone. SEM's directives define work "with employment start" by reference to work for a Swiss employer or Swiss establishment, and work "without employment start" by reference to work for a foreign employer or self-employment. Those same directives also apply a factual employer concept for permit compliance and warn against disguised foreign labour leasing. A file that claims "secondment" but gives the Swiss host full managerial control is therefore vulnerable on both salary review and structural legality. 10

Operational transfer can ease one part of the labour-market test, but not the salary test. SEM says that in operational transfers of senior managers and indispensable specialists within international companies, the domestic-priority rule does not apply; similarly, Zurich says that seconded staff in temporary service-provider cases are not subject to in-country priority. None of the reviewed official sources suggest that such transfers are exempt from the Swiss salary and working-condition review. 1

### Primary legal texts

The reviewed official materials consistently identify the following federal texts as the legally decisive foundation for compensation and structure issues in Swiss ICT-type cases. The SEM labour-market guidance expressly lists the FNIA/AIG and the VZAE/OASA as the main admission instruments, and the SEM directives repeatedly apply the following article clusters. 24

**Federal** **immigration** **framework.** The key provisions are AIG/FNIA articles 18 to 24,26,30 and 91,read with VZAE articles 14, 19, 20, 22, 22a and 46 as reflected in SEM's official guidance. In practice, article 22AIG anchors salary and local conditions; article 23 AIG anchors the manager/specialist/qualified-worker test; article 24 AIG concerns accommodation; article 26 AIG governs cross-border service provision; article 30 AIG covers derogations; article 91 AIG underpins the employer's compliance duty; and article 46 VZAE is the operational-transfer provision used in multinational-company cases. 25

**Posted-worker** **framework.** For secondments and service-provider assignments, the sources repeatedly point to the Entsendegesetz and Entsendeverordnung. SECO's official page on international wage comparison says the Posting of Workers Act obliges foreign employers to guarantee the salary and working conditions that are mandatory in Switzerland, and SECO's instruction identifies article 2EntsG and article 1 EntsV as the core legal basis for the wage comparison. 26

**General labour-law** **overlay.** SEM's directives additionally invoke article 327a CO for necessary business expenses once the special 12-month secondment-expense rule has fallen away, and the posting materials tie working time, rest, holidays, safety and equal treatment back to Swiss mandatory labour standards. This means that an ICT salary pack should treat Swiss employment-law minima as part of the local-conditions review, even when the primary immigration route remains under the AIG/VZAE. 27

### Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful federal guidance source is SEMM's page on the basis for admission to the Swiss employment market. It is an official overview, available in English and German, and it concisely states the Swiss rule on local salary and working conditioons, the requirement for a signed contract conditional on permit approval, employer registration duties for social security, source-tax handling for non-C-permit workers, and the non-application of priority in operational transfers of senior managers and essential specialists. For a concise internal authority pack, this page is one of the best starting points.

The decisive detailed source is **SEM's AIG** **directives, chapter** 4. Those directives give the most legally useful administrative content on how salary review works in practice, what countsas a documentably creditable salary component, how secondment expenses are treated, how the 12-month expense rule works, how service-provider applications are filed, what information a secondment confirmation must contain, how social-security boundaries are handled in postings, and how to distinguish lawful secondment from prohibited personnel leasing. They are the strongest single authority in the reviewed material for drafting an internal Swiss ICT salary pack. 29

On the posted-worker side, **SECO's international wage-comparison page** and the linked **instruction** **on** **international** **wage** **comparison** are the key sources. The page confirms that foreign employers must guarantee Swiss-mandatory salary and working conditions when posting to Switzerland and that SECO provides a formal comparison method. The instruction then supplies the detailed method: Swiss territoriality, hourly conversion of salary, comparison of "actual" versus "Swiss owed" remuneration, non-crediting of expense reimbursement, and crediting of wage-like detachment allowances. It also flags proof issues around foreign social-contribution treatment where a package looks artificially allowance-heavy. 30

The official federal posting portal, **entsendung.admin.ch,** is important for labour-condition overlays. Its official pages describe the fields protected by the posted-worker regime, including minimum remuneration, working and rest time, minimum holidays, occupational safety, health and hygiene, protection for pregnant women and young people, and equality. The same portal also states that employers must separately compensate travel, accommodation and meal expenses and provide accommodation meeting ordinary standards of hygiene and comfort. 31

For salary benchmarking, the authorities point to official statistical tools rather than a single national wage table. SEM's directives mention the Zurich Lohnbuch, the Federal Statistical Office's **Salarium,** and SECO's **national wage** **calculator.** The FSO says Salarium estimates gross monthly salary and a wage range for a defined profile; SECO says its calculator produces a gross monthly wage and interquartile spread for an individual profile and was developed in the context of the flanking measures. Both are expressly informative tools, not binding wage recommendations, but they are part of the official benchmarking ecosystem referred to by the authorities. 32

### Cantonal practice notes

Zurich. Zurich's official materials are unusually useful because they distinguish local hire from internal and external secondment, and they spell out what the labour-market authority expects in the evidence. For internal and external postings of third-country nationals, Zurich requires either a secondment confirmation for assignments up to four months or a secondment contract for longer assignments. It expects the file to identify the employee, the home and host companies, the Swiss function, the task scope, the worksite, the duration, the home-country base salary, any Swiss assignment allowance, confirmation that all secondment expenses are borne, insurance, social charges, and explicit regulation of holidays and working time or a statement that Swiss law governs any missing terms. Zurich also notes that additional evidence may be requested depending on the case. This is highlyyvaluable cantonal practice, but it should be treated as Zurich practice rather than a universal federal form. 33

A particularly striking Zurich practice point is its treatment of posting expenses where a flat-rate method is used instead of actual-cost proof. Zurich's guidance says the employer must either confirm assumption of all posting expenses under the legal rules or pay a lump sum of at least CHF 3,000 per month plus actual travel costs for at least the first 12 months from entry. Because this figure appears in Zurich's own guide and not in the reviewed federal materials as a universal Swiss number, it should be cited only as cantonal practice, not as a federal rule. 34

<!-- 7 -->

**Geneva.** Geneva's materials show a more form-driven approach. The canton's guidance for third-country companies or independent service providers says that sector-standard salary and working conditions must be respected, that usual salary conditions can be checked through its online wage calculator, and that the worker may not begin before authorisation is issued. For posted employees, Geneva requires not only the service contract and employment contract, but also a signed secondment letter setting out base remuneration, detachment allowance, travel, lodging and meal treatment, duration and weekly hours. Geneva's official remuneration form then breaks the package down further into salary paid abroad, detachment allowance, employer-paid health-insurance premiums, employer-borne tax, employer-borne employee social deductions and other mandatory Swiss deductions, while insisting that lodging, food and travel costs are borne separately and not deducted from the listed compensation. 17

Geneva also has a developed concept of **"usages"**: documents recording salaries and social benefits customary in a sector. OCIRT describes these as reference documents showing what salary and social benefits are practised in a professional sector in Geneva, and it issues attestations for companies that commit to comply with them. Geneva also maintains a canton minimum wage that is indexed over time. For Swiss ICT cases in Geneva, this means that the wage benchmark may come not only from federal statistical tools and CBAs, but also from Geneva "usages" and the cantonal minimum wage framework. 35

**Ticino.** Ticino's official materials emphasise labour-market supervision and wage-dumping conntrol.For third-country nationals, Ticino states that a work permit remains subject to the usual labour-market analysis, including quotas, domestic-priority review, control of salary and working conditions, and qualifications. For notified postings, Ticino requires an employer declaration on compliance with the Swiss minimum salary and work conditions, and incomplete notifications are refused. Ticino also publicly maintains a canton minimum-wage systemn by economic sector, with an official calculator and annually updated executive decree. In practice, a Ticino ICT file should therefore be checked not only against federal salary standards and any applicable CBA or NAV, but also against the canton's sectoral minimum-wage framework where relevant. 36

### Practical legal analysis

The safest starting proposition for Swiss ICT work is that **Swiss local standards apply** **atthe** **Swiss** **place** **of work,** even where the employee remains employed and paid abroad. SEM says this directly for posted workers in operational transfers and service provision, and SECO's posted-worker guidance applies the same logic under the international wage-comparison method. The internal content system should therefore avoid any phrasing that suggests a foreign home salary is assessed only by reference to home-country norms. The correct test is whether the package, once properly characterised, reaches Swiss local, occupational and sectoral standards. 37

On **home-country salary, Swiss allowances and bonuses,** the safest rule is to separate components by legal character. Contractually enforceable cash salary is the starting point. Wage-like detachment allowances can be added if they compensate the Swiss/home-country gap. Reimbursement of travel, accommodation and meals cannot be used to make up a salary shortfall. Non-cash or employer-borne items, such as health-insurance premiums, tax or assumed employee social deductions, should be disclosed separately because Geneva's official form asks for them separately; however,the reviewed federal materials do not state a general rule that these items automatically count towards the salary

benchmark in ICT cases. Guaranteed bonuses may help only if they are clearly contractual; the reviewed official guidance is otherwise thin on bonus treatment, so reliance on bonuses should be conservative and heavily evidenced. 38

**On assignment letter versus Swiss employment contract,** the distinction should be framed by structure, not preference. Where the case is a Swiss local hire, SEM expects a binding written employment contract, with thestandard permit-condition wording. Where the case is a genuine secondment or service-provider posting, the reviewed materials accept a secondment confirmation or secondment contract, but only if it is substantive. Geneva is stricter, because it asks for both the home employment contract and a separately signed secondment letter. Future content should therefore avoid broad statements such as "an assignment letter is enough" or "a Swiss contract is always mandatory". The accurate proposition is conditional and route-specific. 39

On **payroll location** **and** **employer-of-record,** the official materials are functionally rather than formally focused. They contemplate salary paid abroad, Swiss allowances, employer-borne Swiss costs and continuing foreign employment. They do not, in the reviewed sources, impose a blanket immigration rule that the worker must be on a Swiss payroll merely because work is done in Switzerland. At the same time, SEM's factual-employer concept prevents overreliance on nominal employer-of-record language: if the Swiss host effectively employs and directs the worker, the host can still be treated as an employer for immigration-compliance purposes. Internal content should therefore say that payroll location is not the decisive immigration test, but should never imply that a foreign EOR or home payroll eliminates Swiss salary, permit, social-insurance or source-tax issues.40

**On working hours, holidays, expenses and general working conditions,** SEM expects those essentials to appear in thecontract material, and Zurich explicitly requires either express clauses on holidays and working time or a clause that Swiss law governs any unregulated terms. In secondment cases, posted-worker rules can additionally import Swiss minimum rules on work and rest periods, minimum annual leave, safety, hygiene and equality. This means that salary drafting should not be isolated from labour-condition drafting. Weak holiday, working-time or expense clauses can undermine both the salary analysis and the plausibility of the structure. 41

On the **manager** **or specialist** **profile,** there is no reviewed official rule saying that a particular salary figure proves or disproves management or specialist status. Even so, there is a strong and sensible practice inference: because admission is reserved to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, and because salary must still match Swiss local standards, a salary that sits materially below Swiss norms for the claimed level of responsibility is likely to weaken both the article 22 and article 23narratives at once. Internal content should therefore describe this as a practice point, not a black-letter rule: compensation should be coherent with the claimed seniority, indispensability and scarcity narrative. 42

**On** **duration** **of** **assignment,** the most important official interactions are structural rather than rhetorical. SEM's directives distinguish permit categories for short projects and longer service periods; Zurich uses different forms for secondments up to four months and over four months; the 12-month rule changes the mandatory expense treatment in postings; and Zurich notes more generally that the longer the planned work stay, the higher the expectations around sustainable integration. Internal content should therefore avoid simplistic duration statements. A short, genuinely temporary secondment can support a posted-worker structure, but a longer assignment tends to demand more complete contractual, wage and compliance architecture. 43

**On** **posted-worker interaction,** the legally conservative position is that a Swiss ICT file may have to satisfy both immigration approval logic and posted-worker logic where the employee remains

employed abroad and is sent to Switzerland to work temporarily. That is why SEM cross-refers to the SECO wage-comparison instruction, why Geneva and Zurich demand secondment-specific remuneration data, and why the postings portal is relevant to accommodation and work conditions. In internal drafting, "posted-worker issues" should therefore be framed as an overlay that can apply in ICT-type secondments, not as a wholly separate world. 44

### Drafting cautions

Future Swiss ICT salary content should be drafted conservatively and should avoid familiar traps.

·Do not say or imply that a foreign contract, foreign payrolI, or foreign employer-of-record avoids the Swiss salary and local-conditions test. The reviewed official materials point the other way. 45

·Do not collapse salary, allowances and expenses into a single compensation figure. Swiss authorities distinguish contract salary from expense reimbursement, and this distinction affects both approval and compliance. 14

·Do not overstate what allowances or benefits can count. Wage-like assignment allowances can count in posting comparisons; expense reimbursement cannot; the treatment of employer-paid insurance, tax and some benefits is not stated as a general automatic credit in the reviewed federal material. 14

·Do not present an assignment letter as universally sufficient. The correct instrument depends on whether the structure is a local hire, an internal secondment or a service-provider posting. 46

·Do not ignore anti-labour-leasing risk. If the host directs the work and bears the result risk, the "secondment" label may not survive scrutiny. 6

·Do not treat cantonal benchmarking tools as interchangeable. Geneva "usages",Zurich's Lohnbuch, Ticino's sectoral minimum wage, Salarium and the SECO wage calculator all play different roles. 47

### Open questions and limitations

Official guidance is strongest on salary review, posting expenses, secondment documentation and labour-leasing risk. It is thinner on three points that should therefore be stated cautiously in future content: first, the exact general treatment of employer-paid health-insurance premiums, tax and social deductions as creditable salary components outside specific forms; secondly, the extent to which guaranteed bonuses or a thirteenth salary may be pro-rated into the immigration salary benchmark in a generic ICT case; and thirdly, whether any canton outside the reviewed examples applies materially different documentation expectations for split-pay or foreign-payroll structures. On those points, the pack should stay conservative and avoid categorical claims. 48
