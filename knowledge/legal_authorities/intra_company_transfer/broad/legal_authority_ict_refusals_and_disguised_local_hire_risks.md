## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss intra-company transfer cases sit on a relatively narrow legal base. The federal statutory anchor is Article 30 paragraph 1 letter h FNIA, which allows derogations from the ordinary admission requirements in order to simplify the transfer of senior management staff and essential specialists within internationally active companies. The implementing rule is Article 46 OASA, which still requires an employer-led application, a general economic interest, available quotas, compliance with Swiss salary and working conditions, and adequate accommodation. Official SEM materials present this as a facilitative exception, not as a free-standing entitlement. English translations exist for information,but the authentic legal texts are the German, French and Italian versions.1

Official Swiss materials do not provide a single codified list of "ICT refusal grounds". The refusal logic therefore has to be reconstructed conservatively from the converse of the admission criteria, the SEM's route-characterisation guidance, and cantonal evidential requirements. The most important negative pattern is not merely a missing document; it is mischaracterisation. A case is at risk where the facts point away from a genuine temporary intra-group transfer and towards ordinary local employment, third-party service provision, or labour supply to a Swiss user undertaking. 2

For internal drafting purposes, the strongest refusal-risk themes are these: weak proof of the corporate link between the home and Swiss entities; an assignment purpose that is generic, replaceable or commercially under-explained; a candidate profile that does not credibly match a senior manager or indispensable specialist; a factual set-up in which the Swiss entity or Swiss customer looks like the real employer; or pay documentation that does not clearly meet Swiss locaIl standards once non-salary expenses are stripped out. In practice, inconsistent contracts, payroll statements, secondment letters and task descriptions are especially damaging because the Swiss authorities repeatedly ask for those documents to prove the true route and conditions of stay. 3

As to remedies, official SEM guidance states that a cantonal foreign-national decision is challenged before the competent upper cantonal appeal body, while a decision of the SEM is generally appealable to the Federal Administrative Court within 30 days. The same guidance also explains reconsideration: it is not an ordinary appeal, it is not there to cure failures of co-operation in the first filing, and it generally requires materially changed circumstances or genuinely new important facts or evidence. That is the safest official foundationfor analysing re-filing after an ICT refusal. 4

## Structural overview and legal propositions

### B. Structural overview of ICT refusals and disguised local hire risks

The route question should usually be asked before the evidential question. In Swiss practice, four neighbouring categories matter. A genuine Article 46 OASA intra-group transfer concerns senior management or essential specialists mnoving within an internationally active group. A local hire under Article 18 FNIA concerns a Swiss employer taking on a worker in Switzerland and ordinarily triggers labour-market priority evidence. A cross-border service case under Article 26 FNIA concerns temporary service provision from abroad. A labour-supply case raises the separate and often more serious issue of personnel leasing, for which SEM and SECO materials are notably restrictive, especially where third-country nationals are being newly brought into Switzerlannd. 5

The practical problem is that documents are often drafted in business language that blurs those legal categories. Swiss guidance is strongly route-sensitive. Geneva defines secondment as a temporary mission abroad while keeping the original employment contract. Zurich, in its internal-secondment paperwork, requires the file to identify the home company and host company, the exact functions and tasks, the location, the duration, the salary in the home country, the Swiss assignment allowance, and the employer's undertaking to bear posting expenses for at least the first 12 months. Where those elements are missing or contradictory, the authorities have less reason to accept the applicant's chosen characterisation of the case. 6

For automated content, the conservative rule is to treat "ICT refusal risk" as a composite of route-characterisation risk and evidence-quality risk. A file may fail because the facts belong to another route, because the material for the correct route is too weak, or because the documents are too internally inconsistent to let the authorities rely on the applicant's account. Official sources are much clearer on those structural points than on any one federal list of refusal labels. 7

#### Primary legal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts

The federal legal texts that matter most are these. First, Article 18 FNIA sets the ordinary local-hire rule for salaried employment: economic interest, employer application, and fulfilment of Articles 20 to 25FNIA. Secondly, Article 21 FNIA embodies labour-market priority, and Article 22 FNIA requires locally customary salary and employment conditions, while making clear that expenses for posted employees are treated separately. Thirdly, Article 23 FNIA restricts work permits in principle to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, with narrower derogations for specific categories including executive transfer cases. Fourthly, Article 26 FNIA governs temporary cross-border services. Fifthly, Article 30 FNIA permits certain derogations, including the ICT derogation in paragraph 1 letter h. 21

Article 46 OASA thnen operationalises the ICT derogation. It is precise on what remains mandatory: economic interest, employer application, quota compliance, Article 22 compliance, and adequate accommodation. Notably, Article 46 does not itself spell out a bespoke documentary list, define group-link evidence in detail, or set a general maximum period. That silence is why SEM and cantonal practice materials become important when identifying how weak ICT cases are likely to fail. 10

<!-- 5 -->

On procedure, SEM's own directions on the consent procedure explain that where a permit category requires SEM approval, the canton cannot issue, renew or extend the permit without SEM consent; but a canton that wants to refuse such a permit may do so in its own competence and mmust then give the cantonal appeal information. This is important in refusal analysis because the applicant may face either a cantonal refusal or a federal SEM refusal depending on how far the file progressed. 22

##### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM chapter 4 is the most directly useful federal administrative source for ICT merits. It states that Article 46 OASA is intended to facilitate recruitment by internationally active companies and research institutes in Switzerland of executives, highly qualified experts and indispensable specialists. It expressly says that the simplified procedure allows a temporary or permanent engagement without applying labour-market priority, but only where the critical company-and employee-specific facts justify the exception. That sentence is especially important for refusal-risk drafting, because it makes evidential sufficiency central to route acceptance. 23

SEM's GATS section is also useful, even though it should be treated as GATS-specific rather than universally decisive for every Article 46 case. It defines an intra-corporate transfer as a temporary activity in a Swiss branch, subsidiary or office of the foreign company, after at least one year of prior employment abroad. It also gives workable definitions of managers and highly qualified specialists. Those definitions are the safest official benchmarks to use when automated content needs to explain why a claimed "specialist" or "manager" profile is too weak. 24

For assignments that resemble project work or external services, SEM's annex on project staff and service providers is highly relevant. It stresses that migrant project staff should be limited to specialists with project-specific know-how who cannot readily be replaced locally, and that the authorities must prevent posted project workers from competing with local and European workers by doing generic IT work. The annex also says that a project change is treated like a change of job and requires a permit, and that if a "new" fixed-term direct employment with the Swiss establishment is created, a new admission procedure generally applies, especially under Article 21 FNIA. 25

SEM's guidance on service provision and labour leasing gives the clearest official markers for disguised local hire. A true contract-for-work situation remains under the foreign employer's control and responsibility for the result; the Swiss undertaking has only the limited authority that a site owner normally has. By contrast, if the Swiss customer or host directs the day-to-day work, if the worker is integrated organisationally and temporally into the Swiss user undertaking, if hours are billed rather than a result delivered, and if the Swiss user bears the performance risk, the authorities may characterise the arrangement as personnel leasing. SEM further states that third-country nationals are generally not allowed to newly enter Switzerland for personnel leasing. 26

On salary and expense analysis, SECO's official international wage-comparison materials matter because they explain the Swiss posting-law logic used when assessing whether foreign pay packages meet mandatory Swiss standards. SEM's model "Confirmation of assignment" also matters because it forces the file to separate base salary paid abroad, Swiss allowances, tax assumptions and Swiss deductions, and it expressly states that location-typical meal, accommodation and travel reimbursements do not count as salary under Article 22 paragraph 2 FNIA. For refusal-risk drafting, that is the safest official basis for warning against trying to dress expense reimbursements up as salary. 27

## Cantonal practice notes and case law notes

### F. Cantonal practice notes

Zurich's materials are valuable because they show what a canton expects in the file for an internal secondment. The canton publishes a specific checklist for "Firmeninterne Entsendung" and requires a specific case justification, a secondment confirmation or secondment contract depending on duration, and a document set that identifies the employee, the home company and host company, the assignment function and tasks, the work location, the start and duration, the home-country base salary, the Swiss assignment allowance, the undertaking to bear all posting expenses for at least 12 months after entry, insurance, social charges, and the contractual treatment of Swviss law, holidays and working time. Zurich also requires CVs, qualification documents and identity papers. 28

Zurich's broader explanatory paper for third-country employment is useful mainly by contrast. It states that there is no legal entitlement to a permit or to its extension, that first L and B permits are quota-bound, and that the authorities may ask for further documents beyond the statutory minimum. In the local-hire context it emphasises labour-market testing, signed current contracts and qualifications matched to the proposed role. In the cross-border service context it says labour priority is usually waived because the assignee is only temporarily in Switzerland and then returns abroad.For internal systems,the safest reading is that Zurich's practice treats documentary precision as critical, regardless of the route label used in the federal text. 29

Geneva's official pages are particularly helpful on route separation. Geneva defines secondment as sending an employee on a temporary mission in another country while keeping the original employment contract. For third-country posted workers or independent service providers, Geneva requires a cantonal authorisation, says the person cannot start before approval, insists on economic interest, allows only managers, specialists and qualified workers, and requires a service contract, an employment contract, a motivation letter explaining the necessity of using a third-country provider, CVs and diplomas, and a secondment letter setting out remuneration and who bears travel, accommodation and food costs. That is a strong cantonal signal that the authorities expect a complete and economically reasoned evidential file. 30

Geneva's ordinary third-country employment page is equally useful when distinguishing local hire from ICT. It makes the employer prove labour-market priority through vacancy notification and broader Swiss/EU recruitment steps, limnits admission to managers, specialists and qualified workers, requires available B/L quotas, and says the permit can be time-limited and issued subject to conditions, including that renewal or prolongation depends on whether the objectives stated in the application are actually achieved. That last point is especially useful for renewal-risk content: even where a permit is granted, the canton may tie any later extension to the business case originally presented. 31

Ticino's official pages are briefer but still useful on process. They say that the application for a permit for temporary service activity subject to authorisation must be filed by the employer or independent worker, that the worker waits abroad for the authorisation where applicable, and that the service may not begin before the authorisation is received. That is not ICT-specific,but it is still relevant to any re-filed or rebuilt case: premature work commencement is a separate compliance risk and should noot be normalised in automated content. 32

### G.Case law notes

<!-- 7 -->

The most useful published case-law point for this topic is not an ICT merits case but an employer-characterisation case. In BGE 128 IV 170, the Federal Supreme Court treated the immigration-law employer concept broadly enough to catch the person who lets the foreign worker start work without first checking whether the worker is entitled to take that post. SEM's own guidance builds on that functional approach and explains that, in immigration law, the employer is the entity that employs the foreign national under its instructions, with its tools, or on its premises; in personnel leasing, the Swiss user undertaking may therefore be the factual employer. That is directly relevant to disguised local hire analysis. 33

For reconsideration and re-filing, the most useful case-law notes are the authorities cited by SEM itself. SEM explains that reconsideration cannot be used to keep challenging administrative decisions indefinitely or to circumvent appeal time limits, and that it cannot serve to cure facts or evidence that the party could and should have produced in the first procedure. The same guidance says reconsideration is generally obligatory only where there has been a substantial change of circumstances or where genuinely new important facts or evidence have emerged. That is the safest judicially anchored framework for analysing whether a refused ICT case should be appealed, reconsidered or rebuilt from scratch. 19

## Practical legal analysis

### H.Practical legal analysis

A weak corporate-link case usually fails because the law is group-specific but the evidence is not. Article 46 OASA only applies to transfers within "internationally active companies", and SEM's GATS guidance speaks in concrete corporate terms of a Swiss branch, subsidiary or office of the foreign undertaking. Zurich's checklist correspondingly requires the home company and host company to be identified expressly. Where the file shows only brand affinity, commercial cooperation, or vague shared ownership without a documented group structure, the authority has a straightforward route-characterisation objection. 34

A weak assignment purpose is another common vulnerability. SEM expressly says that the file must prove, through the key company-and employee-related facts, that the exception from labour-market priority is justified. Zurich asks for a specific statement of the company, task and assignee. Geneva requires a motivation letter explaining why a third-country provider is needed. In a genuine ICT case, the safest business rationale is therefore one that is concrete, time-bound and linked to knowledge that is genuinely embedded in the group or in the employee, rather than a generic nneed for extra hands in Switzerland. 35

A mismatch between the candidate and the claimed title is a high-risk issue. SEM describes Article 46 as a route for executives, highly qualified experts and indispensable specialists. Its GATS definitions focus on leadership of the undertaking or department, or indispensability based on advanced knowledge and experience. Zurich's third-country guidance likewise requires qualifications that match the proposed role and, in shortagje-occupation cases, usually at least vocational qualification or around five years' experience. A junior or broadly replaceable employee described as a "specialist" is therefore vulnerable even if the paperwork repeatedly uses that label. 36

The "disguised local hire" risk becomes acute where the Swiss entity looks like thereal employer in substance. Geneva's secondment definition requires retention of the original employment contract. SEM's project/service annex goes further and says that if a new temporary direct employment with the Swiss establishment is created, the case is generally treated as a change of purpose triggering a new

<!-- 8 -->

admission procedure, including Article 21 FNIA. Zurich's local-hire materials then show what that means in practice: labour-market testing, a signed Swiss employment contract, and local-hire scrutiny. For automated content, the safest wording is that a Swiss direct employment model is a route switch, not a mere ICT evidential weakness. 37

The "disguised service provision" risk is distinct. Where the Swiss facts show a concrete project for a client, a service contract, project-specific delivery and temporary project presence, Article 26 FNIA or Swiss posting-law rules may be the better legal lens. SEM's annex specifically warns against letting posted project staff undertake generic IT tasks that compete with local labour and treats a project switch as a fresh permit event. Content should therefore separate "group knowledge transfer into the Swiss affiliate" from "delivery of an external service to a recipient in Switzerland"; the latter is legally adjacent but not the same route. 38

The most serious characterisation problem is when the arrangement looks like labour supply. SEM says that, in a true contract-for-work case, the foreign employer remains responsible for the work result and keeps the worker under its own authority, while the Swiss site has only limited house rules. Once the Swiss customer or host directs the work itself, the foreign company no longer controls the result, the worker is integrated into the Swiss undertaking, and the arrangement is charged by hours worked,the indicators point towards personnel leasing. SEM's further warning that new entry of third-country nationals for personnel leasing is generally not allowed makes this a particularly strong refusal risk. 26

Salary problems should be analysed in two layers. The first is the Article 22 FNIA baseline: locally customary Swiss salary and employment conditions must be met. The second is package composition: under Article 22 paragraph 2 and SEM's assignment-confirmation model, travel, accommodation and meal compensation are not salary. Zurich and Geneva both insist on detailed pay and expense data, and Zurich also asks for an express undertaking to bear posting expenses for at least the first 12 months. Where the file is silent on expenses, or where allowances are rolled into gross salary without clarification, the case becomes harder to defend. 39

Duration and renewal require careful route-specific drafting. Article 46 OASA itself does not lay down a general ICT maximum stay, so content should not invent one. The clear federal duration rules identified in the reviewed materials are GATS-specific: at least one year of prior foreign-group employment and an initial three-year Swiss period, extendable to four. Beyond that, the practical constraints are quota availability, permit type, and what the authorities see as the actual temporary purpose of the stay. Geneva also states that renewal may be conditioned on the objectives originally announced by the employer. 40

Document inconsistency is not separately codified as a refusal ground, but official materials make its importance obvious.SEM's service-provider checklist asks for the project contract, project description, justification, implementation plan and an assignment confirmation containing role, place, dates, foreign pay, Swiss allowances, insurance, tax and benefits information. SEM's model confirmation of assignment asks for a similar breakdown. Zurich and Geneva require overlapping but not identical data points. A file in which the task description, salary split, duration, controlling entity and legal employer differ from document to document is therefore highly exposed. 41

Temporary nature and return planning should be handled carefully. The reviewed official sources are stronger on temporary structure than on any formal "return-plan" requirement. Geneva defines secondment by its temporary foreign mission character and continuation of the original contract. SEM's GATS definition likewise frames the transfer as temporary and rooted in prior foreign employment. The safest drafting point is therefore not that Swiss law always demands a formal written return plan, but that the file should demonstrate temporary assignment logic through continued home-country

<!-- 9 -->

employment, a defined end point, and a credible reason why the employee is being moved into Switzerland only for that period. 42

On appeal, reconsideration and re-filing, official material supports a tiered approach. A cantonal refusal is challenged within the cantonal system; an SEM decision goes to the Federal Administrative Court, generally within 30 days. Reconsideration is narrower: there is no formal deadline, but it is not a substitute for appeal and cannot be used to fix omissions from the first filing. For internal content, the safest conclusion is that a refused ICT file should be rebuilt rather than simply re-submitted where the first application failed because critical evidence was missing but could have been produced at the time. A genuinely fresh filing is safest where there is materially improved evidence, a corrected route characterisation, or a changed factual matrix. That final sentence is an inference from the official reconsideration rules, not an express statutory re-filing provision. 4

### I. Drafting cautions

Use "may be refused" or "is at risk of refusal", not "will be refused", unless the point is tied to a clear legal prohibition. Swiss official materials are largely requirement-based rather than refusal-formula based, and Article 30 derogation cases are discretionary. 43

Do not collapse ICT, local hire, posting and service provision into one narrative. The federal texts separate Article 18 FNIA local employment, Article 26 FNIA cross-border services, and Article 30paragraph 1 letter h plus Article 46 OASA intra-group transfer. SEM and cantonal guidance reinforce those distinctions. 44

Do not describe the Article 46 route as though labour-market priority is always irrelevant in the abstract. The safer formulation is that Article 46 omits the labour-priority requirement, but SEM expects the file to justify why the exception is properly engaged. 10

Do not state that a group label alone proves a corporate link. Safer wording is that the file should evidence the home company, Swiss host company, and their group relationship, preferably alongside proof of continuing home-country employment and assignment authority. 45

Do not treat "specialist" as a broad business adjective. Safer wording is that Swiss practice expects a specialist to be highly qualified or genuinely indispensable in light of the employee's knowledge and experience, and that junior or generic functions are harder to defend. 36

Do not say thata new Swiss contract is merely an ICT variant. The safer line is that a direct Swiss employment contract may shift the case into local-hire territory or trigger a new admission analysis. 13

Do not let automated content count reimbursements for travel, accommodation or meals as salary. The authorities and their forms separate those items from salary. 46

Do not promise that a re-filed case can simply correct omitted evidence. Official SEM reconsideration guidance cuts against that. Safer wording is that a renewed filing should be built on materially changed facts, genuinely new evidence, or corrected route classification. 19

Do not imply that Federal Supreme Court merits review is routinely available for ICT refusals. SEM's rights-of-appeal guidance notes that, in foreign-national matters, appeal to the Supreme Court is generally excluded for permits with no legal entitlement and for derogations from admission

conditions. That makes Article 30 derogation cases particularly poor candidates for optimnistic onward-appeal language. 47
