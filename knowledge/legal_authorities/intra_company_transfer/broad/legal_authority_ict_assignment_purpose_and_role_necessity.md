## Executive framing

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss law does nnot appear to operate a single, self-contained statutory "ICT permit" route for third-country nationals. Instead, intra-group transfers, postings and assignment-based deployments are assessed through the general third-country labour-admission framework in the LEI/AIG and OASA/ VZAE, together with more specific rules for staff exchange within international companies, cross-border service provision, and-where applicable-Switzerland's GATS commitments on intrafirm transferees and service suppliers. For internal drafting, that means "ICT" should be treated as a **fact** **pattern,** not as a standalone Swiss permit category.

Across those routes, the **assignment purpose** is legally important because it connects the application to the statutory admission criteria. Official federal guidance requires the applicant to show that admission serves Switzerland's overall economic interest, and expressly says that this interest must be **justified** **and** **evidenced.** SEM's dossier guidance then requires a written explanation of the necessity of the employment, a job description or duties profile, information about the company, and-where the case is project-based-documentary proof of the project, including the relevant contract, project description and implementation plan. 2

Swiss official materials accept business rationales such as implementation of new technology, specialised engineering projects, installation and commissioning of complex systems,training of operating staff, restructuring, know-how transfer and other extraordinary tasks for which the Swiss operation needs genuinely specialised expertise. Those examples show that authorities are not looking for abstract statements of convenience; they are looking for a **concrete** **Swiss** **business** **need,** tied to identifiable deliverables, deadlines and specialised knowledge.3

**The** **temporary** **logic** is route-dependent. It is strongest in project and cross-border service-provider cases: SEM guidance defines a project as time-limited and unique, says permit length should follow the actual project calendar, and makes clear that short-stay permits are purpose-specific and cannot be chained to disguise durable stay. GATS intrafirm transfers are also expressly temporary, with a three-year limit extendable to four years and a prior twelve-month employment requirement abroad. By contrast, the staff-exchange route in OASA art. 46 is not framed as purely temporary in SEM's guidance; SEM states that the simplified recruitment procedure may apply to recruitment for a fixed **or** **indefinite** period, provided the person is a senior executive, highly qualified worker or indispensable specialist in an internationally active enterprise or research institute. 4

<!-- 1 -->

The clearest legal risk is that the facts no longer describe a genuine assignment, secondment, service contract or intragroup specialist transfer, but instead look like **ordinary** **local employment** or even prohibited labour leasing. SEM's directives distinguish a genuine mandate from labour leasing by asking who directs the worker, who bears responsibility for the work result, whether the worker is organisationally integrated into the Swiss client, and whether billing is based simply on hours worked. For third-country nationals, SEM states that entryfor labour leasing is generally not permitted, subject only to narrow exceptional project cases. 5

The public cantonal materials reviewed show that cantons operationalise these principles by demanding concrete evidence. Zurich asks for a specific written justification, and for project-based cases requires the project contract and project description; Geneva requires a motivation letter stating the duration, place of performance and reasons why a third-country service provider is necessary, together with service and employment contracts and a secondment letter; Zug requires a written justification, recruitment evidence, and for project cases the project contract, short project description, implementation plan and description of the worker's tasks within the project. Public cantonal material is therefore consistent with a conservative drafting rule: **the** **more** **the** **case** **depends** **on** **assignment** **purpose, the more that purpose must be documented with operational detail.** 6

### B. Structural overview of assignment purpose and role necessity

For internal analysis, Swiss "ICT" cases are best separated into four legal patterns.

**Ordinary Swiss employment by a Swiss employer** sits under the general third-country work-admission rules. The employer must show overall economic interest, respect local wage and working conditions, and demonstrate that the person is a manager, specialist or otherwise qualified worker. Assignment purpose matters here because it is the factual explanation for why the role should be admitted at all. 7

**Cross-border service provision or secondment** is treated as a service-provider/ posted-worker pattern. Here the official materials focus strongly on the defined project or mandate, fixed duration, Swiss place of performance, and evidence that the foreign employer remains the true employer. This is the route where "temporary deployment logic" is at its clearest. 8

**Staff exchange within international companies under OASA art. 46** is **a** specific facilitation for senior executives, highly qualified workers and indispensable specialists. Its core legal feature is not simply temporariness, but the international-company context and the indispensability / seniority of the individual, together with an exception to the priority rule. 9

**GATS** **intrafirmn transfer** is narrower and expressly temporary. It requires temporary work in a Swiss branch, subsidiary or affiliate, prior employment abroad for at least the preceding year, and a stay limited to three years, extendable to four. 10

## D. Primary legal texts

The principal statutory framework is the **LEI/AIG** **(RS** **142.20)** and the **OASA/VZAE** **(RS** **142.201).** On SEM's official legal-bases page, these are identified as the core foreign-nationals texts governing admission to work, together with the DFJP ordinance on cases subject to federal approval. The same page also identifies the Posted Workers Act and Posted Workers Ordinance, the Employment Service Act on private placement and labour leasing, and relevant international law materials including GATS. 21

<!-- 5 -->

Within that framework, the provisions most relevant to assignment purpose and role necessity are, at minimum, the LEI rules on overall economic interest, priority, wages and working conditions, personal qualifications, service providers and purpose-specific permits, and theOASA rules on short-stay categories, staff exchange within international companies, purpose of stay, renewal / succession of short permits, and exceptional labour-leasing cases. The official SEM guidance repeatedly anchors its analysis in those provisions rather than in any self-contained "ICT chapter". 22

For assignment cases that involve a service contract or secondment, the **Posted** **Workers** Act / **Ordinance** and related SECO guidance are not the immigration gateway, but they are legally important because they determine the mandatory Swiss employment conditions that must be guaranteed to posted workers. For cases that drift toward staffing rather than mandate-based delivery, the **Employment** **Service** **Act** becomes critical because SEM's directives use it to police the line between genuine service provision and illicit labour leasing. 23

# Federal administrative guidance

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's general labour-market guidance for non-EU/EFTA nationals is the clearest federal statement that assignment purpose must be evidenced. It says admission is possible only where it serves Switzerland's overall economic interest, and that the petitioner must justify and document that interest. It then explains the usual priority, wage and qualification rules, and makes clear that a written employment contract is part of the authority review. 7

SEM's application-documents page translates that general framework into dossier mechanics. The page expressly requires **a justification of the necessity of the employment, a job description or** **statement** of **duties,** and company information. In project cases it additionally requires the **project** **contract or order confirmation, project description** and **implementation plan.** This is particularly important for internal content drafting, because it shnows that Swiss authorities expect the business rationale to be substantiated by underlying transaction or project documents, not only by narrative explanation. 13

SEM's directives add the most detailed route-specific analysis. Inthe project-workers section, SEM defines a project as time-limited and unique, with concrete tasks, deadlines, qualitative and quantitative objectives, a description and budget, and planned human-resource needs. The directives also give examples of "extraordinary tasks" that can justify foreign specialist deployment, including major restructuring, introduction of new production techniques,installation of complex IT systems and highly specialised engineering projects. 14

In the service-provider section, SEM explains that service providers from third countries cross the border to deliver a service in Switzerland for a fixed duration under a mandate or contract for work. Official examples include assembly and commissioning of technical installations, supervision of putting them into service, training of operating staff, software development and installation for transnational companies, and individual specialists needed on large projects because the Swiss entity lacks the know-how or could not recruit the expertise in Switzerland or the EU/EFTA area. These examples are the closest federal guidance to accepted business-rationale categories for assignment-based work permit cases. 24

<!-- 6 -->

SEM's directives also make two important points about **duration.** First, for project and service-provider cases, the permission granted should reflect the effective time needed by reference to the project calendar, and services should as far as possible be handled through short-stay authorisations, including the four-month / 120-day authorisation for short projects. Secondly, short-stay authorisations are tied to a specific purpose, and a change of job or project triggers a fresh authorisation requirement; the renewal rules are specifically designed to stop a sequence of short permits from replacing a proper residence-permit analysis. 25

The **staff-exchange** **route** under OASA art. 46 is narrower but highly important for true intra-group cases. SEM says it is a simplified recruitment procedure for senior executives, highly qualified workers and indispensable specialists in internationally active enterprises and research institutes, and that the priority principle does not apply for that category. However, the directives also say that the file must contain the information needed to justify the exception. In other words, even on the facilitated route, a bare assertion that the person is "important to the group" is not enough. 9

**For** **GATS** **intrafirm** **transfers,** SEM's directives are stricter on temporary character. They define intrafirm transfer as temporary work in a Swiss branch, subsidiary or affiliate by an employee of the foreign group company, require prior employment abroad for at least the preceding twelve months, andcap the stay at three years, extendable to four. This material is highly relevant where the application relies on treaty-based service-sector mobility rather than only domestic law. 10

SECO's materials are especially useful on the **detachment concept** and on labour standards. SECO defines posting as sending workers to another country for a determined period while the employment relationship with the foreign employer continues. SECO also states that posted workers must receive the mandatory Swiss wage and employment conditions, and that foreign employers must guarantee those conditions. For drafting purposes,this supports a clear distinction between a temporary assignment under the home employer's control and a disguised shift into ordinary Swiss staffing. 26

The most specific federalI circular located in the reviewed materials concerns IT **service** **postings** **from** **thirdI countries.** Although sector-specific, it is highly informative. It says that third-country IT deployments should be restricted to specialists whose project-specific expertise cannot be replaced by local labour; that project changes amount to a change of employment requiring renewed authorisation; that permit duration should follow the project calendar; that, in all detachment cases, the worker remains bound to the home-employer contract; that if there is a new direct fixed-term employment with the Swiss subsidiary, ordinary Swiss admission rules should in principleapply; and that IT staff may be posted only in the framework of service contracts, with labour leasing excluded. That is not a general ICT circular, but it is powerful official guidance on the distinction between real specialised deployment and disguised ordlinary local employment. 27

Case law appears in the official materials mainly as support for **general** principles, especially overall economic interest and the classification of activity as independent or employed, rather than as a separate body of ICT-specific assignment-purpose jurisprudence. SEM's directives cite Federal Administrative Court decisions when discussing what counts as durable positive economic impact and how to distinguish independent activity from employment, but the reviewed official materials did **not** surface a dense body of published ICT-specific case law on assignment-purpose evidence. That gap should bestated openly in internal content. 28

# Cantonal practice

## F. Cantonal practice notes

Public cantonal guidance is uneven, but where it is detailed it strongly reinforces the federal pattern that assignment purpose must be concretely documented.

**Zurich** is particularly useful. Its public guidance says that, for secondments, the worker remains subject to the employment contract in the home country and the deployment in Switzerland is limited in time and generally project-based. Zurich's detailed PDF then requires, for internal secondments, a specific written justification with information about the business, the task and the seconded employee. For external service cases it requires, as mandatory documents, a specific written justification with information about the business, the project and the seconded employee, plus the contract/works contract/ project contract. Zurich's secondment documents also require the home and host company, function during the secondment, task area, place of assignment, and start and duration of the secondment. 29

**Geneva** is even more explicit on the assignment-purpose narrative. For third-country detachments, Geneva requires the application to include the service contract, the employment contract,and a motivation letter stating the **duration, place of performance** and the **reasons demonstrating the** **necessity** of using a third-country service provider. It also requires a secondment letter signed by employer and employee, setting out the secondment conditions including base pay, secondment allowance, travel, accommodation, food, duration and weekly working time. Geneva also states that the detached worker or independent must reside in Switzerland during the mission. 30

**Zug** has a particularly strong public form for non-EU/EFTA work-permit applications. It asks for the function and title of the role, and for the description of requirements and main tasks by reference to the job description or project description. The supporting-documents section then requires a written justification, evidence of recruitment efforts, and-where the case is project-related-the project contract with the customer and a short project description including the implementation plan and the description of the worker's activity within the project. It alsorequires the signed employment contract, CV, qualifications and passport. 31

**Vaud** provides a detachment-conditions form rather than a fuller narrative checklist, but that form still shows the level of factual detail expectedd. It asks for the worker's function, date of engagement with the foreign company, gross foreign salary, posting-related salary adjustments, other salary elements, travel / accommoddation / meal costs, start and end dates, weekly hours and the exact place of work in Vaud. That is less directly about legal necessity, but it is useful evidence that cantons scrutinise the practical reality of the posting rather than accepting a generic secondment label. 32

**Ticino** does not appear, in the public materials reviewed, to publish a comparably detailed assignment-purpose permit checklist for these cases. However, the labour inspectorate's public page defines a posted worker as a worker sent to Switzerland **for** a **limited** **period** either to provide services under a contract with the recipient or to work in a branch or company belonging to the employer's group. That is labour-market guidance rather than permit guidance, but it supports the same concepts of limited duration and corporate / contractual linkage. 33

# Applied analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis

The official materials support a conservative analytical approach: in Swiss ICT-type cases, the **assignment purpose is not bbackground colour.** **It** is the fact pattern that allows the authority to classify the case under the right legal route and then test the statutory conditions. A weak or generic purpose statement therefore does not only make the application less persuasive; it may make it legally ambiguous. If the file does not clearly show whether the case is a Swiss employment case, a service-provider case, an intragroup staff-exchange case or a GATS intrafirm transfer, the authority may default to the stricter ordinary-employment analysis or may conclude that the case is insufficiently documented. 34

**The** **business** **rationale** that is most defensible in the official materials is one that is specific, time-anchored and operational. Strong formulations are those tied to implementation of a named project, a commissioning phase, a defined knowledge-transfer exercise, introduction of new systems or technologies, exceptional restructuring, specialised engineering, or training of Swiss staff on systems delivered by the foreign entity. Weak formulations are open-ended references to "support", "coordination", "general business development", "commercial assistance" or "various projects as needed", unless the underlying route is ordinary Swiss employment rather than assignment-based service work. 35

**On** **physical presence,** the Swiss federal materials do not articulate a universal freestanding "onsite necessity" test in those words. Even so, the official examples and cantonal forms point strongly in that direction. Assembly, commissioning supervision, instruction of operating staff, installation of complex software or technical systems, and delivery of specialised project tasks at a Swiss site are all federal examples of acceptable service rationales. Geneva's checklist then asks for the place of performance and reasons showing the necessity of using the third-country provider, while Zurich requires the place of assignment and project details. The safest drafting approach is therefore to explain why the work must be done in Switzerland rather than remotely, and why the Swiss host entity specifically needs this individual on site. That conclusion is an inference from the reviewed official materials, but it is a strong one. 36

**The relationship between role necessityand candidate profile** is also central. SEM and cantonal guidance require the foreign national to be a manager, specialist or otherwise qualified worker, and Zurich expressly says the professional qualifications must correspond to the activity to be performed. In practice, the role description and the CV / qualifications must fit together.A highly technical assignment purpose paired with a generic CV, or a senior-manager route paired with a candidate who lacks senior decision-making authority, will undermine the case. Likewise, a knowledge-transfer rationale should be backed by evidence that the individual actually possesses company-specific or project-specific know-how. 37

The **relationship between assignment purpose and corporate link** needs separate treatment. **A** corporate link can be legally decisive for the route-especially under art. 46 OASA and under GATS intrafirm-transfer rules-but it does not by itself prove necessity. Zurich's and Geneva's materials show this clearly: they ask not only who the home and host entities are, but also what the person will do, where, for how long, and why that deployment is needed. In other words, group membership helps classify the case, but the operational business need still has to be proven. 38

<!-- 9 -->

The strongest warning signs of **disguised** **ordinary** **local** **employment** are disclosed by the labour-leasing and short-stay rules. A case becomes vulnerable where the Swiss client or host directs daily work, the worker is integrated into the Swiss organisation like local staff, billing is essentially for hours rather than deliverables, the role is generic and open-ended, or the worker moves from project to project without fresh authorisation. The IT circular adds a further useful distinction: if what is really happening is a new direct employment with the Swiss subsidiary, then the ordinary Swiss admission rules should in principle apply. That is highly relevant for internal drafting because many weak "ICT" narratives collapse at precisely that point. 39

**The** **temporary** **logic** should also be handled carefully. For project and service-provider deployments, the official materials expect the date range to be justified by the project plan, and they contain anti-avoidance rules against repetitive short permits masking durable employment. GATS intrafirm transfers are also temporary by express rule. But art. 46 OASA is different: SEM's own guidance says the simplified recruitment route may be used for fixed or indefinite recruitment. Internal content should therefore avoid blanket statements such as "Swwiss ICTs must always be temporary". A safer formulation is that **temporary deployment logic is central to project and service routes, and often central in practice** **in intragroup cases, but not identical across all Swiss legal routes used for intragroup transfers.** 40

## H. Drafting cautions

Do **not** treat "assignment", "secondment", "posting", "project work", "staff exchange" and "intra-company transfer" as interchangeable labels. Swiss law assigns different consequences to a Swiss employment case, a cross-border service-provider case, an art. 46 staff-exchange case, a GATS intrafirm-transfer case, and an unlawful labour-leasing case. Internal content should separate those categories expressly. 41

Do **not** rely on corporate affiliation alone. The file should separately evidence the corporate link, the Swiss host / client, the exact role, the concrete deliverable, the reason Switzerland needs the role performed there, the expected duration, and the person's qualifications for that task.Zurich, Geneva and Zug all publicly demand that level of detail. 42

Do not use vague purpose language. Statements such as "supporting Swiss operations", "assisting the affiliate", "overseeing multiple functions" or "providing expertise as required" are risky unless immediately tied to a named project, implementation phase, system, restructuring exercise, training programme, or other defined operational task. Purpose-specific authorisations can require a fresh application if the project or role changes, so drafting should identify the project boundary from the start. 43

Do **not** present temporary deployment facts if the commercial reality is a new local role in Switzerland. The IT circular is especially clear that, where there is a new direct employment at the Swiss subsidiary, ordinary admission rules should in principle apply. More generally, if the Swiss entity directs the work and the arrangement resembles staffing rather than a deliverables-based mandate or true intragroup specialist transfer, the "ICT" framing may be legally counterproductive. 44

Do **not** omit the duration logic. Where the case is project-based, the assignment letter and supporting dossier should tie the reqquested period to milestones, commissioning windows, implementation phases,training dates, or other dated operational events. Open-ended periods are difficult to reconcile with SEM's project guidance and with the rules against using chains of short permits to avoid a proper residence-permit assessment. 45

# Open questions and limitations

Official Fedlex article text was not directly machine-readable through the browsing tool in this session. The article references in this pack are therefore grounded through official SEM / SECO pages and official SEM directives that identify and apply the relevant LEI/OASA provisions. 48

Detailed federal guidance on assignment-purpose evidence is **fragmented.** The most detailed general federal sources are SEM's application-documents page and directives, while the most concrete mission-document requirements appear in cantonal materials. The only highly specific federal circular located in the reviewed material was the 2009 IT-services circular. 49

Published,directly surfaced **ICT-specific** case law on assignment-purpose evidence appears limited in the reviewed official materials. SEM's directives cite case law, but mainly for broader questions of economic interest and classification rather than for a discrete ICT-purpose doctrine. Internal content should therefore avoid overstating the role of case law in this area and should lean primarily on legislation, ordinances, SEM directives and explicit cantonal documentary requirements. 50
