# Swiss legal authority pack on worker profiles in Swiss intra-company transfer work permit cases

**Suggested filename:**

legalauthorityictmanagerspecialistorkeypersonnelprofilepack.pdf

## A Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not appear, on the official materials reviewed, to create a single stand-alone EU-style "ICT permit" class. Instead, ICT-style cases are dealt with inside the ordinary third-country work-admission framework of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and its ordinance, especially the rules on economic interest, quotas, labour-market access, salary and working conditions, personal qualifications, and housing, together with the specific deroggation for operational transfers in international companies in Article 30(1)(h) FNIA and Article 46 OASA. A subset of cases is also shaped by Switzerland's WTO/GATS market-access commitments for intra-corporate transferees and "other essential persons". This is a structural reading of the official texts, not an express statutory formula. ①

For the **core** **Swiss** **ICT** **exception** **route,** Article 46 OASA is the key implementing provision. It is framed for the operational transfer of **members of senior management and indispensable specialists** in internationally active companies, and it still requires overall economic interest, an employer application, quota availability, compliance with Swiss salary and working conditions, and suitable housing. SEM's own directives explain that Article 46 OASA is intended to facilitate recruitment by internationally active companies and research institutes of executives, highly qualified experts, and indispensable specialists. On SEM's public overview page, management or specialist transfers in international companies are listed as an Article 30 FNIA exception, and the precedence rules are said not to apply to those operational transfers. 2

**The** **worker** **profile** remains central. Article 23 FNIA allows short-stay and residence permits for work only for **managers, specialists and other qualified workers,** and residence permits require an additional forward-looking assessment of sustainable integration based on professional qualification, professional and social adaptability, language skills and age. SEM's public guidance adds that qualifications are checked through a CV, education certificates and employment references, and that supplementary material about the training institution and course content can be helpful where foreign education systems differ materially from Switzerland's. 3

**Prior employment within the group is relevant, but not always for the same legal reason.** For GATS-defined intra-corporate transferees, the rule is clear: the transferee must have been employed by the overseas enterprise for **at least one year immediately before the application,** and the Swiss host must be a branch, subsidiary or affiliate in Switzerland. For the domestic Article 30(1)(h) FNIA / Article 46OASA route, the legislation reviewed does not itself set out a universal one-year prior-employment rule. The safer internal drafting position is therefore that prior group employment is a **clear** **requirement** **only where the case depends on the GATS ICT category,** but it is still highly probative evidence of genuine seniority, specialisation and group-specific know-how in domestic same-group transfer cases.

Swiss authorities do not treat group membership alone as enough. The official materials consistently tie admissibility to a genuine Swiss-side need: economic interest for the Swiss economy, a concrete assignment or project, compliance with Swiss pay and work conditions, and a profile that is genuinely managerial, specialist or otherwise highly qualified. SEM's project-worker guidance is especially clear that admissions should be confined to specialists with **specific** **project know-how** who cannot be replaced locally, and that authorities must prevent posted staff from carrying out merely general IT tasks. That guidance is a strong warning against presenting ordinary operational staff as "ICT specialists". 5

**Trainees and stagiaires are different routes, not lighter variants of the manager/specialist route.** Article 30(1)(g) FNIA covers international exchanges, training and continuing education; OASA Articles 41 and 42 regulate international exchange and stagiaire cases; and SEM's directives treat internships and stagiaire placements as structured training or exchange arrangements, not as substitutes for ordinary recruitment or executive/specialist transfers. Internal content should therefore keep trainee/ stagiaire profiles separate from manager, specialist, key personnel and essential employee profiles. 6

## B Structural overview of manager, specialist or key personnel profile

## D and E Primary legal texts and official guidance

The most important **primary** **legal texts** for the worker-profile issue are Articles 22, 23, 26 and 30 FNIA, together with Article 46 OASA. Article 22 imposes local salary and working-condition compliance and treats posting expenses separately from salary. Article 23 is the general personal-qualification filter. Article 26 applies the quota, salary and personal-requirement rules by analogy to temporary cross-border service provision. Article 30 allows derogations, including the facilitation of transfers of senior

<!-- 7 -->

executives and indispensable specialists in international companies. Article 46 OASA then operationalises that transfer route. 26

**For administrative** **guidance,** the SEM directives are the strongest source. They add three points of particular importance. First, Article 46 OASA isexpressly described as a facilitation mechanism for internationally active companies and research institutes to recruit executives, highly qualified experts and indispensable specialists. Secondly, the SEM directives set out the clearest Swiss official definitions of executives and highly qualified specialists in the GATS ICT context. Thirdly, the directives explain that, for GATS ICT cases, one year of prior employment in the foreign group company is required, market access is time-limited, and even then quotas, salary/working conditions and mobility restrictions still apply. 27

SEM's public pages are useful **procedural and** **evidential summaries,** but they are not the full legal documentation. The English "Non-EU/EFTA nationals" page states that the full documentation is in German, French or Italian, while still confirming the basic position that third-country workers must be highly qualified, that employers must prove the lack of suitable Swiss or EU/EFTA labour for the vacancy, and that terms of employment must respect local conditions. The English "Basis for admission" page adds helpful evidence points: CV, diplomas, references, and where necessary explanatory material on the foreign training system. 28

**The thinness of official federal guidance on documentary proof of "seniority" or "proprietary** **knowledge"** should be acknowledged. Swiss official materials are much stronger on the legal categories and on the required documents than on any exhaustive federal checklist of what proves "key personnel" status. The best official proxy for group-specific or project-specific know-how is SEM's project-worker annex, which limits admissions to specialists whose project know-how cannot realistically be replaced locally and warns against permitting general IT functions under a posting/ service-provider label. 29

**On** **case** **law,** accessible official and quasi-official material was relatively limited in this research, and the judgments found were not squarely ICT-specific. One accessible Federal Administrative Court judgment, C-2638/2010, shows the court upholding a refusal because recruitment efforts under Article 21 were insufficient; the court expressly noted that the worker might fit well in the practice, but that this was not decisive, and it therefore did not need finally to resolve Article 23 qualification. SEM also cites BVGer C-1123/2013, C-2638/2010 and C-2216/2010 in connection with individualised assessment under Article 23. The safe internal use of case law is therefore limited and should remain conservative. 30

## F Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** provides some of the clearest published practice material. For internal transfers it asks for a **specific** **statement** of **reasons** covering the business, the task and the posted worker; for transfers over four months it requires an assignment contract identifying the employee,home company, host company, function during the assignment, duties, place of work, duration, home-country base salary, Swiss posting allowance, expense coverage, insurance and social-security arrangements, and either Swiss-law working terms or a clause that Swiss law governs unregulated points. Zurich also asks for CVs, diplomas/certificates and identity documents, and says documents should be in German or English. Its longer explanatory note states that for internal temporary service provision the worker must still meet Article 23 qualification standards and that the role's qualifications must match the role to be performed. 31

<!-- 8 -->

Zurich's explanatory note is also useful because it states, for internal and external temporary postings under Article 26 FNIA, that the **domestic-priority** **requirement** **is** **waived** because the personnel stay in Switzerland only temporarily and then return to the home country. That is a cantonal practice statement, and it fits the structure of Article 26 FNIA, which cross-refers to Articles 20,22 and 23, but not Article 21. Internal drafting should nevertheless present this as a **practice-backed** **reading,** not as an unqualified nationwide rule unless the selected route really is a temporary service-provision/posting case. 32

**Geneva** publishes a more general third-country employment page, but it stilI contains two useful points for ICT work. First, it confirms that only **cadres, specialists** **and** **qualified** **workers** may be admitted. Secondly, in its "documents to attach" section it requires proof of Swiss/EU recruitment efforts **except** **for intra-group** **cadre** **transfers,** which is a strong cantonal practice indicator that genuine intra-group executive transfers are treated differently from ordinary third-country hires. Geneva also reserves the right to issue a different authorisation than the one requested and to make renewal or extension conditional on the achievement of the objectives stated in the application. 33

The practical significance is that **cantonal implementation** **matters,** but cantonal pages should not be allowed to displace the federal legal architecture. The safest internal position is that cantonal notes are highly valuable for **document design, evidential emphasis and local process,** whereas the underlying legal categories remain governed by FNIA, OASA, SEM guidance and, where relevant, GATS commitments. 34

## G and H Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

The **central Swiss question is not simply whether the worker belongs to the same corporate** **group,** but whether the worker's profile and the assignment purpose match a lawful route and whether the Swiss host's need is sufficiently specific. The strongest files are those which tie the employee's seniority or specialisation to a clearly described Swiss-side function: leading a Swiss department, implementing a group-specific technology, completing a defined specialist project, or carrying out a time-limited transfer that cannot credibly be staffed from the local market without losing essential know-how. Bare statements such as "trusted employee", "key person" or "important to the group" are too abstract on the materials reviewed. 35

**On** **prior** **employment** **within** **the** **group,** the internal drafting rule should be carefully split. If the case is being framed within the **GATS ICT** category, the one-year prior overseas employment requirement should be stated expressly and evidenced clearly. If the case is being framed under the **domestic** **operational-transfer route** in Article 30(1)(h) FNIA and Article 46 OASA, it is better to say that prior intra-group employment is not expressly prescribed in the domestic provision reviewed, but is strong evidence of genuine transfer status, enterprise-specific know-how, continuity of managerial authority, and the authenticity of the corporate link. This distinction matters because over-generalising the one-year rule would make internal content legally imprecise. 36

**On** **managerial authority,** the official definitions point to real leadership rather than version-control or team-coordination labels. A persuasive manager case should therefore show responsibility for a business unit or department, a high reporting line, and a role that is mainly one of direction rather than hands-on service delivery. On **specialist knowledge,** the better argument is not simply "experienced employee", but advanced knowledge that is essential to a defined service, system, technology, research set-up, or management method used by the group or the Swiss host. Where terms like "proprietary knowledge", "group-specific processes" or "knowledge transfer" are used, they should be linked back to those recognised legal ideas rather than used as free-floating rhetoric. 37

<!-- 9 -->

The official materials do not provide a single exhaustive evidence checklist for proving "essentiality", but they do show what the authorities actually expectto see. Expressly required items include a CV, diplomas and work certificates/references, a detailed employer explanation, and transfer-contract details covering the home and host companies, function, duties, place, duration, salary, allowances, expenses, insurance and social-security arrangements. As a matter of conservative drafting, the content system should also anticipate the usefulness of a group-structure chart, a reporting-line chart, and a short explanation of why the Swiss task depends on this worker rather than a more generic role. Those last items are practical inferences from the published official criteria rather than express statutory requirements. 23

**Salary** is **relevant, but** it is **not** **magic.** A high or Swiss-market salary can support the credibility of a senior or highly specialised profile, especially where it matches the asserted level of responsibility and expertise. But a high salary does not turn an ordinary operational worker into an indispensable specialist. Conversely, even a strong profile can fail if local salary and working conditions are not met. Swiss law also requires a clear separation between salary and posting expenses; the employer must cover posting-related travel, accommodation and subsistence expenses in addition to the salary, subject to the long-term posting rules in OASA Article 22a. 38

**Training and knowledge-transfer cases require route discipline.** If the real purpose is training, exchange or structured professional development, the file should not be drafted as an executive/ specialist ICT transfer merely because the worker belongs to the group. Swiss law already has separate training/exchange tooIs under Article 30(1)(g) FNIA and OASA Articles 41-42. Equally, if the real purpose is to fill an ordinary operational gap in Switzerland, using an ICT or "key personnel" label is legally risky. The internal content system should therefore force a route choice: local hire,temporary service provision/posting, operational same-group transfer, or trainee/stagiaire. 39

### Drafting cautions for future ICT profile content

·Do not say or imply that being employed somewhere in the corporate group is enough on its own. The profile, the Swiss need, and the route conditions must still be shown. 40

·Do **not** universalise the **one-year** **prior** **employment** rule. It is clear for GATS ICT admissions, but not expressly stated as a universal domestic Article 46 condition in the materials reviewed. 4

·Do **not** use "key personnel", "essential employee" or "senior employee" as if they were self-standing Swiss legal categories. Tie each phrase back to **manager, specialist, other** **essential** **person or trainee/stagiaire.** 41

·Do **not** assume that labour-market priority is always waived. It is clearly waived for the Article 30/46 operational-transfer exception in SEM's public guidance, and Zurich says it is waived for temporary posting/service cases, but ordinary third-country hiring remains subject to the priority regime. 42

·Do **not** present routine or interchangeable operational work as specialist work. SEM's project-worker guidance is specifically designed to stop that, especially in IT/project settings. 24

·Do **not** let "knowledge transfer" become a vague slogan. Describe wvhat knowledge is being transferred, why it is group- or project-specific, why it matters in Switzerland, and how long the need lasts. The sources support precise, assignment-linked explanations, not general assertions. 43

·Do **not** use trainee or stagiaire wording for a role that is actually productive headcount. The training and exchange routes are separate and time-limited. 15

·Do **not** treat salary as a substitute for proof of seniority or specialisation. Salary is a compliance and credibility factor, not a replacement for profile evidence. 44

## I Source list

### Primary legal texts and official Swiss administrative sources used in this pack

The core federal legal sources were the consolidated texts of the **Federal Act onForeign** **Nationals** **and Integration** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment,** including the provisions corresponding to Articles 22, 23, 26 and 30 FNIA and Articles 22, 22a, 41, 42 and 46 OASA. In the materials accessible for this research, those consolidated texts were reached through downloadable PDF exports carrying fedlex source filenames. 45

The most important federal guidance sources were the **SEM** **directives** **on** **residence** with **employment,** the **SEM public pages on non-EU/EFTA employment-market access,** and the **SEM** **annex on project workers and service providers.** These sources were especially important for the functional content of manager/specialist profiles, GATSICT conditions, qualification evidence, and the warning against using posted project staff for generic tasks. 46

For international commitments, the pack relied on an official **SECO** **schedule** **of Swiss** **specific** **commitments** that sets out the Swiss treatment of intra-corporate transferees and other essential persons, including the one-year prior-employment requirement, the executive/specialist definitions, and the shorter-term essential-person categories. 47

For cantonal implementation, the pack relied principally on the **Canton** **of Zurich** practice notes and checklists on internal transfers and third-country employment, and the **Canton** **of** **Geneva** page on salaried employment of third-country nationals. Those materials were used only as cantonal practice notes and evidential/process guidance, not as substitutes for federal law. 48

For case law, the research located one accessible **Federal Administrative** **Court** judgment, **C-2638/2010,** and the SEM directives' references to **C-1123/2013, C-2638/2010 and C-2216/2010.** Because directly accessible ICT-specific case law proved limited in the material found, case-law use in this pack remains restrained. 30

**Open** **questions** **and** **limitations.** The official federal materials are strong on the legal categories and baseline evidence, but relatively thin on an exhaustive federal evidential test for "internal seniority", "proprietary knowledge" or "essential employee" status outside the GATS definitions and the project-worker annex. Accessible case law found in this research was limited and not squarely ICT-specific. Finally, SEM's English pages are expressly not the complete documentation; the full materials remain in German, French and Italian. Internal content shoould therefore keep a conservative tone and avoid over-claiming beyond what the official texts clearly support. 49
