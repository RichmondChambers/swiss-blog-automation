# Swiss Trainee and Stagiaire Legal Authority Pack

# Executive legal summary

A.Switzerland does recognise a genuine trainee or stagiaire work-authorisation route, but it is narrow, temporary and strongly purpose-bound. The clearest route is the SEM "young professionals" channel under Article 42 OASA for nationals of specified treaty states. SEMA's current public material lists Argentina, Australia, Canada, Chile, Indonesia, Japan, Monaco, New Zealand, the Philippines, Russia, San Marino, South Africa, Tunisia, Ukraine and the United States; the same material states that EU/EFTA nationals no longer need a permit to work in Switzerland because of free movement. The route is framed as occupational and linguistic further development, not as a general graduate-employment scheme. 1

The decisive legal question is substance, not label. OOfficial SEM guidance on international exchanges states that an exchange can only be authorised under Article 41 OASA where the exchange is the main purpose of the stay; persons who primarily want to work cannot come to Switzerland under that heading. For treaty stagiaires, the placement must be in the profession already learned, the employment contract must contain an integrated training programme, and the SEM standard contract requires learning content and a named person responsible for the training period. A host that is really filling an ordinary operational vacancy, with weak learning content or no credible supervision,therefore runs a material risk that the case will be treated as ordinary employment rather than genuine training. That last point is an inference from the official rule-set, but it is a strong one. 2

Pay is not relaxed merely because the worker is called a trainee. SEM's treaty instructions require remuneration at the going local and sectoral rate, as for a job starter; where a collective agreement exists it must be followed, and otherwise cantonal wage guidance and professional-association recommendations apply. SEM's broader labour-market guidance repeats that foreign workers must receive local, professional and sectoral salary and employment conditions, and SECO's collective-agreement and normal-employment-contract materials remain relevant reference points when testing whether a proposed package is defensible. 3

The route is also tightly limited in duration and flexibility.Treaty young-professional permits are granted for a maximum of 18 months; part-time work, self-employment and staff leasing are not permitted; more than one trainee may not exceed 5% of the firm's workforce; and a change of employer requires SEM apoproval, while a change of profession is not possible because the training must stay within the learned profession. Renewal is exceptional in the wider trainee/internship guidance, and repeated or analogous internships are expressly disfavoured. 4

For related trainee-type categories, Swiss law distinguishes at least five different situations: treaty young professionals under Article 42 OASA; broader training / exchange arrangements under Article 30(1)(g) FNIA and Article 41 OASA; compulsory internships linked to foreign or Swiss studies; youth-exchange programmes run on a reciprocal basis by recognised organisations; and intra-group knowledge-transfer or staff-exchange arrangements within international companies. These categories have different legal tests and should not be collapsed into one "trainee" concept. 5

A final structural point is important for drafting: for non-EU/EFTA nationals, ordinary work in Switzerland is governed by the normal third-country admission framework of overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, salary and working conditions, and personal qualifications. The trainee route is an exception, not a way around those rules. If the facts do not fit the training or exchange framework, authorities can fall back on the ordinary employmnent test, which is much stricter and is usually limited to highly qualified workers. 6

## Structural overview of the trainee and stagiaire route

B. The clearest Swiss "trainee / stagiaire" authorisation is the SEM young-professionals route under Article 42 OASA. It is treaty-based, nationality-limited, age-limited, tied to completed vocational or academic training, and intended to deepen occupational and linguistic skills in Switzerland. It is not open-ended employment. The role must be in the profession or discipline in which the person has trained, and the host contract must embed a concrete training programme. 7

Alongside that treaty route, SEM guidance recognises broader training-related stays under Article 30(1) (g) FNIA and Article 41 OASA. These include internships before, during or after studies; training stays supported through international organisations; and reciprocal youth-exchange programmes. Here too, the exchange or training purpose must be the principal purpose of the stay, reciprocity matters, and the stays are temporary. The official materials become more fragmented outside Article 42 OASA: the detail is mostly in the SEM directives and selected cantonal pages rather than in one consolidated public guide. 8

A compulsory internship during studies is treated differently from a post-study trainee post. For students enrolled abroad, SEM guidance and cantonal guidance allow a compulsory internship in Switzerland for up to 12 months, generally once per study cycle, with proof from the university that the placement is mandatory and integral to the course. For students enrolled in Switzerland,compulsory internships and diploma-thesis placements fall under Article 39 OASA and the gainful work must not exceed half of the total duration of studies. 9

An intra-group internship or staff-exchange arrangement is again distinct. SEM guidance treats transfers within international companies under Article 46 OASA as a separate exception focused on managers, highly qualified workers and indispensable specialists whose transfer within a transnational group is required. The post-study internship example in the SEM directives also recognises intra-group training with a view to later work abroad inside the same group or for a major client of the group. That is not the same as a local trainee vacancy with a Swiss host company. 10

EU/EFTA nationals should be analysed separately. SEM's current public material says that they no longer need a permit to work in Switzerland because of free movement, and the SEM directives add that the old EU/EFTA stagiaire agreements are practically unused. For short-term cross-border service provision, the correct framework is the EU/EFTA notification procedure for posted workers, self-employed service providers, or short-term emnployment with a Swiss employer, not Article 42 OASA. 11

### Primary legal texts and official materials

D. **Primary** **legal texts.** The legal base is the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, SR 142.20,and the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, SR 142.201. SEM's legal-bases pages identify those two instruments as the core federal law and ordinance; SEM's labour-market pages and directives then tie the trainee / stagiaire analysis to specific provisions including Articles 18, 20, 21, 21a, 22,23,24, 30 and 45 FNIA, and Articles 19, 39,41,42,46,56 and 57 OASA. Public treaty material on SEM's programmes de stagiaires page identifies the bilateral agreements by state and gives the treaty references and entry-into-force dates, including the new US agreement in force since 30 November 2024.27

**E. Official guidance and administrative materials.** The most operationally useful federal materials are: the SEM young-professionals page and country instructions; SEM Directives I, chapter 4 "Séjour avec activité lucrative" / "Aufenthalt mit Erwerbstätigkeit"; the SEM application form; the SEM standard employment agreement; SEM's workplace-rights sheet; and the SEM notification-procedure guide for EU/EFTA short-term work and posted service provision. Together they supply the governing administrative framework, documentary expectations, and the clearest official indicators of what Switzerland regards as genuine training rather than ordinary employment. 28

F. **Cantonal practice** **notes.** Public cantonal practice is uneven, but Geneva, Zurich, Basel-Stadt and Lucerne all puublish useful official material. Zurich gives clear English-language rules for student internships and post-study traineeships; Geneva publishes route-specific pages for study internships and ordinary third-country employment, including evidence requirements and OCIRT pre-approval; Basel-Stadt states that third-country internships are only possible for intra-group or compulsory university placements and directs treaty stagiaires to SEM; Lucerne emphasises that there is no right to a particular permit and no automatic right to extension. Theseare practice materials, not substitutes for federal law, but they are valuable for internal drafting and file-preparation standards. 29

### Practical legal analysis

G. For internal-content purposes, the cleanest "Swiss trainee route" is the bilateral young-professionals model, and it should be described as such. It is available only where a current treaty covers the person's nationality; the applicant must fall within the relevant age band; they must have completed vocational

<!-- 7 -->

or academic training; and the Swiss role must sit in the samelearned profession or studied discipline. The route is expressly aimed at furthering professional and linguistic knowledge. It is therefore best characterised as a temporary professional-development exchange authorisation rather than a graduate-employment route. 30

The evidence burden should be drafted around training purpose. At minimum, the official materials support asking for: the SEM application form; the signed employment contract; an integrated training programme; copies of the diploma or degree; a current CV; work references where available;and passport identification. The standard SEM contract further indicates what good evidence looks like: job title, duration, gross salary, working time, holidays, insurance, travel costs, the programme of training, the linguistic and professional learning content, and the named person responsible. For Canadian and US students, and for certain US apprentices, SEM requires additional study or apprenticeship evidence. 31

A strong internal test for genuineness is whether the file shows a structured learning design that can be explained independently of the host's operational staffing needs. Official sources do not use the phrase "disguised local hire", but they do insist on the learned-profession match, a training programme in the contract, a named responsible person, proper wages, and a cap on trainee numbers within the workforce. Geneva's ordinary third-country employment page also asks employers for a motivation letter, evidence of labour-market search, and company staffing data when the case is treated as normal employment. The practical inference is that where the facts look like a normal vacancy dressed up as training, authoorities may reject the trainee framing and apply the ordinary work-permit rules instead. 32

Low pay and weak conditions are obvious refusal or compliance risks. Federal material is consistent that foreign workers, including trainees and interns, must receive local and sectoral conditions. SEM's treaty instructions peg pay to the going local and sectoral rate for a job starter; the model contract says conditions must match those of comparable domestic employees and requires the employer to address holiday, illness and accident insurance, and any supplementary benefits. For wage benchmarking, official reference points include extended collective agreements, cantonal normal employment contracts with binding minimum wages, and the Federal Statistical Office's Salarium calculator, though the latter is informational and not a legal safe harbour. 33

The route also narrows where the host wants flexibility that the regime does not tolerate. For treaty young professionals, part-time work is not permitted; self-employment is not permitted; staff leasing is generally prohibited; and the person cannot switch into a different profession. Those are strong indicators that the route is intended for supervised, structured, field-specific development rather than open labour-market participation. As a result, content should not suggest that a trainee can simply move around the Swiss market, add side work, or use the permit as a general foothold in Switzerland. 34

Study-linked internships require especially careful route separation. A compulsory internship during foreign studies is potentially available for up to 12 months, once per study cycle, but only where the university confirms that the placement is compulsory and integral to the academic programme. A Swiss-study compulsory internship is different again and falls under Article 39 OASA. Before-study internships are, in principle, expected to occur abroad, with only narrowexceptions where the internship cannot be completed in the home country and is supervised by the educational institution. These distinctions matter because many "trainee" narratives actually belong in the student-permit framewvork, not in the bilateral stagiaire framnework. 9

For indefinite residence planning, the official materials are nnotably unsupportive. SEM frames the relevant stays as temporary and focused on training or exchange; treaty young-professional permits top out at 18 months; continuing-trainingJ renewals are rare; repeated analogous internships do not justify renewal; and a new ordinary work authorisation after the training period would have to stand on its own legal basis. For non-EU/EFTA nationals, that normally means satisfying the ordinary third-country admission test, which is intentionally restrictive and geared to highly qualified workers. Swiss-university graduates may have separate post-study employment and six-month job-search options, but those are distinct from the trainee route and should not be blended into it. 35

A local-hire versus posting distinction should also be kept sharp. SEM's directives define employment "with taking up employment" as work for a Swiss-based employer or a Swiss establishment of a foreign company. By contrast, cross-border services for an employer abroad fall within work "without taking up employment", with separate permit or notification rules. For EU/EFTA posted workers and short-term EU/EFTA hires, the notification guide is the correct authority; for young professionals working for a Swiss host employer, the case is not a posting case. Misstating that distinction creates real drafting risk, especially for multinational groups trying to choose between trainee, service-provider and intra-company-transfer language. 36

### Drafting cautions

H. Internal content should say that the Swiss trainee or stagiaire route is available only where the facts fit a legally recognised training or exchange category, and that the title "trainee", "intern", "volunteer" or "stagiaire" is never determinative by itself. It should not imply that low or symbolic pay is acceptable, that the route is a mainstream graduate path for all nationalities, or that it supports long-term residence planning as such. It should distinguish carefully between treaty young professionals, compulsory study-related internships, youth exchanges, intra-group knowledge-transfer placements, ordinary local hire, and posted-worker situations. It should also avoid suggesting that unpaid work escapes immigration control: SEM's own guidance treats unpaid work as capable of being gainful activity, and cantonal Geneva practice expressly applies work-authorisation rules to trainees, apprentices and volunteers. 37

Where the available official materials are canton-specific or practice-oriented, content should say so expressly. That is especially important for non-treaty internships, salary assessment methods, and operational filing expectations. The safest drafting approach is to present the bilateral young-professionals route as a discrete, treaty-based exception and to present all other "trainee-type" cases as fact-sensitive variants that may instead need to satisfy the ordinary third-country work-permit regime. 38
