## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does recognise trainee and stagiaire routes, but only as tightly defined exceptions or route-specific categories. The two most important structures for this topic are, first, the treaty-based **Young** **Professionals** **/ jeunes** **professionnels** route under the trainee exchange agreements and Article 42OASA, and, secondly, other **training** **or** **exchange** admissions under Article 30 FNIA together with Articles 39 and 41 OASA. On the official SEM materials, these routes are training-led, temporary, and evidence-heavy. They are not a general permission to place a foreign national into a low-paid "intern" role. 1

For the **Young** **Professionals** route, the official SEM position is clear: the applicant must normally be a national of a partner state, within the agreement age limits, and must already have completed qualifying training; the work must be in the learned profession or field of study; part-time work and self-employment are not authorised; remuneration must follow local and sectoral standards; the stay is temporary and capped at 18 months; and the written employment contract must include a training programme. The SEM instructions also add two especially important anti-abuse features: service leasing is, in principle, not permitted, and where several young professionals are engaged, they may not exceed 5% oftheworkforce. 2

**For** **non-treaty** **or study-linked internships,** the official materials are even more explicit that training must be the true purpose of the stay. SEM's directives state that an exchange under Article 41 OASA is permissible only where the exchange itself is the main purpose of the stay, with reciprocity and temporary departure playing an essential role; persons who primarily wish to work cannot use that route. Likewise, a mandatory internship under Article 39 OASA is available only if training remains the principal purpose, the programme is recognised, the internship is compulsory under the curriculum, and the internship does not exceed half of the total training period. Pre-study internships are, as a rule, to be done abroad. 3

The practical refusal problem, therefore, is usually not a free-standing legal doctrine called "disguised employment"; it is that the facts no longer fit the route. The risk becomes acute where the role looks like an ordinary vacancy, productive work visibly outweighs structured training, the training programme is generic or missing, the host cannot identify a responsible supervisor, salary is below local or sectoral norms, the candidate's profile does not match the stated training purpose, or the proposed duration and extension history are inconsistent with a temporary training stay. In those circumstances,the application may be refused, not approved, or treated as requiring the ordinary third-country work-permit criteria instead. 4

Swiss compliance consequences also matter. SEM states that **any gainful activity,whether** **paid** **or** **unpaid, requires** **authorisation,** and its directives add that the employer must verify that the foreign worker has the necessary permission. Swiss foreign-national law uses a factual concept of "employer":a host that directs the work, provides the tools, or uses its own premises may be treated as the employer even without a written employment contract. The worker cannot lawfully start before the relevant permit or assurance of authorisation has been issued, and SEM expressly points to the sanction provisions in Articles 115-120 and 122 FNIA together with the federal law combating undeclared work. 5

## Structural overview of refusal and disguised employment risks

The first risk question is always **route** fit. Swiss official sources separate treaty-based young professionals, exchange programmes, compulsory internships during recognised studies, development-project trainees, and narrow sectoral trainee regimes such as hospitality. A refusal risk arises immediately if the facts are pressed into the wrong box: for example, where a post-study case is presented as a "trainee" but the candidate is not from a partner state, has no completed training for treaty purposes, or is not doing work in the learned profession or field. Equally, a compulsory-study internship route fails if the internship is not an integral, mandatory part of the curriculum. 6

The second risk question is **whether the training purpose is evidenced with enough precision.** Swiss officiaI materials repeatedly require more than a job title. The SEM instructions require a written contract including the nature of the work and a programme of further training, such as duties, work plan and courses. The SEM standard contract asksfor detailed linguistic and professional learning content and the person responsible for the training. The application form asks for education, work experience and languages, and the supporting documents must include the contract with training programme, diplomas, CV and other evidence. Where the programmme is vague, supervision is anonymous or the learning outcomes are formulaic, the route loses credibility. That is not always an express statutory refusal ground, but it is a serious evidential and administrative weakness. 7

The third risk question is **whether the role stilI looks like training rather than ordinary labour.** Swiss official sources express this in several different ways. For Article 41 exchanges, the exchange must be the main purpose of the stay and not a disguised work stay. For Article 39 internships, training must remain the principal purpose. In development-project agriculture, the directives expressly say trainees may not be engaged in place of workers. In hospitality, the directives exclude general duties that have no direct link to the candidate's course or sector, such as unrelated administrative work or auxiliary functions. Read together, these sources support a conservative administrative inference: if the productive labour element dominates and the structured learning element recedes, the trainee label will not save the case. 8

The fourth risk question is **salary** **and** **working** **conditions.** Article 22 FNIA remains central. SEMM's general directives say the purpose of that rule is both to protect foreign workers against abusive conditions and to prevent cheaper foreign labour undercutting the domestic workforce. For young professionals, SEM states that remuneration must follow local and sectoral practice and should at least reach the level of newly entering professionals. The authorities expect a written and binding employment contract, reviewed before authorisation, setting out the core employment terms. Low stipend language, trainee branding, or offers of "experience" do not displace this wage-control logic. 9

The fifth risk question is **temporariness** **and** **sequencing.** The official sources are strict about duration. Young Professionals are capped at 18 months in total, with extensions needing justification and job changes granted only exceptionally. Foreign students at universities abroad may normally do only one compulsory internship per cycle, for up to 12 months. Youth-exchange internships are generally limited to 12 months. Hospitality trainee stays are in principle limited to 12 months. These time limits matter

because repeated placements, successive "trainee" appointments for the same host, or a planned seamless move into ordinary staffing can make the original training rationale look doubtful. Official sources do not say that any later ordinary employment is unlawful, but they do insist that the trainee route itself remain genuinely temporary and training-led. 10

The sixth risk question is **compliance** **posture.** Geneva expressly states that the permit request must be lodged before the internship, including where the internship is unpaid, and the student must wait for authorisation before starting. SEM's general materials say any gainful activity, paid or unpaid, requires a permit, and the worker may not begin before authorisation is issued. Where a host starts the placement first and attempts to regularise later, the problem is no longer merely evidential; it becomes a potential unauthorised-work case. 11

### Primary legal texts and official administrative materials

This section combines the primary legal texts and the main official administrative materials.

The formal federal framework is the **Foreign Nationals and Integration Act** and the **Ordinance on** **Admission, Residence** **and** **Employment,** which the SEM's legal-basis pages identify as the governing federal texts for admission and work. Within that framework, the route-specific provisions most relevant here are the ordinary employment provisions in Articles 18-23 FNIA; the exception and trainee-related provisions in Article 30 FNIA, Article 32 FNIA, and Articles 37-42, 55, 56 and 85 OASA; and the compliance provisions in Article 91 and the sanction provisions in Articles 115-120 and 122 FNIA. SEM's Chapter 4 directives then explain how those provisions are applied in practice. 38

<!-- 8 -->

**For Young** **Professionals,** the most operational federal materials are SEM's public page, the detailed instructions, the standard employment contract, and the official application form.Taken together, they supply the current partner-state list, the central eligibility criteria, the salary rule, the 18-month time limit, the local/sectoral pay requirement, the profession/field match, the prohibition on part-time work and self-employment, the requirement for a training programme in the contract, the need to identify the responsible person, the list of mandatory enclosures, and the rule that the candidate should apply through the competent authority in the home state and wait for approval before entering and working. 39

**For** **other** **trainee** **and** **exchange** **routes,** the decisive federal material is the SEM directives. They explain that Article 41 OASA exchanges are available only where exchange is the principal purpose; that Article 39 OASA mandatory internships require a recognised full-time study programme and a compulsory internship making up no more than half of the total course; that pre-study internships are in principle to be undertaken abroad; and that several specialist sub-routes, including youth exchange, agriculture and hospitality, carry additional documentation and anti-abuse restrictions. Those directives are particularly useful because they show where the official Swiss position is a **clear** **rule** and where it is **a practice-based screening mechanism.** 40

The main official materials on **salary** **and** **working** **conditions** are SEM's Article 22 directives and SECO's wage resources. SEM states that Article 22 aims both to protect foreign workers against abusive conditions and to prevent undercutting of domestic labour, and that foreign workers must receive the same local and profession-standard conditions as comparable local workers. SEM also points authorities to current statistical wage sources, expressly including the federal statistical wage tools and the SECO national wage calculator. SECO describes that calculator as a tool for estimating monthly gross wages for a defined individual profile, while also warning that it is not itself a wage recommendation. 41

Finally, the federal materials also matter for **illegal-work and misclassification consequences.** SEM states on its labour-market basis page that every gainful activity, paid or unpaid, requires a permit. Its directives add that the employer must verify authorisation, that the Swiss foreign-national law concept of employer is factual rather than purely contractual, and that a host enterprise may be treated as the de facto employer where it directs the work. In a disguised-employment scenario, those rules matter because the legal risk attaches to the reality of the relationship, not simply to the label "intern" or "stagiaire". 42

## Cantonal practice notes and practical legal analysis

This section combines the cantonal practice notes and the practical legal analysis. The main point is that Swiss trainee files are partly federal and partly cantonal: the cantonal labour-market or migration authority screens the case first, and depending on the route there may then be SEM approval or SEM-central handling. That structure means that official cantonal guidance can sharpen how refusal risk is assessed in practice, even where the governing rule is federal. 43

**Zurich** is useful because it states the study-linked internship rules in a relatively operational way. For third-country students studying abroad, Zurich says compulsory internships in Switzerland are possible only in defined cases, only once, and only up to 12 months; the host company must stay within the quota on short-term foreign hires; local, professional and sectoral wage and working conditionsmust be observed; and the file must containn a letter from the foreign university confirming enrolment and that the internship is compulsory. For students enrolled in Switzerland, Zurich also requires an **Ausbildungsprogramm** and insists that the work may not exceed half of the total training duration. Zurich's separate third-country ordinary-employment guidance is equally useful on the contrast point: if the file is really ordinary employment, the employer bears the burden on economic interest, priority, Article 22 wage conditions and Article 23 personal qualifications. 44

<!-- 9 -->

**Geneva** is especially clear on timing and on unpaid placements. Its official page says that a study-linked internship must form an integral part ofthe course, must be shorter than half of the total programme, and must be applied for whether it is paid or unpaid. The application must be filed **before** the internship, and the student must wait for work authorisation before starting. For refusal-risk drafting, that is valuable because it confirms that "unpaid" does not move the case outside the work-authorisation regime, and because it shows how quickly an evidential problem can become an unauthorised-work problem if the host starts the placement early. 45

**Basel-Stadt** publishes a notably restrictive practice note for third-country internships: it states that work permits are available only for **intra-group or university compulsory internships,** and that the application proceeds through IAESTE or AIESEC. Basel also links employers of third-country nationals to the general SEM labour-market-admission criteria and to the federal salary tools. That is not a federal rule that only those two internship types are ever lawful in Switzerland, butit is an important cantonal practice signal showing that cantons may screen very conservatively where they perceive ordinary-employment risk. 46

**Lucerne** offers a useful reminder on the status of the file once it leaves the trainee rhetoric and re-enters ordinary permit logic: there is no legal entitlement to a particular permit, and no entitlement to extension; any extension must be justified. In a trainee context, that reinforces the risk attached to long or rolling placements. 47

On the practical analysis, the safest way to describe **disguised** **ordinary** **employment** **risk** is this: Swiss official sources do not formulate a universal statutory test in those words, but they do impose route-specific requirements that are incompatible with an ordinary vacancy being masked as "training".The strongest official anchors are that the training or exchange must be the main purpose of the stay, the programme must be sufficiently described, the work must match the learned profession or mandatory curriculum, salary must satisfy Article 22, temporariness must remain credible, and sector-specific rules may forbid replacing workers or usingJunrelated auxiliary tasks. When those anchors are missing, the file becomes vulnerable either to outright refusal or to an ordinary-permit analysis that it may not survive. 48

That framework helps with the specific risk factors the content system needs to surface. A **weak** **or** **generic** **training** **plan** is risky because the officialcontract and instructions ask for the actual occupation, work plan, courses, learning content and responsible person. **Unclear supervision** is risky because the standard form asks who is responsible and because several routes are premised on educational follow-up or host acceptance. **Low pay** is risky because Article 22 continues to apply and SEM expects at least local and sectoral rates, often explicitly at entry level. **Mismatch** **with** **the** **candidate's** **career** **path** is risky because the route often requires work in the learned profession or mandatory curricular integration. **Long** **duration, extensions** **or** **repeated** **stays** are risky because the official materials repeatedly cap duration and treat extensions as justified exceptions rather than routine progression. 49

The issue of **overqualification** requires careful handling. None of the official sources reviewed here says that a candidate can be refused simply because they are "too qualified". The more accurate official analysis is narrower: if the person has training andexperience at a level that makes the proposed Swiss placement look like ordinary autonomous work rather than authentic career development or mandatory study-linked learning, the stated training purpose may cease to be credible. That is therefore a **fact-sensitive** **practice** **point,** not a codified ground of refusal. By contrast,

<!-- 10 -->

**underqualification** is sometimes a clearer route problem: for example, where the Young Professionals route requires completed training, or where a study-linked internship must truly be part of an existing recognised course. 50

The issue of **using the trainee route to avoid labour-market or wage scrutiny** also needs precise wording. For treaty-based Young Professionals, SEM expressly says the route operates independently of the labour-market situation. But that does **not** remove Article 22 wage control, the profession-match rule, the training-programme requirement, or the temporary-exchange logic. So the exact legal point is not that trainees can never avoid the ordinary labour-market test; it is that they may do so only **within** **a** **route whose own conditions are strictly observed.** Where those route conditions are not genuinely met, the trainee label does not immunise the case against refusal. 51

## Drafting cautions

Future refusal-risk drafting for the content system should stay close to what the official sources actually support.

·Do not write that a trainee or stagiaire label, a "training atmosphere", or low remuneration makes the case easier. Swiss official materials insist on local and sectoral conditions under Article 22,and for young professionals the pay should at least correspond to the level of someone newly entering the profession. 52

·Do not present "ordinary vacancy filling" as a universal, standalone statutory refusal ground. The safer formulation is that it is a **risk** **inference** drawn from route-purpose rules, profession-match rules, wage rules, duration rules and, in some sectors, express non-replacement language. 8

·Do **not** suggest that weak training evidence can always be repaired after filing. The official materials define mandatory filing documents and contractual content, but they do not promise that defects will routinely be curable later. Missing programme detail is therefore best described as a serious filing and credibility risk, not as a mere administrative inconvenience. 53

·Do **not** collapse treaty-based Young Professionals into the ordinary non-EU/EFTA work-permit route. The former is an exchange-based route with its own eligibility and anti-abuse conditions; the latter is governed by broader third-country labour-market admission rules such as economic interest, labour-market priority, salary conditions and personal qualifications. 54

·Do **not** imply that unpaid internships fall outside Swiss work-authorisation law. Geneva and SEM both say the opposite. 55

·Do not describe "overqualification" as a clear rule of refusal. Where relevant, frame it as a fact-sensitive credibility issue: the more senior and autonomous the profile, the harder it may be to show that the Swiss role is genuinely training-led and tied to career development rather than ordinary employment. 15
