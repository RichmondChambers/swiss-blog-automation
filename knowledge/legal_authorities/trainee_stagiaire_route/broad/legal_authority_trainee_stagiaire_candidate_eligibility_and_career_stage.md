# Swiss legal authority pack on trainee andstagiaire candidate eligibility and career stage

**Suggested filename:**

legalauthoritytraineestagiairecandidateeligibilityandcareerstagepack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

The Swiss trainee or stagiaire route is not a general graduate-employment route. It is a narrow, treaty-linked admission category for **young** **professionaIs** that sits on the derogation power in Article 30(1)(g) FNIA and the implementing rule in Article 42 OASA. Article 30(1)(g) allows derogations from the ordinary admission rules in order to facilitate international economic, scientific and cultural exchange and basic and continuing professional education and training; Article 42 OASA then provides that the **procedure** **and granting of permits are governed by the trainee agreements and intergovernmental** **administrative** **arrangements.** In other words, candidate eligibility is driven first by the existence and content of a relevant bilateral trainee agreement, and then by SEM guidance and programme materials that operationalise those agreements.1

Nationality is therefore a hard threshold issue. On the current SEM public route pages, the operative in-bound programme is limited to nationals of the current contracting non-EU/EFTA states: South Africa, Argentina, Australia, Canada, Chile, USA, Indonesia, Japan, Monaco, New Zealand, Philippines, Russia, San Marino, Tunisia and Ukraine. The same SEM materials state that EU/EFTA nationnals no longer need a permit to work in Switzerland because of free movement. The SEM foreign-nationals directives still reproduce an older historic table of 35 agreements, but they expressly note that the EU/EFTA trainee agreements remain formally unrevoked yet are no longer of practical relevance after the end of the transitional free-movement arrangements. 2

Age limits are also genuine eligibility conditions, not merely soft indicators. The current SEM instructions state a general range of 18 **to** **35,** with specific treaty-based variations: **Australia** **20** **to** **30**and **New** **Zealand** **and** **Russia** **18** **to** **30.** The 2026 SEM directives present the same structure in their route chapter and country table. 3

Education and career stage matter in two distinct ways. First, they operate as baseline eligibility conditions: the current SEM public pages describe the route as open to young professionals who have completed training, stated publicly as at least a bachelor's degree or a completed vocational training programme of at least two years. The detailed English SEM instructions likewise require completed vocational training and add explicit country-specific exceptions: **students** **from** **Canada** **and** **the** **USA** **are admitted,** and **US apprentices with at least two years of training in the relevant occupation** **are** **also** **admitted.** Secondly, education and career stage shape the evidential assessment of whether the proposed role is genuinely a training stay rather than ordinary labour migration.4

There is **no universal Swiss federal rule requiring recent graduation** for in-bound trainee permits. That point must be stated conservatively. The current general SEM route pages do not impose a recent-graduation deadline. Some agreements or country-specific arrangements do, howvever, vary the profile. The 2024 Swiss-US agreement, for example, allows US participants in Switzerland who have completed technical or professional training, completed post-secondary education, are currently studying at post-secondary level, or are apprentices enrolled for at least two years in technical or professional training. The Philippines guidance is even more clearly country-specific, accepting either tertiary/post-secondary completion or, in some cases, completed secondary education plus at least one year of relevant experience, together with moral, fitness and security-related conditions. 5

The route is expressly tied to **continuing** **professional** **training.** SEM's current public pages and directives require that the placement be undertaken in **the** **profession** **learned** **or** **the** **field** **of current** **studies,** that the stay serve continuing professional development, and that the worker be paid according to the usual local and sectoral standards, at least at the level of a person newly entering the profession. The SEMcontract form reinforces the same point by requiring an integrated **training** **programme** and "linguistic and professional" learning content. This is why experienced workers cannot safely be re-labelled as trainees merely because the employer calls the role a "stage" or "traineeship". Where the factual matrix looks like ordinary productive employment with no serious training structure, the route fit is weak even if the candidate happens to satisfy nationality and age. 6

Evidence is correspondingly structured around the candidate's profile and the training logic of the stay. The SEM application form requires, at minimum, the official application form, the employment contract with training programme, copies of diplomas, an updated CV, copies of work certificates, and a passport copy. The detailed SEM instructions add special enrolment evidence for Canadian and US students and for US apprentices. For regulated professions, the SEM instructions require the corresponding recognition or authorisation to be enclosed. 7

From a drafting perspective, the safest internal position is this: **nationality** **and** **age** **are** **hard** **legal** **gatekeepers; completed training and profession-match are core route conditions; recent** **graduation is not universally required; and career-stage analysis mainly matters as a** **genuineness and route-fit issue rather than as a free-standing statutory test.** Overqualification, lack of a coherent education-to-role link, or a role that looks indistinguishable from normal employment are therefore best treated as **practice** **risks** **or evidential weaknesses,** not as always-explicit black-letter prohibitions. 8

## Structural overview and table of legal propositions

### B. Structural overview of candidate eligibility and career-stage analysis

The route is best analysed in five layers. The first layer is the statutory derogation in Article 30 FNIA. The second is Article 42 OASA, which sends the decision-maker to the trainee agreements and administrative arrangements. The third layer is the relevant bilateral instrument or country-specific arrangement. The fourth layer is SEM's public guidance, directives, application forms and standard contracts. The fifth layer is cantonal handling of adjacent issues such as wage benchmarking, regulated professions, and the distinction between trainee placements, student internships and ordinary third-country employment. 9

This layered structure matters because not every proposition carries the same legal weight. Some points are clear rules laid down by legislation or by SEM's operative programme instructions; some are qualified rules that depend on the particular agreement; some are administrative practice points; and some are careful inferences drawn from the route's stated purpose. Internal content should preserve that distinction and avoid flattening them into universal propositions. 10

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts

The black-letter starting points are limited but important. Article 30 FNIA provides the derogation framework; Article 42 OASA delegates the concrete trainee regime to the underlying agreements and arrangements, authorises SEM to issue permits for stays of up to 18 months, and-where the relevant agreement sets no permit numbers-allows short-stay permits to be issued within the specific federal quota framework. The ordinance version reviewed here also shows that, for the US agreement, Annex 1fixed a federal short-stay quota of 300 for calendar year 2025; because quota figures are annual and may change, the number itself should always be re-checked for the processing year. 25

The official SEM page on Swiss traineeships abroad is useful because it lists the current partner countries and links each agreement on Fedlex. It confirms that Switzerland currently has operative trainee or young-professional agreements with Argentina, Australia, Chile, Indonesia, Japan, Canada, Monaco, New Zealand, the Philippines, Russia, South Africa, Tunisia, Ukraine and the USA, and it also identifies San Marino in the accompanying country list. That page is not itself the legal basis for admission into Switzerland, but it is a reliable official index of therelevant bilateral framework. 26

The 2024 Swiss-US agreement illustrates why agreement-specific review matters. For US participants coming to Switzerland, the agreement permits four categories: persons who completed technical or professional training in the US; persons who completed post-secondary education in the US; current students at post-secondary institutions in the US seeking a Swiss contract in their field; and apprentices enrolled in technical or professional training for at least two years who seek Swiss field-related employment to complete that training. The same agreement fixes the age band at 18 to 35 and makes clear that US participants must still satisfy Swiss entry and admission law unless the agreement provides otherwise. 27

Country-specific arrangements can materially alter the education and profile question. The Philippines guidance is the clearest example in the sources reviewed: it requires candidates to be 18 to 35 years old, of good moral character, with no criminal record and fit for work; it accepts either tertiary or post-secondary completion, or-if the person lacks tertiary/post-secondary completion-completed secondary education plus at least one year of relevant professional experience; and it asks for documents including a CV in English, educational records, police clearance, and medical/psychological

certificates. That is a strong reminder that one should not state universal education or evidential rules without checking the relevant country instrument. 28

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's current public route pages and detailed instructions are the most operationally useful sources for candidate analysis. Taken together, they state that the route is for young professionals seeking to improve professional and linguistic skills in Switzerland, that labour-market conditions are not examined in the same way as under the ordinary third-country route, that the candidate must be from a contracting country, within the relevant age band, trained in the profession concerned, working in the learned profession or study field, and paid at the going rate. They also state expressly that part-time work and self-employment are not possible. 29

The SEM foreign-nationals directives add several important practice elements. They define stagiaires as persons aged 18 to 30/35 with completed professional training who go to the partner state for a limited period to improve professional and linguistic knowledge; they state that the placement must be in the learned profession; they repeat that pay must match local and professional norms; they cap the number of trainees in one undertaking, as a rule, at 5% of the workforce in order to preserve the training character; and they require the further-training programme to be set out in the employment contract. They also confirm that change of profession is not possible, because the stay serves continuing training in the learned profession. 30

The SEM application form and standard employment contract are particularly valuable for evidential drafting. The form asks not just for identity and nationality, but also for professional education, job experience, current work, languages and the proposed Swiss employment.It also specifies the supporting bundle: the employment contract including training programme, diploma copies, a current CV,work certificates and the passport copy. The standard contract then requires the parties to specify the training programme, including the linguistic and professional learning content, the responsible person, remuneration, working hours, insurance and travel expenses. 31

The current SEM route pages also contain an important salary formulation that is useful for candidate-profile assessment. They describe young-professional engagements as professional experiences for someone who has completed training, and say the remuneration should at least reach the level of people newly entering the profession, while still complying with collective agreements and cantonal wage guidance where relevant. That supports a conservative early-career reading of the route, even if the legislation itself does not expressly use the term "job starter". 15

## Cantonal practice notes

### F. Cantonal practice notes

Official cantonal materials on the federal young-professional route itself are relatively thin. In practice, cantons tend to publish fuller information on the ordinary third-country work route and on student internships, with SEM remaining the principal federal reference point for the trainee-exchange scheme. That thinnessshould be stated plainly in internal content. 32

Canton Zurich is useful on distinctions. Its official "Students & Interns" page says that internships for foreign students studying abroad are only available where the internship is **mandatory,** only once, and for a total of up to 12 months, normally through placement organisations such as IAESTE or AIESEC; it also requires a university letter confirming enrolment and the compulsory nature of the internship.The same page distinguishes those internships from post-study **traineeships** for vocational or university graduates from countries with agreements, which may last up to 18 months and fall under SEM's competence. Zurich therefore offers an official, route-adjacent distinction between student internships on the one hand and treaty-based trainee stays on the other. 33

<!-- 7 -->

Zurich's general third-country labour-market guidance also shows the type of evidence cantonal authorities expect when they are assessing skilI and profile: diplomas, work certificates, CV and passport copy, with documents filed in German or English and diplomas translated where necessary. That is not a special rule of the trainee route, but it is a credible cantonal indicator of how documentary profile evidence is handled in employment-admission cases. 34

Canton Geneva likewise provides helpful distinction points. Its student-stage page requires a permit even for **unpaid** internships, requires the internship to be an integral part of the studies, caps it at less than half of the overall course length, and demands supporting school certification. Its general third-country employed-work page states that any Swiss employer engaging a third-country national-including as trainee, apprentice, volunteer, social worker, artist or missionary-must in principle obtain prior authorisation, and it asks for CV, diplomas, work certificates annd an employer motivation letter. As with Zurich, these Geneva materials are not route-specific trainee-exchange rules, but they are useful for evidential comparison and for showing that cantonal practice takes profile evidence seriously. 35

Taken together, the cantonal materials support two cautious internal propositions. First, Swiss authorities distinguish sharply between **mandatory student internships, treaty-based young-professional exchanges,** and **ordinary employed work.** Secondly, where a case does not **fit** comfortably in the trainee-exchange framework, cantonal authorities will revert to the normal evidential logic of the third-country work route rather than treating the "trainee" label as decisive. 36

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

### G. Practical legal analysis

For internal case assessment, nationality and age should be treated as the first screening gates. If the candidate is not a national of a current contracting state, or is outside the treaty age band, the Article 42 OASA trainee route is not the correct route. If those thresholds are met, the next question is whether the candidate's **education and work history make the Swiss placement look like genuine further** **professional development** rather than ordinary employment. That second questionn is where most difficult candidate-profile cases will sit. 37

The most persuasive candidate profile is therefore an early-career or still-developing professional whose diploma, apprenticeship or current studies map directly onto the Swiss role and whose employment contract contains a concrete training programme. The official materials do not require a separate "career plan" document, but the integrated training programme in the contract performs much of that evidential function. In difficult cases, a short explanatory statement linking the diploma, prior work history and anticipated post-Swiss professional use of the training is prudent, even though it is better described as a **practice** **aid** than a formal legal requirement. 38

A poor-fit profile usually presents in one of three ways. The first is the **under-qualified** **candidate,** where there is no credible education or field link to the proposed role. The second is the **misaligned** **candidate,** where the candidate is trained in one field and the Swiss role is in another. The third is the **ordinary-worker candidate,** where the role is productive employment with little or no structured learning, especially if the salary, seniority, or responsibilities point away from entry-level continuing

<!-- 8 -->

training. The first two risks track clear official requirements that the role match the learned profession or field of study; the third is an inference from the route's declared training purpose, the requirement for a training programme, and the "new entrant" salary benchmark. 39

The "overqualified candidate" problem should be handled carefully. No reviewed official text says that a senior or highly experienced worker is automatically barred from trainee status. The safer proposition is narrower: the further a candidate's seniority, remuneration expectations and job content move away from early-career professional development, the harder it becomes to demonstrate that the stay is genuinely a young-professional training exchange. Internal drafting should therefore avoid absolute statements and instead explain the risk as one of **genuineness, route fit and evidential credibility.** 22

Recent graduation needs equally careful treatment. For Switzerland as destination, the materials reviewed do not support a universal rule that the candidate must have graduated recently. Internal content should therefore not say that the route is only for recent graduates. A correct and conservative statement is that the route is generally aimed at young professionals who have completed relevant training, while some agreements or country arrangements create special student, apprentice or background variants. 40

The route must also be distinguished from other nearby categories. A **student internship** is normally tied to ongoing studies and to a mandatory curricular requirement. A **Swiss** **post-studly** **graduate** **worker** case concerns a third-country national with a Swiss university degree seeking work of high scientific or economic interest. An **intra-corporate internship** is a multinational corporate placement linked to subsequent deployment abroad and presupposes an existing foreign employment relationship. An **ordinary** **skilled-worker** case is governed by Articles 18 to 24 FNIA and the full labour-market and qualified-worker framework. A treaty trainee case is not a substitute for any of those categories when its own conditions are not met. 41

### H.Drafting cautions

Internal content on this route should be drafted with the following cautions in mind. First, do not describe the route as though it were generally available to foreign graduates; it is nationality-limited and treaty-based. Secondly, do not imply that age limits are soft or negotiable; they are part of the route conditions. Thirdly, do not collapse "intern", "student", "trainee", "graduate employee" and "skilled worker" into a single concept; Switzerland's official materials treat them as distinct categories with different legal bases and evidence. Fourthly, do not state that ordinary experienced workers can simply be re-labelled as trainees; the role must still be a genuine training stay in the learned field. Fifthly, do not overstate "recent graduation" as a general rule; where the point is agreement-specific or only evidentially relevant, the content shnould say so. Finally, where official texts are thin-especially on overqualification, career plans, or the level of prior experience-label the proposition as a **practice** **point** or **inference,** not as clear law. 42

**Open questions and limitations**

This pack is intentionally conservative where the public materials are thin. In particular, the official sources reviewed do not lay down a universal recent-graduation test or an express "overqualification" prohibition for in-bound Swiss trainee cases. Those issues have therefore been treated as route-fit and evidential matters rather than as hard legal bars. Likewise, not every bilateral agreement was reviewed line by line in machine-readable treaty text; where country-specific variation clearly mattered, this pack relied on the official SEM route pages, the SEM directives, and agreement-specific materials that were accessible in the reviewed sources.
