# Swiss legal authority pack on training purpose and genuine trainee or stagiaire route in work authorisation cases

# Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not create an open-ended "trainee" label that can be attached to ordinary employment. The legal architecture is narrower. At federal level, the relevant gateway is the derogation power in Article 30 paragraph 1 letter g FNIA, which allows exceptions from the ordinary admission rules in order to facilitate international exchange and basic or continuing professional education and training. The route-specific implementing rule is Article 42 OASA/VZAE, which sends the procedure and permit issuance for stagiaires to the applicable bilateral trainee agreements and intergovernmental administrative arrangements. The short-stay permit remains purpose-specific, and a change of purpose requires a new permit.1

The official legislative text is deliberately sparse, so most of the operational content on what makes a placement genuinely training-led appears in SEM administrative materials rather than in a detailed statutory definition. SEM's directives, public "young professionals" instructions, treaty list, and standard employment contract all point in the same direction: the route is for people who have already completed training, who will work in the profession in which they were trained, for a limited period, on local/sectoral pay, under a written training programme, and not as leased, part-time, or self-employed labour. 2

Swiss authorities also regulate "gainful employment" broadly. Under Article 11 FNIA, gainful employment includes work normally carried out for payment wvhether or not payment is actually made, and Article 1a OASA/VZAE treats any activity for an employer as salaried employment even if it is temporary or paid abroad. The practical consequence is that simply calling a placement an internship, volunteer role, or trainee assignment does not take it outside immigration and labour-market control. Where a proposed arrangement does not fit the specific stagiaire or study-related internship routes, ordinary local-hire rules reassert themselves, including economic-interest, priority, wage and qualification requirements. 3

The safest legal inference from the official materials is therefore this: a Swiss authority is likely to accept a placement as a genuine trainee or stagiaire arrangement where the candidate's pre-existing qualifications, the training content, the role description, the written programme, the supervision structure, and the temporary/treaty framework all align. The risk of reclassification towards ordinary employment increases sharply where the duties are indistinguishable from a normal vacancy, where the role is unrelated to the person's training, where the employer's operational need dominates, where the pay structure appears discount-driven, or where the "training" content is generic or missing altogether. This is an inference from the federal and cantonal materials rather than an express single-test formula in the statute.(4

## Structural overview of training-purpose analysis

The official materials support a structured analysis with five recurring questions. First, identify the legal route.Is the proposed arrangement truly under a bilateral stagiaire agreement, or is it instead a student internship, a post-study job-search situation, a general third-country hire, an intra-group transfer,or a posting/cross-border service? Swiss law treats those channels separately. 5

Second, test whether the arrangement has a route-specific training aim. SEM's materials for young professionals repeatedly describe the route as a way to broaden occupational and linguistic skills, and the standard SEM contract requires a dedicated on-the-job training programme with details and a responsible person. 6

Third, test alignment between the proposed work and the person's formation. For treaty-route young professionals, the work must be in the profession in which the person was trained. For mandatory student internships, the internship must be an integral partof the studies, and the institution must confirm that fact. Swiss cantonal material also treats unrelated or merely general tasks with caution. 7

Fourth, test whether the placement remains meaningfully different from an ordinary vacancy. SEM and cantonal materials use several control devices: local/sectoral salary rules, a 5 per cent trainee cap for young professionals, bans on part-time work, self-emnployment and staff leasing, quarter-workforce limits in some internship settings, and in some project routes an explicit rule that interns may not be employed in place of workers. 8

Fifth, test whether the arrangement is genuinely temporary and purpose-bound. Treaty-route stagiaire permits are capped at 18 months; extension and repeat-use rules are restrictive; profession changes are not allowed because the stay serves continuing training in the learned profession; and any change of purpose requires a fresh immigration basis. 9

### Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's route-specific material is the main operational source for what Swiss authorities expect fromna genuine stagiaire arrangement. The public English instructions explain that Switzerland has concluded trainee-exchange agreements with a limited group of countries; that the route simplifies work-authorisation regardless of the labour-market situation; that applicants must generally be 18-35, have completed training, work in the profession in which they were trained, receive local/sectoral pay, and stay within a maximum 18 months; and that part-time work, self-employment and staff leasing are not allowed. The same instructions add that multiple trainees in one firm may not exceed 5 per cent of the Workforce. 18

SEM's own directives reinforce the same points in more legal-administrative language. They define stagiaires as persons with completed vocational training who spend alimited period in the partner state to broaden professional and language knowledge; require the deployment to be in the learned profession; require salary at local/professional rates; state that, to ensure continuing training, trainee employment is in principle limited to 5 per cent of a firm's staff; and require the continuing-training programme to be written into the employment contract. The directives also state that profession changes are not possible because the stay serves continuing training in the learned profession. 44

The SEM standard employment agreement is particularly important evidence of the administration's training-purpose expectations. It is not just a normal employment form: it includes a dedicated section headed "program of training during the stay as a Young Professional", asks forthe linguistic and professional learning content,and requires identification of a responsible person. In addition, its notes reiterate local/professional conditions and the ban on part-time work. 45

SEM's treaty overview page is also valuable because it consolidates the current bilateral agreements and states the route's core purpose in plain terms: professional internships serve advanced training; the placement may be undertaken only in the learnedd profession or studied discipline; self-employment

and part-time work are not authorised; and trainees must be paid according to local and sectoral standards. The same page links directly to the Fedlex treaty texts, including the agreements with South Africa, Argentina, Australia, Canada, Chile, Indonesia, Japan, Monaco, New Zealand, the Philippines, Russia, Tunisia, Ukraine and the United States. 46

Country-specific programme materials can add evidential detail. The Philippines implementation guidelines, for example, require Philippine nationality, age 18-35, completed secondary or higher education, good health and good character, and-crucially-that the deployment occur in the learned profession or training field. That reinforces the broader SEM message that the route is career-linked professional development, not free-form entry-level work. 47

SECO's public material reviewed for this pack is general rather than route-specific. It mainly explains the foreign-law permit framework and directs employers to SEM and cantonal authorities for substantive permit conditions. It is relevant as background on employer compliance, bout it does not supply a separate route-specific "genuine trainee" test comparable to SEM's materials. 48

### Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal materials are not a substitute for federal law, but they are useful indicators of how labour-market and migration authorities operationalise training-purpose evidence in day-to-day cases.

**Zurichh.** Zurich's guidance is unusually explicit. It says that internships for third-country nationals are possible only in certain cases. For foreign students studying abroad, the internship must be obligatory, is limited to a total of 12 months and one use, and requires a university letter confirming enrolment and mandatory status; permits are issued only where short-term foreign workers do not exceed one quarter of the workforce. For students in Switzerland, a mandatory internship requires a training programme and must not exceed half the total duration of studies. Zurich also states that a pre-study company internship should generally be completed abroad. After graduation, further practicaI training is possible either as a treaty "Stage" for nationals of agreement countries or as an internship within an internationally active group, but the group-internal option requires an existing employment relationship abroad and an onward deployment abroad after the Swiss period. 3⁰

**Basel-Stadt.** Basel-Stadt's immigration-facing page draws a sharp line for third-country internships: work permits are available only for group-internal internships or mandatory university internships, and it points separately to the SEM stagiaire programme for treaty-route young professionals. That is a strong cantonal signal against treating general local internships as an open category for third-country nationals. 49

**Geneva.** Geneva's pages are useful for two reasons. First, in its student context, Geneva requires that an internship form an integral part of the studies under the course plan, that its duration remain below half the full training period, and that the work permit be obtained before the internship starts,whether paid or unpaid. Second, in its general third-country employment guidance, Geneva makes clear that any gainful activity-including as a trainee, apprentice, volunteer or social worker-falls within the work-authorisation regime; that ordinary third-country hiring requires proof of Swiss economic interest, labour-market priority, unsuccessful Swiss/EU recruitment, qualified-worker status, customary wages and full-time employment; and that OCIRT may impose conditions, including making renewal dependent on the achievement of the objectives presented by the employer in the application. 50

Taken together, these cantonal materials support a conservative practice conclusion. The more a proposed arrangement looks like a mandatory curriculum-linked internship, a treaty-route young-professional placement, or a tightly structured group-internal development assignment, the easier it is to classify it as training-led. The more it looks like a normal Swiss vacancy for which the employer simply prefers a foreign national, the more likely the case is to be channelled back into ordinary third-country labour-market scrutiny. That conclusion is an inference from the cantonal materials, not a verbatim statutory rule. 51

### Practical legal analysis

A Swiss authority deciding whether a proposed stagiaire or trainee arrangement is genuine is likely to focus first on the legal fit between the person, the role and the route. The strongest positive indicator is that the person already has completed vocational or academic formation and the Swissrole sits squarely within that formation. SEM says exactly that for young professionals, and its external stagiaire material repeats that the internship may be undertaken only in the learned profession or studied discipline. Where the duties drift into unrelated, general or auxiliary work, the hospitality guidance shows that Swiss administration is willing to say the connection is too weak. 52

The second major factor is documentary structure. A genuine route should be capable of being described as a fixed-term professional-development placement with identifiable learning outcomes. Here the standard SEM contract matters a great deal: it asks for concrete linguistic and professional learning content and a responsible person. The broader SEM directives on first-labour-market internships-although aimed at refugees and temporarily admitted persons rather than Article 42stagiaires-are still instructive because they define an internship as a fixed-term employment relationship with a training character designed to acquire or deepen skills in practical application. That is not the legal basis for the treaty route, but it is a useful administrative analogy when assessing whether a purported trainee role is genuine or merely rhetorical. 53

The third factor is the relationship between productive work and learning. Swiss sources do not require that the trainee produce no useful work; indeed, the route is expressly "on the job". But they do require features inconsistent with a pure labour-substitution model: local/sectoral wages rather than discounted trainee rates; bans on part-time work, self-emnployment and staff leasing; a 5 per cent trainee cap in SEM young-professional practice; in some project routes, a rule that interns may not be employed in place of workers; and in some internship routes, workforce-ratio limits. These signals point to the same principle: productive output may be poresent, but it must remain subordinate to a real development programme. 54

The fourth factor is whether the placement is being used to fill an ordinary Swiss vacancy. Where the treaty route genuinely applies, SEM says authorisation is simplified regardless of labour-market conditions. But that simplification is available only inside a tightly limmited treaty framework. Outside that framework, Swiss authorities return to ordinary labour-market controls. Geneva's public guidance is especially revealing here: it requires proof that the employer announced the vacancy, searched in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area, and could not train an available worker within a reasonable time. Zurich and Basel likewise restrict third-country internships to mandatory study-linked or group-internal cases. The practical inference is straightforward: if the employer's true objective is to fill a normal vacancy in Switzerland, authorities are likely to treat the case as ordinary employment rather than a genuine training route. 55

The fifth factor is temporariness and onward logic. Treaty-route stagiaire stays are capped at 18 months. Repetition and chaining are restricted. SEM says profession changes are not allowed because the stay is for continuing training in the learned profession. Zurich's guidance for post-study intra-group internships also insists on an existing employment relationship abroad plus a subsequent assignment

<!-- 10 -->

abroad after the Swiss period. All of that points away from using the trainee category as a disguised route into ordinary indefinite locaI employment. 56

The distinctions from neighbouring routes should be made carefully in internal drafting. A student internship is governed by the rules on study-related work and mandatory practical training, not by Article 42 stagiaires. A Swiss post-study graduate work case is different again; Zurich expressly notes that not every Swiss degree automatically permits work, and that only roles of high scientific or economic interest qualify, with a separate six-month job-search permission after studies. An intra-group transfer sits on a different statutory basis aimed at senior managers, specialists and similar corporate transfers, not at trainee development. A posting or cross-border service is another different concept again, defined inthe OASA/VZAE as the temporary provision of a service in Switzerland within a contractual relationship by a person or company resident abroad. 57

For internal case assessment, the most defensible positive evidence bundle is therefore: treaty-country fit where Article 42 is invoked; completed relevant training; a role confined to the person's trained professionor studied discipline; a fixed term that clearly ends; local/sectoral remuneration at least at starter level; a written programme of professional and linguistic development; a named mentor or responsible person; credible supervision and periodic review; and a narrative showing thatthe arrangement is developmental rather than vacancy-filling. Conversely, the highest-risk fact pattern is a loosely drafted "trainee" title applied to a full-time operational role with generic duties, low pay, no route-specific nationality fit, no curriculum tie, no training plan, no named supervisor, and an obvious business need to fill ordinary labour demand. This is a synthesis from the official material reviewed. 58

### Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

·Do not write as if the phrase "trainee", "stagiaire", "intern", or "young professional" determines the immigration outcome. Swiss law regulates gainful work broadly, including unpaid work, and the route-specific legal basis must still fit. 59

·Do not imply that ordinary employment can be converted into a genuine trainee route by adding training language. If the case falls outside the treaty or mandatory-internship framework, ordinary third-country labour-market rules apply. 60

·Do not blur the treaty stagiaire route with student internships, Swiss post-study graduate work, ICT/group transfers, or postings. Each sits on different legaI foundations and evidential assumptions. 61

·Do not present the route as generally available to all foreign nationals. Official SEM material limits the practical programme to specified agreement states and imposes route-specific age and formation conditions. 62

·Do not describe trainee remuneration as permissibly low because the placement is "developmental". Official sources require local and sectoral salary conditions, and SEM says the salary should at least match career-starter level. 20

·Do not omit the written training programme, responsible person, and profession-alignment evidence. Those are among the clearest indicators of a genuine route in SEM materials. 53

·Do not normalise part-time models, self-employment, or staff leasing in future route content. SEM's young-professionals material excludes them. 21

·Do not overstate the role of case law. In the material reviewed, the operative detail on training-purpose assessment is primarily administrative and practice-based rather than elaborated in a single leading statutory or judicial test. That conclusion follows from the contrast between the spare text of Article 42 OASA/VZAE and the much fuller SEM guidance and forms. 63
