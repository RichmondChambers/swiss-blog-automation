# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Training Plans, Supervision and Skills Development in Trainee and Stagiaire Work Authorisation Cases

## A. Executive legal summary

In Swiss immigration law, a trainee or stagiaire placement is not treated as legally neutral simply because it is labelled an internship. Federal ordinance text treats activity carried out as an apprentice, trainee or volunteer as gainful employment, and the cantonal labour-market authority decides whether a case counts as gainful employment, wvith SEM deciding in cases of doubt. That matters because the whole evidential exercise is about classification: is the placement genuinely a training stay, or is it ordinary labour under a softer label? 1

The federal legislation does not lay down one universal statutory definition of a "training plan" applicable across every trainee route. The stronger legal basis is more indirect. Article 30 paragraph 1letter g FNIA permits derogations from the ordinary admission rules to facilitate international exchange and basic or continuing professional education and training. Article 42 OASA then provides that, for treaty-based stagiaires, procedure and permit issuance are governed by the applicable trainee agreements and bilateral administrative arrangements. In parallel, the current SEM Young Professionals route expressly requires a written employment contract with an integrated training programme, and the official application form requires an employment contract "with training program(me)". 2

For the treaty-based Young Professionals route, the current SEM position is clear: the route is for people who have completed their education, it is for career development, the person must work in the learned profession or field of study, part-time work and self-employment are not allowed,and remuneration must follow local and sectoraI standards. The SEM instructions add that the trainnee permit is granted on the basis of a written employment contract with an integrated training programme, and that the contract must include at least the type of employment and training programme, includling job description, schedule and courses. 3

A credible training plan therefore functions as evidence of purpose. At minimum, it should show what professional and linguistic skills are to be improved, why those skills relate to the person's prior education or profession, how the training content will be delivered over time, annd who is responsible for the training. That last point is not decorative: the standard SEM Young Professionals contract has a separate page for the training programme, asks for details of the on-the-job linguistic and professional skills to be perfected, and contains a dedicated line for the person "Responsible". Cantonal practice reinforces that expectation. St Gallen, for example, requires a business trainingprogramme and the contact details of the person responsible for training. 4

Swiss authorities also distinguish supervised training from ordinary operational labour. A generic job description is not enough. St Gallen states that an internship is not there to test a worker's personal or professional suitability; if that is the aim, the employer should use a trial day or a probationary period in a normal employment contract instead. The SEM directives also exclude activity areas having no direct link to the studies or training concerned, such as generic administrative work, unrelated posts and

auxiliary functions, and in one training context they state expressly that trainees must not be engaged in place of workers. 5

The practical consequence is that the stronger the operational labour element, the more the file needs structured training evidence to keep the case within a trainee or stagiaire classification. Supervision alone does not prove trainee status, because ordinary employees are supervised too. What helps is a training architecture: route-specific eligibility, a curriculum or professional link, identified learning objectives, a time-bound programme, a named training lead, and evidence that productive work remains subordinate to skills development rather than vacancy-filling. Official materials are much thinner on rotation, mentoring reports and evaluations than they are on programme content, but including those features is prudent precisely because they make the training character easier to verify. 6

A further caution is route confusion. "Stagiaire" is used narrowly on the SEM Young Professionals page for Article 42 OASA treaty cases, but the SEM Chapter 4 directives also contain a broader stagiaires chapter dealing with other training or exchange placements under Article 30 paragraph 1 letter g FNIA and, in that chapter, referring in general terms to Article 41 OASA and short-stay provisions. The correct legal route must therefore boe identified before treating any checklist item as a rule.7

## B. Structural overview of training-plan and supervision evidence

The permit authority is not merely checking whether training language appears in the paperwork; it is checking whether the whole placement is recognisably a training stay rather than ordinary labour. ## D. Primary legal texts

The primary federal legal texts establish the framework, but they do not by themselves spell out the granular content of a training plan. That detail is supplied mainly through SEM administration and, in some routes,cantonal practice. The legal pack should therefore distinguish between what is expressly stated in legislation and what is required through official forms and guidance. 29

<!-- 6 -->

**FNIA** **Article** 22. Foreign nationals may be admitted for work only if salary and employment conditions customary for the location, profession and sector are met. In trainee cases, this means that "training" does not displace the wage floor. A training plan may explain why the role is developmental, but it does not permit substandard conditions. 30

**FNIA** **Article** 27. For education or training stays without gainful employment as the main residence purpose, the person must be eligible for the course, have accommodation and means,and satisfy the personal and educational requirements for the planned course. This matters because many internship cases sit at the boundary between "study" and "work", and the file must show which legal basis is actually being used. 16

**FNIA** **Article** 30 **paragraph** **1** **letter** g. This is the key derogation provision allowing admissions to facilitate international exchange and basic or continuing professional education and training. It underpins several structured trainee routes, including treaty-based stagiaires and other training placements. 16

**OASA** **Articles** **39,** 41 **and** **42.** Article 39 covers mandatory internships within full-time education in Switzerland and limits work to cases where the internship does not exceed half of the total training duration and where ordinary work-permit conditions such as local employment conditions are met. Article 41 enables residence and short-stay permits for international exchanges of an economic, scientific or cultural nature subject to the usual admission conditions. Article 42 deals specifically/with stagiaires under agreements and bilateral administrative arrangements, with SEM able to issue permits for training stays of up to 18 months. 17

**OASA Article** **1a** **and** **Article** 4. These are the classification provisions. Article 1a confirms that traineeship activity is gainful employment for the ordinance. Article 4 places the initial classification function with the cantonal labour-market authority and reserves doubtful cases to SEM. These provisions explain why "it is only a training placement" is never enough as a legal answer. 1

**Bilateral trainee** **agreements.** The agreements are highly relevant where Article 42 OASA is the operative route. The Ukraine agreement defines stagiaires as persons who take up a position in the learned profession in the other country for a limited period in order to develop their professional and language skills; permits under the quota are issued irrespective of the host labour-market situation; the trainee may not take another job than the authorised one; employment conditions must comply with the host state's labour and social-insurance rules. The Italy agreement is structurally similar, butSEM's own directives note that EU-related trainee agreements are now largely of little practical importance after the lifting of transitional free-movement arrangements. 31

## E. Official guidance, administrative materials and F. Cantonal practice notes

At federal level, the most important administrative materials are the SEM Young Professionals page, the instructions, the official application form, the standard employment agreement with training programme, and the SEM LEI Chapter 4 directives updated to 1 January 2026. These materials are where the evidential role of the training plan becomes concrete. The SEM page currently lists the eligible Young Professionals countries, confirms that the purpose is career development, restricts the work to the learned profession or the field of study, prohibits part-time work and self-employment,and directs applicants to the official form and standard contract. The instructions state that trainee permits are granted on the basis of a written employment contract with an integrated training programme and that the contract should include the type of employment and training programme, including job

<!-- 7 -->

description, schedule and courses. The application form requires, as an enclosure, the employment contract with training programme. The standard contract then operationalises that requirement through a dedicated training page for on-the-job linguistic and professional skills and a named responsible person. 32

The SEM LEI directives are especially useful because they show how broader internship and exchange cases are assessed administratively outside the narrow Article 42 treaty route. In the "stagiaires" chapter, the directives indicate that applications are often filed through student-placement organisations, professional associations or youth-exchange organisations, and they say that, for youth-exchange placements, the file must include the candidate's CV, information on the training programme, its duration and planned remuneration, a declaration from the company or organisation that will provide the training, and proof of funding for stay and travel. Elsewhere in the directives, SEM requires a detailed training programme for certain mandatory practice placements and excludes activity areas with no direct link to the studies or sector concerned. In the agriculture-development context, the directives say expressly that trainees must not be engaged in polace of workers. 33

There appears to be little SECO material dealing specifically with the immigration-law evidential role of training plans in trainee permits. In the sources reviewed, SEM guidance and cantonal work-permit practice are the dominant administrative authorities on this topic. Where salary analysis is relevant, SEM materials point back to local and sectoral wage norms and, in the directives more broadly, to labour-market assessment tools. 34

Cantonal practice is useful but must be marked as canton-specific. Zurich states that,for foreign students enrolled at Swiss higher-education institutions who need a compulsory internship or write their diploma thesis with a Swiss company, approval is possible if an educational programme is submitted, the work does not exceed half of the total training period, and salary and employment conditions are respected. Geneva states that the stage must form an integral part of the studies according to the plan submitted. Lucerne requires school confirmation including the training plan demonstrating that the internship is mandatory. Solothurn requires an extensive training programme to accompany the employer's application for a compulsory internship. Ticino states that, where the stage is foreseen by the training cycle, the school must attest its obligatory nature; third-country students may only start after authorisation. St Gallen defines an internship as fixed-term employment with a training character, says it is not for testing suitability, requires a business training programme, and asks for the contact details of the person responsible for training. Neuchâtel requires, depending on the route, a signed training or stage contract, school attestation of mandatory characteror training link, and an explanatory employer letter; for third-country students studying abroad, the canton says approval is exceptional and the employer must justify the recourse to a non-Swiss/non-EU trainee. 35

These permissions and checklists do not create universal federal rules. They do, however, show what Swiss authorities find persuasive in practice: route-specific educational proof, a recognisable training programme, named training responsibility, and a clear explanation of why the placement is a real training stay rather than ordinary manpower. 36

## G. Practical legal analysis

The evidential role of the training plan is best understood as a problem of **demonstrable** **purpose.** Swiss authorities do not simply need to know that the employer intends to "train" the worker in a broad sense. They need to see that the legal route being relied on is actually the right one. For Article 42 OASA Young Professionals, the file must show a career-development stay in the learned profession or field of study, with a formal integrated training programme. For study-linked internships, thefile must show

<!-- 8 -->

that the stage is genuinely embedded in the education pathway, usually as a compulsory or academically integrated component. For broader exchange placements, the file must show a structured programme organised by the appropriate body. A training plan is therefore not a soft HRR annex; it is part of the legal evidence of route fit. 37

A credible plan should first show **substantive educational** fit. The placement should align with the candidate's prior education, apprenticeship, diploma or recognised study programme. Where the candidate studied one subject but the proposed internship involves another, the file needs a precise explanation of the bridge between them. Swiss materials repeatedly insist on a direct professional or academic link. General business exposure, generic office assistance or "first Swiss work experience" are not strong legal descriptors on their own. 38

Secondly, the plan should show **structured skills development rather than business-as-usual work** **allocation.** The safest drafting pattern is to identify the skills to be developed, the workstreams through which they will be developed, the approximate sequence and timing, and any formal instructional components such as courses, shadowing, internal workshops, or guided exposure to systems and methods. Federal materials do not expressly require "rotation" as a formal legal criterion, but they do require a programme, schedule and sometimes courses. A route involving several departments can therefore be strengthened by presenting them as training modules with distinct learning outcomes rather than as ordinary staffing rotations. That is a practice point, not an express universal rule. 39

Thirdly, the file should show **training responsibility as distinct from ordinary line management.** The standard SEM contract's "Responsible" field and the St Gallen requirement for the contact details of the training-responsible person show that authorities expect a person or organisational unit to own the training process. A credible file therefore identifies the mentor or training supervisor, their role, the reporting line, how often the trainee will receive feedback, and who will confirm progress. Ordinary managerial oversight of output does not by itself prove trainee status; what matters is responsibility for skills acquisition and programme delivery. This distinction is inferential but strongly grounded in the official forms and cantonal practice. 13

Fourthly, the evidence should address **vacancy substitution** **risk.** The weakest files are those where the employer appears to be filling a normal operational post at trainee cost. Helpful indicators against substitution include: limited duration tied to the programme; tasks framed as developmental rather than standalone operational ownership; time earmarked for guided learning or courses; evidence that the post was not created to remedy ordinary staffing shortages; and wording that avoids treating the trainee as the accountable holder of a stable business function. This is where a generic job description is dangerous. If the proposed duties read like those of a standard employee and the training content is added as an afterthought, the classification risk rises sharply. St Gallen's warning that an internship is not for testing suitability, and the SEM directive language excluding unrelated or auxiliary functions,are particularlyuseful authority points here. 5

Fifthly, the plan should show **productive** **work** **as** **ancillary** **to** **training.** Swiss sources do not contain a single universal formula stating that productive work must be "ancillary", but the concept is built into the structure of the official materials. The programme has to explain the skills to be perfected; compulsory internships must remain within training-duration limits; youth-exchange dossiers mnust disclose the training programme and the organisation that will provide it; and certain directives exclude unrelated routine functions. In practice, if the work output is the obvious centre of gravity and the training narrative is weak, authorities may reclassify the case as ordinary labour. 40

Sixthly, **milestones, evaluation points and expected outcomes** are not prominently prescribed in federal law, but they are sensible drafting devices. Because official materials ask for programme

<!-- 9 -->

content, duration and responsible person, it is prudent to convert those elements into review points: for example, induction and system familiarisation; supervised execution of defined tasks; intermediate assessment; advanced tasks under reduced supervision; and a final skills summary. These features help show that the placement is planned educationally, not merely operationally.They should, however, be presented as supporting evidence rather than as mandatory legal elements unless a particular canton expressly demands them. 41

Finally, the legal analysis should remain **route-specific.** For treaty-based Article 42 OASA cases, the decisive features are the completed training, fit with learned profession or field of study,integrated training programme, local employment conditions and compliance with the relevant bilateral framework. For compulsory study internships, the decisive features are educational integration, mandatory character, duration relative to the overall course, and the educational programme. For broader youth exchange or sector-specific further-training schemes, the decisive features are the organised programme, responsible training body, and route-specific documents. A single "internship template" is therefore legally unsafe. 42

## H. Drafting cautions and I. Source list

### Drafting cautions

A future training plan for Swiss immigration use should avoid treating the internship as a normal position with a thin training gloss. It should not rely on a generic job description, because the official materials distinguish between a training programme and an ordinary employment role. The safer approach is to attach or integrate a route-specific training programme that identifies skills, sequence, supervision and training ownership. 43

Do not treat supervision alone as proof of trainee status. Nearly all employees are supervised. The file should instead draw a clear line between managerial supervision of work and pedagogic or professional supervision aimed at skills acquisition, ideally by naming the responsible trainer and describing the learning process. 13

Do not assert that any internship can be justified because the candidate needs "Swiss experience" or stronger language skills. The Young Professionals route requires work in the learned profession or field of study, and St Gallen expressly states that lack of language skills does not justify an internship. 44

Do not blur the line between a compulsory study internship and a post-qualification trainee placement. Swiss materials apply different legal routes and different evidence expectations. Mandatory school integration, half-duration limits, and school attestations are central in the study route and cannot simply be replaced by an employer letter. 45

Do not allow the task list to drift into unrelated generic functions, vacancy cover, or open-ended auxiliary work. SEM materials exclude unrelated or auxiliary activity in the training contexts cited, and at least one SEM directive states that trainees mmust not be engaged in place of workers. 46

Do not universalise canton-specific documennt requests. A detailed training programme, a school confirmation with training plan, a training-responsible contact, or an employer explanatory letter may be required or expected in particular cantons or routes, but those features should be described as practice evidence rather than as universally codified federal requirements unless the federal source itself says so. 47

### Open questions and limitations

Published case law specifically analysing the evidential sufficiency of a training plan in Swiss trainee or stagiaire work-permit cases appears thin in the readily accessible official sources reviewed. The strongest authorities are therefore the federal statutes, OASA, bilateral agreements, SEM1 directives, and official SEM and cantonal forms and checklists. In addition, federal law is clearer on the legal routes than it is on the detailed content of mentoring, rotation and evaluation mechanisms. Where this pack recommends those features, it does so as a conservative practice point grounded in official administrative materials rather than as an express universal statutory rule. 62
