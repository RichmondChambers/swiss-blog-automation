# Swiss legal authority pack on trainee and stagiaire salary, allowances and working conditions

# Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law does not treat a role as immigration-safe merely because it is labelled a trainee, stagiaire, intern, volunteer or training placement. For Swiss work-authorisation purposes, any activity that would normally generate remuneration is treated as gainful activity even if it is carried out unpaid, and the implementing ordinance expressly treats interns and volunteers as forms of dependent employment. In other words, "unpaid" or "educational" wording ddoes not remove the need for immigration and labour-market scrutiny. 1

Where a trainee or stagiaire arrangement falls within the ordinary third-country labour-market framework, the core salary rule is Article 22 oof the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration: admission for gainful activity is only possible if local, occupational and sectoral pay and working conditions are respected. The implementing ordinance says those standards are judged by reference to legislation, collective agreements, standard employment contracts, conditions for the same work in the same undertaking and sector, and statistical wage surveys; it also requires the employer to submit a contract or equivalent confirmation stating duration, employment conditions and remuneration. 2

For the bilateral **young** **professionals** **/ stagiaires** route under Article 42 OASA, SEM's current public guidance is explicit that the trainee must be paid the local and sector-standard wage and that remuneration should at least reach the level of a career starter. The role must sit within the profession or field already learned, and part-time work or self-employment is not permitted. The SEM guide and standard contract require a written contract with an integrated training programme covering pay, hours, holidays, insurance and travel costs, and SEM's directives add that the training programme must appear in the employment contract and that the number of stagiaires should not exceed 5% of the undertaking's workforce. 3

The compliance risk in low-paid trainee structures is therefore twofold. First,the authorities may conclude that the contract fails Article 22 because the wage and conditions are below local or sector standards. Secondly, they may conclude that the supposed internship is in substance ordinary operational labour. Geneva's official guidance states that a placement presented as an internship but in reality equivalent to a job or first job must be paid at least at the applicable minimum, CBA, standard contract or local "usages", and mislabelling can trigger sanctions. Ticino's official tripartite guidance likewise tests whether the arrangement is genuinely formative, supervised, and only marginally profitable to the employer.4

Official materials are comparatively strong on salary benchmarking, written contractual evidence, training content, supervision and anti-abuse concerns, but comparatively thin on whether housing, board, stipends or third-party maintenance can substitute for a compliant wage in trainee permits. The safest authority-based drafting position is therefore to treat cash salary, allowances,expense reimbursements and benefits in kind as separate concepts; to disclose each separately; and **not** **to**

assume that private support or in-kind benefits cure an otherwise non-compliant salary under Article 22.5

# Structural overview of trainee salary and working-condition analysis

For internal immigration content, the safest Swiss analytical sequence is as follows. First,identify the route:bilateral young professional / stagiaire under Article 42 OASA, a study-linked compulsory internship under Articles 39 or 40 OASA, an exchange programme under Article 41 OASA, or an ordinary third-country employment case under Articles 18 to 24 FNIA/AIG. That classification matters because the bilateral stagiaire route relaxes some ordinary labour-market barriers, but it does not remove salary and working-condition scrutiny. 6

Secondly, test whether the activity is genuinely a training placement rather than ordinary productive labour. Official Swiss materials repeatedly look for a real training purpose, a link to the person's prior studies or learned profession, a written training programme or objectives, supervision, and temporary duration. Zurich requires obligatory foreign-study internships to remain limited and tied to university evidence; Geneva distinguishes internships from jobs and requires recognised training or insertion conditions for exemption from minimum wage rules; Ticino uses a structured set of criteria focused on learning objectives, supervision, non-repetitive work and the absence of substantial profit motive for the employer.7

Thirdly, assess salary and conditions under Article 22 AIG and Article 22 ODASA. That means looking at local and sector norms, collective agreements where they exist, standard employment contracts where they exist, same-employer comparators where available, and official wage statistics or recognised wage tools. SEM's directives name sources such as the Zurich Lohnbuch, ICT salary guidance, the Federal Statistical Office's Salarium tool, SECO's national wage calculator and the SGB wage calculator as aids for salary assessment. 8

Fourthly, examine the full remuneration package rather than the headline figure alone. The authorities expect written information on place of work, function, duration, working time, base salary, supplements, social benefits and deductions. For young professionals, the SEM standard form separately requests gross salary, hours, holidays, health and accident insurance and travel costs,and requires additional benefits to be stated. This is why internal content should always separate salary, stipend, allowance, expenses, housing and benefits in kind instead of blurring them together. 9

Finally, if the proposal asks the migrant to rely on unusually low pay, free accommodation, broad deductions, or private support from a host or family, treat the case as higher risk. Official Swiss immigration guidance does not create a general trainee carve-out for underpayment. On the contrary, it stresses protection against wage and social undercutting, and cantonal practice explicitly targets abusive use of internships as cheaplabour. 10

## Primary legal texts

The core federal statute is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration.** For salary and working-condition analysis, the load-bearing provisions are Article 11 on gainful activity and the need for authorisation; Articles 18 to 24 on paid employment, quotas, priority, salary/working conditions, qualifications and housing; Article 30 on exceptions to ordinary admission conditions; and Article 40 on cantonal labour-market pre-approval. Article 22 is the pivotal salary norm, and Article 24 adds the separate requirement of appropriate accommodation. 22

<!-- 5 -->

The implementing text is the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment.** Article 1a is especially important because it explicitly classifies interns and volunteers as dependent employment. Article 22 specifies how local and occupational standards are judged and what the employer must submit. Articles 39, 41 and 42 are the most relevant trainee provisions in practice: compulsory study internships require Article 22 compliance and appropriate housing; international exchange under Article 41 also requires Article 22 compliance and appropriate housing; and stagiaire permits under Article 42 follow the relevant treaties and administrative arrangements, with SEM able to issue and extend permits within the 18-month limit. 23

On working-time and holiday baselines, the most relevant federal labour-law texts are the **LabourAct** and the **Code** **of Obligations,** not because this pack is giving employment-law advice, but because Swviss immigration authorities assess whether the offered conditions are ordinary and lawful. SECO summarises the Labour Act position that the maximum working week is ordinarily 45 hours for office/ technical/industrial staff and 50 hours for other workers, with daily and weekly rest minima. SECO also summarises Article 329a CO: the minimum annual holiday entitlement is four weeks, rising to five weeks for workers up to age 20. The SEM standard young-professional contract repeats the same holiday minimums. 24

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notesOfficial guidance and administrative materials

SEM's current public page on **young** **professionals** is the most directly usable authority for the bilateral stagiaire route. It states that Switzerland has bilateral agreements enabling a maximum 18-month stay for persons with completed training who wish to improve their professional and language skills in Switzerland; that the stay must be in the learned profession or study field; that part-time work andself-employment are not permitted; and that the trainee must receive a local and sector-standard wage. The same page also states that pay should at least reach the level of career starters and links to the official guide, application form, standard contract and workplace-rights note. 25

**The** **SEM** **young** **professionals guide** adds operational detail of real importance for drafting. It says remuneration must be local and sector-standard "as for career entrants"; where a collective agreement exists, the pay must follow it, and otherwise cantonal wage guidance and sector association recommendations apply. It also states that labour leasingis not generally allowed, that several stays are possible only up to a total of 18 months, and that multiple young professionals in one undertaking must not exceed 5% of the workforce. The same guide requires a written contract with an integrated training programme stating the type of work, training content, duration, pay, probation period, notice,working hours, holiday entitlement, health and accident insurance and travel costs. 26

**The** **SEM** **standard** **employment agreement** is one of the strongest forms-based sources in this area because it shows what the administration expects to see on paper. It contains fields for gross salary, daily/weekly working hours, holidays, health and accident insurance and travel costs, plus a distinct training programme section. Its notes say conditions must match the local and occupational norm or the collective agreement, that all additional benefits should be indicated, that the minimum holiday entitlement is four weeks per year or five weeks under age 20, that part-time employment will not be approved, and that the employer must ensure appropriate protection against the economic consequences of illness; in the Philippine special case, repatriation of remains must also be insured. 27

<!-- 6 -->

SEM's broader **foreigners-directives chapter on gainful activity** is equally important outside the Article 42 route. For Article 22 assessment generally, it states that only contractual salary elements can in principle be considered in salary evaluation; that authorities should ensure payment of social-security contributions and adequate sickness and accident insurance; and that authorities need written information on the place of work, function, duration, working time, base salary, supplements, social benefits and deductions, supported by a binding written contract. It also points officers to salary tools including the Zurich Lohnbuch, the FSO's Salarium, SECO's national wage calculator and union wage calculators. 28

The same SEM directives contain two further points highly relevant to low-paid trainee structures. First, in the dedicated stagiaire section, SEM describes stagiaires as persons aged 18 to 30/35 with completed vocational training who go to the partner country for a limited period, normally up to 18 months, to improve professional and language knowledge, and expressly says that remuneration must correspond to local and sector tariffs. Secondly, in the special chapter on internships for provisionally admitted persons and refugees, SEM explains that internships exist to improve employability and protect against wage and social undercutting, warns against abuse through recruitment as cheap labour under the cover of an internship, and allows temporary weighting of the usual wage only in that special integration context and subject to parity or tripartite confirmation. That latter flexibility should not be imported into ordinary third-country trainee cases. 29

SECO materials are useful for the **working-conditions** **baseline,** rather than for route-specific immigration rules. SECO summarises the Labour Act rules on maximum working week,daily rest, weekly half-days and overtime, all of which matter because trainee contracts that undercut ordinary Swiss labour standards are more likely to fail an Article 22 review or to attract cantonal labour-office concern. SECO likewise summarises the minimum holiday entitlement under Article 329a CO. 30

## Cantonal practice notes

**Geneva** is the clearest canton for anti-abuse guidance. Its official pages say an internship must be distinguished from a job; an internship is recognised in particular if it forms part of a training or insertion scheme provided by cantonal or federal law or if it complies with conditionns fixed by the Conseil de surveillance du marché de l'emploi. To be exempt from Geneva's minimum wage, the internship must be legally recognised and satisfy those conditions; otherwise it must be paid according to the applicable minimum wage, CBA, standard contract or local "usages". Geneva is explicit that this includes placements which are in reality equivalent to a job or first job, and that sanctions follow if a person engaged as an intern is actually occupying such a role without proper remuneration. Geneva also states that foreign trainee recruitment is handled as ordinary gainful employment, and that third-country possibilities are very limited and subject to the normal work-authorisation criteria. 31

**Zurich** provides strong evidence guidance. Its general third-country guidance says the employer must provide a current, complete employment contract matching local and sector-standard conditions. Its student-and-intern page says compulsory internships for foreign students are only possible in specific cases; the internship may be granted once for up to 12 months; the number of short-term foreign workers in the undertaking must not exceed one quarter of the total workforce; and local, occupational and sector-standard salary and working conditions must be met. Zurich also publishes the annual Lohnbuch Schweiz, which the SEM directives themselves cite as a recognised salary-assessment tool. 32

**Ticino** provides the most useful structured "real internship" test. Its 2024 tripartite guidelines say that an internship is primarily a supplement to a person's training path or, in certain cases, a reintegration or reorientation measure, and not a simple occupational programme. A real internship exists only if at least one first-level training or reintegration criterion is met and at least eight of ten second-level

formative criteria are satisfied. Those second-level criteria include written objectives, identified supervisory persons, planned review meetings, an end-certificate setting out learning achieved, non-repetitive work, training-based limits on overtime, only marginal profit for the employer, no guaranteed hire at the outset, and a sustainable proportion of interns within the undertaking. This is powerful official mnaterial for identifying roles that are really cheap labour rather than training. 20

## Practical legal analysis

A Swiss trainee or stagiaire case should be analysed on the assumption that **salary** **scrutiny** **remains** **live** **unless** **a specific** **official source** **says** **otherwise.** The fact that the route is educational, temporary, bilateral or attached to a training programme does not of itself displace Article 22 AIG. The decisive immigration question is usually whether the role is genuinely formative and whether the overall conditions reflect Swiss local and sector standards rather than a disguised effort to import low-paid labour. 33

**On** **salary** **versus stipend versus** **allowance,** Swiss official materials focus on substance rather than labels. If the role is gainful activity, the authorities will examine remuneration against local and sector norms. SEM's current public guidance for young professionals says the pay must be local and sector-standard and should at least match career-starter remuneration. SEM's general directives then say that, in salary assessment, only contractually agreed salary elements can in principle be counted. The safer internal drafting position is therefore this: if a "stipend" or "allowance" is really the consideration for the work, it should be described as remuneration and benchmarked under Article 22; if a payment is genuinely expense reimbursement, it should be shown separately and should not be relied upon to make up a deficient wage. 34

**On** **expense** **reimbursement,** Swiss federal law gives an explicit rule for posted workers: travel, food and lodging reimbursements linked to posting are not treated as salary. The immigration materials located for local trainee or stagiaire hires do not provide an equally explicit general formula. That gap matters. The conservative answer for case preparation is to keep expense reimbursements distinct from salary, document them separately, and avoid presenting them as though they cure an under-mnarket wage. The same caution should be applied to one-off relocation payments. 35

**On** **housing,** **board** **and** **benefits** in **kind,** the federal immigration rules do not provide a single general conversion formula for trainee permits. What they do show is that housing can be an admission requirement in some routes, since AIG Article 24 requires appropriate accommodation and OASA Articles 39 and 41 expressly make it a condition for compoulsory internships and exchange-based admissions. The young-professional contract separately asks for salary and for additional benefits, and SEM's general directives require the authorities to see the whole remuneration package including deductions. The safest internal drafting line is therefore to identify any accommodation or board arrangement clearly, say whether it is employer-provided or privately arranged, describe its contractual treatment, and avoid assuming that it replaces the need for a compliant cash wage unless a directly applicable CBA or standard contract clearly allows that treatment. 36

On **working hours, holidays annd working conditions,** immigration analysis should use labour-law minima as a floor, not as a complete answer. Young professionals must have contractually stated working hours and holidays, and part-time work is not allowed on that route. More generally, Swiss authorities expect the contract to set out woorking time and other conditions, and SECO summarises that the maximum weekly working time is normally 45 or 50 hours depending on the category, alongside mandatory daily and weekly rest. Holiday entitlement cannot fall below four weeks annually, or five weeks for workers up to age 20. If an application proposes trainee-style pay but ordinary full-output hours, that combination is more likely to attract scrutiny. 37

<!-- 8 -->

On **deductions,** SEM's directives matter because they say the authorities need written information on deductions as part of the Article 22 assessment. As a result, internal content should treat deductions as a separate disclosure item rather than subsuming them into vague "package" language.A trainee contract that advertises an apparently acceptable gross salary but then contains broad unexplained deductions for accommodation, meals, transport or administration will be structurally weaker than a contract that transparently sets out each item and still leaves the worker on a clearly compliant wage basis. 17

**On maintenance and third-party support arrangements,** the official trainee materials located in this research do not establish a general rule allowing maintenance from parents, the host family, the sending institution or another sponsor to replace compliant remuneration where Article 22 scrutiny applies. All the route-specific materials focus instead on the Swiss employer's contract, the wage, the training programme and working conditions. For internal drafting, the safe legal position is to treat third-party maintenance as separate from remuneration: it may helpshow practical subsistence, but it should not be presented as a substitute for a compliant salary unless a route-specific official source expressly permits that approach. 38

On **evidence,** a strong trainee/stagiaire file should normally include: the signed employment contract; the integrated training programme or objectives; proof of the applicant's qualification and its match to the role; a wage-benchmark explanation using the relevant CBA, CTT, cantonal wage guide, Lohnbuch, Salarium or other recognised tool; stated working hours, holidays, insurance and travel-cost arrangements; a schedule of any supplementary benefits and deductions; evidence of adequate housing where the route or facts require it; and, where the case depends on a recognised internship structure, evidence of supervision and training content. That evidence set follows directly from the ordinance, SEM's directives, the SEM guide and cantonal practice. 39

The principal **underpayment** risk **indicators** are now fairly clear in the Swiss sources. They include: no meaningful training plan; work outside the learnned profession or study field; little or no supervision; repetitive productive tasks; a role that looks like first-line operational labour; remuneration far below local entrant level; unexplained reliance on housing or allowances instead of wages; part-time on a route where part-time is prohibited; a high proportion of trainees within the undertaking; or contractual silence on deductions, insurance or travel costs. Geneva's and Ticino's materials are especially useful authorities for articulating these abuse indicators. 40

The one area where care is needed is the **special integration-stage regime for provisionally** **admitted** **persons and** **refugees.** SEM's directives allow some temporary wage weighting there because the explicit policy goal is improved employability, and even then the variationshould be fixed or confirmed by parity or tripartite bodies. That special permission should not be generalised to bilateral young professionals, foreign students on compulsory internships, oor ordinary third-country trainee cases. Internal content should flag it as a special-case regime only. 21

## Drafting cautions and source list

When drafting future internal content, the following cautions should be treated as hard rules. Do **not** say or imply that trainee status removes Article 22 salary scrutiny. Do **not** say or imply that unpaid internships are immigration-safe, because activity that normally yields gain remains gainful activity even if carried out free of charge. Do **not** blur salary, stipend, allowance, expense reimbursement,

<!-- 9 -->

housing and benefits in kind into a single "package". Do not present third-party maintenanceas curing a substandard wage unless an official route-specific source expressly says so. Do not generalise the refugee/provisionally admitted integration-stage wage flexibility to ordinary trainee permits. And do **not** describe low-paid, productive, unsupervised operational roles as compliant merely because a training note has been added to the paperwork. 41
