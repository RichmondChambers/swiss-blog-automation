# Swiss authority pack on notification procedure and permit-required work

Prepared for internal use in knowledge/legalauthorities/workpermits/routespecific/ postedworkernotification/ . Suggested filename:

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law starts from a broad concept of gainful employment. The SEM's AIG guidance states that gainful activity is interpreted widely and can cover both employed and self-employed activity as well as cross-border service provision; the decisive question is not whether the person is paid in a particular case,but whether the activity is ordinarily directed towards gainful work in Switzerland. The SEM's VFP guidance then adds that, as a rule, all gainful activity in Switzerland by foreign persons or by undertakings seated abroad is permit-controlled unless a specific notification regime applies. That means the notification procedure is not "no authorisation"; it is a distinct statutory form of authorisation for narrowly defined short-term cases. 1

For route-design purposes, the key divide is between five different situations that must not be collapsed into one another. First, some short business-presence activities are not treated as gainful employment at all, such as short business meetings, negotiations, strategy meetings limited to information exchange, and passive attendance at conferences. Secondly, short employment in Switzerland by a Swiss employer can, for eligible EU/EFTA nationals, use notification if it does not exceed three months in the calendar year. Thirdly, genuine cross-border service provision by an EU/EFTA undertaking or EU/EFTA self-employed provider can use notification up to 90 effective working days in the calendar year, subject to the specific conditions below. Fourthly, posted-worker labour-law rules apply to workers temporarily sent to Switzerland by a foreign employer to perform a service, regardless of whether the migration route is notification or permnit. Fifthly, ordinary Swiss employment, cross-border labour leasing,and longer or non-eligible assignments fall into the permit route instead. 2

Notification may be sufficient only for specifically eligible persons. The SEM's 2026 VFP guidance identifies three core categories: EU/EFTA nationals taking up a Swiss job limited to three months; EU/ EFTA self-employed service providers established in an EU/EFTA state; and workers posted by an undertaking established in an EU/EFTA state, regardless of the worker's nationality. For posted third-country nationals, there is an additional condition: before posting to Switzerland they must already have been durably integrated into the regular labour market of an EU/EFTA state, meaning at least 12months' residence status of the relevant kind. The same guidance explains that the UK now sits outside the AFMP, but a separate Swiss-UK Services Mobility Agreement keeps a similar notification route open for UK-based service providers and workers posted by UK-based undertakings; that does not, however, restore notification for short local employment with a Swiss employer. 3

Where the assignment exceeds the notification conditions, or where the facts do not fit a notification category, a permit route is required. For EU/EFTA service provision above 90 effective working days, SEM guidance is explicit that the AFMP no longer gives an entitlement, the case falls under the AIG and VZAE, and the permit decision is discretionary, subject to labour-market review and overall economic

interest. For non-EU/EFTA nationals, official SEM material states that admission is generally restricted to highly qualified workers and proceeds through an employer-led cantonal and SEM permit process; even for stays under four months, this remains an application-and-approval procedure rather than the EU/ EFTA notification route. 4

Posted-worker rules must also be kept analytically separate from migration authorisation. The Posted Workers Act and related ordinance regulate Swiss minimum pay and working conditions for workers posted to Switzerland by foreign employers. The SECO posting materials describe posting as a situation in which a foreign employer temporarily sends an employee abroad to perform services on the employer's behalf and at the employer's expense, while the employment contract remains in force.This is different from local Swiss employment and also different from labour leasing. 5

The public official materials are strong on the broad architecture, the day thresholds, and the exclusion of labour leasing, but thinner on a single codified test for all borderline factual cases. In practice, the business-visit/work distinction and the service-provision/labour-leasing distinction remain highly fact-sensitive and are illustrated mainly through SEM examples and administrative guidance rather than one exhaustive statutory checklist. Future content should therefore be drafted conservatively and escalate borderline cases. 6

## Structural overview of notification versus work permit

**A** **true** **business** **visit** sits outside gainful employment only where the activity matches the conservative official examples: short representative visits, contract negotiations and signing, information-only strategy meetings, preparatory visits before a later work stay, and passive attendance at conferences. The same SEM annex treats on-the-job training integrated into the productive process, internships, repair and maintenance work, project implementation, film and artistic production, pastoral work, and repeated management visits for staff appraisals or goal-setting as gainful activity instead. A label such as "business trip" or "project visit" is therefore not decisive. 7

**A** **short** **local** hire **by** a **Swiss** **employer** is not a posting. It is a Swiss employment relationship. For EU/ EFTA nationals, official SEM and cantonal material says that employment with a Swiss employer for up to three months in the calendar year can use notification; above three months, an L poermit or another ordinary stay/work authorisation route is required. The Swiss employer files the notification, and for this category the filing deadline is no later than the day before work starts. For workers resident abroad, the ordinary route may later become a frontier commuter permit rather than a Swiss residence permit, but that is stilI an ordinary permit route rather than a service-provider notification route. 8

**A** **genuine** **cross-border** **service** **assignment** can use notification only if it is areal service carried out by an eligible provider for a limited duration. The AFMP/VFP guidance describes this as execution of assignments or works contracts for a service recipient without relocating the provider's residence or seat to Switzerland. For EU/EFTA self-employed providers and for workers genuinely posted by an EU/ EFTA undertaking, notification is possible up to 90 effective working days per calendar year, subject to the additional posted-third-country-national rule and the branch-specific notice rules. 9

**A** **posted** **worker** remains employed by the foreign employer during the Swiss assignment. The foreign employer stays the employer; the worker performs the service on the foreign employer's behalf and at its expense; and the home-country employmment relationship stays in force. That foreign-employer continuity matters because where the reality is that labour is simply being supplied into the Swiss undertaking's operational structure, the case starts to look like labour leasing or local Swiss employment rather than posting. 10

<!-- 2 -->

**A** **labour-leasing** **or** **staff-leasing** **arrangement** cannot be rescued by calling it "service provision".The SEM user guide and VFP guidance are explicit that the AFMP does not liberalise job placement and staff leasing, andI that direct and indirect leasing of staff from abroad into Switzerland remains prohibited under the Recruitment Act. This is one of the main error points for automated content: a foreign company cannot simply "post" staff to a Swiss end-user where the legal and factual arrangement is really hiring-out of labour. 11

**A** **permit route** is required where the facts fall outside those narrow boxes: the work exceeds 90effective working days for cross-border services; the worker is locally hired in Switzerland for more than three months; the provider is a non-EU/EFTA person who does not fall under a specific short posted-service rule; the employer is established in a third state; the worker fails the 12-month integration condition; the arrangement is labour leasing; or the activity is gainful work but not within an eligible notification category. For EU/EFTA services over 90 days, there is no AFMP entitlement; for non-EU/EFTA nationals, the route is the ordinary work-authorisation system. 12

### Primary legal texts

The core primary texts for this route cluster are the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** **between Switzerland and the EU** and **Annex** I; the **Foreign Nationals and Integration Act;** the **Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment;** **the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons;** the **Posted Workers Act** and its ordinance; and the **Federal Act on Employment Services and the** **Hiring** **of Services.** Official SECO posting material expressly identifies AFMP art. 5 and Annex I arts.17-23, the free-movement ordinance, and the Posted Workers Act as the key labour-law framework for postings, while SEM's legal-source pages list the AIG and VZAE, and SECO's employment-services pages list the AVG as the relevant legal basis for placement and staff leasing. 32

<!-- 8 -->

For the **notification route** **itself,** the most directly useful official articulation appears in the SEM's VFP directives. They state that the AFMP permits gainful activity of up to three months or 90 days in a calendar year by simple prior notification; they identify the eligible categories; they link the route to VFP art. 9 and AIG art. 14; and they continue by tying the simplified filing to Posted Workers Act art. 6, Posted Workers Ordinance art. 6, and VFP art. 9(1bis). The same directives also provide the key legal anchors for the 12-month integration rule for posted third-country nationals and for the first-day sector list. 33

For the **boundary** **between** **notification** **and** **permit,** the same SEM directives are again the most operationally useful official source. They identify VFP art. 15 and AFMP Annex I arts. 17(b) and 20 as the basis for saying that cross-border services over 90 working days fall outside the liberalised AFMP regime; and they state that such cases are then governed by the AIG and the VZAE, including the labour-market and economic-interest conditions. 34

For the **posted-worker** **labour-law** **overlay,** the Posted Workers Act and ordinance matter independently of whether the migration route is notification or permit. Official posting materials explain that the PWA applies Swiss minimum labour and pay rules to posted workers and that foreign employers must guarantee at least the mandatory Swiss conditions identified by that legislation and related instruments. 35

For the **staff-leasing** **exclusion,** the controlling legal combination is AFMP Annex I art. 22(3) with AVG/ RecA art. 12(2). The SEM's own user guide and VFP directives expressly use those provisions to say that direct and indirect staff leasing from abroad remnains excluded and cannot be done under the notification route. 36

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal administrative sources presently available are the SEM's **2026 VFP** **directives,** the SEM's **2026 AIG chapter 4 directives,** the SEM's **notification-procedure pages and** **user guide,** the SEM's **cross-border service-provision factsheets,** the SEM annex on the **concept of** **gainful employment,** SECO's **"Arbeit korrekt** **melden"** pages, and the fedderal posting information platform at **entsendung.admin.ch** **/ posting.admin.ch.** Together these materials provide the current operational explanation of who may use notification, when a permit is required, how the day counting works, how the posted-worker regime overlays the migration route, what proof self-employed providers must carry, and what sanctions follow from breaches. 38

The official online notification guidance is especially important for content control because it supplies the practical boundary rules that are often misstated in general summaries. It states that each gainful activity must be notified separately; that posted/self-employed activities usually require filing at least eight days before work begins; that Swiss local hires of up to three months must be notified no later than the day before work starts; that only clearly defined emergencies allow work to begin during the eight-day period; and that the route is now administered through EasyGov. 39

The SEM annex on the **concept** of **gainful employment** is unusually valuable for route drafting because it gives concrete examples of what is and is not work. It confirms that short business meetings and passive conference attendance sit outside gainful employment, but that productive training, service work, project implementation, and repeated managerial functions in Switzerland count as gainful activity. This annex should be treated as a high-value administrative source for drafting boundaries, even though it is not itself legislation. 40

SECO's "Arbeit korrekt melden" pages are the best current federal pages for an operational split between **Swiss employment, poosting,** and **self-employed service provision.** They repeatedly distinguish employment in Switzerland by Swiss employers from the posting of workers by foreign undertakings; set out different obligations for employers, workers, and self-employed providers; and provide the current first-day sectors and third-country sector rules. 41

## Cantonal practice notes and practical legal analysis

Cantonal practice pages broadly mirror the federal framework, but they are useful because they show how the federal split is operationalised. **Zurich** states that EU/EFTA nationals working up to 90 days per calendar year can use notification, that activity clearly exceeding 90 days needs a permit before work starts, and that extending cross-border services beyond 90 days is only exceptionally allowed; Zurich also emphasises overall economic interest, qualifications, and local salary and working conditions for such cases. **Basel-Stadt** explains the same federal split between notification up to 90 effective working days and authorisation beyond that threshold. **Geneva** frames the route similarly, saying that an EU/ EFTA undertaking or self-employed person either notifies the Confederation or obtains a cantonal permit depending on duration, while third-country undertakings and self-employed providers must submit a full cantonal file, in Geneva's published practice, six to eight weeks before the assignment. 42

As a matter of practical legal analysis, the most reliable internal test is not "how short is the stay?" but rather **what legal structure the facts actually disclose.** If the person comes only for negotiations, information exchange, or passive conference attendance, the case may be a business visit outside gainful employment. If the person is locally hired by a Swiss employer, it is Swiss employment, not posting. If the person stays employed by a foreign undertaking to fulfil a defined external service contract, it may be a genuine posting or self-employed cross-border service. If the Swiss end-user is in reality receiving labour under its own operational control, the case may be labour leasing or local employment instead. The SEM and SECO materials do not provide one single codified control test, but those are the recurring structural markers visible across the official sources. 43

This is why "short-termproject work" is a legally weak route label. A short project can fall into very different buckets. An EU/EFTA engineer locally employed by a Swiss entity for two months is a short Swiss hire and may use notification. A technician sent by an EU/EFTA company for six weeks to perform a true service contract in Switzerland may be a posted worker and may also use notification.A foreign technician carrying out maintenance work in Switzerland is nonetheless doing gainful work. A foreign manager coming repeatedly to Switzerland to conduct staff evaluations is also doing gainfuI work according to SEM examples. By contrast, short negotiations on a future acquisition or passive attendance at a conference are not, on the official examples, gainful work. Content should therefore analyse function and legal structure first, then duration. 44

The **role** **of the** **Swiss** **recipient** **of** **services** also needs careful drafting. In migration terms, the foreign employer usually files the notification for posted workers and the self-employed provider files it for themselves; the Swiss client is not normally the filing party. But the Swiss side can still matter legally. For self-employed providers, the official proof package may require a written confirmation from the

Swiss client if there is no written contract. In construction, the first contractor has a statutory diligence burden and can face joint liability if its subcontractor breaches mandatory minimum conditions. In other words, the Swiss recipient is not automatically a bystander, even where it is not the formal migration filer. 30

Two recurring threshold points also deserve emphasis. First, the **Swiss** **notification** **cap** **for** **cross-border services is 90 effective working days per calendar year,** counted for both undertaking and worker; it is not the Schengen short-stay rule. Secondly, the **eight-day** **notification-free** **period** for many service assignments is not an eight-day-per-trip rule but a cumulative calendar-year rule, again counted for both undertaking and worker. Official entry guidance on Schengen short stays uses the completely different 90 days in 180 days test for Schengen presence. Future route content should never collapse these two systems into one sentence. 45

### Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

Do not say that notification means the worker "does not need work authorisation". For this route, the safer formulation is that Swiss law allows the work to be carried out **by** **prior** **notification** **instead** **of** by **permit,** but only where the facts fit the statutory categories and thresholds. 46

Do not use "posted worker", "service provider", "business visitor", "short-term worker","cross-border commuter", "self-employed contractor", and "temporary worker" as if they were interchangeable. The official materials treat them as different legal categories with different consequences. 47

Do not assume that a short visit is a business visit. The SEM's own examples treat productive training, implementation work, repair and maintenance work, and even some recurring management functions as gainful employment. 48

Do not assume that a foreign company can "post" staff when the Swiss end-user is in substance simply receiving labour. Cross-border staff leasing remains excluded, and direct or indirect labour leasing from abroad is not permitted. 24

Do not state that any EU/EFTA link is enough for notification. For posted third-country nationals, the prior 12-month durabole integration condition matters. For UK nationals, notification remains available for qualifying UK service provision but not for short Swiss local employment. 49

Do not present permits above 90 days for EU/EFTA service provision as routine. The official line is that there is **no** **legal entitlement,** the route is discretionary, labour-market controlled, and to be handled restrictively. 50

Do not forget parallel **labour-law** **and** **regulated-profession** overlays. Notification may solve the migration filing question without removing Posted Workers Act obligations, and some professions or sectors may still need separate regulatory permissions. 51

Do not confuse the Swiss notification thresholds with Schengen stay counting. The Swiss route uses effective workdays in a calendar year; Schengen short-stay rules use days of presence in a rolling 180-day period. 52

Official public materials are not equally detailed on every borderline issue. For automated content, that means any borderline scenario involving operational control by the Swiss recipient, recurring managerial presence, hybrid training/work, or contractor/self-employed status should be treated as escalation territory rather than as routine notification content. ⁵3
