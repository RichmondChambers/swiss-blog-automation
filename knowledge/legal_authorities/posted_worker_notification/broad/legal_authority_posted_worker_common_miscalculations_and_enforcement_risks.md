# Swiss Authority Pack on Posted Worker andNotification Route Miscalculations and

## Enforcement Risks

## Scope and executive summary

This pack is designed for internal drafting on Swiss posted-worker and notification-route compliance. It focuses chiefly on the EU/EFTA notification route and the posted-worker enforcement regime under the Entsendegesetz, while flagging the main deviations for third-country employers, third-country posted workers, Swiss direct hires, and UK service-provider scenarios where the official guidance expressly does so. 1

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss posted-worker compliance sits at the junction of two distinct legal questions. The first is an **admission-route** **question**: whether the activity may start under the short-term notification procedure or instead requires a prior work permit. The second is a **labour-market enforcement question**:once Swiss work is being performed, which Swiss minimum pay and working-condition rules apply, how compliance is checked, and what administrative consequences follow if the rules are breached. Official materials repeatedly separate these two layers, and future drafting should do the same. 2

The most common failures described by the official materials are not limited to simple late filings. They include using a business-visitor label for work that is in fact gainful activity, using notification where the service falls outside the notification route, miscounting the eight-day and ninety-day thresholds, starting work before the eight-day waiting period has elapsed, entering the wrong site or personnel data and not re-filing correctly, underpaying posted workers by miscalculating Swiss wage components, failing to keep or produce inspection documents, and misdescribing labour supply or control relationships as self-employment or subcontracting. 3

The enforcement picture is material but structured. Cantonal authorities impose administrative sanctions under the Entsendegesetz; paritarian commissions and tripartite commissions carry out much of the checking; and SECO maintains a public list of service providers who have received final Swiss service bans. The headline statutory sanctions described in the official material are fines of up to CHF 5,000 for notification breaches and, for serious breaches of wage or other Entsendegesetz duties, fines of up to CHF 30,000 and Swiss service bans of one to five years, with control costs and, in some sectors, additional collectively agreed penalties also possible. 4

Inspection risk should not be portrayed as theoretical. In the 2024 SECO FlaM report, the enforcement bodies checked 25% of posted workers and 27% of crooss-border self-employed service providers from the EU/EFTA area. The same report recorded a 20% wage-undercutting rate at company level and a 17% rate at person level among checked posting companies, while 7% of completed self-employment checks raised suspected bogus self-employment. In the same year, cantonal authorities imposed 1,986 fines and 619 Swiss service bans. 5

One important nuance must be kept in view. Swiss enforcement is strongest where there is a **binding** **minimum** under an allgemeinverbindlich erklärter Gesamtarbeitsvertrag or a Normalarbeitsvertrag with mandatory minima. In sectors without such binding minima, tripartite commissions monitor customary local, occupational, or sectoral wages and seek wage adjustment through conciliation, but the public enforcement materials state that those commissions do **not** themselves have sanctioning power merely for undercutting customary wages; other legal breaches remain reportable and sanctionable. Future drafting should not collapse these different wage-enforcement situations into one undifferentiated "salary breach" category. 6

### B. Structural overview of common miscalculations and enforcement risks

For drafting purposes, the official materials are best organised into five risk clusters. First, **classification** **errors**: business visit versus gainful work, self-employed versus employed status, and subcontracting versus prohibited labour leasing. Secondly, **route** **errors**: using notification when the activity lies ooutside the notification route because of the employer's location, worker status, sector, or duration. Thirdly, **filing** **and** **counting** **errors**: eight-day exemptions, eight-day waiting periodds,ninety-day calculations, repeat assignments, project restarts, and correction mechanics. Fourthly, **substantive** **employment-condition** **errors**: Swiss minimum wages, customary wages, working time, holiday pay, supplements, and the treatment of expenses. Fifthly, **inspection** **and** **consequence** **errors**: failure to produce documents, failure to cooperate, public listing, service bans, and principal-contractor exposure in construction chains. 7

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

In practice, the most operationally useful public sources are not the bare statutes, but the federal guidance that sits around them. The key materials are the SEM pages on the notification route and related FAQ; the VFP guidance on day-counting, over-ninety-day services, and excluded categories; the SEM annex on what is and is not gainful activity; the SECO note on international wage comparison; the SECO self-employment verification guidance; the SECO sanction recommendation addressed to cantonal authorities; the annual SECO FlaM enforcement repoort; and the RESA list showing currently banned service providers. These materials should be treated as guidance or enforcement practice unless they are directly reproducing statutory rules. 32

A particularly important drafting distinction is that the **SECO** **sanction** **catalogue** **is** **not** **itself** **legislation.** It is expressly framed as a recommendation, based on Article 9 EntsG and Article 17 EntsV, issued by SECO to cantonal sanctioning authorities. It is still highly useful, because it shows how the competent authorities are officially advised to scale late notices, false notices, wage breaches, non-payment of final sanctions and information-duty violations. 33

The annual FlaM report is the strongest official source for enforcement intensity, sector focus and the distinction between suspected breaches and final sanctions. It also explains the institutional division of labour: paritarian commissions monitor sectors with generally binding collective agreements, while tripartite commissions monitor the rest of the labour market and pursue conciliation where customary wages are undercut. That distinction should shape how future content describes "inspection risk" and "salary non-compliance". 34

### F. Cantonal practice notes

The cantonal sources located for this pack are most useful as **operational notes,** not as stand-alone legal bases. Zurich's official notification page is especially detailed. It states that the day of filing counts towards the eight-day waiting period, that work may start only on the ninth day, that urgent exceptions are limited to genuine emergencies, that all days entered into the system are counted against the quota, that wrong worksites require a new filing with a fresh eight-day period, that additional or substitute wvorkers require a new filing before work begins, and that same-day credit for unused days requires notice by noon. It also states that direct pay/condition breaches can attract fines up to CHF 30,000 or one- to five-year Swiss service bans, and that seIf-employed persons unable to show the three core documents risk a fine of up to CHF 5,000 plus a prohibition on continuing the work. 35

Geneva's official pages are useful for two recurring drafting points. First, they underline that only **effective** **workdays** should be announced for short posted-worker or self-employed service cases. Secondly,they show the operational shift once the activity exceeds ninety days orthree consecutive months: the case moves into permit territory, the cantonal file should be sent 6-8 weeks in advance, the authorities examine economic interest, qualifications, quotas and salary conditions, and work cannot begin before authorisation is issued. 36

Ticino's official pages broadly mirror the federal rules but are useful for emphasis. They restate the first-day sectors, the more-than-eight-days rule in other sectors, the twelve-month EU/EFTA labour-market integration requirement for certain third-country posted workers, and the fact that SECO keeps an online list of service providers finally sannctioned with service bans. That is useful supporting authority where content needs to reflect cantonal-facing administration in an exposed enforcement canton. 37

## Case law and practical analysis

### G. Case law notes, if relevant

Publicly accessible official case-law material on routine notification and posting mistakes appears much thinner than the federal guidance and enforcement material. The most relevant official item located for this pack is the Federal Supreme Court search synopsis for **2C843/2015** **(24 July** **2017),** which states that the claim failed because the application for notification had been filed late under the rules then applicable. Used cautiously, that official synopsis supports the broader point already made in SEM and cantonal guidance: late filing can be fatal to reliance on the notification route. Beyond that, the operative public sources remain the statutes, ordinances and administrative materials rather than case law. 38

### H.Practical legal analysis

**Misclassification of business visitors and workers.** The official SEM annex **is** clear that short meetings, representation visits, negotiations, information-only coordination and passive participation at events may fall outside gainful activity. But the same annex treats on-the-job workplace training,

<!-- 10 -->

maintenance and repair work, project implementation, reorganisation work inside the Swiss entity, and regular staff review or target-setting work as gainful activity. In content terms, this means that "the foreign employer remains abroad" and "the visit is short" are not enough to classify the person as a business visitor. Where the person is actually working on the Swiss client's machinery, systems,staff or project delivery, the safer official starting point is that Swiss gainful-activity rules are engaged. 10

**Using** **notification** **where** **a** **permit** **is** **required.** The official materials identify several recurring route failures. One is duration: above ninety workdays, the case moves into permit territory, usually without a treaty-based right to the permit. Another is worker or employer ineligibility: third-country workerssent by EU/EFTA employers may use the route only if they were already durably integrated into the regular EU/EFTA labour market; if not, a permit is required before Swiss work begins. A third is employer location: for employers based outside the EU/EFTA, SECO guidance makes the position materially stricter, with sensitive sectors permit-required from day one and other sectors permit-required where the work exceeds eight days in the calendar year. A fourth is sectoral exclusion: foreign labour leasing is prohibited, and certain financial services and regulated professions carry additional prior-authorisation or recognition requirements. 39

**Day-counting** **errors** **and** **repeated** **assignments.** The official sources are unusually specific here. The entitlement runs by calendar year. For small general-sector assignments, the eight-day exemption is cumulative, not project-specific. If an undertaking uses the eight meldefreie days early in the year, all later postings in that year must be notified even for different workers. Likewise, the worker's own prior days matter: SEM's examples show that a worker who has already exhausted the eight-day cushion with another employer still requires notification by the new employer. For the ninety-day ceiling, the days count both against the employer and the individual worker; several workers present on the same day count only once for the employer's quota, but they still matter for the workers themselves. Zurich and Geneva add a practical point: enter only actual workdays, because the system counts what is declared. 40

**The eight-day waiting period is strict and technical.** Zurich's official page states that the filing day counts, weekend days count, and work may start only on the ninth day. It also narrows the emergency exception: repairs, accidents, natural disasters and other unforeseeable events may justify same-day notice, but short-notice client orders and tight staffing plans do not. Future drafting should therefore avoid loose formulations such as "urgent business need" or "commercial urgency" as if they were enough. The official standard is significantly narrower. 41

**Late, incomplete** **or** **inaccurate** **notifications.** Federal guidance does allow correction, but only in structured ways. Official SEM guidance allows certain changes by email to the competent canton: postponement of start dateif the eight-day period is still met, change in duration, interruption of work, and cancellation. For other changes, a fresh online notification is required and must refer back to the original notice. Zurich goes further and states expressly that an incorrect worksite requires a new filing and renewed respect of the eight-day period. By contrast, a plain initial notification by email is not accepted: the SEM page says the online route is standard, with post or fax only exceptionally for technical reasons, and email is not an admissible initial filing channel. Official public sources are thinner on the taxonomy of "incorrect employer/client/project details", so conservative drafting should say that non-listed material changes generally require a new notice rather than assuming that an email will cure the problem. 42

**Salary** **and** **local-condition** **non-compliance.** Official SECO materials are precise about methodology. The comparison is between the wage effectively paid and the Swiss wage owed, on a Swiss gross-hourly basis. The instruction then layers in mandatory elements such as periodic increases, holiday pay, public-holiday pay, the thirteenth and sometimes fourteenth month, and mandatory supplements for

<!-- 11 -->

overtime, night, Sunday or public-holiday work. By contrast, reimbursement of actual travel, food and accommodation costs for the Swiss deployment is not wage and cannot be used to make up a shortfall. Social-security contributions are likewise not wage. This is the main source of common miscalculation: employers treat the posting package as generous in aggregate, but the Swiss comparison splits wage from expenses and attaches specific value to holiday/public-holiday and monthly-salary components. 18

A further nuance is essential for compliance-risk drafting. In sectors with a binding minimum wage under an ave GAV or NAV, direct administrative sanctions for pay breaches are straightforwardly described in the official materials. In sectors without such binding minima, the official system instead works through the tripartite commissions' monitoring of customary local, occupational or sectoral wages and through conciliation designed to secure wage adjustment. The 2024 report states that 88% of those conciliation procedures were successful with posting undertakings. This means that "salary non-compliance" must be drafted carefully: some cases are direct minimum-wage breaches; others are customary-wage undercutting handled through market monitoring and conciliation. 6

**Documentation failure and inspection exposure.** Documentation risk is not secondary. For self-employed providers, the proof package is rigid: notice or permit, A1, and contract or client confirmation in an official language. SECO and Zurich say these must be shown on request at the inspection. If they are missing, the person may be given a short period to file the missing or equivalent documents; if they still do not do so, the competent authority may order an immediate work interruption and removal from the worksite. The more detailed SECO guidance further explains that equal-value documents may sometimes be accepted and that, where the controlled person cooperates, a service ban is not necessarily the first recommended step; but if the deadline passes unused and later document requests are ignored, sanction pathways open. 43

Inspection exposure is alsoevidenced by the enforcement data. In 2024, 25% of posted workers and 27% of cross-border self-employed providers were checked. Over 90% of suspected bogus self-employment cases were in anncillary construction or manufacturing-related sectors. The highest control volumes were recorded in Ticino, Zurich and Geneva. Those figures justify firm but non-alarmist drafting: the risk of audit or site inspection is real, especially in sectors and cantons already identified by the official material as exposed. 44

**Labour leasing, subcontracting and the role of the Swiss recipient.** The official materials are particularly strict on foreign labour leasing. Direct and indirect labour leasing from abroad is prohibited, and SEM specifically explains that indirect labour leasing includes the case where workers are lent to a foreign company which then posts them into Switzerland. The SECO guidance on bogus self-employment also gives a practical example: a foreign undertaking that in reality uses workers borrowed from a Swiss temporary work agency and reports them as its own posted workers makes a false notification and may be sanctioned. Separate from that, in the main and ancillary construction sectors, the Swiss first contractor may be civilly liable for subcontractors' wage and condition violations unless it can show due diligence, and SECO expects documentary, contractual and organisational diligence rather than mere passive reliance on contractual wording. 45

**Sanctions, bans** **and** **corrective** **pathways.** The official sources distinguish the sanction types rather than collapsing them together. Notification breaches can trigger administrative fines up to CHF 5,000; serious wage or other EntsG breaches can trigger higher fines and service bans; cooperation or information-duty failures can themselves justify service bans; and non-payment of final fines can trigger further service bans. The 2024 figures are striking: 958 fines were issued for notification breaches, 484for wage breaches, 544 for other EntsG breaches, 87 service bans for non-trivial wage breaches, 357service bans for information-duty breaches, and 135 bans for non-payment of final fines. Correction is

possible, but only in defined ways: by timely email amendment of certain fields, by a new linked notification before work starts, by same-day credit requests for unused days, by submitting missing equal-value self-employment documents within the short deadline, or by moving into the permmit route before the work begins if the route is in truth permit-required. ## Drafting cautions

### I. Drafting cautions

**·Keep route breaches separate from labour-condition breaches.** "Notification where **a** permit was required" is not the same legal problem as "notification filed correctly but Swiss minimum pay later undercut". The sources treat them under different legal bases and different enforcement logics. 47

**·Do not describe the notification route as a non-work route.** **It** is a short-term admission mechanism for specific categories of gainful activity, not proof that the person is outside Swiss work-authorisation law altogether. 8

**·Do not overuse the expression "business visitor".** Official SEM examples show that apparently light-touch visits can still be gainful activity if they involve implementation, maintenance, productive workplace training, project execution or managerial control over Swiss personnel. 10

**·Be** **exact** **on** **day** **counting.** The sources support calendar-year counting, effective workday logic, an eight-day waiting period that counts filing day and weekends, and company-plus-worker counting for posted-worker cases. Avoid phrases such as "eight free days per project" or "ninety days per assignment" unless the source expressly supports them, which the reviewed materials do not. 48

**·Do not imply that any notification error can simply be fixed by email.** Federal guidance limits email correction to specific fields and events. Wrong worksite data and other non-listed material changes require a new filing, and the fresh eight-day period may apply. 49

**·Keep salary-declaration errors distinct from salary breaches.** The SECO sanction recommendation expressly says that declaring a wage in the online proocedure that does not match Swiss minimum or customary wage rules is not itself a notification offence. The legal issue then becomes the substantive wage breach, not automatically a "false notice" sanction. 50

**·Treat self-employed status as a Swiss-law question.** Home-state registration, tax position or even genuine self-employed treatment abroad do not control the Swiss classification. The Swiss authorities assess the facts under Swiss criteria, and if bogus self-employment is found,the wage-comparison and employment-law logic may reappear. 51

**·Describe sanctions conservatively and accurately.** The maximum statutory sanctions are one thing; the SECO tariff tables are enforcement recommendations to cantons, not the statute itself. At the same time, enforcement should not be understated, because the annual official figures show large volumes of final fines and service bans, with cooperation/documentation failures generating a substantial share of bans. 52

<!-- 13 -->

**·Do not say that the Swiss client has no legal exposure.** In ordinary posted-worker cases the foreign employer is usually the primary addressee, but in construction chains the Swiss first contractor may face civil joint liability and related diligence-based exposure. In self-employment cases, the client or ordering party may also be operationally relevant because the contract or written confirmation is one of the key status documents. 53

**·State explicitly where the official sources are thin.** Public federal guidance is detailed on timing, counting, self-employment proof and wage method, but thinner on an exhaustive taxonomy of what counts as a "minor" rather than "serious" false notification, thinner on retrospective regularisation, and thinner on routine published case law for ordinary filing mistakes. Future internal content should say so rather than fill the gap with confident but uncited generalisations. 54
