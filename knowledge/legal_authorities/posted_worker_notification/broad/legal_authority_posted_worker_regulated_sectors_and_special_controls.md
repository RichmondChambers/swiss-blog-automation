# Swiss legal authority pack on regulated sectors and special controls in posted worker and notification-route cases

This pack is drafted for internal content-system use on Swiss posted worker and notification-route cases. It focuses on regulated sectors, enhanced controls, licensing, qualification recognition, prior authorisations, collective-agreement overlays, and enforcement risk. It is written conservatively and is not a substitute for sector-specific professional-licensing advice. The suggested internal filename is **legalauthoritypostedworkerregulatedsectorsandspecialcontrolspack.pdf.**

# Executive legal summary

Swiss posted worker analysis has to be separated into three layers that often overlap but are not interchangeable: the **immigration** **route** for short-term service provision or work (notification versus permit), the **labour standards** **layer** under the posted-worker / flanking-measures regime, and the **profession-** or **sector-specific** **regulatory** **layer** such as qualification recognition, police authorisations, technical licences, or health-sector practice permissions. Official Swiss materials repeatedly show that compliance with one layer does not remove obligations under the others. In particular, the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment states that trade, health-police and similar professional authorisations do **not** replace the required immigration authorisation; equally, a migration notification does not itself authorise regulated practice. 、

For EU/EFTA-based posted-worker and short-term service cases, the general Swiss model is a **notification route up to 90 working days per calendar year,** with notification normally required no later than eight days before work starts. That general rule is displaced in several risk sectors where notification is required from the **first** day, namely construction / civil engineering / finishing industries, gardening and landscaping, hotel / restaurant / catering, industrial and domestic cleaning, guard and security services, itinerant trade, and the sex industry. Official SEM guidance and the OASA/VZAE provision on cross-border work both reflect this structure. 2

The notification route is **not** **universal.** Three especially important exclusions emerge from official SEM material. First, once the 90-working-day threshold is exceeded, the case moves into the permit regime. Secondly, cross-border job placement, staff leasing and financial services are outside the AFMP services liberalisation logic; the SEM states that direct and indirect staff leasing from abroad is not permitted, and that such activities require prior permit applications with no entitlement to approval. Thirdly, employer location matters: SECO states that where workers are posted by employers from outside the EU/EFTA, certain sectors are permit-required from day one and others after day eight. 3

The sector-specific complications most likely to matter in posted-worker and notification-route content are these. **Construction** **and** **finishing** **trades** are first-day sectors and also engage strong CBA enforcement and, in subcontracting chains, statutory joint liability in main and ancillary construction. **Hospitality, cleaning, domestic work and staff leasing** are heavily shaped by generally binding collective agreements or federal standard-contract minimums. **Private** **security** is not just a labour-law sector; it may trigger cantonal/intercantonal authorisation regimes and, in the SBFI declaration system, additional criminal-record documentation. **Healthcare** **and** **other** **regulated** **professions** may require an SBFI declaration and qualification check before practice, plus separate recognition or cantonal practice rights. **Electrical installation** can require ESTI authorisation. **Transport** is a special-risk area because it contains distinct sub-regimes, including aircraft-maintenance regulation and road-transport cabotage restrictions. 4

Swiss enforcement is not merely theoretical. SECO publishes a public list of service bans imposed for final and binding infringements under the Posted Workers Act. Cantonal practice materials show active inspection in sensitive sectors. In Vaud, the tripartite commission reported 2,473 undertakings checked in 2024, with construction and food/hospitality sectors receiving dedicated controls, and 93 sanctions against foreign undertakings or independents, including 36 Swiss service bans of one year or more. Ticino reports targeted checks on posted-worker cases at first posting and when activity restarts with new employees, and reported sanctions against foreign service providers active for undder 90 days in uncovered sectors. Geneva police state that they keep an authorised list of private-security undertakings and perform regular concordat-compliance checks. 5

For automated legal-content drafting, the core lesson is that **sector-specific** **risk** **should** **be** **framned** **as** **a structured legal screening issue, not as a list of blanket prohibitions or blanket exemptions.** Official material supports cautious statements such as "the notification route may still require parallel profession or sector authorisations" and "construction, hospitality, cleaning, security and domestic/ private-household work are high-control areas", but it does **not** support generic statements that all work in a sector is licensed, or that immigration notification alone settles legality. 6

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

The core federal texts for this topic are the **Posted** **Workers Act** and its implementing ordinance; the **Foreign Nationals and Integration Act** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and** **Employment;** and, for regulated professions, the **Federal Act on the Duty to Declare and the** **Verification of Professional Qualifications of Service Providers inRegulated Professions** together with the **VMD** implementing ordinance. Official SEM and SBFI guidance ties those texts together by explaining when the notification route applies, when permits are required instead,and when separate professional declarations are needed. 33

Two texts are particularly important for **route separation.** First, the OASA/VZAE provision on cross-border work up to eight days states the general permit position and lists sectors requiring a permit irrespective of duration in that route. Secondly, the FNIA/AIG provision on salary, employment conditions and reimbursement of posting expenses makes clear that cross-border service provision still sits inside Swiss labour-standards control. These are fundamental anchors for distinguishing "migration permission", "posted-worker standards" and "regulated-profession control". 34

Official guidance then adds the operational rules that content systems usually need. SEM's short-term notification page sets out the eight-day notice rule, the first-day sectors, the 90-working-day ceiling,the route exclusion for staff leasing and the fact that permit applications govern excluded or longer cases. SBFI's 90-day services pages explain the parallel declaration procedure for regulated professions, including the current list of professions subject to declaration and the need for an additional EasyGov work-day notification. 35

For collective agreements and minimum conditions, SECO's official CBA and standard-contract portals are the primary sources for whether a sector is currently covered by an extended collective agreement or a mandatory-minimum standard contract. For hospitality, staff leasing, regional cleaning and

For profession-law overlays, the main official authorities are **SBFI** / **recognition.swiss** for recognition and declaration architecture, **BAG/FOPH** and the **Swiss** **Red** **Cross** for health professions, **ESTI** for electrical installations, **Suva** for crane-safety licensing and construction safety rules,and **FOCBS/BAZG** for cabotage in road-goods transport. # Cantonal and sector practice notes

The official practice material collected for this pack shows why generic national wording is often insufficient. In **Vaud,** the 2024 tripartite commission report shows dense control activity under the flanking measures, including 796 controls in construction-CBA sectors and 250 in food/hospitality sectors, plus 93 sanctions against foreign undertakings or independents and 36 service bans for one year or more. That is not a statement of substantive law, but it is a strong indication that these sectors attract heavier operational scrutiny. 38

In **Ticino,** the cantonal communication on labour-market surveillance states that posted-worker checks are targeted when an undertaking posts staff for the first time and when activity resumes with new employees.It also reports sanctions against foreign service providers active for under 90 days in sectors nnot covered by generally binding collective agreements. That is again a practice point, not a universal national rule, but it supports a cautious statement that Ticino operates targeted enforcement patterns in cross-border service cases. 39

**In** **Geneva,** cantonal materials show two distinct sector-specific interfaces. The OCIRT foreign-labour directorate states that it decides work authorisations for third-country nationals active in the canton, including persons posted by a structure based outside the EU/EFTA, and for EU/EFTA undertakings or independents offering services for more than 90 days in the canton. Separately,Geneva police state that the BASPE is the reference authority for private-security undertakings, keeps an authorised-company list, and carries out regular concordat-compliance checks. 40

An additional cantonal/territorial example comes from the SBFI factsheet on **building** **contractors,** which states that in the absence of federal legislation this profession is regulated by the cantons and, at the time of the factsheet, only **Ticino** had chosen to regulate it. That is precisely the sort of point on which automated drafting should avoid national over-generalisation. 41

# Practical legal analysis

The most important practical distinction is between **general** **posted-worker** **compliance** **and** **sector-specific** **legality.** A notification accepted through EasyGov or another SEM channel does not establish that the work may lawfully be carried out in a regulated profession. Official texts showv separate profession-law channels: SBFI declaration and qualification review for regulated professions; BAG/ SERI / SRC recognition processes in healthcare; ESTI technical authorisation in electricity; cantonal/ intercantonal authorisations in private security; and safety-professional or machine-operation credentials in certain construction contexts. 42

The next practical distinction is between **sectoral labour-law overlays** and **professional licensing. A** generally binding collective agreement does not itself turn a sector into a "licensed profession", but it can impose binding wage, working-time, enforcement-cost and inspection rules that materially change compliance risk. Hospitality, personal leasing, cleaning and domestic work illustrate this clearly. Official SECO materials show that these sectors are often governed by extended agreements or mandatory-minimum standard contracts, and that their current content can change by extension decision. Automated content should therefore avoid fixed wage statements unless tied to an up-to-date authoritative text. 43

<!-- 9 -->

**Installation** **work** is a particularly poor area for blanket drafting. Official sources support only carefully segmented statements. If the work falls in construction / finishing / landscaping, first-day sector rules may apply. If the work is electrical installation, ESTI authorisation may be required. If the person will perform work corresponding to a regulated electrical profession for up to 90 days and is an EU/EFTA service provider, the SBFI declaration regime may also be engaged. The collected official material does **not** support a generic sentence such as "installation work is exempt from Swiss licensing provided it is temporary". 44

**Healthcare** **content** also needs sharp route discipline. SBFI and recognition.swiss make clear that regulated professions require a separate professional assessment. BAG/FOPH and the Swiss Red Cross indicate that recognition and practice rights depend on the profession and competent body. A safe formula is that short-termn notification may coexist with professional declaration or recognition requirements; an unsafe formula is that health professionals can "usually work for 90 days on a notification alone". 45

**Private** **security** should be presented as a multi-source control area. It is a first-day sector in immigration notification law; it is also a regulated field for SBFI declaration purposes, with added criminal-record documentation in the VMD; and it may be subject to cantonal or inter-cantonal police authorisation frameworks. Automated content should therefore avoid wording that treats security as merely another posted-service industry. 12

**Subcontracting** creates a separate risk channel in construction. The statutory prime-contractor liability rule means that content on construction postings should not stop at the employer-employee relationship; the contractual chain matters. Where a prime contractor cannot show due diligence regarding downstream compliance with minimum pay and working conditions, liability and sanctions may follow. 27

**Day** **counting** also needs careful drafting. The 90-day ceiling is a working-day concept, not a three-month residence shorthand, and SEM examples show that company day-counting can operate differently depending on whether employees work simultaneously or sequentially. Content that reduces the rule to "90 days per worker" risks being misleading. 46

**Transport** should be treated with special caution because it is not a homogeneous sector. Official material supports at least two warning points: aircraft-maintenance work is regulated through a specific professional regime, and road-goods operations may be affected by cabotage restrictions that sit outside ordinary posted-worker messaging. Automated articles should therefore avoid broad statements that "transport work follows the standard notification route", unless the transport sub-sector has been specifically checked. 16

# Drafting cautions

Automated content for this route should follow a conservative drafting pattern.

<!-- 10 -->

It should say that the **Swiss notification route mainly answers the migration question for short-term work,** but that **separate sectoral controls may still apply.** That formulation is supported by OASA/VZAE and by the SBFI / regulator materials. It should not say, or imply, that a completed notification "licences" the activity. 47

It should distinguish clearly between: **posted workers, EU/EFTA self-employed service providers,** **workers employed directly by a Swiss employer,** and **cases involving third-country employers or** **nationals.** The first-day sectors operate differently depending on the legal route, and SECO's third-country employer page makes clear that permit duties can arise from day one in the listed sectors. 32

It should treat the following as **high-caution** **trigger** **sectors**: construction and finishing trades; gardening and landscaping; hospitality; industrial and domestic cleaning; domestic/private-household work; private security; healthcare and other regulated professions; electrical installation; road transport and aircraft maintenance; and cross-border staff leasing. Those are the sectors in which the official material most clearly identifies first-day rules, regulated-profession checks, separate authorisations or intensified inspections. 48

It should refer to collective agreements carefully. The safe formulation is that **posted** **workers** **may** **have to receive the minimum conditions laid dowwn in generally binding collective agreements or** **mandatory-minimum standard employment contracts that apply to the relevant sector and area.** The unsafe formulation is to present one sector's CBA as a universal rule across Switzerland. 49

It should avoid unsupported professional-licensing advice. For example, content may safely say that healthcare, electricity, security and selected construction or transport activities **can** **require** **separate** **recognition** **or** **authorisation,** and that the competent professional regulator must be checked. It should not say that a person "will be authorised" or "does not need recognition" unless that conclusion follows from a current profession-specific source. 50

It should treat **staff hiring-out** / **labour** **leasing** as an express red-flag issue. Safe wording: "cross-border staff leasing from abroad is generally prohibited and should be analysed separately from ordinary posting." Unsafe wording: "temporary agency workers can use the same notification route as posted workers." 23

It should not hard-code rates or validity periods without an update step. Hospitality, cleaning, staff leasing, the domestic-work NAV and other sector instruments are actively amended. If the system cannot look up the current authoritative instrument, it should speak in structural terms rather than reproducing figures. 30

It should also mention enforcement conservatively. The safest wording is that **cantonal authorities,** **tripartite commissions and joint enforcement bodies actively inspect higher-risk sectors and may** **impose fines or service bans in case of non-compliance.** That is strongly supported by SECO, Vaud, Ticino and Geneva official materials. 51

# Open questions and limitations

This pack is intentionally conservative. It does **not** provide exhaustive cantonal licensing advice for every regulated activity, because several profession-law questions remain canton-specific and fact-sensitive even within an official source set. The mnost reliable use of this pack is therefore as a **screening** **and** **drafting-control document**: it identifies where sector-specific escalation is legally required, and where general notification-route wording becomes unsafe. 59
