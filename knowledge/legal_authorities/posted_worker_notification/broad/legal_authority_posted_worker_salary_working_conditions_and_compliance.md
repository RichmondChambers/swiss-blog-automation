# ChatGPTSwiss legal authority pack for posted worker salary, working conditions and compliance

# Executive legal summary

Swiss posted-worker compliance sits on two separate tracksthat must not be conflated in automated content. The first track is **immigration** **access**: for eligible EU/EFTA and certain UK cross-border service cases, a short assignment can proceed under the **notification** **procedure** rather than a work permit, usually up to **90** **effective** **working** **days** **per calendar** **year,** subject to the eight-day prior notice rule and sector-specific day-one notification categories. The second track is **labour-condition** **compliance**: regardless of whether the immigration route is notification-based or permit-based, the employer must comply with Swiss mandatory minimum salary and working-condition rules applicable to the assignment. A notification does **not** amount to labour-law approval.1

The core labour-law rule is that a foreign employer posting workers to Switzerland mnust guarantee at least the Swiss minimum terms in the protected fields identified by the Posted Workers Act:**minimum** **remuneration including supplements, working and rest time, minimum holiday, occupational** **safety and health, protection of pregnant workers, new mothers, children and young persons,** **and non-discrimination, in particular equal pay for men and women.** Those mandatory standards may derive from **federal legislation, Federal Council ordinances, collective labour agreements** **declared generally binding,** and **standard employment contracts with mandatory minimum** **wages.** In sectors without generally binding collective agreements, Swiss authorities stilllook to **local,** **occupational and sectoral customary pay.** 2

For salary analysis, Swiss enforcement practice is not limited to the figure entered in the notification form. SECO's official wage-comparison directive requires a comparison between the **wage** **effectively** **paid** and the **wage** **owed** **in** **Switzerland,** normally on a **gross** **hourly** basis, with attention to the applicable Swiss classification, working time, wage supplements, holiday and public-holiday compensation, and other elements that genuinely have wage character. At the same time, **reimbursement of actual posting expenses** for travel, meals and accommodation is **not wage** and must be treated separately; if an allowance exceeds or cannot be linked to actual expenses, the excess may be treated as a wage component. 3

Accommodation is its owvn compliance issue. Official Swiss guidance states that the employer must provide accommodation meeting the **usual standard** **of hygiene** **and** **comfort,** and the statute states that deductions for accommodation and meals must **not** **exceed locally** **customary** **levels.** These points should be separated carefully from salary and expense analysis. 4

Documentation risk is real and should not be understated. The official notification materials require disclosure of the employer, assignment place and period, known Swiss contact person, worker identity, activity, qualification, social-security number, and **Swiss** **gross** **hourly** **wage;** the employer also signs a declaration acknowledging Articles 2 and 3 EntsG and undertaking to comply for the entire assignment. In subcontracting chains, the ordinance specifically contemplates documentary proof such as posting confirmations, identification of salary class, minimum wage and working time, lists of workers, and worker confirmations regarding receipt of the required minimum wage. 5

Enforcement is decentralised but active. SECO is the federal supervisory authority; cantonal authorities process notifications; joint commissions enforce generally binding collective agreements; tripartite commissions monitor labour-market conditions in uncovered sectors; cantonal labour inspectorates run local controls. Official SECO materials state that sanctions can reach **CHF** **30,000,** with **service** **bans** in Switzerland and recovery of **inspection** **costs;** Zurich's official guidance also notes the possibility of criminal proceedings in appropriate cases. SECO publishes a monthly public list of final **service** **bans,** which, as of April 2026, contains numerous entries from multiple cantons, showing that enforcement is practical rather than theoretical. 6

For internal drafting purposes, the safest formulation is this: **notification** **eligibility** **only** **answers** **whether the person may enter/work short-term without a permit; it does not remove or reduce** **Swiss** **wage** **and** **working-condition** **obligations.** If the assignment exceeds notification thresholds or falls outside the notification regime, a permit route applies, and Swiss authorities will in any event review **local, occupational and sectoral wage and working conditions** under immigration law as part of admission. 7

## Structural overview of salary, working conditioons and complianceLegal architecture

The basic federal framework is the **Posted Workers Act** and the **Posted Workers Ordinance,** embedded in the wider "accompanying measures" regime linked to the free movement of persons. SECOO's 2024 explanatory paper identifies EntsG as **SR** **823.20** and EntsV as SR **823.201,** and explains that the regime exists to prevent abusive undercutting of Swiss labour conditions while preserving fair competition between Swiss and foreign undertakings.8

For short-term cross-border service provision, the **notification** **procedure** sits alongside that labour regime. The SEM explains that the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons liberalises the cross-border provision of services for up to **90** **effective** **working** **days** **per** **calendar** **year,** subject to notification rules. Past that threshold,a work permit is required; there is no legal entitlement to the permit. 9

SECO's official wage-comparison directive is important because it shows how authorities operationalise the law. It states that the minimum conditions in the Posted Workers Act also apply to postings that are subject to permits, but in those cases the assignment is additionally subject to the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** **Act** and the **OASA/VZAE;** the cantonal labour-market authorities then review compliance with local and sectoral wage and working-condition standards under immigration law as part of the permit process. 10

## Salary sources

Swiss minimum salary obligations do **not** come from a single national wage floor. The applicable minimum can derive from a **generally binding collective labour agreement, a standard** **employment contract with mandatory minimum wages,** **or,** where no such binding instrument exists, the local, **occupational** or **sectoral customary** **wage** used in Swiss labour-market monitoring. Zurich's official guidance states this directly, and SECO's posting pages confirm the same source hierarchy. 11

This matters because salary content is highly dynamic. SECO maintains official lists of federally and cantonally extended collective agreements and of standard employment contracts with mandatory minimum wages. Those lists, not static narrative text, are the safer authority for sector-specific salary numbers. 12

## Working conditions

The protected Swiss minima extend beyond pay. Official SECO and SEM materials identify mandatory rules on **working and rest time, minimum holiday, occupational safety and health, protection of** **pregnant workers and young wvorkers, and equal treatment.** Zurich's cantonal page repeats this framework and additionally points to the Swiss minimum holiday baseline under **Article** **329a** **CO.** 2

The content of these working-condition rules is often sector-specific. In practice, that means internal systems should describe working-time and rest obligations as deriving from the **applicable** **Swiss** **federal law and any applicable generally binding collective agreement or standard employment** **contract,** rather than asserting a single universal rule for all sectors. SECO's materials make that source-based structure explicit. 13

## Expenses, accommodation and allowances

Swiss law distinguishes sharply between **salary** **and** **posting-related** **reimbursements.** The official notification form reproduces Article 2(3) EntsG and states that posting-related payments count as wage **unless** they are reimbursement for **actually** **incurred** **expenses,** such as travel, meals and accommodation. SECO's wage-comparison directive goes further: actual expenses must be excluded from wage, and if they cannot be evidenced, authorities may work with flat-rateexpense assumptions; any difference between a posting allowance and the recognised expenses can be added back as wage. 14

Accommodation is dealt with separately from wage. The official form reproduces Article 3 EntsG, requiring accommodation that meets the **usual local standard** **of hygiene** **and** **comfort,** while the posting portal explains that this entails, at minimum, sanitary facilities, personal lockable storage and a proper bed. Deductions for accommodation and meals must not exceed the locally usual amount. 15

## Documentation and compliance declarations

The notification regime itself is documentary. SEM's user guide states that, for posted workers, the employer must be ready to provide worker identity details, assignment place and duration, social-security number in the worker's state of residence, wage during the Swiss assignment,qualification, activity, and-where relevant-evidence of the third-country national's residence status in the sending state. The paper notification form adds the Swiss contact address and requires the employer to confirm awareness of, and compliance with, Articles 2 and 3 EntsG. 16

In subcontracting chains, the ordinance is even more concrete. Official ordinance text provides that the principal contractor may require a subcontractor to produce, among other things, signed posting confirmation, current home-country salary, posting allowances, Swiss wage-class allocation, applicable minimum wages and working time, lists of workers, written confirmations from workers that they receive the prescribed minimum remuneration, and recognised certificates relating in particular to safety and health. The ordinance also requires the principal contractor to take contractual and organisational measures enabling proof to be demanded from downstream subcontractors. 17

## Inspection and sanction structure

SECO's official explanatory paper describes a deliberately decentralised enforcement system. In sectors covered by generally binding collective agreements, the **joint commissions** enforce compliannce;in uncovered sectors, **tripartite commissions** and cantonal authorities monitor wages and conditions. SECO remains the federal supervisory authority and issues instructions and training to enforcement bodies. 18

The enforcement risk is practical. SECO's 2024 report explains that controls of posted workers differ fromn controls of Swiss employers because inspections of posted workers are limited to the period of Swiss service, but nonetheless form a significant share of enforcement activity; in 2024, **25%o** of **business** **controls** were carried out on posting undertakings. SECO also publishes a current public RESA list of final **service** **bans** imposed under the Posted Workers Act. 19

## Authorities and guidance

### Primary legal texts

The principal federal instruments are the **Posted** **Workers** **Act** **(EntsG,** SR **823.20)** and the **Posted** **Workers** **Ordinance** **(EntsV,** SR **823.201).** SECO's 2024 enforcement explainer identifies those instruments expressly and treats them as the backbone of the accompanying-measures regime. 8

For immigration interaction, the relevant instrumnents are the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** **Act** **(AIG/FNIA, SR 142.20)** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment (VZAE/OASA, SR** **142.201),** because permit-requiring postings are reviewed against Swiss local and sectoral wage-and-condition standards under immigration law. SECO's directive makes that link expressly. 10

The short-term notification regime also depends on the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** and the Swiss ordinance implementing the free-movement regime. SEM's official pages explain the operational rules for up to 90 days, including the eight-day prior notification logic and the sectors subject to day-one notification. 29

The Swiss **Code** **of** **Obligations** remains relevant, particularly **Article** **329a** **CO** on minimum holiday and **Article** **360a** CO on standard employment contracts with mandatory minimum wages. Zurich's official page and SECO's posting pages rely on those provisions in describing the applicable minimum regime. 11

### Official guidance and administrative materials

The most operationally useful federal guidance is the official **SEM** **notification** **page, the** **SEM** **user** **guide** for the online notification procedure, and **Form** **3** for posted workers. Together, these sources explain who may use the route, the deadlines, the required data fields, and the employer's substantive compliance declaration. 36

On the labour-law side, the most important administrative source is SECO's directive **"Vorgehen** **zum** **internationalen** **Lohnvergleich"'.** It is not a statute, but it is the clearest official explanation of how Swiss authorities carry out the wage comparison: territorial scope, gross-hourly methodology, treatment of wage supplemnents, holiday and public-holiday compensation, 13th month salary, overtime/night/Sunday/holiday premiums, social-security contributions, and the distinction between wage and reimbursed expenses. 37

SECO's **sanctions** **catalogue** is the most concrete official source for enforcement gradation. It shows escalating sanctions for late or incorrect notifications and sets out the framework for administrative wage sanctions, while the monthly **RESA** list demonstrates that final service bans are made public in practice. 38

SECO's **FLAM** **report** and explanatory note are also important because they explain how the enforcement system works institutionally and show that controls of foreign service providers are a standing part of Swiss labour-market supervision. 39

## Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** is useful for clear route-compliance language. The canton states that foreign employers must guarantee Swiss minimum wage and working conditions; where no generally binding CBA exists, local and occupational customary wages still apply; and violations can attract fines up to CHF 30,000, service bans and control costs. 23

<!-- 8 -->

**Basel-Stadt** is useful for two reasons. First, its posting guidance clearly distinguishes **up** to **eight** **days,** **nine** **to** **ninety** **days,** and **over** **ninety** **days.** Secondly, Basel-Stadt's official minimum-wage FAQ expressly states that the cantonal minimum wage also applies to posted foreign workers, while sectors covered by a generally binding CBA or a standard employment contract with minimum wages remain governed by those special regimes. As of 1 January 2026, Basel-Stadt states its cantonal minimum wage at **CHF 22.20 per hour.** 40

**Geneva** is useful because its **OCIRT** openly links labour-market control, posted-worker processing, sectoral "usages", and public procurement/business-attestation practice. Geneva also has a cantonal minimum wage, and the official page states that from 1 January 2026 the general minimum is **CHF** **24.59** **per** **hour,** with separate minima for agriculture and floriculture. Geneva also publishes lists of employers infringing the cantonal minimum wage and of employers infringing "usages". 41

**Ticino** is useful as a short official statement of enforcement reality: the cantonal labour inspectorate says its labour-market tasks cover both **control of posted workers in Switzerland and monitoring of** **wages** **and conditions** **in** **Swiss** **businesses,** confirming the practical integration of posted-worker checks into normal canton-level labour-market enforcement. 42

#### Practical legal analysis

The central compliance lesson is that **salary compliance begins before the assignment starts but is** **tested** **during** **or after** **the** **assignment.** The employer must declare a Swiss gross hourly wwage in the notification process, but Swiss authorities do not stop there. SECO's directive shows that the real control exercise is a structured comparison between the wage effectively paid and the Swiss wage owed, with recalculation into a gross hourly rate and adjustment for wage-character items such as holiday compensation, holiday pay, 13th salary and relevant overtime or premium payments. 44

That makes **classification** **risk** a major weak point. The Swiss "owed" rate depends on the correct Swiss legal source and, within that source, the correct wage category, region, qualification level, age bracket, experience bracket, and working-time basis. SECO's wage-comparison guidance expressly refers to classification by reference to the employment contract, completed vocational training, age and the actual work performed, while Canton Zurich stresses that Swiss, not sending-state, collective norms determine the applicable minimum. 45

<!-- 9 -->

The second major weak point is **mischaracterisation** **of allowances.** Swiss law does not allow the employer to use travel, meal and accommodation reimbursements to top up an otherwise deficient wage. Article 2(3) EntsG, as reproduced in the official form, and SECO's wage-comparisoon directive both insist on the distinction between actual expense reimbursements and wage. Where evidence is thin and allowances are bulky relative to basic pay, the risk of reclassification is obvious and expressly recognised in SECO's guidance. 46

The third weak point is **subcontracting-chain** **opacity.** Swiss law is much less tolerant of undocumented subcontracting than generic international mobility content often assumes. The ordinance specifically supports due-diligence checks by the principal contractor and contemplates the use of wage-class documentation, worker lists, worker confirmations and joint-commission attestations. In Swiss construction-style supply chains, the Swiss client or contractor is therefore not legally irrelevant; there may be a concrete documentary due-diligence role. 17

The fourth weak point is **assuming that notification thresholds soften wage obligations.** They do not. The eight-day threshold and the 90-day threshold answer whether and wvhen a notification or permit is needed. They do not reduce the underlying duty to comply with Swiss labour conditions for a posting. This distinction is explicit in SEM and SECO materials and should be reflected consistently in internal copy. 47

The fifth weak point is **ignoring cantonal overlays.** In some cantons, notably **Geneva and Basel-Stadt,** cantonal minimum-wage regimes can matter in addition to the federal posted-worker framewvork, subject to statutory exclusions and interaction with generally binding CBAs or mandatory standard contracts. Geneva's regime also interacts with local "usages" and OCIRT attestation practice, especially where access to public contracts or certain cantonal advantages is involved. These are not universal Swiss rules, but they are real and can materially change the salary analysis in route-specific content. 48

The sixth weak point is **underestimating** **inspection** **risk.** Official SECO reports show that controls of foreign service providers are embedded in Swiss labour-market supervision, not a marginal activity. The public RESA list of service bans further shows that final sanctions are regularly imposed across cantons and sectors. Internal content should therefore describe inspection and documentary follow-up as normal features of the route, not exceptional events. 49

A conservative internal compliance message for permit cases should also distinguish two legal layers.If a posting exceeds notification limits or falls outside the notification regime, the case moves into a permit framework under AIG/VZAE. But that does not displace Swiss salary scrutiny; it adds to it. SECO's directive states that permit-subject postings are reviewedby cantonal labour-market authorities against local and sectoral Swiss wage and working-condition standards under immigration law. 50

#### Drafting cautions

##### Content rules for automated drafting

Do **not** describe the notification procedure as a wage approval, clearance or endorsement. The notification route is an immigration filing mechanism for eligible short-term cases; Swiss wage and working-condition compliance remains an independent legal obligation and may be tested later by labour-market enforcement bodies. 47

Do **not** hard-code a single "Swiss minimum salary" for posted workers. Swiss salary compliance is source-based and highly sectoral: generally binding collective agreements, mandatory standard

<!-- 10 -->

employment contracts, local/occupational/sectoral customary wages, and in some places cantonal minimum-wage rules can all matter. 51

Do not merge salary, posting allowance, expenses and accommodation into one undifferentiated amount. Swiss law requires those categories to beseparated. Travel, meals and lodging that reimburse actual expenditure are not wage; accommodation has its own minimum-standard rule; only the wage-character portion belongs in the salary comparison. 52

Do not imply that a 13th salary is always part of the Swiss minimum entitlement. SECO's directive is more nuanced: whether a 13th month appears on the Swiss "owed" side depends on the Swiss legal basis, although certain amounts effectively paid on the "actual" side may still be counted in the comparison. 53

**Do** **not** omit sectoral ancillary obligations. Official federal materials show that generally binding CBAs can extend to holiday or family-benefit fund contributions, mandatory training-cost contributions for longer postings, deposits and contractual penalties. In some routes these obligations are as material as the base wage. 20

Do **not** present foreign staff leasing as a lawful posting route. SEM states that leasing staff from abroad into Switzerland is prohibited. 33

Do **not** understate enforcement risk. Official sources support saying that late or incorrect notifications can be sanctioned, wage infringements can trigger fines up to CHF 30,000, service bans and costs, and public publication of final service bans is routine. 54

#### Open questions and limitations

This pack is intentionally conservative. It does **not** attempt to provide current sector-by-sector wage figures, because those change frequently through generally binding collective agreements, standard employment contracts and cantonal rules. Any production content that states a number should be generated only after checking the **current effective instrument, territorial scope, wage class and** **date.** 55

A few ordinance points were most accessible in this research run through official SECO and SEM guidance,official forms, and official ordinance extracts rather than a fully parsed current Fedlex consolidation. The legal propositions above therefore rely on official Swiss authority, but production drafting shouId still cite the current consolidated Fedlex text where article-level pinpointing is required in the final product. 56
