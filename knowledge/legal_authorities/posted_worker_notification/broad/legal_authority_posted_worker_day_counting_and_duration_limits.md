## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

For Swiss notification-route cases, the core distinction is between **cross-border service** **provision** and **direct** **employment** **by** **a** **Swiss** **employer.** For cross-border service provision by EU/EFTA-based undertakings or self-employed EU/EFTA service providers, Switzerland liberalises work **up** **to** **90effective** **working** **days** **per** **calendar** **year** through the notification route. Official SEM guidance states expressly that, in posting cases, this **90-day ceiling is counted both for the posting company and for** **the** **posted** **worker.** In ordinary service cases exceeding that ceiling, a **work** **permit** is **required** and there is **no** **legal entitlement** to approval. 1

The **eight-day threshold** and the **eight-day advance notification period** are different concepts and must not be collapsed. In non-listed sectors, posted workers and self-employed service providers become notifiable only once the Swiss activity **exceeds** **eight days** **in** **a** **calendar** **year;** but in the higher-risk sectors listed in the ordinance and guidance, notification is required **from** **the** **first** **day.** Separately, where notification is required, the filing normally has to reach the authority **at least eight** **calendar** **days** **before** **work** **starts,** unless a recognised emergency applies. 2

The federal guidance is unusually specific on **how** **days** **are** **counted.** Only the **notified** **workdays** are counted. **A** day counts as **a** workday **even if only one hour is worked. A pure travel day** is not notifiable, but if preparatory work is done on the travel day, that day becomes notifiable.Where several workers are posted **simultaneously** on the same day, the company generally uses **one** **company** **day** for that date; where workers are sent **sequentially** on different dates, each date is counted. A single worker may work at two sites on the same day only **sequentially,** not simultaneously, and that still counts as **one** **day.** 3

Repeated assignments do not reset the counters. The system is built around the **calendar** **year,** the **legal entity** and, in posting cases, the **individual worker.** Official guidlance also makes clear that each order and each place of work usually has to be notified separately, although there are tightly-defined simplifications for the same client/same site and for certain maintenance/service jobs across different sites. For follow-up work on the same project, a new notification may be needed, but in some circumstances it does **not** restart the eight-day waiting period; by contrast, a later restart after three months or a change of worksite can restart the advance-notice clock. 4

For **direct employment** **by** **a Swiss employer** under the notification route, the structure is different. SEM guidance frames this route around **employment** **for** **up** **to** **three** **months** and requires notification **no later than the day before the first day of work.** **If** the intended work for the Swiss employer is for **more than three months or more than 90 days in the calendar year,** **a** permit is required instead. **5**

UK cases must be separated carefully from EU/EFTA cases. Switzerland's temporary services mobility arrangement preserves a **similar notification route for UK-based service providers up to 90 days** **per calendar year,** but **UK nationals taking direct employment with a Swiss employer** can no longer use the short-term notification route and instead require a permit under national law. 6

**For third-country** **employers,** the liberalised EU/EFTA notification route is not generally available. SECO's official guidance says that postings by third-country employers are **permit-required** **from** **day** **one** in the listed sensitive sectors and otherwise become **permit-required** **once** **the** **activity** **exceeds** **eight** **days** **in** **the** **calendar** **year.** Third-country nationals posted by an EU/EFTA undertaking may use the notification route only if they were already **durably integrated into the regular EU/EFTA labour** **market,** generally evidenced by at least **twelve** **months** of lawful labour-market admission before posting. 7

The official material gathered for this pack also supports two important negative propositions. First, **foreign labour leasing/personnel hire from abroad is prohibited,** and certain financial services remain outside the normal liberalised service route. Secondly, the notification system's confirmation message does **not** validate compliance with Swiss minimum pay rules and does **not** cure late filing; federal guidance explicitly warns that notification breaches can still be sanctioned. 8

A second structural point is that the **notification threshold is not project-based or client-based** in the federal guidance gathered here. ## Primary legal texts

## D.Primary legal texts

The legally operative framework is a **stack,** not a single rule. SEM's current federal guidance identifies the main instruments as the **Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons,** especially **Article 5(1)** and **Annex** **I** **Articles** **6(2) and** **20(1)** for short service provision, together with the federal **Posted** **Workers Act** and the **Posting of Workers Ordinance,** and the Swiss **Free Movement Ordinance for** notification mechanics. SEM's directives expressly connect the notification route to **Article** **6** **EntsG,** **Article 6 EntsV** and **Article 9(1bis) VFP.** 30

For the **advance notice period,** SEM's directives tie the rule to **Article 6(3) EntsG and Article 9(1) VFP,** and for day-one sectors they cite **Article** 6(2) **EntsV.** The same body of guidance also points to **Article** **14 VZAE** as part of the statutory reason for stricter treatment in sensitive sectors. 31

For cases that fall **outside** the short notification route, the legal basis shifts into the **AIG/FNIA** and the **VZAE/OASA.** SEM's directives state that ordinary service provision exceeding the 90-day/three-month ceiling is dealt with under national law, subject tolabour-market examination and quotas, and that the cantonal authorities ddecide in their discretion. 32

For sanctions, the key federal text is **Article** **9** **EntsG.** SEM's directives say Article 9 EntsG governs sanctions for breaches of the notification rules under the posting regime, while **Article** **32a** **VFP** provides the sanctioning basis for self-employed service providers and Swiss-employer short hires. 33

Because the direct Fedlex article text was not fully retrievable in the gathered material, this pack identifies the decisive provisions through **current federal SEM/SECO guidance that expressly cites** **them.** For internal content work, that is sufficient for a source-grounded authority pack, but if a future public-facing note needs article-by-article quotation, Fedlex extracts should be appended separately.

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

### E. Official guidance / administrative materials

The most operationally valuable federal sources are the **SEM** **Notification** **Procedure** **User's Guide** (March/April 2026), the **SEM VFP directives** (January 2026), the **Annexes to the V/FP directives** (especially the annexes on day counting and on reportable/non-reportable activities), the **SEM** **FAQ** **on** **Free Movement,** and SEM's short **factsheet on cross-border service provision.** Together, these sources supply most of the working rules lawyers actually need: who can notify, how days are counted, how late changges are handled, what counts as a reportable activity, and when the case falls into the permit route. 35

SECO's official pages are also important because they restate the federal rules in a more compliance-oriented form and squarely address the harder border cases: first-day sectors,third-country employers, UK cases and the practical meaning of "effective duration" in Switzerland. The SECO/entsendung materials are also where the federal administration explains that late or defective notification may still be sanctioned and that sanctions can rangge from warnings and fines to service bans. 36

### F. Cantonal practice notes

The cantonal material checked for this pack broadly **tracks** **the** **federal** **rules,** but adds useful examples.Geneva states explicitly that **only** **effective** **workdays** **are** **to** **be** **declared** and gives a worked example in which Thursdays and weekends are left out because no work is performed on those days. That is a practical confirmation of the federal rule that only workdays count, not the full span of presence in Switzerland. 37

Zurich provides a particularly clear practical explanation of the **eight-day** **advance** **notice** **period,** stating that the day of filing counts and that weekends are included. Zurich also lists the core papers that self-employed service providers must produce during checks: the notification confirmation or permit, the A1 social-security form, and the Swiss assignment contract or a written customer confirmation. 38

Ticino's material usefully confirms in Italian that the listed sectors are notifiable **from** **day** **one,** while in other sectors notification is needed only if the Swiss activity lasts **more** **than** **eight** **days** **per calendar** year,regardless of whether the work is carried out continuously or by separate days. 39

The cantonal materials therefore do **not** appear to depart from the federal counting model in any material way in the sources gathered here. Their value is mainly evidential and pedagogic:they reinforcethe federal rules with concrete examples and filing practice. 40

#### Practical legal analysis

### G. Practical legal analysis

The safest internal way to analyse a Swiss posted-worker or notification-route case is to ask five questions in order.

**First, whhat route** **is** **it?** If the person is being sent by a foreign employer or is an EU/EFTA self-employed cross-border service provider, the working measure is the **service-provider route.** If the person is being employed by a Swiss company, the case is the **Swiss-employer short-employment route.If** the employer is based in a third country, one should assume a **permit analysis,** not an EUJ/EFTA-style service notification analysis, unless a specific source shows otherwise. 41

<!-- 10 -->

**Secondly,** **what exactly** **counts** as **a** **Swiss** **workday?** The official materials support a strictly functional approach. Only the **working** **days** **in** **Switzerland** count. Presence in Switzerland does not itself equal a countable workday. That is why a pure arrival day is excluded, while a day with preparatory work is included. It is also why Geneva's example excludes non-working weekdays and weekends. However, once work is done on a given day, the time spent is irrelevant: **one** **hour** is **one** **day** for counting purposes. 42

**Thirdly, whose counter is being used?** In posting cases, there are effectively **two counters:** the posting company's counter and the individual worker's counter. This is the basis for a frequent compliance error: a firm assumes that because a new employee has never been to Switzerland before, the firm's own counter is unaffected. SEM's worked examples show the opposite. Once the company has used up its eight non-notifiable days in a non-listed sector, later postings in the same year are notifiable even for employees who are personally new to Switzerland. 43

**Fourthly,** is **the** **work** **simultaneous** or **sequential?** In company-level counting, the decisive question is whether work happens on the **same** **dates.** Three workers on the same site on the same five dates cost the company **five** days, not fifteen. But if the company sends them on different dates, the company uses a separate day for each date. This same logic explains why a single worker can do sequential morning/afternoon work at two sites on one date and still use only one day. 23

**Fifthly, how should repeated assignments be handled?** The federal guidance does not treat every return visit as a fresh legal universe. Same-order/same-site repeat work can sit under one notification if all dates are stated. Certain maintenance or service work across multiple sites can be grouped exceptionally. For later changes, SEM draws a careful distinction between corrections that are simply reported to the canton and situations that require a new notification. A replacement employee or added employee requires a new notification. A later restart after a long pause or after a site change can also restart the eight-day notice clock. Conversely, some follow-up work on the same project can proceed under a fresh filing without a fresh eight-day wait, provided the federal conditions are met. 24

This is also where **day-credit** **problems** arise. Companies frequently leave the original notice untouched even when rain, early completion or an interruption means the worker did not actually work on the notified date. Federal guidance gives only a narrow route to get those days back: the authority must be told **by** **noon** on the relevant day if the current day is to be credited. Internal systems should therefore treat the notification calendar as a live legal instrument, not a static pre-arrival filing. 25

**On** **multiple** **Swiss clients** **and** **projects,** the better legal reading from the gathered sources is that notifications are drafted **per** **assignment/place,** but the quota is not client-specific. The company/ person counting model cuts across clients and projects. Internal content should therefore avoid saying or implying that a fresh Swiss recipient creates a fresh 90-day allowance. The official texts gathered here do not spell that out in one sentence, so this point should be labelled as a **strong** **administrative** **inference** rather than as a quoted statutory formula. 14

**On** **assignments** **spanning** **two** **calendar years,** the safest internal formulation is similar. The official materials consistently speak in terms of **calendar** **years,** so the conservative reading is that December days consume the old year's quota and January days consume the new year's quota. But because the collected federal sources do not provide a worked year-end example, blog or knowledge-base copy

should not give overconfident illustrations without saying that the allocation is derived from the calendar-year wording of the rules. 15

**On** **special sectors and** **exclusions,** three further points matter. First, the list of day-one sectors is real and should not be summarised away. Secondly, foreign labour leasing/personnel supply from abroad is not merely permit-heavy; it is generally **not allowed.** Thirdly, some special EU-Switzerland service agreements create a more favourable regime for service providers, but those exceptions should be named precisely rather than blended into the ordinary notification route. 44

**On** **remote** **work,** the official source base is much thinner. The 2022 SEM circular does not create a special remote-work counting rule for posted-worker notifications. Instead, it answers a prior question: whether home office in Switzerland is even to be treated as gainful activity in Switzerland. For EU/EFTA persons working in Switzerland for a foreign employer **without** a **direct** **Swiss** **labour-market** **nexus,** the circular treats the person as non-gainfully active for migration classification purposes; by contrast, where the work has a **direct** **effect on** **the** **Swiss** **market,** including in a posting context, Swiss gainful activity may exist. Internal content should therefore avoid blanket statements such as "Swiss home office never counts" or "every Swiss laptop day counts". The answer depends on the labour-market nexus, and the gathered official source speaks to **qualification,** not a full day-counting methodology. 29

**On** **record** **keeping,** the clearest express federal/cantonal document list in the gathered material is for **self-employed** **service** **providers**: notification confirmation or permit, A1, and the Swiss contract or written customer confirmation. For posted workers, the sources gathered here do not provide a single equivalent federal checklist, but because the system counts **notified** **workdays,** allows only tightly-timed day credits, and warns that notification confirmations do not prove wage-law compliance, prudent internal practice should assume that employers need a retrievable audit trail showing the notice filed, the days actually worked, later corrections, and the pay data underlying the salary notification. That latter point is **a practice recommendation grounded in the structure of the federal** **controls,** not an explicit single-source checklist. 45

### Drafting cautions

### H.Drafting cautions

Internal day-counting content should be drafted conservatively.

**·Do** **not** say "90 daysper project", "90 days per client", or "90 days per site". The federal materials anchor the route to the **company** **and** **worker,** while notifications are filed per order/place. 14

·Do **not** say that each employee gets a fresh eight-day non-notifiable allowance regardless of the firm's prior Swiss work. The eight-day threshold in posting cases also runs against the **posting** **company.** 46

·Do **not** say that half-days or short visits are ignored. SEM says that **one** **hour** **counts** as **one** **day.** 21

·Do **not** count every presence day as a workday. Pure travel days are excluded, but travel days with preparatory work are included. 22

·Do **not** present the notification confirmation as if it were a substantive approval of timing or wage compliance. Federal guidance says it is not. 25

·Do **not** assume follow-up work is automatically covered by the old filing. Changes in personnel, site or timing may require a fresh notice, and some changes restart the eight-day wait. 25

<!-- 12 -->

·Do **not** imply that the UK is treated exactly like the EU/EFTA in all notification contexts. UK **service provriders** retain a similar short route, but UK **direct hires by Swiss employers** do not. 47

**·Do** **not** merge notification-route limits with **visa** or **wider** **Schengen** **entry** **rules** unless those separate entry rules are sourced separately. The federal service-provider factsheet itself treats visa questions as a distinct inquiry. 48

### Source list

#### I. Source list

The following sources were the most useful and should be retained with this pack.

**·SEM, Notification procedure, User's Guide, March/April 2026.** Core operational source for the 90-day rule, company/person counting, notice periods, UK coverage and filing architecture. 49

**·SEM, Weisungen** **VFP, January** **2026.** Core legal-administrative source for route classification, filing deadlines, emergency exceptions, repeated assignments, day counting, day credits, and sanctions. 50

**·** SEM, **Anhänge zu** **Weisungen** **VFP, January 2024.** Especially valuable for the annexes on day counting and on the boundary between reportable and non-reportable activities. 51

**·SEM, FAQ on Free Movement / notification procedure.** Useful for the short statement of company/person counting and the rule on multiple orders and places of work. 52

**·SEM, Factsheet on cross-border service provision, February 2023.** Useful short-form statement of the special-agreement exception, person/company counting, exclusion of foreign labour leasing, and the 12-month rule for third-country workers posted by EU/EFTA employers. 48

**·Cantonal notes**: **Geneva, Zurich** **and** **Ticino.** Useful for effective-workday examples, notice-period computation and frontline document expectations. 40

### Open questions / limitations

The official source base is strong on **posted-worker day counting, notice periods,** and **repeated** **assignments,** but materially thinner on three points. First, the gathered sources do not provide a worked official example for an assignment crossing **31 December** **/** **1 January.** Secondly, the remote-work authority located is about **migration-law** **classification,** not a full posted-worker day-counting formula. Thirdly, the explicit federal/cantonal document checklist gathered here is much clearer for **self-employed** **service** **providers** than for posted employees. Where this pack draws a conclusion on those thinner points, it labels the proposition as a **qualified rule, practice point, or strong administrative** **inference** rather than as a direct article-text rule. 53
