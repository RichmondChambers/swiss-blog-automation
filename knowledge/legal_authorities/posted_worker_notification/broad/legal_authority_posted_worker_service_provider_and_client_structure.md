## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

For Swiss posted-worker and notification-route analysis, the decisive starting point is not the label used in the contract, but the underlying legal and factual structure. Under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, cross-border service provision is liberalised only in a limited way: as a rule, service provision is permitted for up to 90 days of actual work per calendar year, and Swiss official guidance channels that treaty right through the notification procedure for eligible cases. Beyond 90days, or outside the liberalised field, a permit is required and there is no treaty entitlement to one. The notification route is available only for defined categories, including posted workers from an EUJ/EFTA-based undertaking, self-employed EU/EFTA service providers, and EU/EFTA nationals taking short-term employment with a Swiss employer; all other categories fall outside the ordinary notification route. 1

A posted-worker structure is legally coherent only where the foreignemployer remains the real employer and the real service provider. Swiss official sources define posting by reference to the worker providing services in Switzerland **on** **behalf** **of** **and** **at the** **expense** **of** the foreign employer, while the employment contract remains in force. By contrast, labour leasing or staff hiring-out exists where the essential rights of direction and control pass to the Swiss user undertaking, especially where the worker is absorbed into the Swiss organisation and the arrangement is really one of supplying labour rather than delivering a foreign undertaking's own result. 2

The Swiss client or service recipient can matter in several distinct ways, and those roles must not be conflated. The Swiss side may simply be the commercial recipient of a defined service, the Swiss contact person named in the notification, the first contractor in a subcontracting chain, or the actual employer. Swiss official guidance expressly requires the notification to identify the Swiss place of work, a Swiss contact who can answer the authorities' questions, and the purpose of the service as ordered by the recipient. But if the Swiss entity is, in substance, the undertaking that recruits, directs and uses the worker as part of its own workforce, the case should not be presented as an ordinary posting merely because a foreign company appears in the paperwork. That conclusion is not stated in one single provision; it follows from the official separation between the "posted worker" route and the "SwNiss employer" route, and from SECO's labour-leasing analysis. 3

Labour leasing risks are especially acute in three recurring structures. First, where the Swiss client gives day-to-day instructions, fixes hours, provides the tools and bears the performance risk, SECO treats the arrangement as labour leasing rather than a works or services contract. Secondly, cross-border labour leasing from abroad into Switzerland is prohibited, both directly and indirectly; that prohibition also reaches cases where one foreign company lends staff to another foreign company that is itself operating in Switzerland. Thirdly, SECO treats payrolling and Employer of Record models as labour leasing where one company formally hires the worker while another company actually directs and uses the work. 4

Intra-group or affiliate cases require separate care. Swiss official materials recognise that intra-group **posting** exists where the foreign employer sends staff to a Swiss group company but retains the employer's authority and responsibility. They also show that some internal meetings are not notification-triggering, whereas practical project work at a Swiss group entity is treated as notifiable work. At the same time, SECO's AVG guidance stresses that the Employment Services Act prohibition on foreign labour leasing remains fully relevant in the group context, and that group structures cannot be used to re-label unlawful hiring-out as posting. Narrow administrative tolerance for occasional, time-limited intra-group lending tied to experience or know-how transfer exists in SECO guidance, but it is an exception and not a general route. 5

Misclassification can produce both immigration and labour-market enforcement consequences. The immediate immigration consequence may be that notification is unavailable and a permit is required before work starts, or that a canton refuses a purported notification model and insists on the permit regime. On the labour side, the Posted Workers Act permits fines, inspection costs and market exclusion; the labour-leasing regime allows fines for unlicensed labour leasing and for knowingly using an unlicensed labour lender. In construction, the Swiss first contractor can also incur due-diligence and subsidiary liability exposure for subcontractors' breaches of minimum working and pay conditions. Regulated professions and some financial services raise additional approval layers even where the general posted-worker route would otherwise exist. ⁶

## Structure and legal propositions

### B. Structural overview of service-provider and client structure

The legally safer **posted-worker** / **foreign** **service-provider** structure is a four-corner arrangement in which the foreign undertaking has a real contract with the Swiss recipient for an identifiable service, the worker remains employed by that foreign undertaking, the foreign undertaking continues to direct the worker and remains answerable for the result, and the Swiss side appears as the recipient of the service and often as the Swiss contact person for enforcement or migration authorities. The notification system itself assumes this kind of structure, because it asks for the specific order placed by the service recipient, the Swiss work location, and the Swiss contact details, while also stating that the employer may notify only its **own** employees.7

The structure becomes legally unstable when the foreign undertaking stops looking like a genuine service provider and starts looking like a payroll shell or labour intermediary. Red flags, on the published official criteria, include transfer of operational instructions to the Swiss business, personal, organisational and temporal integration into the Swiss operation, billing by hours rather than by agreed result, use of the Swiss business's tools and working-time system, and the Swiss business bearing the risk of defective performance because the foreign undertaking only gjuarantees the selection of labour rather than the completion of a result. SECO also states that duration and the type of work do not

Intra-group structures must be split into three categories. Internal meetings, reporting obligations and some coordination events may fall outside the notification requirement; project implementation, software roll-out, reorganisation work or similar practical work at the Swiss entity are treated in SEM's annexes as notifiable work. Separately, group-internal arrangements must still be checked under the labour-leasing rules: SECO says there is no labour leasing where a group company performs a real centralised service without being subordinated to another group company's instructions, but group staffing-company models and longer-term workforce supply remain labour leasing territory. 9

#### Primary legal texts

### D. Primary legal texts

The core treaty-level source is the **Agreement** on **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons.** Article 5 gives service providers, including companies acting under Annex I, the right to provide services in the other Contracting Party for up to 90 days of actual work in a calendar year. Annex I then operationalises that right: Article 17 removes restrictions on cross-frontier services up to 90 days; Article 20 confirms that no residence permit is required for service provision up to 90 days; Article 21 restates the 90-day total cap; and Article 22 preserves national rules on temporary/interim employment agencies and certain financial services. Article 23 deals with persons receiving services, but in Swiss posted-worker practice the operational questions are usually handled through the provider-facing notification regime rather than through a separate immigration status for the Swiss commercial recipient. 23

The next layer is Switzerland's **OLCP/VFP regime and the SEM directives on the Free Movement of** **Persons.** Those materials explain when the notification procedure applies, when the permit regime applies instead, and how to distinguish notifiable from non-notifiable activity. They also confirm that labour leasing and private employment agency activity from abroad are excluded from the partial liberalisation, and that direct and indirect leasing from abroad remain prohibited under the AVG. 24

For cases outside the 90-day service righnt, the controlling legislation is the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration Act** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment.** SEMM's directives and factsheets tie permits for longer or non-liberalised service provision to overall economic interest, labour-market review, salary and working-condition review, and qualification requirements, with no treaty entitlement. 25

<!-- 6 -->

The labour-standards regime is the **Posted** **Workers** **Act** and **Posted** **Workers Orddinance.** Official Swiss materials explain that the PWA ensures Swiss minimum working andpay conditions for posted workers, provides for notification, inspections and sanctions, and, in construction, supports first-contractor liability through Article 5 EntsG and Articles 8b/8c EntsV. 26

The key classification regime for labour leasing is the **Employment Services Act / AVG** and the **Employment** **Services** **Ordinance** **/** **AVV.** Official SECO materials repeatedly rely on Article 12 AVG for the prohibition on foreign labour leasing into Switzerland, and on AVV provisions for the forms and indicators of labour leasing, including temporary work, labour hire, occasional supply and group-internal supply. These sources also contain the clearest official multi-factor distinction between genuine service contracts and labour leasing. 27

Published Swiss migration guidance in this area is driven more by statute and administration than by reported case law. The main official case-law signal in the materials located is SECO's reference to **BGE** **119** **V** **161** as an important authority on distinguishing self-employment from subordinate work. That is useful background, but in route design and enforcement practice the more operational authorities are the SEM and SECO materials cited throughout this pack. 28

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

### E. Official guidance / admninistrative materials

For notification-route mechanics, the most useful sources are the **SEM** **notification** **page** and the **2026user** **guide.** They identify the eligible categories, the filing party, the 8-day notice rule, the 90-day threshold, the place-of-work and assignment rules, the Swiss contact-person requirement, and the need to describe the service one is performing for the recipient. Those materials are especially valuable because they show how the Swiss authorities expect the provider-worker-client structure to appear in the filing itself. 29

For the migration-law boundaries of the route, the most useful federal source is the **SEM** **factsheet** **on** **cross-border service provision (EU/EFTA)** together with the **SEM VFP directives and annexes.** Those materials confirm the partial nature of Swiss serviceliberalisation, the 90-day cap per provider and per undertaking, the exclusion of labour leasing and certain financial services, and the migration-law consequences once the 90-day route is exhausted. The annexes are also the clearest published official source for differentiating notifiable project work from non-notifiable internal meetings and similar presence in Switzerland. 3⁰

For labour-leasing analysis,the most authoritative operational materials are the **SECO** **2024** **AVG** **directives** and the shorter **SECO labour-leasing leaflet.** They do four things that are central to this pack: they define labour leasing by transfer of essential instructions; they give a non-exhaustive but concrete list of distinguishing features between a service contract and labour leasing; they discuss group-internal cases; and they expressly classify payrolling / Employer of Record models as labour leasing where one business directs the work and another merely performs the formal employer role. 31

**The posted-worker portal** and **SECO pages on accompanying measures and joint liability** are useful because they explain Swiss enforcement consequences and employer and first-contractor exposure in plain administrative form. The posted-worker portal clearly distinguishes posting from labour leasing and states that foreign labour leasing is prohibited; the PWA page summarises available sanctions; and the solidarity-liability page explains the construction-sector due-diligence and subsidiary-liability model in Article 5 EntsG and EntsV. The same distinction is repeated in the French-language official posted-worker portal, which describes hired staff as working under the user undertaking's direction and notes that foreign hiring-out is prohibited. 32

A supplementary source, relevant but to be used carefully, is SECO's **2024** **guidance** **on** **reviewing** **self-employed** **foreign** **service** **providers.** It is not directly addressed to posted employees, so it should not be treated as the primary worker-posting rule. But it is useful in two narrower respects: it shows the kinds of documents Swiss inspectors value when they test whether a foreign "service provider" is genuine, and it discusses sham subcontractor patterns and prohibited direct and indirect foreign labour leasing found during PWA controls. 33

### F. Cantonal practice notes

Published cantonal material on this topic is comparatively thin, and what is published is mostly procedural or derivative of federal law. The clearest cantonal note located is Zurich's guidance for EU/ EFTA nationals: it states that posted workers from EU/EFTA undertakings can work permit-free up to 90days only via notification by the foreign employer, while work that is obviously longer than 90 days requires a permit before work starts and is only exceptionally extended. Zurich also restates the federal criteria that longer service provision must serve Switzerland's overall economic interest and is generally limited to managers, specialists and otherwise qualified workers. 34

Geneva's cantonal material for **third-country** enterprises or independents is similarly useful at the procedural edge: it states that a company or self-employed person based outside the EU/EFTA that wishes to post staff or provide services in Geneva must obtain cantonal authorisation, apply well in advance, and may not start work before the authorisation is granted in ordinary cases. It also clarifies that, in a posted-worker case, the employer files the application. 35

Cantonal publications on labour leasing tend mostly to restate federal licensing law. Zurich's licensing page explains that labour leasing and private employment agency activity require cantonal permission and, when cross-border, also SECO permission. Vaud and Ticino materials located in French and Italian similarly describe labour leasing as regulated supply of personnel and repeat the traditional categories of temporary work and labour hire. No published cantonal authority was found that articulates a materially different substantive immigration test for "genuine service provision" as opposed to labour leasing; in practice, the published canton-level material located largely implements the SEM/SECO federal framework. 36

## Practical legal analysis

### G. Practical legal analysis

The safest way to analyse a foreign provider / Swiss client case is to ask four questions in order. First, who is really promising the Swiss-side result? Swiss official materials consistently distinguish result-oriented works or services contracts from labour-only supply. The stronger the foreign undertaking's obligation to deliver an identifiable output, rectify defects and stand behind the completed result, the stronger the case for genuine service provision. The more the arrangement is framed and paid as the supply of hours under Swiss operational control, the more it begins to resemble labour leasing. 8

**Secondly,who** actually directs the worker day to day? Official SECO guidance makes this the core dividing line. Swiss client instructions about site access, sequencing with other contractors, building safety, or the service recipient's commercial requirements do not necessarily disqualify a posting.But once the Swiss undertaking decides what the worker does each day, how the work is done, when the worker attends, and how the worker is integrated into the recipient's teams and processes, the indicator set shifts hard towards labour leasing. The official guidance is explicit thnat instructions can already count as transferred even where the parties formally "share" them. 15

<!-- 8 -->

**Thirdly,** is the foreign undertaking functioning as a real employer and real service provider, or merely as an employer of record? That issue matters acutely in modern contractor stacks. SEM's system says the employer may notify only its own employees. SECO's directives say payrolling/ Employer of Record models are labour leasing where one company formally hires and pays the worker but another company actually consumes and directs the labour. So where a foreign company has little role beyond payroll, social insurance and immigration administration, while the Swiss client or Swiss affiliate exercises the real operational authnority, the official Swiss materials point away from a genuine posted-worker structure and towards labour leasing or disguised local employment. 37

**Fourthly,** what exactly is the Swiss client's role in the filing and in reality? The notification mechanism itself shows that the Swiss side can lawfully appear in the picture without becoming the employer: the filing may name the client, general contractor, architect or foreman as the Swiss contact person, and it must describe the order placed by the recipient of the service. That is normal. What changes the analysis is not visibility, but functional control. In other words, being the recipient of the service is entirely compatible with a valid posting; being the business that uses the worker as part of its own workforce is not. 38

This also explains why **indirect foreign** **labour leasing** is so important. A common multi-party structure is: foreign company A contracts with the Swiss client, but company A has no real workforce function and obtains the worker from foreign company B. Swiss official materials treat that as prohibited indirect labour leasing from abroad, not as a neutral subcontracting variation. The same logic means that a chain of foreign entities does not solve the problem if none of them is the real employer-service provider retaining control and result responsibility. 39

Intra-group cases require an additional split between **coordination, project implementation,** and **group** **staffing.** SEM's annexes show that attendance at internal strategic meetings or coordination sessions at the Swiss headquarters may be non-notifiable, whereas working on a specific project in the Swiss business, implementing software, or carrying out a reorganisation programme in a Swiss affiliate is treated as notifiable work. SECO's labour-leasing guidance then adds a second screen: group reporting and certain centralised internal services may still be non-leasing, but group staffing companies or broader workforce-supply models remain subject to the AVG analysis. That is why "intra-company assignment" is not, by itself, a route answer. The legal question is still whether this is a real posting or a workforce-supply model. 9

Where the facts show that the Swiss entity is really filling its own labour need rather than receiving an independently delivered foreign service, the correct route is usually **Swiss** **employment** rather than foreign posting. Official Swiss materials do not spell this out in one sentence for every variant, so this is necessarily an inference. But it is a strong one: the SEM system distinguishes notifications by Swiss employers from notifications by foreign posting employers; the filing party changes; the timing rules differ; and SECO's EOR/payrolling guidance shows that formal employer labels cannot override the user-enterprise reality. For EU/EFTA nationals hired by a Swiss company for up to three months, the Swiss employer uses the short-term employment notification route; otherwise the permit regime applies. For longer service provision or non-liberalised sectors, the service case also moves into the permit regime. 40

<!-- 9 -->

Official sources are stronger on **negative** **criteria** than on a closed "safe" checklist of contract evidence for posted employees. Put differently, Swiss authorities tell you more clearly what makes a structure look like labour leasing than they tell you which exact clauses guarantee a valid posted-worker service contract. The published materials located do, however, show what the authorities expect to see in practice: a real employer filing for its own staff, an identified Swiss worksite, an identified Swiss contact, a concrete description of the service ordered by the recipient, and a structure in which the foreign undertaking remains responsible for the work product and retains the employer's authority. For self-employed providers, Swiss controls may additionally require specific documentary proof such as notification confirmation, A1 and the client contract; that list should not simply be transplanted into posted-worker cases, but it does show the general evidential direction of travel. 41

Sector overlays must then be checked. In **regulated** **professions,** a separate declaration and qualifications review by SERI can be required even where the 90-day service route is otherwise open. In **financial services** requiring prior authorisation and prudential supervision, the partial service liberalisation does not remove Swiss authorisation requirements. Those sector points are not peripheral drafting details; in the wrong sector, they can defeat an otherwise plausible notification analysis. 42

## Drafting cautions

### H.Drafting cautions

For internal content drafting, the following cautions should be treated as hard editorial rules:

·Do **not** present notification availability as turning simply on the existence of a foreign services agreement. Swiss official materials place decisive weight on who directs the worker, who bears performance responsibility and whether the arrangement is really the supply of labour. 8

· Do **not** describe payrolling, Employer oof Record or "foreign payroll employer" models as ordinary postings if the Swiss client or Swiss affiliate directs the day-to-day work. SECO expressly classifies those models as labour leasing. 17

·Do **not** imply that intra-group status solves the problem. Swiss official sources distinguish non-notifiable internal meetings, notifiable project work, genuine intra-group posting and prohibited or permit-bound group staffing; thnose are different analyses. 9

·Do not use "posted worker", "service provider", "subcontractor", "EOR", and "labour leasing" interchangeably. Swiss sources treat them as distinct legal categories with different consequences. 43

·Keep **contractual structure** **and** **factual control** separate in the drafting. A contract may say "works contract" or "service agreement", but SECO's test looks at the real instruction, integration, billing and risk allocation. 28

·Keep the Swiss-side roles separate in all content: **service** **recipient, Swiss contact person,first** **contractor, Swiss employer,** and **Swiss user undertaking** are not synonyms. Each role carries different legal consequences. 21

·Do **not** suggest that foreign workforce supply can be cured by adding another foreign entity into the chain. Swiss official guidance prohibits both direct and indirect foreign labour leasing into Switzerland. 39

<!-- 10 -->

·Where content touches self-employed service providers, say expressly when the cited rule is **self-employment-specific.** The documentary checklist in SECO's self-employment enforcement guidance is not a complete statement of the posted-worker rule, even though it isuseful by analogy. 44

·State clearly when official sources are thin. No published official source located provides a closed model contract or exhaustive mandatory checklist proving a "genuine" posted-worker service contract. The official materials instead provide route mechanics and fact-based indicators pointing away from genuine service provision. 45

#### Source-quality note

This pack relies almost entirely on **official Swiss federal and cantonal sources** plus **EUR-Lex for the** **treaty** **text.** Published official materials are strong on route mechanics, exclusions, labour-leasing indicators and sanctions.
