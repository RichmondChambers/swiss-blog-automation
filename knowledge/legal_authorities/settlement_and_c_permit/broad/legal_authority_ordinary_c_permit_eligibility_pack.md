# Swiss Ordinary C Permit Eligibility Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary
Black-letter law. The ordinary statutory route to a Swiss settlement permit (permit C) sits in Article
34(2) of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, usually cited by its German abbreviation AIG and in
French as LEI. A settlement permit is granted for an unlimited duration and without conditions, but the
ordinary grant is framed as a discretionary power: the authority may grant it, rather than being
automatically obliged to do so in every case. On the ordinary route, the applicant must have
accumulated at least ten years in Switzerland on a short-stay permit or residence permit, must have
held a residence permit continuously during the last five years, must present no revocation ground
under Articles 62 or 63(2), and must be integrated. The Ordinance on Admission, Residence and
Employment then concretises the ordinary language threshold for grant of a C permit at A2 oral / A1
written in the national language spoken at the place of residence.
Route differentiation matters. The ordinary ten-year route under Article 34(2) is not the only way to a
C permit. Swiss law and official guidance distinguish it from: the early settlement route under Article
34(4), which can operate after five uninterrupted years on a residence permit and requires a higher
language threshold of B1 oral / A1 written; family-based statutory entitlement routes (notably
under Articles 42 and 43); and treaty- or reciprocity-based five-year routes for certain nationalities.
For EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM states that settlement permits are governed not by the Free Movement
of Persons Agreement itself, but by the FNIA/AIG together with settlement arrangements.
Counting residence is not mechanically uniform across all categories of stay. Article 34(5) states
that temporary stays are not counted in the decisive uninterrupted five-year period under Article 34(2)
(a) or Article 34(4). It makes a special rule for stays for education or continuing training under Article
27: those periods may be counted if, after their completion, the person has held a durable residence
permit for two uninterrupted years. SEM guidance adds an important practical distinction: in the first
five years of the ordinary ten-year period, all short-stay and residence permits are generally counted,
whatever their purpose; but the last five years must consist of uninterrupted residence-permit status,
with uninterrupted residence in Switzerland, subject to guidance-based tolerance for some short
interruptions.
Integration is broader than language alone. Article 58a FNIA/AIG requires the authority to assess
four criteria: respect for public safety and order, respect for constitutional values, language skills, and
participation in economic life or the acquisition of education. The ordinance then explains, among other
things, that public-order non-compliance includes disregard of legal rules or official decisions and wilful
non-fulfilment of public- or private-law obligations; that participation in economic life means being able
to cover living costs and maintenance obligations through income, assets, or legally claimable thirdparty benefits; and that personal circumstances such as disability, serious illness, poverty despite

employment, or caring responsibilities can justify departures from the language/economic-participation
criteria.
Special-status routes require care. SEM guidance states that recognised refugees with asylum,
recognised stateless persons, and former asylum seekers who later receive a residence permit on
hardship grounds all fall back onto Article 34 FNIA/AIG for settlement-permit timing, but time spent
during the asylum procedure, provisional admission, or humanitarian action is not counted
toward the settlement clock. For former asylum seekers admitted under Article 14(2) AsylA on hardship
grounds, SEM says the ten-year period starts with the SEM decision granting that admission.
## B Structural overview of the ordinary C permit route
Route selection. The first task is to determine whether the case is truly an ordinary Article 34(2) case,
or whether it instead falls into an early-settlement, family-entitlement, treaty-based, reciprocity-based,
or other special route. Internal drafting should never collapse these categories into a single “five years
or ten years” rule.
Residence counting. The ordinary route requires two clocks to be satisfied together: a total period of
ten years on L and/or B status, and a final period of five uninterrupted years on B status. Article 34(5)
and the SEM directives then modify how particular residence categories are counted, especially
temporary study/training residence.
Continuity and subsistence of status. Continuity is not only about permit labels. The SEM directives
treat the last five years as requiring uninterrupted residence-permit possession and uninterrupted
residence in Switzerland. Separately, Articles 61 FNIA/AIG and 79 OASA/VZAE govern when a permit
expires, including the six-month rule for departure abroad without notice, and the possibility of
maintaining a C permit for up to four years on request.
Negative conditions. Even where the residence period is complete, the ordinary route fails if there are
revocation grounds under Articles 62 or 63(2). The relevant grounds include false statements, serious
threats to public order or security, certain social-assistance dependency, and non-compliance with
integration agreements in the relevant contexts.
Integration evidence. The authority must positively assess integration under Article 58a, with
language thresholds concretised by the ordinance. For the ordinary route, the federal minimum is A2
oral / A1 written; for the early five-year route under Article 34(4), the federal minimum rises to B1 oral /
A1 written.
Cantonal filing and federal timing. Permits are issued by the cantons. At the same time, the SEM
guidance states that the SEM fixes, in advance, the earliest date on which a canton may grant a
settlement permit, usually via an automated ZEMIS process, while still retaining scope in individual
cases to refuse later consent on substantive grounds.

## Primary legal texts and official guidance
Primary legal texts. The core statutory provisions are: Article 34 FNIA/AIG (ordinary, early, special and
re-grant settlement rules); Article 58a FNIA/AIG (integration criteria); Articles 61 to 63 FNIA/AIG (expiry,
revocation and retrogradation relevance); and, where route differentiation is needed, Articles 42, 43 and
50 FNIA/AIG (family-based entitlement routes). At ordinance level, the key provisions are Articles 60 to
62 OASA/VZAE (ordinary C, re-grant after absence, re-grant after retrogradation, early C), Article 63
OASA/VZAE (presentation of the C-permit document before expiry), Articles 77a to 77g OASA/VZAE
(integration criteria concretised), and Article 79 OASA/VZAE (expiry mechanics during absence abroad).

Official federal guidance. The most useful current administrative source is the SEM’s Weisungen und
Erläuterungen I. Ausländerbereich, standing at 1 January 2026. For present purposes, the important
sections are the settlement-agreement material, the section on the SEM’s fixing of the earliest possible
grant date, the residence-counting section at 3.5.2.1, the integration section at 3.5.2.3, the special-rules
sections at 3.5.3 and 3.5.4, and the expiry section at 3.5.5. The SEM’s public pages on EU/EFTA
settlement permits, language requirements, and integration FAQ are helpful but should be treated as
secondary to the legislation, ordinance and 2026 directives where nuances differ.
Translation caution. The Refworld page carrying the English FNIA text expressly states that the English
text is an official translation but has no legal force, and that legally operative texts are the German,
French and Italian versions. Internal legal writing should therefore quote operative wording from the
official-language texts where precision matters.
## F Cantonal practice notes
Cantonal role. Substantive C-permit eligibility is set at federal level, but permits are issued by the
cantons, and the SEM itself tells applicants to consult the competent cantonal authorities for concrete
application procedure, forms and timings. Cantonal practice therefore matters mainly for filing
mechanics, evidence collection, communications and card-renewal steps, not for rewriting the
federal legal test.
Zurich. The Canton of Zurich’s public guidance tracks the federal ten-year and five-year structures,
states the familiar conditions for the ten-year route, and expressly tells applicants that temporary
residence such as education, cure or internship does not generally count toward the uninterruptedresidence analysis. It also provides procedural guidance through dedicated application pages and
explanatory videos. This is useful as a procedural example, but the substantive criteria remain federal.

Geneva. Geneva’s OCPM states that holders of a durable B or a C permit will normally receive a notice
of expiry one month before expiry of the residence document; if no notice arrives, the person must
submit Form K at least 15 days before expiry. Geneva also publicly states that, for permit-C purposes,
the locally relevant language is French. These are practical canton-specific filing and evidential points,
not autonomous Geneva legal tests.
Ticino. Ticino’s migration authority states that a foreign national who has reached the requirements for
a C permit should file the request with the migration office at least 14 days before expiry of the B
permit, and warns that failure to file in time may lead to a fine. Again, this illustrates procedural timing
rather than a distinct Ticino substantive standard.
G Practical legal analysis
The safest internal framing is to treat Article 34(2) as the default ordinary route for applicants who do
not fall within a more specific entitlement or accelerated framework. That route is discretionary. By
contrast, a number of other routes either shorten the time requirement or confer a stronger legal
position: family-based routes under Articles 42 and 43 can create a statutory right to a C permit after
five years where their conditions are satisfied; Article 34(4) creates a distinct early route after five
uninterrupted B years if integration is especially well evidenced; and treaty/reciprocity frameworks may
reduce the residence period for certain nationalities. Internal content should therefore ask, before
anything else, “which route governs this person?” rather than “has five or ten years passed?” 32

Residence counting under the ordinary route is asymmetrical. The statute allows the overall ten-year
total to be built from periods on short-stay permits and residence permits, which means that lawful
L-permit years are relevant. But the statute separately requires the last five years to be spent without
interruption on a residence permit, i.e. a B permit. The practical consequence is that an L-to-B history
can contribute to the ten-year total, while still making the person wait until five uninterrupted B years
have accrued. Article 34(5) reinforces the point by excluding temporary residence from that decisive
uninterrupted five-year segment. The same provision, however, allows education/training periods to be
counted if followed by two uninterrupted years on a durable B permit. SEM guidance then goes further
by stating that, in the first half of the ten-year clock, all L/B periods generally count regardless of
purpose, including temporary-purpose stays, and that some short interruptions may still be tolerated.
That last point is guidance-based, not express statutory text, and should be labelled accordingly.
Nationality distinctions require particularly careful drafting. The SEM’s 2026 directives state that a fiveyear treaty-based entitlement exists for nationals of Liechtenstein, Belgium, Denmark, Germany,
France, Greece, Italy, the Netherlands, Austria, Portugal and Spain. The same directives separately
state that, without a legal entitlement, a C permit may be granted after five years to nationals of
Andorra, Finland, Ireland, Iceland, Luxembourg, Monaco, Norway, San Marino, Sweden, Vatican
City, the United Kingdom, Canada and the United States. That distinction between an enforceable
five-year claim and a five-year grant without claim is important. An older SEM public EU/EFTA page uses
broader wording, grouping some reciprocity nationalities together and saying they are “granted”
settlement permits after five years if the conditions are met. For internal legal drafting, the more
precise and more recent 2026 SEM directives are the safer authority for describing whether the fiveyear position is an entitlement or only a possibility.
A further nationality-related nuance appears in the SEM directives: if a third-country national later
acquires the nationality of a state with which Switzerland has a settlement agreement, the residence
accrued before acquisition of the new nationality is not counted toward the treaty-based five-year
route, although it is counted toward the Article 34(2) and Article 34(4) routes. That is exactly the kind of
issue that can produce incorrect automated content if treaty-based eligibility is stated too broadly.
Integration should not be reduced to a single language requirement. The federal framework is
composite. Public-order compliance and respect for constitutional values are expressly relevant; the
ordinance defines non-compliance broadly enough to include disregard of laws or official decisions and
wilful failure to meet public- or private-law obligations. Economic participation also matters. There is no
freestanding Article 34 requirement labelled “financial independence”, but self-sufficiency enters the
analysis in two ways: first through the Article 58a integration criterion regarding participation in
economic life or the acquisition of education; and secondly through the revocation grounds, especially
where the person or a dependant is on social assistance, in the case of C permits on a durable and
substantial basis. At the same time, Article 58a(2) and Article 77f OASA/VZAE require appropriate

allowance for personal circumstances such as disability, serious illness, learning difficulty, poverty
despite working, or caring responsibilities.
The federal migration framework also shows why special-status cases should not be auto-mapped onto
the ordinary route without careful checking. SEM guidance says that recognised refugees with asylum,
recognised stateless persons, and former asylum seekers later admitted under the hardship route all
use Article 34 for settlement timing, but asylum-procedure time, provisional-admission time and time
spent under a humanitarian action do not count. For former asylum seekers admitted under Article
14(2) AsylA, the ten-year clock begins with the SEM’s hardship-admission decision. The same directives
also say that time spent in Switzerland under a Federal Department of Foreign Affairs legitimation card
does not count because that status is inherently temporary; the same non-counting logic is applied to
certain foreign officials. Those points are not obvious from Article 34 alone and need guidance-level
treatment.
Continuity of residence and subsistence of permit status also need to be kept analytically separate.
Under the SEM directives, the last five years of ordinary eligibility require uninterrupted B-permit
possession and uninterrupted residence in Switzerland for settlement-clock purposes. Separately,
Article 61(2) FNIA/AIG says that a B or C permit expires after six months abroad if the person leaves
Switzerland without deregistering notice, although a C permit can be maintained for up to four years
on request. Article 79 OASA/VZAE adds that short returns to Switzerland for visits, tourism or business
do not interrupt that six-month expiry clock. For drafting purposes, the safe formula is therefore: a
person may fail the continuity test without yet having formally lost permit status, and a person may also
formally lose permit status through prolonged absence irrespective of their subjective intention to
return.
H Drafting cautions
      • Do not say that ten years’ residence automatically gives a right to a C permit. On the
        ordinary route, Article 34(2) is discretionary. “Eligible to be considered” is safer than “entitled”,
        unless a separate family- or treaty-based right is the real legal basis.
      • Do not collapse all five-year routes together. Article 34(4) early settlement, treaty-based five-
        year routes, reciprocity-based five-year practice, and family-right provisions are legally distinct
        and carry different consequences for entitlement analysis and language thresholds.
      • Do not say that only B-permit years count. L-permit years can count toward the overall ten-
        year total. But equally, do not say that every L or B year counts identically: temporary and study/
        training residence is treated differently, especially in the last five years.
      • Do not present “financial independence” as a standalone statutory test without
        explanation. The stronger formulation is that economic self-sufficiency matters through the
        integration criteria and through the revocation-ground analysis, especially in relation to social
        assistance.
      • Do not reduce integration to language. Language is only one of the four federal integration
        criteria, and personal circumstances may temper the language/economic-participation
        assessment.
    • Do not state that the Free Movement of Persons Agreement itself gives EU/EFTA nationals
      a C permit. The SEM’s position is that settlement permits in this area are governed by FNIA/AIG
      and settlement arrangements, not by the free-movement agreement as such.
    • Do not assume that all physically present time in Switzerland is countable residence. SEM
      guidance specifically excludes certain asylum/provisional/humanitarian periods, FDFA
      legitimation-card periods, and certain other temporary official statuses from the settlement
      clock.
    • Do not confuse an indefinite C status with an indefinitely valid card. The status is unlimited,
      but the document itself can expire and must be presented for renewal; cantonal filing deadlines
      may apply.
    • Do not rely on the English translation for operative wording. The English FNIA text is
      expressly stated to have no legal force; legally controlling wording is in German, French or
      Italian.
