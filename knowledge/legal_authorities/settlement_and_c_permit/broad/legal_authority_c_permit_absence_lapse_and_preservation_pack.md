# Swiss C Permit Absence, Lapse and Preservation Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary
A Swiss C permit is, as a matter of federal law, a settlement permit granted for an indefinite period and
without conditions; however, that substantive status can still lapse under the statutory extinction rules.
The two core federal lapse triggers relevant to absence are: deregistration abroad and, where the
person leaves Switzerland without deregistering, an actual absence abroad of six months for B and C
permits. The associated permit card is only issued for control purposes for five years; expiry of the card
is not the same question as lapse of settlement status.
The federal preservation mechanism is narrow. Under Article 61 paragraph 2 AIG, a settlement permit
may be preserved during a temporary stay abroad for up to four years, but the request must be made
before the six-month period expires; Article 79 VZAE adds that short visits to Switzerland for visits,
tourism or business do not interrupt that clock. On the current SEM instructions, the cantonal migration
authority decides such requests in its own competence and in its discretion; there is no automatic
entitlement and the request must be adequately reasoned.
The SEM’s current administrative guidance treats preservation as reserved for genuinely temporary
absences, meaning the person must actually intend to return to Switzerland within the approved
period. Federal guidance gives examples such as military service abroad, further education, and a fixedterm posting abroad for a Swiss employer; it also says that foreign imprisonment can in principle be
temporary enough, but in practice may run into independent revocation grounds. The same SEM
guidance also states that, during preservation, visa-required third-country nationals need a visa to enter
Switzerland and may not work in Switzerland until they are again in possession of the C permit.
If the C permit has lapsed, the person is generally treated as a new entrant and becomes subject in
principle to the ordinary admission rules. There are, however, limited re-entry and re-grant pathways in
federal law and SEM guidance, including possible credit for earlier Swiss residence and a route for
renewed grant after prior long possession of a C permit; these are not the same as seamless continuity
of settlement status and should not be drafted as such.
The most important drafting risk is to blur federal black-letter law with cantonal procedure. Federal
law sets the six-month lapse rule, the pre-six-month application deadline, and the four-year statutory
ceiling. By contrast, filing expectations such as “apply before departure”, commune deregistration
practice, documentary requirements, departure-notice timing, staged extensions, and some narrower
local limits are canton-specific and not safe to universalise.
## Structural overview of absence, lapse and preservation
Settlement status at baseline. The C permit itself is a status of indefinite duration, but it remains
subject to the statutory extinction regime in Article 61 AIG. For EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM expressly
states that the Free Movement of Persons Agreement does not itself regulate settlement permits; the C
permit remains governed by the AIG and settlement treaties.
Departure with deregistration. Federal black-letter law says that a permit lapses “with deregistration
abroad”. That is the cleanest statutory lapse trigger and is separate from the six-month actual-absence
rule.
Departure without deregistration. If the foreign national leaves Switzerland without deregistering,
the C permit lapses after six months abroad. The ordinance then makes clear that temporary visits,
tourism or business trips to Switzerland do not break that six-month period. SEM guidance further
states that such short returns do not help where the person’s centre of life has in fact moved abroad.

Request for preservation. Before the six-month period expires, the holder may ask for preservation of
the C permit for up to four years. Federal law uses permissive language, not entitlement language. The
current SEM instructions say the request goes to the cantonal migration authority, must be sufficiently
reasoned, and is decided in the canton’s own competence and discretion.
Preserved status while abroad. The SEM’s current federal guidance adds practical consequences
during the preservation period: visa-required third-country nationals need a visa for entry into
Switzerland and cannot work in Switzerland during temporary stays until the preserved C status has
been reactivated by return. Some cantonal sheets also require commune deregistration, surrender of
the permit card, and re-entry formalities for definitive return, but those points are procedural overlays
rather than federal black-letter rules.
After refusal, late filing or late return. If no request is filed in time, if the request is refused, or if the
person returns only after the approved preservation period, the C permit is treated as lapsed. Federal
and cantonal guidance then treat the person as a new entrant in principle, albeit with limited possible
crediting or re-grant routes in some cases.

## Primary legal texts and official guidance
The core federal legal architecture is compact. Article 34 paragraph 1 AIG defines the settlement
permit as indefinite and unconditional, while Article 41 paragraph 3 AIG limits only the control
document to a five-year period. The actual loss of status is dealt with separately under Article 61 AIG,
which distinguishes between immediate lapse on deregistration abroad and lapse after a six-month
actual stay abroad where the person left without deregistering.
The implementing rule is Article 79 VZAE. It does two important jobs. First, it prevents holders from
defeating the six-month rule by making short visits back to Switzerland. Secondly, it fixes the procedural
cut-off: the request for preservation must be filed before the six-month period ends. That is the key
federal timing rule; there is no federal text here imposing a general “30 days before departure” rule or a
universal “apply before deregistration” rule.
The most useful current federal administrative material is the SEM AIG Instructions, in force as of 1
January 2026. Those instructions explicitly restate that the C permit lapses through deregistration or six
months’ actual absence abroad, that short visits do not stop the clock where the centre of life has
moved abroad, and that the request is both discretionary and required to be sufficiently reasoned.
They also add the operative filters missing from the statute: preservation is for temporary absences
only, the person must truly intend to return within the maximum period, and the request is decided by
the canton in its own competence.
The same SEM instructions provide the best official federal examples of acceptable temporary reasons.
They mention military service, further training, and the exercise of a fixed-term activity abroad on behalf
of a Swiss employer. They also say that second-generation young people and people who have reached
retirement age may seek preservation while testing reintegration possibilities in the home state.
Importantly, the instructions presently state that those latter categories can seek preservation for up to
four years; this matters because at least one cantonal leaflet uses a narrower local formulation.
For practical consequences during the preservation period, the federal material to use is the current
SEM instruction text together with the SEM’s June 2024 change note. Those materials state that visarequired third-country nationals need a visa for entry into Switzerland during the preservation period
and may not take up employment in Switzerland until they are again in possession of the C permit. That
consequence should be drafted with the nationality caveat intact.
The federal-law position after lapse is also materially clearer in SEM guidance than in the bare statutory
text. The current instructions say that a person returning after expiry of the six-month period, or after
expiry of the extended preservation period, is generally treated as a new entrant under the AIG and
VZAE. At the same time, the official guidance points to Article 34 paragraph 3 AIG and Article 61 VZAE
for limited re-grant possibilities and partial credit for earlier Swiss residence. It further says, based on
case law, that the person will generally need to spend at least two years again with a residence permit
before early re-grant of settlement can be invoked.
One source-currentness note is necessary. In this review environment, the Fedlex-hosted accessible
official PDF consolidations located for the AIG and VZAE were dated 1 January 2019 and 1 January 2022
respectively; however, the SEM’s official instructions in force on 1 January 2026 continue to apply and
describe the same six-month/four-year absence-preservation framework. For internal drafting, that
means the operative rule-set is current at least at the SEM-instructions level and consistent with the
consolidated texts reviewed here.
## Cantonal practice notes
Official canton material is highly relevant to how applications are made, but it should be read as
procedural and evidential overlay, not as national law. Zurich’s migration office is the most analytically
useful source. It states that a request filed before the six-month deadline generally has suspensive
effect, so that the C permit does not automatically lapse while the request is pending; it also says
expressly that there is no entitlement to preservation and that the authority decides in its discretion.
Zurich further states that the same timing rule is used for both third-country nationals and EU/EFTA
nationals.
Zurich is also the clearest canton on the uneasy relationship between deregistration and
preservation. Its official guidance says that a deregistration accompanied immediately by a
preservation request is not, from the outset, treated as extinguishing the permit. But if deregistration
happens first and the request follows later, the authority examines whether the deregistration showed a
definitive intent to leave. Zurich lists indicators such as giving up the family home, giving up
employment, early withdrawal of pension-fund assets, and cancelling health insurance. This is not
federal black-letter law, but it is a serious drafting signal that the issue is fact-sensitive and cantonsensitive.
Bern’s official leaflet is concise and useful as a mainstream German-canton procedure note. It says the
request should generally be filed no later than deregistration with the commune, and if the person
has already gone abroad without deregistering, then before the six-month period expires. It lists study
or language stays, a foreign work assignment for a Swiss employer, military service, special medical
reasons, and reintegration assessment for second-generation persons or persons of pension age as
standard grounds. It also says expressly that there is no entitlement and the authority decides in
discretion.
Basel-Stadt’s current official form goes further into documentary practice. It requires the request to be
filed before the date of departure, asks for a deregistration certificate, and accepts extensions only
within an overall four-year cap. It also requires tailored documents for study, employer posting,
medical treatment and military service, and states that where pension-fund assets are drawn down in
whole or in part, an already granted preservation may be revoked. Those points are useful for internal
procedure logic, but they remain canton-specific.
Geneva’s official material is helpful because it is public, recent and bilingual in structure. The canton’s
general departure page says departure from Geneva must be notified two weeks before the actual
departure date. Its “leave of absence (C permit)” page then says the preservation request should be sent
30 days before departure or, at the latest, within six months after departure; it also allows extension
of a first leave granted for less than four years, provided the total absence never exceeds four years.
Those are procedural rules of the Geneva OCPM, not federal statutory deadlines.
Ticino’s official Italian material adds another important caution. The general online page says that a C-permit holder who will be abroad for more than six months but less than four years may request
absence abroad in order to preserve the permit. The 2024 warning sheet for third-country C holders
says departure abroad must be notified 14 days in advance, that the permit loses validity upon
notification of departure or after an uninterrupted stay abroad of more than six months, and that
within six months the person may request absence abroad for up to four years for justified reasons. The
separate 2024 EU/EFTA warning sheet adds a further condition: the request may be made within six
months only if departure has not already been notified. That extra condition is not safe to generalise
nationally, especially given Zurich’s more permissive simultaneous-deregistration practice.
Lucerne’s official leaflet is useful partly because it reveals a possible local narrowing. It follows the
federal model on the six-month filing window and the general four-year cap, but then states that
second-generation minors and persons of retirement age seeking to test reintegration are limited to
two years. The current SEM AIG instructions, by contrast, indicate up to four years for those
categories. Lucerne therefore reads as an example of canton-level practice that should not be restated
as a rule of federal law without qualification. The same Lucerne leaflet also requires commune
deregistration and surrender of the permit card, and for definitive re-entry says visa-required thirdcountry nationals need a national visa D and must re-register with the commune within 14 days after
return.
## Practical legal analysis
The decisive legal distinction is between lapse/extinction and revocation. Absence abroad is
principally an Article 61 AIG lapse issue, not an Article 63 revocation issue. That matters doctrinally and
for drafting: content should not describe a six-month absence case as though the authority were
exercising a standard revocation power. At the same time, independent revocation grounds may still
matter in the background; the SEM’s imprisonment example expressly says that although imprisonment
abroad may in principle be a temporary absence, a long custodial sentence will often create a
revocation obstacle under Article 63 AIG.
The most difficult analytical point is the interaction between Article 61(1)(a) and Article 61(2) AIG. The
statute literally says that a permit lapses on deregistration abroad, yet it also creates a preservation
mechanism tied textually to cases where the person leaves without deregistering. In practice, cantons
operationalise this tension differently. Zurich treats a deregistration filed together with a preservation
request as not automatically extinguishing the permit; Basel-Stadt and Lucerne operationally require
deregistration once preservation is being processed or has been granted; Ticino’s EU/EFTA warning
sheet appears more restrictive. For internal drafting, the safe statement is therefore not “deregistration
always kills the C permit” and not “deregistration is harmless if you ask for preservation”, but rather: the
federal text is strict, and simultaneous or related deregistration practice is canton-sensitive and
should be checked at the competent canton.
The second important point is that the preservation route is not a general permission to live abroad for
up to four years. It is a temporary-absence exception, assessed on reasons and return intention. The

SEM instructions anchor this expressly in the notion that Swiss immigration status is generally exercised
through actual personal presence, and they use examples that are inherently time-limited. Cantonal
forms reinforce that logic through documentary demands: matriculation confirmations, employer
letters showing the duration of a posting, medical evidence, military orders, and explanatory supporting
documents. Internal content should therefore frame preservation as a justified exception for
temporary situations, not as a portable four-year freeze right.
A timely application does not create a substantive entitlement, but it may matter to interim status.
Zurich’s official note says that a request lodged before expiry of the six-month period generally has
suspensive effect and that the permit therefore does not lapse automatically while the request is
pending. That is a useful operational point, but it remains best described as practice informed by case
law, not as an explicit rule in Article 61 or Article 79. Drafting should therefore distinguish carefully
between the deadline to apply, which is black-letter, and the status while the request is pending,
which is more practice-driven.
On consequences after lapse, the federal and cantonal materials are consistent enough to support a
strong internal rule: once the holder returns after the six-month period without approved preservation,
or after an approved preservation period has ended, the C permit is treated as lapsed and the person is
generally dealt with as a new entrant. What must then be avoided is overstating the “comeback”
options. The federal materials do recognise limited re-grant possibilities and partial crediting of
previous residence, and the VZAE contains a route where someone who previously held a C permit for at
least ten years and has been abroad no more than six years may obtain renewed settlement, subject to
language requirements. But the current SEM guidance also says that, as a rule derived from case law,
the person must first spend at least around two years again in Switzerland with a residence permit
before early re-grant can be invoked. In other words, lapse is a real legal loss, not a trivial administrative
inconvenience.
Finally, EU/EFTA nationals should not be given a softer federal rule for C-permit lapse by absence merely
because they fall under free movement in other contexts. The SEM states that settlement permits for
EU/EFTA nationals are governed by the AIG and settlement treaties because the AFMP does not regulate
settlement permits, and Zurich applies the same preservation-request timing to EU/EFTA and thirdcountry C holders. The internal system should therefore separate C-permit absence rules from other
AFMP residence-right questions.
## Drafting cautions
      • Do not write that a Swiss C permit “remains valid for four years abroad”. The correct formulation
        is that it may be preserved for up to four years if a timely request is made and if the canton
        grants it.
      • Do not describe the six-month rule as being reset by short returns to Switzerland. Article 79
        VZAE says visit, tourism and business stays do not interrupt the deadline, and the SEM applies
        that where the person’s centre of life has moved abroad.
      • Do not conflate permit-card renewal with settlement-status preservation. The card is a five-
        year control document; lapse of settlement status is governed separately by Article 61 AIG.
      • Do not treat deregistration practice as nationally uniform. Federal law is strict on deregistration
        abroad, but Zurich allows an immediately accompanying preservation request to avert automatic
        extinction, while Basel-Stadt, Lucerne and Ticino contain different procedural assumptions.
     • Do not present canton-level reasons, evidence checklists, departure-notice periods, or local
       restrictions as federal law. Examples include Geneva’s 30-day pre-departure filing guidance,
       Ticino’s 14-day departure notice, Basel-Stadt’s pension-fund warning, and Lucerne’s two-year
       formulation for some reintegration cases.
     • Do not say preservation is automatic for EU/EFTA holders or that AFMP rules replace AIG
       settlement rules. The SEM expressly says the AFMP does not regulate settlement permits as
       such.
     • Do not present lapse after absence as an Article 63 “revocation” issue unless there is a separate
       revocation ground in play. The core absence mechanism is an Article 61 lapse/extinction issue.

     • Do not promise seamless restoration of C status after a late return. The safer proposition is that
       the person is generally treated as a new entrant, with only limited re-grant or crediting
       possibilities under federal law and guidance.
