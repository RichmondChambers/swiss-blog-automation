# Swiss C Permit and Family-Life Planning Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

For Swiss immigration analysis, a C permit is best treated as a settlement status with important
downstream consequences for family planning, rather than as an automatic or free-standing family-life
entitlement. For third-country national sponsors, the pivotal change is that family reunification moves
from the discretionary regime for B-permit holders under Article 44 AIG/LEI to the entitlement-based
regime for C-permit holders under Article 43 AIG/LEI, but only if the statutory conditions are met. Those
conditions remain substantive: cohabitation, suitable accommodation, no social assistance dependency,
no relevant supplementary benefits, and language-related requirements for spouses. The permit
therefore strengthens the sponsor’s legal position, but does not eliminate the need to prove household
viability and genuine settlement in Switzerland.
C-permit status also matters because it can improve the long-term residence trajectory of family
members. A spouse admitted through Article 43 does not receive a C permit immediately; the spouse
first obtains a residence permit, and only acquires an entitlement to settlement after five years of
proper and uninterrupted residence in marital union, subject to integration criteria. By contrast,
children under 12 of a C-permit holder have an immediate entitlement to a settlement permit. Children
aged 12 to 18 generally receive a residence permit first and must qualify for settlement later under the
ordinary settlement rules.
Residence continuity is central. The settlement permit is of indefinite duration in principle, but it can
lapse by departure notification or by a real stay abroad of six months if no maintenance request is
made; temporary visits back to Switzerland do not interrupt that period where the centre of life has
moved abroad. A timely request can preserve the C permit for up to four years, but the official guidance
treats this as appropriate only where the absence is genuinely temporary and there is a real intention to
return. Loss of C status can materially change future family-reunification analysis, because the sponsor
may no longer qualify under Article 43 at all.
The strongest drafting caution is therefore to keep four issues separate: settlement status itself; the
sponsor’s current evidential burden; the family members’ immediate permit outcome; and the family
members’ later route to independent or settled residence. Internal content should also distinguish
third-country Article 43 cases from family reunification under the EU/EFTA Free Movement Agreement,
where the legal basis is different and the role of a Swiss C permit is correspondingly different.
## Legal framework
## Structural overview of C permit and family-life planning

A C permit is the Swiss settlement permit. SEM’s guidance describes it as a status granted after five or
ten years of residence, depending on nationality and the applicable settlement treaty or practice, and
says that the right of settlement is not subject to time limits or conditions. That description, however,
must always be read together with Article 61 AIG/LEI on lapse, because the permit can still expire by
operation of law if residence in Switzerland is not genuinely maintained. In other words, “indefinite”
does not mean “immune from loss”.
For family planning, the legal sequence is usually this. A sponsor with only a B permit proceeds under
Article 44 AIG/LEI, which is discretionary. Once the sponsor has C status, Article 43 applies and creates
an entitlement if the statutory conditions are met. That transition can be strategically important in
medium-term planning, especially where the sponsor expects to move from ten-year ordinary
settlement timing to an earlier five-year route under a settlement treaty or recognised SEM practice.

The family members covered by Article 43 are the foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18.
Registered partnerships are treated analogously under Article 52 AIG/LEI, and the ordinance likewise
applies the relevant family-reunification provisions to registered same-sex partnerships. The statutes
and official materials do not place unmarried de facto partners inside Article 43 as such; any treatment
of cohabitants must therefore be handled outside this authority pack unless a separate legal basis is
identified. That last point is an inference from the official texts and should be expressed as such in
downstream drafting.
Family-life planning also depends on timing rules. Article 47 AIG/LEI gives a five-year period to invoke
family reunification, with a special twelve-month rule for children over 12. After that, only “important
family reasons” can justify delayed reunification; the ordinance narrows this further for children by
stating that such reasons exist where the child’s welfare can only be guaranteed by reunification in
Switzerland. This is a particularly high-risk area for internal drafting: delayed child cases should not be
presented as routine.
Finally, the official procedural materials show that family reunification is a multi-stage process involving
Swiss representations abroad, cantonal migration authorities, and in some cases SEM approval. The
sponsor’s C status improves the legal basis, but does not remove the need for proof of family
relationship, residence facts, housing, finances, and—where relevant—custody evidence.

## Authorities
D. Primary legal texts

The core statutory provisions are Articles 43, 46, 47, 49, 50, 51, 52 and 61 AIG/LEI/LStrI. Article 43
creates the family-reunification entitlement for spouses and unmarried minor children of settlementpermit holders, but only if the enumerated conditions are met. Article 46 confers labour-market access
on family members admitted under Articles 42 to 44. Article 47 sets the general filing deadlines and
narrows delayed cases to situations with important family reasons. Article 49 allows exceptions to
cohabitation only where important reasons justify separate residences while the family relationship
continues. Article 50 governs retained residence after marital or family breakdown, and Article 51
extinguishes family-reunification claims where they are abusive or where revocation grounds exist.
Article 52 extends the spousal rules to registered same-sex partnerships. Article 61 governs loss of
permits and is the key provision for C-permit continuity where the sponsor spends substantial time
abroad.
The principal implementing provisions sit in the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment,
especially Articles 58, 60, 61, 73, 73a, 73b, 75, 76 and 79. Article 58 fixes the initial validity of a residence
permit at one year, extendable by two years. Article 60 sets the language standard for ordinary
settlement at oral A2 and written A1. Article 61 deals with re-grant of settlement after a stay abroad and
requires the person previously to have held a settlement permit for at least ten years and to have
stayed abroad no more than six years, subject again to language proof. Articles 73, 73a and 73b
regulate family-reunification timing and language requirements: delayed reunification for B holders, the
language-course/A1 structure for spouses seeking or renewing a residence permit through family
reunification, and the A2/A1 threshold for a spouse seeking settlement through family reunification.
Article 75 narrows late child cases by tying important family reasons to the child’s welfare. Article 76
elaborates the exception to shared residence. Article 79 confirms that short visits to Switzerland do not
interrupt the lapse periods in Article 61 AIG/LEI and that the maintenance request must be lodged
before the six-month deadline expires.
Two additional primary points deserve emphasis. First, Article 43 gives a residence permit to the
reunited spouse and older child, not an immediate settlement permit, except for children under 12.
Secondly, the ordinance explicitly states that preparatory acts such as initiating family or marriage
proceedings, renting a flat, buying property, schooling children, or signing a work contract do not in
themselves create a right in the residence-authorisation procedure. That proposition is especially
important wherever internal content discusses “planning” language.
E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM’s Weisungen und Erläuterungen I. Ausländerbereich in force from 1 January 2026 are the most useful
current administrative source for practical family-life planning analysis. They confirm that Article 43

claims remain subject to cohabitation, suitable accommodation, financial stability, and the
supplementary-benefits bar; they also explain that financial assessment is forward-looking and may
take account of all family members’ means, while leaving room for cantonal judgement as to whether
higher levels of means are needed for social integration. The same directives also explain the spouse
language requirement, the possibility of integration agreements, the five-year route to settlement for
spouses, the immediate settlement right for children under 12, and the later settlement route for
children aged 12 to 18.
The same SEM directives are particularly important on residence continuity. They state that a settlement
permit lapses after six months’ actual stay abroad and that mere short visits to Switzerland do not
interrupt the six-month period where the person’s centre of life has moved abroad. They further state
that a maintenance request may be used only where the absence is genuinely temporary and there is a
real intention to return within four years; during the maintenance period, a visa-required third-country
national may need a visa to enter Switzerland and may not work in Switzerland until back in possession
of the settlement permit. If the person returns only after the legally relevant period has expired, the
prior settlement permit has lapsed and the person is generally treated as a new entrant under the
ordinary admission rules, although prior residence can exceptionally be credited in limited cases.
SEM’s 4 December 2025 directive on third-country family-reunification procedure adds a further
practical layer. It distinguishes the legal bases by sponsor status, confirms that Article 42 and Article 43
cases confer a claim where conditions are met, and sets out the allocation of functions between the
Swiss representation abroad and the cantonal migration authority. It also states that the sponsor bears
responsibility for proving the relevant family ties and must cooperate in establishing the facts.
Importantly, the directive expressly says that AFMP-based family reunification is not covered by that
instruction.
SEM’s public pages also help frame the wider architecture. Its C-permit page explains that settlement
permits for EU/EFTA nationals are still governed by the federal foreigners legislation and settlement
treaties because the Free Movement Agreement itself does not regulate settlement permits. Conversely,
SEM’s AFMP factsheet makes clear that family reunification for EU/EFTA nationals flows from AFMP
residence rights and creates derived family-member rights that persist only so long as the sponsor’s
original AFMP residence right continues. For internal drafting, this means: a C permit held by an EU/
EFTA national is not the source of the AFMP family-reunification entitlement and should not be
presented as such.
## Cantonal handling
## Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal public materials are useful for evidential and procedural reality, but they should be treated as
practice notes rather than as substitutes for the federal texts. Zurich’s migration authority page is
particularly concrete. For family reunification by B- or C-permit holders, it requires evidence of family
status, a lease for the family home, landlord consent, twelve months of payslips, a work contract, debtregister extracts, health-insurance material, and—where children are concerned—formal custody
evidence. Zurich also states that late applications require proof of important reasons for delay, and it
warns that the authority may seek additional documents or overseas verification through the Swiss
representation. That is a useful indicator of the documentary burden behind sponsor “stability” and
household credibility.
Cantonal materials on absence abroad illustrate how strongly authorities operationalise Article 61 AIG/
LEI. Lucerne states that the settlement permit may be maintained for at most four years, that permits
lapse after six months abroad if no action is taken, and that the maintenance request must be
submitted before the six-month deadline. Geneva likewise states that a C-permit holder leaving
temporarily may apply to keep the permit for up to four years, that return must be announced before
the end of the granted period, and that missing the deadline results in expiry of the establishment
permit as from the actual departure date. These materials are useful practical complements to the
federal rules when drafting continuity warnings.
French-language cantonal pages are also useful in showing how the federal rules are operationalised
for end users. Neuchâtel’s public guidance summarises the core conditions for B- and C-permit family
reunification as shared residence, suitable accommodation, no social-aid dependence and A1 oral
French, and reiterates the five-year / twelve-month filing rules. Its C-permit page separately summarises
the ordinary settlement conditions and states that spouses of Swiss nationals or C-permit holders, and
children under 12, may benefit from faster settlement pathways. Fribourg’s C-permit page similarly
presents the five-year marital/common-life route for spouses of Swiss nationals and of C-permit
holders, and lists the practical dossier items demanded for a C application, including proof of common
household and language.
Italian-language cantonal materials point in the same direction. Ticino’s official results indicate that a C-permit holder who will be abroad for more than six months but less than four years may request
permission for absence abroad in order to maintain the permit, and that the settlement permit
otherwise loses validity after an uninterrupted stay abroad exceeding six months. Ticino’s public-facing
foreigner warnings also stress notification of departure and the need to use the designated form. These
are practical handling notes only, but they reinforce the federal message that long absences are highly
consequential for future sponsor-based family planning.
## Analysis
## Practical legal analysis

The central legal planning point is that C-permit status can materially improve the sponsor’s footing
without transforming family reunification into a guaranteed outcome. A person who has moved from B
to C shifts from Article 44’s discretionary framework to Article 43’s claim-based framework. That is a real
legal advantage. But the entitlement exists only if the statutory conditions can still be evidenced. In
practical terms, the best internal shorthand is not “C permit = right to reunify”, but rather “C permit =
stronger legal basis, subject to household, finance, language and continuity tests”.
The second planning point is that genuine Swiss residence is indispensable for future family analysis. The
federal and administrative materials do not treat settlement as a purely formal card status. They treat it
as a status that continues only while genuinely connected to Swiss residence, unless it is preserved
through the formal maintenance mechanism. Once the centre of life moves abroad, short returns for
visits or business do not rescue continuity. If the sponsor loses C status by lapse, future familyreunification claims may no longer fall under Article 43 at all, and any later re-entry may be assessed
under the ordinary admission rules rather than on the footing of a continuing settlement permit. That is
why prolonged expatriation, remote-working abroad, or temporary relocation for family reasons should
be flagged internally as high-risk facts in any C-permit family-planning content.
The third point is that family stability is only partly about status and largely about evidence. SEM’s
current guidance focuses on real cohabitation, an actually sufficient dwelling, and a realistic medium-

term financial picture. Zurich’s evidential list illustrates how that is tested in practice: lease, landlord
consent, salary history, work contract, debt extracts, insurance costs, and, in child cases, custody
documents. Internal drafting should therefore treat sponsor “credibility” as an evidential concept
grounded in housing, finance, and proven family relationships, not as a vague reputational assessment.

The fourth point is that downstream family residence must be described carefully. A reunited spouse of a
C-permit holder normally starts with a residence permit and only later progresses to settlement after
five years of uninterrupted residence in marital union and common residence if integration criteria are
met. Children under 12 are a special case and have an immediate entitlement to C. Older children do
not. This distinction is often lost in shorthand public explanations and should be stated precisely in
internal materials because it affects long-term family planning, school-age timing, and any later
analysis of independent residence rights.
The fifth point is that future family planning and future family retention are analytically separate. Article 43
is about bringing the family member in. Article 50 is about whether residence can continue after family
breakdown. A spouse’s later retained rights after separation or divorce are therefore not simply an
extension of the sponsor’s C status; they depend on a separate statutory test centred on three years of
marital life plus integration, or important personal reasons such as domestic violence or severely
endangered social reintegration in the country of origin. Internal content should not blur those stages.

The sixth point is the regime distinction. Where the sponsor is an EU/EFTA national residing under the
Free Movement Agreement, family reunification derives from AFMP rights, not from Swiss settlement
status as such. The family rights are derivative of the sponsor’s AFMP residence right and last only so
long as that original right subsists. Accordingly, internal content should not generalise third-country C-permit analysis to EU/EFTA sponsors merely because they also happen to hold or later obtain a Swiss C
permit.
## Drafting cautions

      • Do not write that a C permit “guarantees” family reunification. The correct statement is that
        Article 43 gives an entitlement if the statutory conditions are met.
      • Do not collapse sponsor status and legal regime. For third-country nationals, the key
        distinction is Article 43 versus Article 44; for EU/EFTA sponsors, the applicable family-
        reunification basis may instead be the AFMP.
      • Do not say that family members of C-permit holders automatically receive C permits. The
        spouse normally receives a residence permit first; only under-12 children have an immediate
        settlement entitlement under Article 43(6).
      • Do not replace the Article 43 criteria with broad language such as “stable and integrated
        family life”. Internal content should list the operative conditions distinctly: cohabitation, suitable
        accommodation, no social assistance, no relevant supplementary benefits, and spouse language
        requirements.
      • Treat prolonged absence abroad as a major drafting risk. The permit can lapse after six
        months abroad; short returns do not interrupt the clock where the centre of life has shifted

       abroad. Maintenance must be requested in time and is designed for genuinely temporary
       departures.
      • Use strong wording in late child cases. After the normal deadlines, delayed reunification is
        exceptional and, under the ordinance, turns on whether the child’s welfare can only be secured
        by reunification in Switzerland.
      • Differentiate spouses and registered partners from unmarried partners. The official texts
        expressly cover spouses and extend the rules to registered partnerships; they do not create an
        Article 43 route for de facto partners as such. This should be expressed conservatively as an
        inference from the statutory structure.
      • Do not mistake preparatory life steps for legal entitlement. The ordinance expressly says
        that starting family, marriage, schooling, housing or employment arrangements does not by
        itself create a residence right in the authorisation process.
      • Keep sponsor-side family planning and family-member retention analysis separate. Article
        43 governs entry and stay through family reunification; Article 50 governs continued stay after
        breakdown of the family unit.
      • Use cantonal pages for handling detail, not for the core legal rule. Public cantonal
        summaries are useful for forms, evidence, and timing, but federal law and SEM guidance remain
        authoritative where simplifications appear.
