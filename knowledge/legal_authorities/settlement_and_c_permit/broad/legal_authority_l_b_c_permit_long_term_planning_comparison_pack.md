# Swiss L, B and C Permits for Long-Term Residence Planning Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary
Swiss long-term residence planning should treat L, B and C as materially different legal positions, not as
interchangeable labels on the same continuum. In the federal statutory framework, the L permit is a
short-term authorisation for temporary stays; the B permit is a purpose-specific, time-limited
residence authorisation that may carry conditions and is extendable only if no revocation grounds
apply; the C permit is a settlement authorisation granted for an unlimited period and without
conditions. The SEM’s own administrative materials are consistent with that basic statutory structure.

From a planning perspective, the stability gradient is real. L is the least secure because it is designed for
temporary presence, is closely tied to the stated purpose, and in the general regime is capped and
subject to reissue rules. B is the principal platform for building ordinary long-term residence, but it
remains renewal-dependent, purpose-linked, and vulnerable to revocation or non-renewal if the
statutory grounds arise. C is the strongest residence status because it is settlement status, but it is not
absolute: it can lapse through residence pattern changes, be revoked on serious grounds, or be
downgraded to B if integration criteria are not met.
The most important planning distinction is that C is not merely a longer-lasting B permit. It marks a
shift from renewable, purpose-linked residence into settlement. That matters for job changes, family
changes, canton changes, evidence strategy, and tolerance of future uncertainty. For that reason, later
drafting should compare the categories as temporary residence, renewable residence, and
settlement, not as three versions of the same thing.
A further caution is essential: EU/EFTA cases cannot be flattened into the ordinary third-country
framework. Under the AIG/FNIA, Swiss foreign-nationals law applies to EU/EFTA nationals only insofar
as the free movement instruments do not provide otherwise or the AIG is more favourable. SEM
guidance further states that, for EU/EFTA nationals, residence regulation, family reunification and loss
of residence rights are governed exhaustively by the free movement agreements. Settlement status for
EU/EFTA nationals is separately explained by SEM as falling outside the AFMP and being governed by
the AIG plus settlement treaties or equivalent rights.

## Authorities

## Primary legal texts

The core federal legislative authorities are the AIG/FNIA and OASA/VZAE. For this topic, the most
material provisions are AIG arts. 2, 32–34, 37–38, 41, 50, 58a, 61–63, alongside OASA arts. 54–63, 71b–72
and 77d–77e. SEM’s own legal-basis page lists the AIG, VZAE/OASA, VIntA and the SEM directives as the
governing materials for residence regulation.
Within that framework, the decisive legal contrast is clear. AIG art. 32 defines L as the permit for
temporary stays up to one year; AIG art. 33 defines B as a purpose-specific permit for longer stays that
remains time-limited and extendable only absent revocation grounds; AIG art. 34 defines C as
settlement, granted for an unlimited period and without conditions. AIG art. 34 then separates ordinary
settlement from early settlement, while AIG arts. 61–63 distinguish lapse, revocation and downgrading.

For EU/EFTA nationals, the primary legal architecture is dual. The AIG applies only subsidiarily, and
SEM’s AFMP materials are authoritative for route-specific points such as L/B validity periods,
professional mobility and job-loss consequences. SEM also states expressly that the C EU/EFTA permit
does not fall within the scope of the AFMP, but instead is governed by settlement treaties or
equivalent rights considerations.
## Official guidance and administrative materials

For explanatory drafting, the most useful federal administrative source is the SEM AIG directives,
updated to 1 January 2026. They are particularly valuable on the relationship between the residence
card and the underlying permit, on canton/job mobility, on settlement, on lapse abroad, and on
downgrading from C to B. The directives expressly describe the foreign-national card as confirmation of
the residence title and map L/B/C to different legal statuses.
The SEM FAQ on residence and integration criteria is also useful, especially for comparative risk
language. It states that integration criteria matter for the grant, extension and revocation of
residence and settlement permits; summarises the typical reasons for B revocation/non-renewal; and
confirms that C can be revoked or downgraded where integration criteria are no longer met. It also
warns that EU/EFTA residence regulation and loss-of-right questions are governed by the free
movement agreements rather than by a flattened AIG model.
The SEM language-competence FAQ is especially helpful for settlement planning because it identifies
when proof of language ability is required and summarises the required levels. It links the ordinary
grant of C to at least A2 speaking / A1 writing, and the early grant of C to the higher threshold
reflected in OASA art. 62.
The SEM biometric residence permit page is the cleanest official source for documentary-status
explanation. It says that biometric residence permits are issued for L, B and C categories, that the card
indicates the foreigner’s status in Switzerland, and that a new card is issued when the previous one
expires or data changes require replacement. Used together with AIG art. 34 and OASA art. 63, it
strongly supports the distinction between indefinite settlement status and the time-limited physical
card evidencing it.
## Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal practice pages are useful for procedure and operational framing, not for changing the
federal legal hierarchy. Zurich’s official page states that C may generally be applied for after ten years,
with some persons eligible after five, and repeats the core federal point that settlement is in principle
unlimited and cannot be subject to conditions, while still being capable of revocation or downgrading.

Geneva’s official pages are particularly useful for absence-management examples. The canton provides
a return authorisation process for certain non-EU/EFTA B permit holders leaving Switzerland
temporarily for employer-driven or professional-development reasons, for up to four years. It also
provides a leave of absence process for C permit holders, again for up to four years. Geneva’s
departure page states that a declared permanent departure definitively terminates L/B residence rights
and C establishment rights from the following day, and that return then requires a fresh application.

Ticino’s pages are procedurally useful because they operationalise timing. The canton states that a new
C may generally be sought by a person who has resided regularly in Switzerland for at least ten years
with a B permit, and that the application should be submitted at least 14 days before B expiry. Ticino
also requires C-card renewal at least 14 days before the control term expires and asks that certain
registered changes be reported within 14 days.
## Practical legal analysis
The practical distinction between L, B and C is best understood as a distinction between temporary
presence, renewable residence, and settlement. The AIG itself encodes that structure. The main legal
planning consequence is that a person on L is not yet on a settled or even ordinarily stable residence
platform, a person on B is on the principal residence platform but still exposed to renewal control, and a
person on C has crossed into settlement status.
For L, the strongest planning point is negative: it is usually a weak base for long-term security. It is
purpose-bound, the law caps its normal duration, and a change of stay purpose requires a new permit.
Repeated L holding is specifically managed by renewal and interruption rules. Even where L time may
matter to aggregate residence history, ordinary settlement still requires the final five years to be on B.
As a result, internal drafting should avoid implying that L status is an ordinary, low-risk “first stage” of
residence; at most, that is sometimes true factually, not legally. This last point is an inference from the
statutory structure and must be presented as such.
For B, the central feature is that it is the normal planning bridge to settlement, but not settlement
itself. The permit remains tied to a purpose, can carry conditions, can be linked to integration measures,
and can be revoked or refused at extension stage if statutory grounds arise. That matters particularly
for family-based or derivative B permits: after family breakdown, continuity is no longer assumed but
depends on the limited statutory continuation routes in AIG art. 50. B status therefore supports longterm planning only if the underlying basis of stay is durable and can survive foreseeable factual change.

For C, the practical shift is substantial. Settlement status is no longer defined by a continuing specific
purpose in the same way as B. That normally reduces vulnerability to changes in employment and
family structure and produces a much stronger residence platform. But the security is qualified, not
absolute. C can still lapse through absence abroad, be revoked for serious public-order or social-

assistance grounds, and be downgraded to B where integration criteria are not met. In short, C
materially improves security, but it does not eliminate residence-risk management.
The permit-card / legal-status distinction deserves explicit treatment in later drafting. SEM guidance
describes the card as proof/indication of status, and the directives treat foreign-national cards as
confirmation of the underlying residence title. That matters most for C. The AIG makes settlement
legally indefinite and unconditional, while OASA separately requires renewal of the card’s validity
period. By contrast, for L and B the end of the card period usually corresponds to the end of the timelimited authorisation unless a renewal or extension application has been filed and the person benefits
from pending-procedure continuity. Internal content should therefore speak carefully of status,
authorisation period, card validity, and pending renewal as distinct concepts.
The EU/EFTA split is not a technical footnote; it changes the comparative picture. SEM’s AFMP materials
describe EU/EFTA L and B permits with their own validity logic, broader mobility and specific job-loss
consequences, while also confirming that EU/EFTA settlement falls outside the AFMP. Comparative
internal content should therefore compare L/B/C in two layers where relevant: first, the general legal
nature under Swiss residence law; second, the route-specific modification for EU/EFTA cases.
## Drafting cautions
- Do not flatten the legal frameworks. In particular, do not write as if a single L/B/C regime applies
identically across third-country, family-route and EU/EFTA matters. The SEM expressly signals that EU/
EFTA residence rights and their loss are governed by the free movement agreements, and SEM’s
settlement material places the C EU/EFTA permit outside AFMP.
- Do not imply automatic progression. Neither L→B nor B→C is automatic as a matter of legal
structure. A change in stay purpose requires a new permit, and settlement requires satisfaction of
specific statutory conditions. If later content discusses “progression”, it should use that term
descriptively and conditionally, not as a statement of entitlement. The point that L→B is not automatic is
an inference from the legislation and SEM materials and should be labelled as such if made explicitly.

- Do not describe B as permanent or secure in itself. B is renewable, but it remains fixed-term,
purpose-linked and vulnerable to revocation/non-renewal. That is especially important for B permits
based on family life, study, or other special purposes. Not every B carries the same long-term planning
value.
- Do not describe C as irrevocable. C is the strongest status, but it can still lapse after time abroad, be
revoked on serious grounds, or be downgraded to B for integration failures. Avoid client-facing
shorthand such as “permanent residency that cannot be lost”.
- Do not confuse a card expiry with the legal analysis. Especially for C, card renewal is not the same
thing as a fresh grant of settlement status. Conversely, with L and B, the end of the authorisation period
is often the core legal event unless a timely extension application is pending. Drafting should separate
indefinite settlement status, control-term renewal, permit extension, and pending renewal.
