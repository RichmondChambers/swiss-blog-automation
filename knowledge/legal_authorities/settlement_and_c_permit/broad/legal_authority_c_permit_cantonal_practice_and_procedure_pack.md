# Swiss C Permit Cantonal Practice and Procedure Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary
Swiss C permit law is federal as to status, core eligibility, grounds for extinction, revocation and
downgrading, and baseline integration criteria. In particular, the settlement permit is of unlimited
duration and cannot be made subject to conditions; federal materials also place the core substantive
requirements in the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and
Gainful Employment, including residence periods, integration criteria, and rules on extinction after
deregistration or lengthy absence abroad. The SEM determines the earliest date from which the
competent cantonal authorities may grant settlement permits, and the SEM’s directives frame the
substantive federal test.
What is not nationally uniform is the handling architecture. Official Swiss public guidance states that
permits are issued by cantonal migration offices, but the filing route for applications and renewals is
often local: ch.ch says renewal applications are generally submitted to the commune of residence, while
Geneva is expressly flagged as a canton-level exception. In practice, some cantons use communes or
residents’ register offices as the intake and forwarding point, whereas others centralise both population
registration and migration functions in a cantonal office.
For internal drafting, the safest formulation is that federal law governs whether a person may obtain,
keep, lose, or recover a C permit, while canton and commune practice governs how the file is lodged,
what local forms or channels are used, what supporting documents are requested in practice, how
biometrics are scheduled, how fees are collected, how long the case may take, and which office first
handles the dossier. None of those local handling points should be stated as if they were Swiss-wide
black-letter law.
Official local materials show substantial procedural variation. Bern requires applications and controlperiod renewals to be handed to the residents’ office of the commune, which checks completeness and
forwards them to the cantonal migration service. Lausanne allows C-permit renewal by post and, in
many cases, online; social-assistance cases are excluded from the online route. Geneva uses the OCPM
directly, typically expecting a notice of expiry about one month before expiry and a postal filing at least
15 days before expiry if no notice arrives. Ticino requires renewal 14 days before expiry of the control
term and notification of changes to personal data, civil status or Swiss address within 14 days of the
change. Zurich City requires extension of the foreign-national permit no later than 14 days before expiry
and by appointment.
Commune and population-register interaction matters in substance as well as procedure. Arrival,
departure and address changes are recorded through communal or cantonal registration systems;
those records feed immigration handling, affect the continuity of residence narrative, and can trigger or
evidence extinction issues. In Lausanne, C-permit holders must register arrival within 14 days, and

entries already present in the cantonal population register may reduce the civil-status documents
needed. Vaud’s official communal manual states that the communal foreign nationals office records
foreigners in the population register, keeps those data updated, and transmits permit files and
information to the cantonal service. Geneva’s OCPM itself maintains the residents’ register and the
foreign-workers register.
Temporary absence is a particularly important area where federal law and local administration interact
closely. Federal SEM guidance states that a C permit expires on deregistration or if the person is actually
abroad for more than six months, unless a request is made before expiry of that period; a preservation
request may keep the permit alive for up to four years, and the canton decides in its own competence
and discretion. Zurich’s official practice note emphasises that there is no entitlement, that the request
must be lodged before six months abroad, and that repeated preservations cannot simply be chained.
Geneva offers a formal “leave of absence” route, with local forms, attachments, fees and returnreporting requirements. Ticino offers a specific official request for absence abroad for C permit holders.

Evidence practice is also strongly localised. Zurich’s internal practice note asks, depending on route, for
social-assistance confirmations from municipalities, detailed debt-enforcement extracts from
municipalities of residence, recognised language certificates and proof of work or education; for some
categories it also looks at school confirmations for children. Bern’s local guides emphasise criminalrecord cleanliness, debt behaviour and language proof; Basel-Stadt’s forms ask about criminal
convictions, pending proceedings, debt-enforcement history, social assistance and supplementary
benefits. Geneva’s renewal route formally asks for additional explanatory material in long-term
significant social-assistance situations, and Lausanne requests an assistance or exemption attestation
where a person is supported by social assistance. These are best treated as official evidence practices of
the relevant jurisdiction, not as exhaustive federal evidential rules.
## Structural overview of cantonal practice and procedure
The national structure is layered. At federal level, the SEM publishes the operative guidance, determines
the earliest date from which competent cantonal authorities may grant settlement permits, and
provides the administrative framework for residence permits generally. At cantonal level, migration or
population offices issue residence permissions, assess many applications, and organise local
administrative channels. At commune level, where that model is used, residents’ offices or bureaux des
étrangers receive registrations, collect or verify documents, maintain register data, collect fees, and
forward files.
The filing route is therefore not uniform across Switzerland. ch.ch states, at a national level, that an
applicant who needs to extend a permit should submit the application to the commune of residence,
but it expressly carves out Geneva, where the applicant should contact the canton instead. That public
guidance aligns with the more decentralised model in Bern, Zurich, Lausanne and Vaud, and with
Geneva’s more centralised OCPM model.
Communal population registration is not just a background civil-status matter. It is part of the migration
workflow. Vaud’s official communal manual says each commune’s foreign nationals office, integrated
into the residents’ control office, must record foreign nationals in the inhabitants’ register, keep the data
constantly updated, create and transmit permit files to the cantonal SPOP, send B-to-C and L-to-B
requests onward, provide integration documentation to new arrivals, and collect the corresponding
fees. Bern City’s EMF similarly combines residence-related tasks with permit granting, extension and

conversion functions. Geneva’s OCPM centrally maintains both the inhabitants’ register and the register
of cross-border foreign workers.
Biometrics and card production are another example of the division of functions. The SEM’s biometric
permit page states that the application procedure and issuance are decided by the canton of residence,
biometrics are captured by the corresponding cantonal authority, and cards are produced centrally.
Vaud’s official public information for EU/EFTA residents describes a three-step flow: announce arrival or
renewal at the communal residents’ office, await a letter from the cantonal SPOP once the file has been
processed, and then attend the biometric centre in Lausanne. Bern’s cantonal guidance similarly states
that, where biometrics are required, the applicant is invited to provide them before the C permit card is
produced and sent to the commune. Geneva states that the permit is sent by registered post around
five to seven business days after biometrics are taken.
Timing rules also show the national/local split. ch.ch says renewal may be sought no earlier than three
months and no later than two weeks before expiry. Local pages then set out the operational route: Bern
City says B, C and S holders receive a notice of expiry from the SEM one to two months before expiry;
Geneva says a durable B or C holder will normally receive a notice one month before expiry and must
otherwise send Form K at least 15 days before expiry; Ticino says the person must request renewal 14
days before control-term expiry and may chase a file only once it has been pending for at least 30 days.
These timing points are procedural and local, even when they sit on top of a federal control-period
framework.
## Legal propositions and primary legal texts
The official primary texts are the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, the Ordinance on
Admission, Stay and Gainful Employment, and the Ordinance of the FDJP on authorisation and priorapproval decisions in immigration law subject to the approval procedure. Zurich’s official materials page
links directly to the federal AIG and VZAE on admin.ch, and the SEM directives interpret and apply those
instruments in detail. For present purposes, the key federal provisions are those on registration and
permit categories, settlement permits, integration criteria, extinction, revocation and downgrading,
preservation during absence abroad, renewal of the permit document and, in limited categories, SEM
approval.

In practical terms, the primary-text structure supports a careful drafting distinction. Substantive
phrases such as “must have”, “has a right to”, “expires”, “may be revoked”, and “language level required”
should usually be reserved for federal provisions or directly federalised treaty routes. Operational
phrases such as “must file at the residents’ office”, “submit by post”, “book a biometric appointment”,
“city office checks completeness”, or “online renewal unavailable in social-assistance cases” should be
expressly identified as canton or commune procedure.
## Official guidance and administrative materials
The SEM’s AIG directives are the most important federal administrative source for this topic. They state
that the settlement permit is unlimited and not conditional; that the authority determines the
appropriate permit category ex officio rather than leaving the person a free choice between permit
types; that ordinary C-permit grant requires residence time, absence of revocation grounds and
integration; that early C-permit grant must be requested expressly; that language evidence can be

shown through recognised federal pathways including the fide system and recognised certificates; and
that preservation during absence abroad is discretionary, must be sufficiently reasoned, and must be
sought before the six-month period expires.
The SEM’s public residence pages add two useful higher-level points. First, they confirm that permits are
issued by cantonal migration offices. Secondly, on biometrics, they confirm that the application
procedure and issuance of the permit card are decided by the canton of residence, even though card
production is centralised. Those public pages are especially useful when explaining why the substantive
Swiss law may be unified while the procedural journey is not.
ch.ch offers the clearest federal public-facing account of the filing route. It says that first permits are
obtained by contacting the cantonal migration or employment authorities of the commune of
residence, that extensions are generally submitted to the commune of residence, and that Geneva is an
exception where the applicant should contact the canton. It also states that renewal may be filed no
earlier than three months and no later than two weeks before expiry, and that the usual documents
include the current permit, a valid passport or identity card, and the expiry notice if one was issued.
The SEM FAQ on residence and integration adds two drafting-safe support points. It restates that the
grant of a settlement permit cannot be tied to further conditions once the permit is granted, although
the person must satisfy integration criteria to obtain it. It also states that the immigration-law language
levels for residence and settlement permits are federal, not variable from canton to canton; by contrast,
naturalisation language levels may be raised by cantons.
## Cantonal practice notes
Zurich. The canton publicly states that federal law and international treaties govern residence rights,
and that the cantonal migration office implements that law and decides entry and residence
applications. Zurich’s public C-permit page sets out the ordinary ten-year route and some five-year
routes. The City of Zurich requires renewal of the foreign-national permit no later than 14 days before
expiry and by appointment. Zurich’s internal practice note on settlement permits asks, depending on
the category, for social-assistance confirmations from municipalities of residence, detailed debtenforcement extracts from municipalities, recognised language certificates, and work-activity evidence;
for some child cases it asks for school material. Zurich’s official absence note states that preservation
requests must be made before six months abroad, that there is no entitlement, and that the authority
decides in discretion.
Bern. The Cantonal Office for Population Services provides the central C-permit page, but the
commune’s residents’ office remains operationally important. Bern states that C-permit conversion
applications and later control-period renewals are filed with the Einwohnerkontrolle of the municipality
of residence, which checks completeness and forwards the file to the cantonal authority; where
biometrics are needed, production follows data capture. Bern City’s own pages show that its EMF unit
combines residents’ registration, migration and foreign-police functions, and they say B, C and S holders
usually receive a notice of expiry from the SEM one to two months before expiry. Bern’s local guides also
describe evidential expectations around language, criminal records, debt behaviour and social
assistance.
Vaud and Lausanne. Vaud’s communal manual is unusually explicit that each commune’s bureau des
étrangers, integrated into the contrôle des habitants, is the intake and transmission desk for the
cantonal SPOP; it registers foreign nationals, updates the population record, compiles the permit
dossier, transmits B-to-C requests, provides integration information and collects fees. Lausanne’s C-

arrival page requires registration within 14 days and differentiates procedures by provenance: the
process differs between arrival from another Vaud commune and another canton, and for some non-
ALCP C holders a new authorisation must be sought on an inter-cantonal move. Lausanne’s renewal
page permits postal filing and, in many cases, online renewal, but expressly excludes people on social
assistance from the online route. Vaud’s official 2024 circular says the online C renewal service was due
to open to all Vaud communes from 1 January 2026, while still leaving applicants the option of filing at
the local residents’ office.
Geneva. Geneva is materially different because the OCPM centrally combines population and migration
functions. Official Geneva pages say that the OCPM keeps the inhabitants’ register and processes
foreign-national permit matters. For C-permit renewal, Geneva expects the holder normally to receive a
notice of expiry one month before expiry; if not, the person must send Form K by post at least 15 days
before expiry. Geneva also publishes additional documentary requirements for long-term substantial
social-assistance dependence, allows the person to continue working after filing if the expired permit
authorised work, and publishes application-status tracking and follow-up options. For temporary
absence, Geneva offers a formal leave-of-absence route for C holders, with local deadlines, a form,
attachments, fees, and a requirement to announce return before the end of the granted period. Geneva
also states that address data must remain correct for permit delivery after biometrics.
Ticino. Ticino’s Sezione della popolazione states that its migration office issues, renews and amends
permits for foreign nationals. Its C-permit pages say a new C permit may normally be granted after ten
years of regular stay with B status, and they provide specific routes for new C applications, renewals and
modifications, and absence abroad. Ticino’s renewal page states that the person must request renewal
14 days before expiry of the control term and notify changes in personal data, civil status and Swiss
address within 14 days of the change. Ticino also provides an official request form for absence abroad
where a C holder will be outside Switzerland for more than six months but less than four years and
wishes to preserve the permit. It also offers an official reminder or follow-up route once a permit case
has been pending at least 30 days.
Basel-Stadt. Basel-Stadt’s public migration pages and forms are currently partly surfaced through
search snippets, but they still show a recognisable local practice pattern. The canton’s public C-permit
page advertises information on grant, extension and preservation. Its grant form asks about criminal
convictions, pending proceedings, debt-enforcement extracts, past and current social assistance, and
supplementary benefits. Its preservation form states the federal six-month rule and four-year
maximum, and contains a local warning that a full or partial withdrawal of pension-fund capital may
lead to revocation of an already granted preservation. That warning should be treated as local
administrative practice language, not as a free-standing federal rule.
## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
The safe analytical model is this: federal law decides entitlement and status; canton and commune
practice decide workflow. If the question is whether a person can qualify for C, whether absences break
continuity, whether integration criteria are met, whether a permit can expire through absence, or
whether a permit can be revoked or downgraded, the answer starts with the AIG, the VZAE, and SEM
guidance. If the question is where to file, who receives the documents first, whether filing is by post,
portal or appointment, which authority sends the expiry notice, which documents are requested in
practice, how biometrics are arranged, or how a person chases the file, the answer is likely canton- or
commune-specific.
A second drafting point is to keep distinct the C permit as status and the permit document or control
period as administration. Federal and cantonal materials consistently describe the permit itself as
unlimited and not conditional, while local pages also describe a five-year control period or a need to
renew the card or present it for renewal. Drafting should therefore avoid saying that “the C permit lasts
five years”. A safer formulation is that the settlement status is indefinite, but the physical permit
document and its control period are subject to periodic administrative renewal or review under the
applicable procedure.
A third caution concerns inter-cantonal moves. It is generally true that a C permit has Swiss-wide
residence value, and Bern says a C holder may in principle move anywhere in Switzerland. But official
Lausanne guidance states that for some holders whose C was not issued under the free-movement
agreement, a change of canton requires a new authorisation and prior enquiry with the cantonal
population service. The correct drafting is therefore not “a C permit always transfers automatically
between cantons”, but rather “C holders generally have inter-cantonal mobility, subject to the federal
framework and any required re-authorisation procedure in the receiving canton, especially for some
third-country cases”.
Evidence drafting also needs discipline. Local offices often ask for debt-enforcement extracts, socialassistance attestations, employment confirmations, school evidence for children, and recognised
language certificates. Those should not be described as a closed federal checklist unless the point
genuinely comes from federal legislation or nationally applicable SEM guidance. The safer phrasing is
that official cantonal and communal practice commonly requires such documents to evidence the
federal criteria of integration, public-order compliance, economic participation and continuity of
residence.
Temporary absence needs particularly careful wording. Federal guidance supports saying that a C
permit expires on deregistration or on actual absence abroad of more than six months unless
preservation is requested in time, and that even short visits back to Switzerland do not stop the clock if
the person’s centre of life has moved abroad. But the grant of preservation is discretionary, local forms
and deadlines differ, and local authorities can impose their own procedural channels and evidential
expectations. For that reason, internal content should say “may apply for preservation” rather than “is
entitled to preservation”, unless a narrower statement is being made about the ability to lodge the
request.
For content-system drafting, the following formulations are comparatively safe:

Federal formulation: “Under Swiss federal law, settlement permit eligibility depends on the applicable
residence period, absence of revocation grounds and fulfilment of the federal integration criteria,
including the relevant language threshold.” 38

Local-procedure formulation: “The competent filing route, supporting documents, biometric process,
and renewal timeline are handled under cantonal and, in many jurisdictions, communal procedure.”

Preservation formulation: “A C holder who will be abroad for more than six months should consider
applying, before the federal deadline expires, for preservation of the permit under the procedure of the
competent canton.” 40

The principal limitations of this pack are that not all cantons and communes publish the same level of
detail, and not every local source is equally current or equally accessible. The most reliable and recent

procedural examples located here are from Zurich, Bern, Vaud/Lausanne, Geneva, Ticino and Basel-
Stadt. Those examples are broad enough to support internal drafting rules, but not to justify Swiss-wide
procedural statements beyond what federal sources expressly say.
