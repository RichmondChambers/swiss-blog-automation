# Swiss C Permit Rights, Stability and Limits Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary
The Swiss C permit is the settlement permit ( Niederlassungsbewilligung / autorisation
d’établissement / permesso di domicilio ). Official federal and cantonal materials consistently
describe it as a status of indefinite duration which, at the point of grant, is not tied to conditions.
SEM’s public guidance for EU/EFTA nationals states that the right to settle is not subject to time
restrictions or conditions, and Zurich’s migration instruction states that the settlement permit is
unlimited and may not be linked to conditions under Article 34(1) FNIA/AIG.
That said, indefinite duration is not the same thing as an untouchable or unconditional residence
right in every later circumstance. Official SEM directives make clear that a C permit can expire by
operation of law, notably on deregistration abroad, on the grant of a permit in another canton, on
expulsion, or after an actual absence abroad of six months, unless its maintenance has been requested
in time. The same directives and SEM FAQ also make clear that a C permit can be revoked on specified
statutory grounds and can even be downgraded to a B permit if the integration criteria are no longer
met.
In practical terms, the C permit materially improves a person’s position as compared with more limited
permit categories. Under SEM’s AIG directives, the ordinary L and B permits are limited in duration and
may be linked to conditions; by contrast, gainful activity by C permit holders is not subject to
immigration-law, labour-market or economic restrictions, and C holders do not need a permit for a
change of profession or job. They also have a claim to change canton provided no revocation grounds
exist.
The physical permit card must be distinguished from the underlying settlement status. Official
federal and cantonal materials repeatedly say that the settlement permit itself is unlimited, but the
foreign national card for settled persons is issued for a five-year control period and must be presented
for renewal before expiry. Failure to comply can attract a fine. In practice, cantonal authorities routinely
speak of renewing the control period or the card, not of re-granting settlement status from scratch.

For later content drafting, the safest overall proposition is this: a Swiss C permit usually gives much
greater residence stability and day-to-day freedom than L or ordinary B status, but it does not
erase statutory loss, revocation, downgrading, registration, absence, or compliance rules. Content
should therefore avoid calling it “unconditional permanent residence” without qualification.

A necessary nuance for internal drafting is that the B-permit comparator is not uniform across the
whole system. SEM’s AIG directives describe the ordinary B permit as limited and potentially
conditional, whereas SEM’s free-movement pages show that EU/EFTA B permits often run for five years
and can already carry extensive mobility. Content comparing C with B should therefore identify whether
it is describing the ordinary FNIA regime or the EU/EFTA free-movement context.
The legal structure is therefore best described as follows: the C permit is settlement status with
materially greater stability, but not a residence right detached from continued legal compliance
and actual residence links to Switzerland. That conclusion is not a free-standing label in one text; it

follows from reading together the official statements that C is indefinite and condition-free at grant,
with the official rules on extinction, revocation, downgrade and absence.

Primary legal texts. The official SEM legal-bases pages identify the core instruments as the Federal
Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration ( FNIA/AIG/LEI/LStrI , RS/CC 142.20), the Ordinance on
Admission, Period of Stay and Employment ( VZAE/OASA/OASA , RS 142.201), and the Ordinance on
the Integration of Foreign Nationals ( VIntA/OIE , RS 142.205). The operative articles most relevant
to the post-grant C-permit analysis are those cited repeatedly in the SEM directives and official pages:
Articles 32, 33, 34, 37, 38, 41, 58a, 61, 62 and 63 FNIA/AIG, together with VZAE provisions including
Articles 55–58, 61a, 62a, 63, 67–68, 77a–77b, 79 and 90a.
Official federal guidance and administrative materials. The most useful current federal authority is
the SEM’s AIG directives (version state: 1 January 2026), supplemented by the SEM FAQ on residence
and integration criteria, the SEM residence overview, the SEM integration requirements page, the
SEM language requirements page, the SEM EU/EFTA C-permit page, and the SEM biometric
residence permit page. Read together, these materials give a stable and conservative map of what a C
permit changes and what it does not change.
## Cantonal practice notes
Zurich’s internal instruction on the settlement permit is especially useful for drafting because it states,
in one place, both halves of the key distinction: the settlement permit is unlimited and may not be
linked to conditions, but the foreign national card is issued for a five-year control period. Zurich’s
separate maintenance instruction then makes the post-grant limit explicit: if the person remains abroad
for more than six months, the permit lapses by law unless maintenance is sought in time; there is no
entitlement to maintenance, and a late return generally means the person is treated as a new entrant
under the ordinary admission rules.
Bern’s canton page is useful for practical handling of the control period. It says the SEM usually sends
an expiry notice one to two months before the control period ends, and that if the control period can be
extended a new C card is produced. The same Bern page also notes, in practical terms, that the
settlement permit generally applies across Switzerland and that registration formalities arise on
moving. Bern’s maintenance leaflet further stresses that an application to preserve the permit during an
absence abroad must generally be lodged at deregistration or, if the person left without deregistering,
before the end of the six-month absence; it also states expressly that there is no entitlement to
maintenance.
Aargau’s service page is a clear practical statement of the card-status distinction: although the
settlement permit itself is unlimited, the card for settled persons is issued with a five-year duration
and must be presented to the commune at least 14 days before expiry. That is useful internal support
for statements which distinguish expiry of the card from expiry of the settlement right itself.
Geneva’s English-language portal is useful because it shows how cantons operationalise renewal. It
speaks of extending a B or C permit when the document is about to expire, requires filing by set
deadlines if the expiry notice has not arrived, and asks for additional documents in some long-term
social-assistance cases. Geneva’s leave-of-absence page also states that a C holder may apply to keep
the permit for up to four years, but must file within the relevant time limits; if the deadline is missed,
the settlement permit expires with effect from the actual departure date. These pages should be
treated as practice notes, not as a substitute for the federal legal framework.
Zurich’s canton-change instruction is particularly important for third-country cases. It says that, beyond
the wording of Article 37, even C-permit holders need a new permit for the new canton in practice; the
earlier permit expires only once the new canton grants its own permit, and moving first can cause
problems if canton change is refused. The same Zurich note distinguishes people protected by the
Agreement on Free Movement of Persons, who have fuller geographic mobility and a permit valid for
the whole of Switzerland. This is a strong practical warning against over-simple drafting on canton
mobility.
## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
The most accurate internal description is that the C permit removes the ordinary time-limit and
condition structure which characterises L permits and much of the ordinary B-permit architecture, and
it substantially increases labour-market freedom and residence stability. In everyday terms, that
usually means less immigration-law re-testing of the stay purpose, no immigration-law permit
requirement for changing job or profession, and a materially stronger position when relocating within
Switzerland.
The decisive drafting guardrail, however, is that the C permit is not an all-purpose immunity shield.
Official materials preserve at least four major classes of post-grant vulnerability: automatic extinction
rules; revocation on specified statutory grounds; criminal-law expulsion consequences; and
downgrading to B where integration criteria are no longer met. Official SEM materials also show that
post-grant assessment is not limited to classic serious criminality: social assistance dependence, publicorder issues, taxes, debts, and broader integration failures can still matter, even if the threshold for
outright revocation of a settlement permit is higher than for limited permits.
The absence rules deserve particular prominence in later content. SEM’s 2026 directives say that a
short return trip to Switzerland does not interrupt the six-month period if the centre of life has moved
abroad. They also say that maintenance during an absence is possible only if the application is made in
time and is discretionary; Zurich’s instruction adds that late applications generally mean the person is
treated as a new entrant, while Geneva states the permit expires on the actual departure date if the
deadline is missed. During maintenance, visa-requiring third-country nationals may need a visa to enter
Switzerland and may not work in Switzerland until the maintained C status has effectively resumed in
full.
The social-assistance point is often oversimplified in public-facing content and should be handled
carefully. SEM’s directives say that revocation on this ground requires durable and substantial
dependence, with a forward-looking assessment of whether the dependence is likely to continue; they
also explain that the old statutory shelter for some very long-term residents has been removed, so
more than fifteen years in Switzerland does not by itself prevent revocation on this ground. Geneva’s
practice page and the SEM FAQ use monetary figures as reference points, but those figures should not
be drafted as if they were self-standing statutory thresholds.
For later content production, the following cautions are the safest:

      • Use “settlement permit” or “Swiss C permit”, and if needed add “of indefinite duration”;
        avoid calling it “unconditional permanent residence” without immediate qualification. Official
        sources support “indefinite” and “without conditions” at grant, but the same official sources
        preserve extinction, revocation and downgrade.
      • Do not say “the C permit expires every five years”. The safer formulation is that the card/control
        period expires every five years, while the underlying settlement status is indefinite unless it is
        extinguished, revoked or downgraded.
      • Do not say “a C holder can move canton freely with no permission issues” in all cases. The safer
        formulation is that a C holder has a claim to change canton if no revocation grounds exist, but
        third-country cases still involve cantonal procedure and timing.
      • Do not reduce post-grant risk to serious criminality only. Official materials treat durable social-
        assistance dependence, serious public-order issues, and in some contexts debts, taxes and
        broader integration deficits as relevant after grant.
      • Do not say that the C permit removes all other immigration-law limits. By SEM’s own FAQ, even C
        sponsors still face family-reunification deadlines in the general regime, and EU/EFTA settlement
        permits are governed by the FNIA and settlement treaties rather than by the free-movement
        agreement itself.
