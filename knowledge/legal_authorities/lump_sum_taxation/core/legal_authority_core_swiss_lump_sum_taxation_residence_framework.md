# Core Swiss Lump-Sum Taxation Residence Framework Pack

*Suggested internal filename:* `legal_authority_core_swiss_lump_sum_taxation_residence_framework_pack.pdf`

## Executive legal summary

Swiss “lump-sum taxation” is not a separate residence category in its own right. It is a tax method — more precisely, taxation according to expenditure — available to certain foreign nationals who become Swiss tax resident and do not carry on gainful activity in Switzerland. At federal level it is a special method for assessing taxable income, and in cantonal law it also interacts with wealth tax. It does not convert Swiss residence into a flat annual fee: the ordinary tax tariffs still apply, but to a special assessment base determined by expenditure and several statutory minimums and control calculations. The regime therefore sits at the junction of Swiss tax law, cantonal tax practice, and migration law, rather than replacing any of them. 1

The tax-side gateway is comparatively clear. The taxpayer must be a foreign national, must take up Swiss tax residence for the first time or after at least a ten-year break, and must not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland. For spouses living together, both spouses must satisfy the conditions. The federal direct tax base is determined by annual worldwide living expenses, but not below the highest of several statutory comparators, including a federal minimum amount, the seven-times-rent or rental-value rule for taxpayers with their own household, or the control calculation based on specified Swiss-source income and treaty-relief income. Official Geneva materials show the indexed federal minimum for 2026 at CHF 435,000, while cantonal/communal minima and wealth-tax handling remain canton-specific. 2

The immigration-side gateway is distinct. For non-EU/EFTA applicants, the relevant official materials do not describe an entitlement analogous to a “golden visa”. Instead, they place such cases within the discretionary architecture of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and the implementing ordinance for cases involving important public interests, including significant cantonal fiscal interests. SEM’s 2026 AIG directives explicitly describe this as a broad area of cantonal discretion involving an indeterminate legal concept, requiring proof that the applicant will move their centre of life to Switzerland and spend the majority of their time there. For permits granted on the basis of significant cantonal fiscal interests, any gainful activity may only be exercised abroad, with management of one’s own assets expressly carved out. 3

For EU/EFTA nationals, the position is different again. Their residence rights ordinarily arise under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons rather than under the third-country fiscal-interest route. SEM’s official fact sheet for persons without gainful activity states that EU/EFTA nationals may obtain a residence permit if they have sufficient financial means and comprehensive health insurance; the permit is ordinarily issued for five years and is renewable if the conditions continue to be met. In other words, for EU/EFTA nationals, expenditure-based taxation may still matter on the tax side, but immigration law normally starts from the AFMP framework for economically inactive persons, not from a discretionary “fiscal interest” admission model. 4

Canton choice is therefore central. It affects not only tax cost but also evidence, administrative culture, treaty handling, and whether the regime is available at all. The Federal Department of Finance states that Zurich, Schaffhausen, Appenzell Ausserrhoden, Basel-Landschaft and Basel-Stadt abolished the regime, while some other cantons retained it but tightened it. Official cantonal materials retrieved for Geneva, Valais and Ticino confirm that operating cantonal practice can differ materially. Geneva, for example, publishes a dedicated lump-sum taxation workflow, requires application from arrival or before the first assessment becomes final, disallows paid board roles in its practice, and usually documents the arrangement by a convention with the tax authority that is generally valid for five years. Valais uses a joint tax-and-migration information form specifically aimed at obtaining a residence permit. Ticino’s official materials show both continued use of the regime and procedural reforms affecting binding tax attestations from 2023. 5

Practical compliance remains important even where the fiscal contribution is substantial. A tax ruling does not itself confer residence rights. Conversely, an immigration permit does not guarantee continued expenditure-based taxation if the tax conditions are no longer met. Official guidance shows that Swiss gainful activity, Swiss nationality, or a change in residence pattern can all be fatal. Double-tax treaty access can also be narrower than domestic Swiss tax residence, because Switzerland’s modified lump-sum approach requires broader inclusion of income from certain treaty states if treaty relief is sought. Long-term planning must therefore address residence evidence, family strategy, activity restrictions, treaty exposure, renewal, settlement permit timing, and eventual ordinary naturalisation. Naturalisation and Swiss citizenship are incompatible with expenditure-based taxation, and there is no official basis for presenting this route as citizenship-by-investment. 6

## Structural overview and table of propositions

### Structural overview of the lump-sum taxation residence framework

#### Legal nature of expenditure-based taxation

Swiss expenditure-based taxation is a special method of tax assessment, not a distinct tax type and not a visa product. The EFD describes it as a simplified method of assessing income and assets for certain foreign nationals; the ordinary tax tariffs still apply once the base is fixed. That framing is legally important because it prevents later drafting from incorrectly describing the regime as a negotiated flat charge detached from ordinary Swiss tax law. 7

#### Relationship between tax ruling and residence permit

The tax arrangement and the residence permit are legally connected in practice but remain separate. Geneva’s official process requires the tax application to run in parallel with the cantonal migration office, while Valais uses a joint tax-and-migration form directed expressly at residence-permit issuance. At the same time, SEM’s AIG directives treat admissions based on important public interests as discretionary immigration decisions. The practical interaction is therefore real, but the tax ruling does not itself create an immigration entitlement. 8

#### Ordinary eligibility conditions

On the tax side, the core conditions are foreign nationality, first Swiss residence or return after at least ten years’ absence, and no gainful activity in Switzerland. Both spouses living together must satisfy the conditions. The regime is lost if Swiss nationality is acquired or if Swiss gainful activity begins. Official Geneva practice adds that activity “from Switzerland” is also problematic and that a paid directorship is

not accepted in Geneva; that point should be treated as cantonal practice, not universal federal blackletter law. 9

#### Federal and cantonal tax-base rules

The assessment base is the highest of the statutory comparators: actual annual living expenses, the federal minimum amount, the seven-times-rent or rental-value rule for taxpayers with their own household, the three-times-board-price rule for board-and-lodging cases, and the control calculation. The control calculation includes specified Swiss-source income and, if treaty relief is claimed, relevant foreign-source income from treaty states. Cantonal/communal treatment can diverge, particularly for wealth tax and minimums. Geneva, for example, applies a 10% uplift on the retained cantonal base to reflect wealth tax and publishes indexed cantonal and federal minima. 10

#### Canton selection and cantonal discretion

Canton choice determines whether the regime is available at all, what minimums apply, how wealth tax is reflected, what ruling evidence is requested, and how migration authorities approach the file. The federal dossier identifies five cantons that have abolished the regime. Official materials retrieved for Geneva, Valais and Ticino confirm that retained cantons can differ markedly in minimums and procedure. 11

#### Immigration basis and nationality distinctions

For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the official framework is discretionary and grounded in important public interests, including significant cantonal fiscal interests. For EU/EFTA nationals, the ordinary route is residence without gainful activity under the AFMP, requiring sufficient means and health insurance. The nationality distinction is therefore fundamental and should never be flattened in later drafting. 12

#### Residence, centre of life and presence expectations

SEM’s AIG directives require applicants relying on important public interests to show that they will move their centre of life to Switzerland and spend the majority of their time there. ESTV Circular No. 44, on the tax side, treats tax liability as beginning with Swiss domicile or residence and makes clear that temporary departures do not necessarily interrupt residence. The relevant inquiry is therefore broader than a crude “183-day” test. 13

#### Activity restrictions, family members and longer-term planning

Activity restrictions are strict. Federal tax guidance excludes Swiss gainful activity; SEM’s immigration guidance says that, in fiscal-interest admissions, any gainful activity may only be exercised abroad, while private asset management is allowed. EU/EFTA non-workers must likewise avoid a connection to the Swiss labour market. Family members do not derive status from the tax ruling alone; EU/EFTA law expressly protects family reunion for qualifying non-workers, whereas third-country family reunification sits in the ordinary AIG architecture and should be checked case by case. Longer-term planning must therefore consider permit renewal, settlement timing, absence rules, and eventual ordinary naturalisation. 14

## Primary legal texts

This section focuses on the provisions most relevant to expenditure-based taxation residence analysis. Where I did not retrieve a current Fedlex line-by-line consolidated text directly, I give a close paraphrase grounded in official administrative materials and say so expressly.

### Federal direct tax and tax harmonisation

#### Federal Act on Direct Federal Tax

Provision: Article 14 DBG / LIFD, as explained in ESTV Circular No. 44. Official title: Federal Act of 14 December 1990 on Direct Federal Tax. Close paraphrase: A natural person may pay tax according to expenditure instead of ordinary income tax if the person is not a Swiss citizen, becomes subject to unlimited Swiss tax liability for the first time or after an interruption of at least ten years, and does not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland. Both spouses living together must qualify. The assessment is based on annual worldwide living expenses, but not below the highest statutory comparator including the federal minimum, the seven-times-rent/ rental-value test, the board-and-lodging comparator, and the control calculation. Treaty relief for certain states triggers the modified approach. Acquiring Swiss nationality or engaging in Swiss gainful activity ends the regime. 30

Why it matters: Article 14 is the core black-letter tax provision for lump-sum taxation at federal level. Later drafting should treat it as the starting point for all eligibility, tax-base and loss-of-status analysis.

#### Transitional provision

Provision: Article 205d DBG, as described in ESTV Circular No. 44 and the EFD dossier. Official title: Federal Act on Direct Federal Tax. Close paraphrase: Persons already taxed according to expenditure on 1 January 2016 remained under prior law until 31 December 2020. 18

Why it matters: Pre-2016 materials can still appear in practice notes and older rulings; for current drafting they should generally be treated as historical only. 31

#### Tax residence starting point

Provision: Article 3 DBG, read with Circular No. 44. Official title: Federal Act on Direct Federal Tax. Close paraphrase: Swiss tax liability arises from Swiss domicile or residence; when assessing the start of residence for expenditure-tax purposes, prior stays count, multiple stays are aggregated, and temporary departure does not necessarily interrupt residence. 32

Why it matters: This is one reason why a simplistic “183 days” description is inadequate for this route.

#### Tax Harmonisation Act

Provision: Article 6 StHG / LHID / LAID, as identified by cantonal and federal official materials. Official title: Federal Act of 14 December 1990 on the Harmonisation of Direct Taxes of the Cantons and Municipalities. Close paraphrase: Cantons must provide the possibility of expenditure-based taxation subject to harmonised core conditions, while retaining competence over cantonal details such as cantonal minima and wealth-tax treatment. Geneva’s official guidance expressly links its regime to Article 6 LHID, and the EFD dossier states that the 2014 reform tightened cantonal law through the Tax Harmonisation Act before the federal direct-tax reform took effect in 2016. 34

Why it matters: It explains why the architecture is nationally recognisable but still materially divergent among cantons. 35

### Federal ordinance on expenditure-based taxation

#### Ordinance on expenditure-based taxation for direct federal tax

Provision: Ordinance of 20 February 2013 on taxation according to expenditure for direct federal tax, as described in Circular No. 44. Official title: Verordnung über die Besteuerung nach dem Aufwand bei der direkten Bundessteuer. Close paraphrase: The ordinance operationalises Article 14 DBG, including the determination of annual living expenses, valuation mechanics, and the control calculation for direct federal tax. ESTV Circular No. 44 treats the ordinance as the implementing basis for the post-reform federal regime in force from 1 January 2016. 36

Why it matters: For tax computation, the ordinance is the principal implementing text. Where detailed numerical treatment is needed in later work, the current consolidated ordinance should be checked directly on Fedlex for the relevant tax year. 37

### Foreign nationals and integration legislation

#### Scope and nationality distinction

Provision: Article 2 AIG, as described in SEM guidance. Official title: Federal Act of 16 December 2005 on Foreign Nationals and Integration. Close paraphrase: For EU/EFTA persons, the AIG applies only subsidiarily where the AFMP does not provide otherwise or where the AIG is more favourable. SEM’s AIG directives state this expressly. 38

Why it matters: It prevents confusion between the third-country discretionary route and the AFMP route for economically inactive EU/EFTA citizens. 4

#### Admission without gainful activity for retirees

Provision: Article 28 AIG, as referenced by SEM. Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. Close paraphrase: A specific statutory basis exists for certain retirees, but it is not the same as the fiscal-interest route and is not the core basis for high-net-worth third-country expenditure-tax residence cases addressed here. SEM’s AIG directives refer to Article 28 AIG for retirees and distinguish it from Article 30/32 public-interest cases. 39

Why it matters: Later content should not collapse retirees’ residence and fiscal-interest residence into a single legal pathway. 40

#### Exceptions from ordinary admission requirements

Provision: Article 30 paragraph 1 letter b AIG, as described in SEM AIG directives. Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. Close paraphrase: The authorities may depart from ordinary admission requirements where important public interests so require. SEM describes these permissions as “can” provisions that give the competent authority a wide discretion. 25

Why it matters: This is the key immigration hook for non-EU/EFTA high-net-worth residence linked to significant cantonal fiscal interests. 25

#### Settlement permit

Provision: Article 34 AIG, as summarised in SEM AIG directives and SEM residence pages. Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. Close paraphrase: Settlement follows ordinary residence-period and conduct/integration rules; the settlement permit is not a special fiscal-interest reward built into the expenditure-tax regime. SEM’s directives summarise the general conditions, and SEM’s public pages explain that C permits are granted after five or ten years depending on the applicable framework. 41

Why it matters: Longer-term planning must assume the ordinary settlement regime unless a specific treaty or ordinary immigration rule applies. 42

#### Lapse and revocation

Provision: Articles 61 to 63 AIG, as described in SEM AIG directives. Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. Close paraphrase: Residence permits lapse on actual abandonment of residence, and prolonged absence abroad is legally significant; for residence permits, absence beyond six months is treated by SEM as actual abandonment under Article 61 paragraph 2 AIG. Settlement permits can in some circumstances be maintained on timely application, and revocation rules remain separately applicable.

Why it matters: Wealthy applicants who travel frequently can lose immigration status through residence patterns even if they remain fiscally attractive. 44

#### Discretionary decision-making

Provision: Article 96 AIG, as reflected in SEM VFP directives for cases outside AFMP rights and in SEM AIG commentary on Article 30. Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. Close paraphrase: Where there is no treaty-based or statutory entitlement, the decision remains discretionary. SEM’s materials repeatedly emphasise discretion both for Article 30 public-interest cases and, in AFMP materials, for cases outside the AFMP right. 45

Why it matters: This is why a negotiated tax outcome does not produce an automatic residence right.

### Implementing immigration ordinance

#### Important public interests

Provision: Article 32 VZAE, as explained in SEM AIG directives. Official title: Ordinance of 24 October 2007 on Admission, Residence and Employment. Close paraphrase: A short-stay or residence permit may be granted to safeguard important public interests; relevant factors include significant cultural interests, significant state-policy interests, significant cantonal fiscal interests, and the necessity of a person’s presence during criminal proceedings. For admissions based on significant cantonal fiscal interests, any gainful activity may only be exercised abroad; management of one’s own assets is excepted. 47

Why it matters: This is the most important immigration implementing provision for non-EU/EFTA lumpsum residence cases. 25

#### Serious personal hardship

Provision: Article 31 VZAE, as distinguished in SEM directives. Official title: Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. Close paraphrase: Article 31 deals with severe personal hardship and should not be confused with fiscal-interest admissions under Article 32, even though both sit under Article 30 AIG exceptions. 48

Why it matters: It avoids mixing two different exceptional-admission doctrines. 48

#### Retirees

Provision: Article 25 VZAE, as referenced by SEM directives. Official title: Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. Close paraphrase: Article 25 implements the retirees’ route and remains distinct from Article 32 fiscalinterest cases. 39

Why it matters: Again, the retirees’ route is not simply a synonym for lump-sum residence. 40

### AFMP and economically inactive EU/EFTA residence

#### Residence without gainful activity

Provision: Article 24 Annex I AFMP, as implemented and explained by SEM’s fact sheet and VFP directives. Official title: Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, Annex I. Close paraphrase: Economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals have a right of residence if they possess sufficient financial means and comprehensive health insurance, with family members able to join subject to the treaty conditions. SEM’s fact sheet states these conditions and explains the five-year B permit. 4

Why it matters: It provides the ordinary immigration basis for EU/EFTA lump-sum taxpayers or prospective lump-sum taxpayers. The tax regime does not replace these treaty residence rights. 4

### Civil-code domicile provisions

#### Domicile and centre of life

Provision: Swiss Civil Code, particularly the domicile concept. Official title: Swiss Civil Code. Close paraphrase: The civil-law domicile concept turns on residence with the intention of settling. I did not retrieve the current official Fedlex wording directly in the available searches; however, the tax and migration materials retrieved consistently work with the same functional ideas of domicile, centre of life, and actual residence. 13

Why it matters: Later drafting can refer to domicile and centre-of-life evidence, but any verbatim quotation of Civil Code provisions should be checked directly on Fedlex before publication. 13

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

#### Official federal guidance

The most important tax guidance retrieved is ESTV Circular No. 44, which explains the post-reform federal regime. It sets out the subjective conditions, the loss of eligibility on Swiss nationality or Swiss gainful activity, the seven-times-rent method, the control calculation, the filing procedure, and the modified lump-sum treatment required for treaty relief with Belgium, Germany, Italy, Norway, Canada, Austria and the USA. It is the single most important federal tax-administrative source for later drafting.

The EFD dossier on expenditure-based taxation is less technical but highly useful as an official constitutional-style overview. It confirms that the regime is a simplified assessment method rather than a separate tax, that ordinary tariffs still apply, that Swiss-source and treaty-relief income sit inside the control calculation, and that Zurich, Schaffhausen, Appenzell Ausserrhoden, Basel-Landschaft and Basel-Stadt abolished the regime. It also confirms the staggered 2014/2016 implementation of the reform. 15

The federal Form 3 and Form 3a guidance are practically important administrative materials because they show what the tax authorities actually ask for annually. They expressly require disclosure of treatyrelief income and specifically call out the seven modified-treaty states, confirming that a taxpayer seeking treaty relief from those states must include all Swiss-taxable income from those states in the calculation. 49

On the immigration side, the central source is the SEM AIG directives. They make five points especially relevant to this route: first, important public interests under Article 30 AIG / Article 32 VZAE are an indeterminate legal concept; secondly, Article 30 permissions are discretionary “can” provisions; thirdly, significant cantonal fiscal interests are expressly listed as a relevant public-interest factor; fourthly, applicants must show that they will move their centre of life to Switzerland and stay in Switzerland predominantly; and fifthly, for fiscal-interest admissions any gainful activity may only be exercised abroad, with management of one’s own assets expressly excepted. 25

For EU/EFTA nationals, the key federal materials are the SEM fact sheet on residence without gainful activity and the SEM VFP directives. Together they show that the principal immigration basis for an economically inactive EU/EFTA national is not the third-country fiscal-interest exception but the AFMP right of residence for persons with sufficient means and comprehensive insurance, plus family reunion rights and renewable permit protection while the conditions continue to be met. 4

For longer-term planning, the retrieved official SEM materials show that settlement and naturalisation remain part of the ordinary immigration and nationality systems. SEM’s public pages state that C permits are granted after five or ten years depending on the applicable framework, and that ordinary naturalisation generally requires ten years’ residence and a C permit. None of the retrieved official materials created a special settlement or citizenship entitlement for lump-sum taxpayers as such. 50

### Cantonal practice notes

#### Geneva

Source: Official Geneva tax portal on imposition d’après la dépense. 51

What the canton says: Geneva states that the regime is for foreign nationals residing in Switzerland without gainful activity; the applicant must request it on arrival or before the first assessment becomes final; the tax office expects a written explanation, a proposed taxable amount, proof of accommodation, and a specific form covering worldwide living standards. Geneva says the arrangement is generally documented by a convention valid for five years, requires notification of changes in circumstances, and is renewed using the same form. It publishes three-base comparisons, adds a 10% uplift on the retained cantonal base for wealth-tax purposes, and publishes annual indexed minima. It also states that private wealth management is tolerated within jurisprudential limits, while a paid board role is not accepted in Geneva. 52

Classification: Mostly procedural and evidential practice; partly explanatory cantonal tax law; not all points are federal black-letter law. The prohibition on paid board roles should be treated as Geneva practice, not necessarily a universal Swiss rule. 26

#### Valais

Source: Official Valais tax page and the joint tax/migration permit form; official Valais ordinance snippet. 53

What the canton says: Valais publishes an “Impôt sur la dépense” page and a one-page form expressly addressed to the tax authorities and the service of population and migration for obtaining a residence permit. The form requires identity details, planned municipality of residence, planned address, previous tax domicile, confirmation whether the applicant was fully taxable in Switzerland in the last ten years, confirmation whether gainful activity in Switzerland is planned, and a detailed annualised expenditure budget. The officially indexed law snippet retrieved from Valais’ legislative database shows a cantonal minimum amount of CHF 250,000 under its 2016 ordinance. 53

Classification: Strong procedural evidence of the practical linkage between tax and migration handling in Valais; the minimum amount is a cantonal legal rule from the ordinance snippet retrieved. It should nevertheless be checked for current indexed updates before any live advice is given. 54

#### Ticino

Source: Official Ticino tax legislation and official Ticino tax communications. 55

What the canton says: Ticino’s official legislative snippet confirms an “imposizione globale secondo il dispendio” in its tax law, using the familiar foreign-national/first-arrival-or-ten-year-break/no-Swiss-work structure. An official 2025 communication also publishes data on persons taxed according to expenditure in Ticino. Separately, Ticino’s official materials state that “attestazioni vincolanti” were abolished from 1 January 2023. 55

I did not retrieve a Ticino source specifically describing a current lump-sum residence workflow equivalent to Geneva or Valais. 56

## Practical legal analysis

The first analytical point is that the tax ruling and the residence permit are mutually relevant but not legally identical. Official canton materials show that they are often prepared together or in parallel: Geneva expressly coordinates the tax request with the cantonal population and migration office, and Valais uses a single form directed both to tax and migration authorities. But the SEM directives make clear that the immigration decision, at least for third-country public-interest cases, remains a discretionary admission decision under migration law. The correct internal formulation is therefore that a favourable tax ruling is often a necessary practical precondition for a viable residence file, but is not in itself a residence right. 8

The second point is that non-EU/EFTA cases are especially discretionary. SEM’s AIG directives do not present significant cantonal fiscal interests as a bright-line right. They present them as one example within “important public interests”, itself an open-ended legal concept, and they emphasise broad

administrative discretion. That is why later drafting should avoid “entitlement” language for thirdcountry applicants. The better legal formulation is that admission may be granted if the canton considers there to be sufficiently weighty fiscal or other public interests and the applicant meets the factual conditions, but the outcome remains discretionary and sensitive to the public-interest balance.

The third point is that canton choice sits at the heart of both feasibility and ultimate tax burden. Federal materials confirm that some cantons abolished the regime entirely, while the retrieved canton materials show materially different minimums and approaches even among cantons that still operate it. Geneva uses openly published conventions, indexed minima and wealth-tax uplift mechanics; Valais publishes an immigration-facing intake form; Ticino has its own tax-law wording and has altered its procedural environment by abolishing binding attestations. It follows — as a professional inference grounded in these official sources — that “best canton” cannot be answered by tax rate alone. It requires a combined analysis of tax minimums, wealth-tax method, evidence burdens, migration culture, treaty profile, and the client’s intended family and travel pattern. 57

The fourth point is that residence evidence matters even for wealthy applicants. SEM requires proof that the centre of life will move to Switzerland and that the applicant will spend the majority of time here. ESTV’s own tax guidance also works with domicile or residence and treats temporary absences as not necessarily breaking continuity. These sources together support a conservative internal approach: expenditure-tax residence is not secured by tax payment alone, and neither tax nor migration analysis should rely on a fixed days-count shorthand. Residence patterns, housing arrangements, personal and family location, and documentary consistency all matter. 58

The fifth point is that gainful-activity analysis is often the most dangerous compliance area. On the tax side, Swiss gainful activity excludes the regime, and the ESTV guidance takes a broad view that includes principal and secondary occupations as well as personally exercised artistic, scientific, inventive, sporting and board activities. On the immigration side, SEM says that when admission rests on significant cantonal fiscal interests, gainful activity may only be performed abroad, with management of one’s own assets left outside the prohibition. Geneva adds a local practice point excluding paid directorships. The internal lesson is that “family office” activity, paid board posts, Swiss client development, Swiss employee supervision, and entrepreneurial activity physically exercised from Switzerland all require careful legal characterisation before any advice is given. 59

The sixth point is that treaty planning can change the real tax position significantly. Official federal materials show that treaty relief may pull into the calculation income that a casual observer might assume sits outside the Swiss lump-sum base, and that the seven modified-treaty states require a broader “modified” approach if treaty benefits are to be claimed. Geneva’s explanatory materials reinforce this. Accordingly, effective tax modelling for this route cannot stop at the negotiated expenditure amount. It must also test Swiss-source income, Swiss assets, foreign-source income from modified-treaty states, and whether foreign withholding-tax relief is genuinely worth preserving. 60

The seventh point is that this route is not a Swiss golden visa or citizenship-by-investment programme. None of the retrieved official sources uses that language. The immigration framework is discretionary for third-country applicants and treaty-based for qualifying EU/EFTA non-workers. Settlement and naturalisation remain subject to the ordinary C-permit and citizenship systems, and Swiss nationality itself ends eligibility for expenditure-based taxation. The safest internal description is therefore: a taxresidence and discretionary-residence planning route for certain foreign nationals, not an investment-for-status scheme. 61

## Drafting cautions

- Do not describe Swiss expenditure-based taxation as a “Swiss golden visa” in legal analysis. The official materials frame it as a tax-assessment method plus separate residence-law pathways, not as an investment visa. 62

- Do not suggest that a tax ruling automatically creates a right to residence. Third-country immigration cases under important public interests remain discretionary. 25

- Do not describe the regime as a flat annual fee. Ordinary tax tariffs still apply to the relevant deemed base. 63

- Do not omit the control calculation. It is a structural component of the regime, not an edge case.

- Do not omit the no-gainful-activity condition, and do not underplay activity-risk analysis for board roles or Swiss-facing business functions. 64

- Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA positions. EU/EFTA economically inactive residence generally rests on the AFMP, whereas third-country fiscal-interest residence is discretionary. 65

- Do not state that 183 days is the sole residence test. The official materials use domicile, residence, centre of life and majority presence concepts. 33

- Do not treat all cantons as available or equivalent. Five abolished the regime according to the EFD, and retained cantons differ materially. 15

- Do not present cantonal practice points as federal law. Geneva’s approach to paid directorships and five-year conventions, for example, is cantonal practice. 26

- Do not imply that expenditure-based taxation survives Swiss citizenship. Acquisition of Swiss nationality ends eligibility. 18

- Do not imply that citizenship can be obtained by investment. Ordinary naturalisation still follows the standard ten-year/C-permit route in the retrieved official sources. 66

- Do not overstate treaty access. Check the relevant treaty and whether modified lump-sum treatment is required. 60
