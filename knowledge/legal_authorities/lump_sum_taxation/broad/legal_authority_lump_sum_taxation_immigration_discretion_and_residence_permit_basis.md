# Swiss Lump-Sum Taxation Residence

# Immigration Discretion and Residence Permit

## Basis Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

A. **Executive** **legal summary.** Swiss "lump-sum taxation" or taxation according to expenditure is a tax regime, not a residence category in Swiss immigration law. On the tax side, the federal framework is centred on article 14 of the Federal Direct Tax Act and the parallel cantonal harmonisation ruule,article 6of the Tax Harmonisation Act. The Federal Tax Administration's Circular No. 44 summarises the core tax conditions: the person must be a foreign national, become subject to unlimited Swiss taxation for the first time or after a long interruption, and must not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland. The regime therefore explains the fiscal profile of the applicant, but it does not itself create a right to a residence permit. 1

On the immigration side, the architecture differs sharply by nationality. For non-EU/EFTA nationals, there is no stand-alone statutory "golden visa" or investment-residence route identifiable in Swiss law. Instead,wealthy non-working applicants are generally routed either through theordinary **retiree** basis in article 28 FNIA / article 25 OASA, if they are at least 55 and can show particular personal ties to Switzerland, or through the **derogation** **/ important public interests** basis in article 30 FNIA and article 32 OASA, where the canton demonstrates an especially important public interest, including in the fiscal sphere. The SEM's 2026 Foreign Nationals Directives state expressly that article 30 is a potestative provision, so the competent authority has a wide margin of appreciation and the route is discretionary rather than entitlement-based. 2

The official SEM terminology does not appear to use a single fixed expression equivalent to "predominant cantonal fiscal interest" as a statutory term. Instead, the guidance speaks of **"important** **public interests"** under article 30 FNIA and, more specifically, **"important cantonal fiscal interests"/** **"considerable** **fiscal interests** **of** **the** **cantons"** under article 32 OASA. The SEM guidance further states that, in such cases, the foreign national must prove that they will transfer their centre of interests to Switzerland and stay there for the majority of the time. It also states that, where the authorisation is based on important cantonal fiscal interests, any professional activity may be carried on only abroad, with an exception for activity arising from the management of the person's own assets. 3

That official formulation matters. It means that the immigration analysis is not exhausted by agreeing a tax rulingJ. The tax authority may be willing to agree an expenditure-based assessment or fiscal convention; the migration authority must still decide whether to issue the permit under immigration law, and where the case falls within federal approval rules or is submitted upward, SEM approval may also be required. SEM's directives explain that cantonal authorities decide first, but that the SEM's approval procedure allows the Confederation to supervise the correct and uniform application of foreign-national law, and that the canton cannot issue a permit in an approval case unless the SEM has approved it. Geneva's official page for third-country applicants "with forfait fiscal" reflects that structure in practice: it requires an AFC tax convention, housing evidence, a criminal-record extract, a motivation letter, and a written undertaking not to exercise gainful activity in Switzerland, to move one's centre of

interests to Switzerland, and to spend the majority of the year there; it also states that the application is submitted to the SEM for approval. 4

For EU/EFTA nationals, the route is fundamentally different. The relevant residence basis is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, especially Annex I article 24 as reflected in SEM's OLCP directives and factsheets. Economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals have a **right** of **residence** if they have sufficient resources and comprehensive health and accident insurance; those stays are not subject to quotas and the ordinary OASA restrictions in articles 23 to 25 do not apply. Geneva's official EU/EFTA page mirrors that position and treats the case as a rights-based application for persons without activity, not as a discretionary fiscal-interest admission. The tax regime may still be relevant if tax-law conditions are met, but the immigration architecture is not the same as for third-country nationals. 5

Finally, residence in Switzerland for these cases remains legally fragile if the facts do not match the declared basis of stay. SEM guidance emphasises centre-of-life transfer and majority presence in Switzerland for fiscal-interest cases. Geneva requires express written commitments on those points. Public-order and revocation rules continue to apply, including false statements, serious criminal issues, breach of permit conditions, and dependency on social assistance. Swiss health insurance is generally compulsory within three months of taking up residence, and, for EU/EFTA non-active cases, adequate insurance is part of the residence right itself. The route is therefore better understood as a tax-linked, canton-driven, closely scrutinised immigration pathway within ordinary Swiss foreign-national law, not as residence acquired by paying a fee or making an investment. 6

## Structural overview of the legal framework

### B. Structural overview of the legal framework.

### Legal basis for expenditure-based taxation and linked permits

The Swiss expenditure-based regime belongs to tax law. The Federal Tax Administration's Circular No. 44 states that, under article 14 FDTA, natural persons may pay tax according to expenditure instead of ordinary income tax if they are not Swiss citizens, are newly subject to unlimited Swiss taxation or return after the required interruption, and do not carry on gainful activity in Switzerland. The circular also makes clear that spouses living together must both meet the conditions. This is the fiscal platform around which many private-client cases are structured. It does not, however, define an immigration category.7

### Non-EU and EFTA route architecture

For third-country nationals, two different non-working architectures must be kept separate. First, article 28 FNIA / article 25 OASA governs **retirees:** the SEM directives summarise the cumulative conditions as age 55+, particular personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad other than management of one's own wealth. Secondly, article 30 FNIA in conjunction with article 32 OASA governs **important** **public** **interests,** including important cantonal fiscal interests. SEM describes those provisions as discretionary and states that the canton must show an especially important interest, including fiscal interest where relevant. In fiscal-interest cases, the applicant must move the centre of interests to Switzerland, stay there most of the time, and may pursue professional activity only abroad, save for private asset management. 2

This means that a lump-sum taxation case for a third-country national is usually not simply a "retirement permit". It may instead be framed as a discretionary admission in the public-interest/fiscal-interest sphere. Geneva's official practice materials reflect precisely that distinction: Geneva has one page for third-country applicants **without activity** as retirees and a separate page for third-country applicants **with** **forfait** **fiscal,** where "major fiscal interest" and SEM approval are expressly mentioned. 8

<!-- 2 -->

### EU and EFTA route architecture

For EU/EFTA nationals, the legal basis is not article 30 FNIA. Under the AFMP/ OLCP framework, non-economically active EU/EFTA nationals enjoy a residence right if theyhave sufficient resources and full health and accident insurance. SEM's OLCP directives say these stays are not subject to maximum numbers and the OASA restrictions in articles 23 to 25 are inapplicable. Geneva's EU/EFTA guidance uses explicitly rights-based language and treats the case as a permit for a person without activity, provided that sufficient means and insurance exist for the applicant and family members. 5

### Authorities, sequencing and approval

The authorities are institutionally separate. The tax authority handles the expenditure-tax convention or ruling. The cantonal migration authority decides the permit application. The SEM's approval role depends on the federal approval rules and on whether the canton refers the matter. SEM'sdirectives explain that the approval procedure exists to ensure the correct and uniform application of foreign-national law and that, where SEM approval is required, the canton cannot issue the permit without that approval. Geneva's forfait-fiscal page again mirrors this sequence by requiring the cantonal tax convention and stating that the application is submitted to SEM for approval. 4

## Residence, admissibility and longer-term trajectory

Residence is not satisfied by paperwork alone. The SEM's fiscal-interestguidance requires proof that the applicant will transfer the centre of interests to Switzerland and stay there for most of the year. Geneva operationalises that through a written undertaking. More generally, SEM's directives explain that purchasing property, creating a company, renting an apartment or similar preparatory acts do not by themselves create a right to authorisation. Public-order and revocation rules also remain relevant. Residence permits for third-country nationals are generally time-limited; absent a legal entitlement, renewal remains discretionary, and revocation grounds may block extension. Long-term settlement permit planning is possible, but it follows the ordinary settlement rules, not a special lump-sum residence track. 9

## Federal Direct Tax Act and Tax Harmonisation Act

**Article** **14** **Federal Direct Tax** **Act.** Official title: Federal Act on Direct Federal Taxation. FTA Circular No. 44states that article 14allows natural persons to be taxed according to expenditure instead of ordinary income tax if they are not Swiss citizens, are becoming subject to unlimited Swiss taxation for the first time or after at least a ten-year interruption, and do not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland. The circular also explains that both spouses in an intact marriage must meet the conditions. This matters because it shows that the tax regime is available only to a narrow non-working class of foreign nationals and therefore often accompanies, but does not replace, the immigration analysis.7

**Article** **6** **Tax** **Harmonisation** **Act.** Official title: Federal Act on the Harmonisation of Direct Taxes of Cantons and Municipalities. The same 2012 reform aligned cantonal law with the federal expenditure-tax regime. For present purposes, the key point is structural: the tax regime exists at federal and cantonal tax level, but its existence does not itself answer whether the migration authority should issue or renew a permit. 25

### Foreign Nationals and Integration Act

**Article** **28** **FNIA.** Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. As summarised in the SEM directives, article 28 governs admission of retirees. The cumulative conditions are age 55 or over, particular personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient financial means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad apart from management of one's own assets. It matters because it is a distinct route from the fiscal-interest / lump-sum residence pathway and should not be conflated with it. 26

**Article 30 paragraph 1 letter b FNIA and paragraph 2.** Official **title:** Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. SEM's directives explain that article 30 permits derogations from ordinary admission requirements in order to take account of serious personal hardship or important public interests, and that article 30 provisions are potestative. Paragraph 2 authorises theFederal Council to set the general conditions and procedure. This provision is the principal federal gateway for third-country lump-sum cases framed around important public interests. It matters because it is discretionary and open-textured. 27

**Article** **96** **FNIA.** Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. SEM's general procedural guidance states that, when exercising discretion, the authority must take account of public interests, the foreign national's personal circumstances, and integration. This matters because even where fiscal contribution is high, the immigration authority must still carry out a proportionality and public-interest assessment. 28

**Article** **99** **FNIA.** Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. SEM's approval-procedure chapter states that the CouncilI determines which cases are subject to SEM approval and that the SEM may refuse approval, impose conditions, or limit duration. This matters because cantonal support for the application does not necessarily end the matter. 29

**Article** **62** **FNIA.** Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. SEM's directives summarise the revocation grounds, including false statements or concealment of essentiaI facts, serious criminal convictions, serious or repeated breaches of public order, breach of permit conditions, and dependence on social assistance. This matters to both first grants and renewals. 21

**Articles** 43 to **45** **FNIA.** Official title: Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. These are the ordinary family-reunification provisions. The SEM's 2025 family-reunification directive states that articles 42 and 43 create rights where conditions are met, whereas articles 44 and 45 are potestative provisions leaving some discretion to the cantons. This matters because the family position in a third-country lump-sum case will usually depend on the principal's permit category and is not automatically rights-based. 30

## Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment

**Article** **25** **OASA.** Official title: Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment. SEM's directives summarise it together with article 28 FNIA for retirees: age 55+, particular personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad apart from management of one's own assets. This provision matters because it is often confused with the separate fiscal-interest route. 26

**Article** **32** **OASA.** Official title: Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment. SEM's directives explain that "important public interests" are an indeterminate legal concept and that article 32 OASA includes important cantonal fiscal interests. They also say that, in fiscal-interest cases, the canton must show a particularly important interest; the applicant must transfer the centre of interests to Switzerland and stay there most of the time; and any professional activity may only be exercised abroad, except activity resulting from management of private wealth. This is the key implementing provision for immigration approvals linked to lump-sum taxation. 3

**Articles** **85 and** **86** **OASA.** Official title: Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment. SEM's approval chapter states that article 85 delegates the determination of approval cases and allows cantons to submit cases voluntarily; article 86 governs the SEM's approval power and the effect that permits cannot be issued without SEM approval where approval is required. These articles matter because they define the federal supervisory layer over cantonal permit decisions. 29

**Articles 9 to 10 OASA and article 5 OASA together with articles 10 and 17 FNIA.** SEM's directives state that a foreign national planning a stay of more than three months without gainful activity must apply before entry, that a visa-national needs a national D visa, that a prior assurance of authorisation is recommended, and that if the person applies only after entry the canton may require them to wait abroad for the decision. These provisions matter to sequencing. 31

## AFMP and OLCP framework

**Article 24 Annex I AFMP, implemented through the OLCP.** Official title**:** Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU and Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons. ## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

### Official federal guidance and administrative materials

E. **Official guidance** **/** **administrative** **materials.** The most important federal administrative source for third-country analysis is the SEM's 2026 Directives and Comments, Foreign Nationals Domain. In this material, SEM consolidates the non-activity routes: retirees under article 28 FNIA / article 25 OASA; important public interests under article 30 FNIA / article 32 OASA; the need to move the centre of interests to Switzerland and stay there most of the time in fiscal-interest cases; the restriction of professional activity to abroad in those cases; the discretion inherent in article 30; general no-entitlement rules; approval procedures; and renewal / revocation principles. For this topic, that document is the single most important official interpretive source after the legislation itself. 33

For EU/EFTA nationals, the corresponding federal source is the SEM's 2026 OLCP Directives together with the SEM factsheet Residing in Switzerland without gainful activity. Those materials explain that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals have residence rights if they possess sufficient means and full health and accident insurance, and that those cases are not quota-controlled and do not depend on the OASA restrictions used in third-country retiree cases. 32

On the tax side, the Federal Tax Administration's Circular No. 44 remains the key federal interpretive source for expenditure-based taxation. For immigration purposes, its main significance is evidential and delimiting: it confirms that the tax regime is reserved to foreign nationals who are non-gainfully active in Switzerland, and it gives official examples of Swiss personal activities that would be problematic, including artistic activities, scientific activity, inventions, sports, and board mandates exercised in Switzerland. That is highly relevant when assessing whether the migration case is really compatible with the declared "non-working" profile. 11

For health insurance, the Federal Office of Public Health states that anyone settling in Switzerland must obtain health insurance within three months of taking up Swiss residence. That is a general Swiss residence compliance rule. In EU/EFTA non-active cases, insurance is also an express immigration condition under the AFMP materials. For third-country lump-sum/fiscal-interest cases, the research did not locate a single federal lump-sum-specific checklist stating health-insurance evidence as a unique admission condition, but the general Swiss insurance obligation clearly applies once residence is taken up. 34

## Practical legal analysis

G. **Practical legal analysis.** The central legal mistake in many summaries of Swiss "residence by lump-sum taxation" is to treat the tax ruling and the residence permit as the same legal act. They are not. The tax authority is deciding whether the person can be taxed according to expenditure; the migration authority is deciding whether a permit should be issued under the FNIA / OASA or, for EU/EFTA nationals, under the AFMP / OLCP framework. The official sources consistently separate these spheres. That separation is why a negotiated tax convention, even if politically important to the canton, cannot by itself guarantee immigration success. Immigration remains subject to distinct statutory requirements, discretionary balancing and, in some cases, SEM approval. 38

For third-country nationals, the process is especially discretionary. SEM's directives describe article 30FNIA as potestative and an indeterminate legal concept. The canton must persuade the system that there is an especially important public interest, here often articulated as a major fiscal interest. That structure is materially different from rights-based migration regimes, because the applicant cannot point to a statutory entitlement that follows automatically from satisfying objective threshold conditions. Even once the canton is supportive, federal approval mechanisms may still matter. Geneva's official practice page illustrates the point well: the tax convention is merely one document in a broader immigration file that also includes centre-of-life commitments, lodging, criminal-record evidence and SEM approval. 19

<!-- 10 -->

This also explains why Swiss law is better described as **tax-linked** **discretionary** **residence** than as a "golden visa". The official materials reviewed do not reveal a dedicated investment-residence statute under which paying a fixed amount or making an investment automatically generates a permit. Instead, the law offers ordinary foreign-national routes, including retirees, AFMP self-sufficient residence, and discretionary admissions in important public interests. SEM even states generally that buying property, renting an apartment, concluding a contract of employment, creating a comnpany, or participating in a company do not on their own create permit rights. A reader who describes the route as "residence by paying Switzerland" would therefore flatten the actual legal structure. 39

The activity restrictions deserve especially careful handling. The tax regime only prohibits gainful activity in **Switzerland** for expenditure-tax purposes. By contrast, the **retiree** immigration route under article 28 / article 25 is stricter, because SEM says the retiree must not pursue gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad, except own-asset management. The **fiscal-interest** immigration route under article 32 OASA is different again: professional activity may be carried on only abroad, with private asset management excepted. Those distinctions matter greatly in practice. A private investor managing a portfolio is one thing; a person who continues to operate a foreign business from a Swiss home office, or who sits on boards and performs the substance of those mandates from Switzerland, is another. The official sources do not spell out every conceivable fact-pattern, but they make crystal clear that Swiss-side gainful activity is incompatible with both the tax profile and the fiscal-interest migration logic. The FTA's own examples specifically flag activities such as those of artists, scientists, inventors, sportspeople and board members when personally exercised in Switzerland. 40

Residence evidence is likewise substantive, not ceremonial. SEM's fiscal-interest guidance requires proof that the person will move the centre of interests to Switzerland and stay there most of the time. Geneva requires a written undertaking to that effect. On the tax side, Ticino's official material warns that formal declarations of residence do not automatically create tax domicile and that the decisive question is where the objectively strongest vital interests lie. Those sources together show why the route is vulnerable if Swvitzerland is treated merely as an address, a tax platform or a Schengen base while the real centre of life remains elsewhere. 41

The distinction from other Swiss routes should also remain sharp in later drafting. A third-country **entrepreneur** **or** **investor** case is usually analysed through economic-activity and labour-market provisions, not through expenditure-tax status. A third-country **retiree** case falls under article 28 FNIA / article 25 OASA and turns on age, ties and means, not on fiscal contribution. An EU/EFTA **self-sufficient** case is fundamentally rights-based under the AFMP. The lump-sum taxation residence file sits at the intersection of tax law and discretionary migration law, but it does not absorb those other categories. 42

Family members require cautious treatment. The SEM retiree guidance says a retiree may also be authorised to settle with spouse and minor children and that ordinary family-reunification rules apply. The SEM's 2025 family-reunification directive further states that article 44 FNIA is potestative, unlike the rights-based provisions in articles 42 and 43. For EU/EFTA non-active persons, Geneva's page and the SEM OLCP materials are more permissive because AFMP family rights attach to the principal's residence right. For third-country fiscal-interest cases, the family position should therefore usually be described as **potential derivative admission under the ordinary family-reunification framework,** not as an automatic right. 43

<!-- 11 -->

Admissibility and compliance continue after grant. SEM's directives say the competent authority may require a criminal-record extract before first issuing a residence permnit for a person intending to settle in Switzerland, and Geneva expressly requires one in forfait-fiscal cases. SEM also summarises the revocation grounds, including false statements, criminality, public-order problems, breach of conditions and social-assistancedependence. Renewal is generally not a right for third-country B-permit holders unless a separate entitlement exists. The practical consequence is that these files should be built not only around tax capacity, but around an evidence package of credibility, lawful conduct, clear residence intentions, and a sustainable non-working profile. 44

## Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

### H. Drafting cautions.

·Do not describe Swiss lump-sum taxation residence as a statutory "golden visa". That is not the legal language of the official materials, and the route is built from tax law plus separate immigration provisions. 45

·Do not imply that a tax ruling or cantonal tax convention automnatically creates a right to residence. Immigration authorisation is legally distinct and, for third-country cases, discretionary. 46

·Do not collapse the **retiree** route and the **fiscal-interest** route. Article 28 / article 25 cases and article 30/ article 32 cases are different. 47

·Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA positions. EU/EFTA non-active residence is rights-based under AFMP conditions; third-country fiscal-interest cases are discretionary. 48

·Do not state that a third-country applicant may freely work from Switzerland so long as the work is "for abroad". The official materials only clearly permit professional activity to be exercised **abroad** in fiscal-interest cases, with private asset management excepted. 15

·Do not ignore centre-of-life and majority-presence requirements. They are explicit in SEM guidance and Geneva practice. 49

·Do not treat property purchase, company participation or similar preparatory acts as creating permit rights. SEM explicitly warns against that inference. 50

·Do not present third-country family members as automatically entitled in the same way as AFMP family members. The family basis is route-sensitive and often discretionary. 51

·Do not present the route as immune from refusal or later non-renewal because the applicant is wealthy. Public-order, false-information, condition-breach and social-assistance rules still apply, and third-country renewals are generally not rights-based. 52

·Do not reduce tax or immigration residence to a single formal registration step or a crude day-count slogan. Official cantonal tax guidance requires an objective centre-of-interests analysis. 53

## Open questions and limitations

The main limitation of this pack is technical rather than legal: the research interface did not reliably return citable Fedlex article pages for every statutory provision, so several "primary legal text" summaries are close paraphrases grounded in official SEMM and FTA materials rather than direct line-by-line extracts from Fedlex. In addition, this pack found strong official practice material for Geneva, helpful general explanatory material for Ticino and Zug, but not a uniform set of publicly accessible canton-by-canton migration practice notes on fiscal-interest admissions. Those gaps do not undermine the core legal architecture, but they do mean that later canton-specific drafting should still check the target canton's current migration and tax practice before operational use. 64
