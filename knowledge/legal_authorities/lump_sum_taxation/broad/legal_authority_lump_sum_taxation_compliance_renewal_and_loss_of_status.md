# Swiss Lump-Sum Taxation Residence Compliance, Renewal and Loss of Status

## Executive legal summary

Swiss “lump-sum taxation” is not a residence status in its own right. It is a tax method - taxation
according to expenditure - available only if continuing tax-law conditions remain met. Separately, any
right to remain in Switzerland depends on the person’s immigration status: for most non-EU/EFTA lump-
sum cases, that means a discretionary cantonal residence permit granted on the immigration side
under the “important public interests / substantial cantonal fiscal interests” framework; for EU/EFTA
nationals, it usually means residence as an economically inactive person under the Agreement on the
Free Movement of Persons, provided sufficient means and insurance continue. The two systems interact
in practice, but they are not the same legal status and they can fail for different reasons.

On the tax side, ongoing compliance is annual and evidence-heavy. The Federal Tax Administration’s
Circular No. 44 states that a person seeking or benefiting from expenditure-based taxation must submit
the dedicated tax return and prove that the conditions are satisfied, and must spontaneously and
immediately notify the competent tax authority of any factual element capable of affecting the right to
or conditions for the regime. The current federal Form 3 for expenditure-based taxation requires full
completion of the personal-status section, expenditure data, control-calculation income items, and -
where treaty relief is claimed - the modified-taxation annex. The annual mechanics therefore involve
not merely paying an agreed amount, but maintaining a defensible annual tax position based on living
expenses, the control calculation, and any treaty-modified income disclosures.

Loss of eligibility for the tax regime can arise from several classic triggers. Circular No. 44 states that
only foreign nationals qualify; the right ceases when the taxpayer acquires Swiss nationality, and
ordinary income taxation then applies for the whole fiscal period in which Swiss nationality is acquired.
The same circular says that a spouse living in a common household who acquires Swiss nationality or
starts gainful activity in Switzerland causes both spouses to lose the right to expenditure-based taxation
for the whole fiscal period. It also states that Swiss gainful activity is incompatible with the regime and
that a person who voluntarily gives up expenditure-based taxation for ordinary taxation cannot, as a
rule, return to the regime later.

On the immigration side, permit renewal is not automatic for non-EU/EFTA lump-sum cases. SEM
guidance states that article 30 FNIA and article 32 OASA are “may” provisions, giving the authorities a
wide margin of discretion. The same SEM guidance requires applicants in the fiscal-interest category to
show that they are moving their centre of life to Switzerland and will stay mostly in Switzerland; any
gainful activity under that route may only be carried out abroad, except for management of one’s own
assets. Once a residence permit has been issued, renewal may be refused if revocation grounds under
article 62 FNIA exist or if a permit condition has not been met. Those grounds include false statements,
serious or repeated public-order breaches, breach of conditions, dependence on social assistance, and
certain citizenship abuses. SEM guidance further explains that public-order breaches can include
repeated failure to comply with public-law obligations, expressly including non-payment of taxes and
debt accumulation.

Absence and centre-of-life risks are particularly important. SEM guidance states that a B permit lapses
on deregistration, expiry, expulsion, or actual abandonment of the stay in Switzerland; for residence-
permit holders, actual abandonment is generally treated as an absence of more than six months
abroad. The same guidance adds that the decisive point is whether the centre of life has moved abroad,
that short visits back to Switzerland do not interrupt the six-month period if the centre of life has
already moved, and that, as a rule, foreigners must stay in Switzerland for the majority of the year if
their residence permit is to continue. This “majority of the year” language is guidance and practice, not
a self-contained lump-sum tax statute, but it is highly relevant in compliance reviews.

EU/EFTA holders are different. SEM’s official factsheet states that economically inactive EU/EFTA
nationals are entitled to a B permit if they have sufficient means and health/accident insurance, and
that the permit is automatically renewed so long as those conditions continue; it may be limited,
revoked or not renewed if the person no longer has adequate financial resources. That route is legally
distinct from the discretionary third-country fiscal-interest route, even if the same person also seeks
expenditure-based taxation on the tax side.

For long-term planning, ordinary naturalisation requires ten years’ residence and a C permit. Because
expenditure-based taxation ceases on acquisition of Swiss nationality, lump-sum taxation is structurally
incompatible with being a Swiss citizen. The route may therefore support residence and, in some cases,
later C-permit planning, but it is not a tax status that survives naturalisation.

## Structural overview of compliance and renewal

Tax compliance

The tax regime is maintained through ordinary annual filing, not by relying indefinitely on an initial
“ruling”. Federal guidance requires the dedicated expenditure-tax return, proof of continuing eligibility,
and immediate disclosure of any fact affecting the regime. The federal form requires annual disclosure
of worldwide lifestyle expenditure, Swiss-source items relevant to the control calculation, and treaty-
relief income where a modified lump-sum claim is made.

Immigration renewal

For non-EU/EFTA residence permits linked to fiscal interest, renewal remains discretionary. SEM
guidance states that article 30 FNIA is a “may” provision; residence may be granted where important
public interests exist, including substantial cantonal fiscal interests, but the authorities retain broad
discretion. Renewal of a B permit can be refused if article 62 FNIA grounds exist or if conditions
attached to the permit are no longer met.

Centre of life and actual residence

Compliance is not purely financial. SEM guidance requires the centre of life to be transferred to
Switzerland and says the person must stay mainly in Switzerland. A B permit lapses on actual
abandonment of Swiss residence, generally if the holder is abroad for more than six months; short visits
back do not help if the real centre of life has moved abroad.

Activity restrictions

Swiss gainful activity is a tax-law disqualifier for expenditure-based taxation. On the immigration side,
SEM guidance for the fiscal-interest route states that any gainful activity may only be carried out

abroad, except for management of one’s own assets. Geneva’s official renewal/newcomer form also
specifically asks about gainful activity abroad, gainful activity in Switzerland, and Swiss board positions,
including whether they are remunerated.

Change-of-circumstance reporting

The federal circular obliges immediate notification of facts affecting the tax regime. Geneva’s official
renewal/newcomer form separately requires the signatory to report without delay any significant
change in situation and authorises tax-to-migration communication for the residence-permit request.
This is a strong practical indicator that accommodation, family composition, activity profile, and
resources are all treated as live compliance issues at canton level.

Long-term change events

Acquiring Swiss nationality ends the tax regime for the whole relevant tax period. Voluntary transition
to ordinary taxation generally blocks a later return to lump-sum taxation. On the immigration side,
reaching ordinary naturalisation requires a C permit and ten years’ residence, while permit loss may
follow from revocation, non-renewal, or lapse through absence.

## Primary legal texts

Federal tax provisions

Article 14 of the Federal Act on Direct Federal Taxation is the centrepiece. Circular No. 44 reproduces
article 14(1) LIFD as granting foreign natural persons the right to be taxed according to expenditure
instead of income tax if they are foreign nationals, become subject to unlimited Swiss tax for the first
time or after at least ten years abroad, and do not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland; article 14(2)
requires both spouses in a common household to satisfy the conditions. For compliance analysis, the
most important continuing points are the foreign-nationality requirement, the no-Swiss-gainful-activity
condition, and the joint-spousal requirement.

Article 14(3) and (5) LIFD, as summarised in Circular No. 44 and the official Form 3, matter because
compliance is recalculated each tax period. The regime is based on annual worldwide living expenses,
subject to statutory minimums and the control calculation, and a modified method applies where treaty
relief is claimed for specific treaty states. That means the continuing tax burden is not frozen by the
initial agreement: it is re-tested against annual facts and declarations.

The Ordinance of 20 February 2013 on expenditure-based taxation in respect of direct federal tax,
SR 642.123, is expressly identified in Circular No. 44 as the implementing ordinance that replaced the
earlier 1993 ordinance. Circular No. 44 also states that, under article 2 of that ordinance, social
deductions are excluded in expenditure-based taxation and income outside article 14(3)(d) LIFD is not
taken into account for rate setting. For maintenance analysis, this matters because the annual return
must be prepared on the correct statutory basis rather than by importing ordinary-tax deductions
wholesale.

The parallel cantonal/communal framework under the Tax Harmonisation Act was amended
together with the 2012 federal reform, as Circular No. 44 notes. In this research session, an article-level
official pull-quote for the current consolidated harmonisation provision was not directly retrieved from

an accessible official text parser; it should be checked directly in Fedlex before publication-grade
automation. What is clear from the federal circular and cantonal official materials is that cantonal and
communal expenditure-based taxation continues in parallel with the federal regime and is subject to its
own cantonal administrative practice.

Federal immigration provisions

Articles 30 and 33 FNIA, read with article 32 OASA, form the immigration architecture most relevant
to non-EU/EFTA lump-sum residence. SEM guidance explains that article 30(1)(b) FNIA and article 32
OASA are framed by “important public interests”, including substantial cantonal fiscal interests, and that
these are “may” provisions, leaving a wide margin of discretion. The same guidance states that in this
category the applicant must move the centre of life to Switzerland, stay there mainly, and may exercise
gainful activity only abroad, except for management of own wealth. For compliance and renewal, these
continuing factual conditions matter as much as the initial admission decision.

Article 33 FNIA and article 58 OASA, as summarised by SEM, govern the ordinary B-permit framework:
the first residence permit is generally issued for up to one year, then may be extended by up to two
years if the concrete circumstances justify it. SEM further states that renewal can be refused if
revocation grounds under article 62 FNIA exist or if a condition has not been fulfilled. This is highly
relevant because most third-country lump-sum permit holders remain in the renewable-B-permit
category for years before any C-permit question arises.

Article 61 FNIA, as summarised by SEM, governs lapse of permits. SEM states that a residence permit
lapses, among other things, by deregistration abroad, expiry, expulsion, or actual abandonment of the
stay in Switzerland. It further states that a B permit holder is treated as having abandoned the stay if
abroad for more than six months, and that the decisive inquiry is whether the centre of life has moved
abroad. For lump-sum residents with significant mobility, this is one of the most important loss-of-
status provisions.

Article 62 FNIA governs revocation of short-stay and residence permits. SEM’s official guidance
summarises the grounds as false statements or concealment of material facts, serious criminality,
serious or repeated public-order breaches, breach of permit conditions, dependence on social
assistance, abuse in relation to Swiss citizenship, and unjustified breach of an integration agreement.
The same SEM guidance explains that public-order breaches can include non-payment of taxes and
debt accumulation.

Article 63 FNIA governs revocation of settlement permits. SEM’s guidance summarises grounds
including false statements, serious criminality, serious public-order breaches, substantial and durable
social-assistance dependence, citizenship abuse, and expulsion. SEM separately explains that a C permit
lapses after six months abroad unless maintenance is requested in time. For long-term planning, this
means that a move from B to C reduces time-limitation risk but does not remove compliance risk.

Article 99 FNIA with articles 85 and 86 OASA governs SEM approval. SEM explains that in designated
cases the canton may issue, renew or extend the permit only if SEM gives its approval, and that SEM
may refuse approval or make it subject to time limits, conditions or requirements. The guidance also
notes that the canton can submit cases to SEM at any time for review of federal-law conditions. The
exact mapping of lump-sum fiscal-interest renewals into consent-required categories depends on the
applicable ZV-EJPD list and canton-specific handling, so article-level checking remains advisable for
automation.

EU/EFTA provisions

The Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the OLCP framework govern EU/EFTA
economically inactive residence. SEM’s official factsheet states that EU/EFTA citizens may reside in

Switzerland without gainful activity if they have sufficient means and full health/accident insurance, that
a five-year B permit is then issued, and that it is automatically renewed while those conditions continue.
The same factsheet says the permit may be limited, revoked or not renewed if financial resources cease
to be sufficient. For compliance analysis, this means the immigration review question for EU/EFTA
clients is continuing sufficiency of means and insurance, not “substantial cantonal fiscal interest”.

Naturalisation planning

Ordinary naturalisation under federal law requires ten years’ residence and a C permit, according to

SEM’s official naturalisation page. When read together with Circular No. 44’s statement that
expenditure-based taxation ceases on acquisition of Swiss nationality, the result is straightforward: a
person may use the route as part of a longer residence plan, but the tax regime itself does not survive
naturalisation.

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal tax document is FTA Circular No. 44, “Besteuerung nach dem Aufwand /
Imposition d’après la dépense”. For present purposes it is the key official text on continuing eligibility,
spouse-related loss events, gainful-activity incompatibility, annual filing, immediate duty to report
relevant changes, treaty-relief complications, and tax-offence consequences of false statements. It is
the single most important federal administrative source for maintaining or losing the tax regime.

The most useful operational tax document is Federal Form 3: tax return for expenditure-based
taxation. It confirms that annual compliance requires a detailed declaration of personal situation,
worldwide lifestyle costs, control-calculation items, treaty-relief disclosures, and a signed certification
that the declaration is correct and complete. It also shows that the federal minimum used for the 2025
tax period was CHF 434,700.

On the immigration side, the principal source is the SEM Guidelines on the Foreign Nationals and
Integration Act. Those guidelines supply the operative federal-administrative position on important
public interests, substantial cantonal fiscal interests, renewal and non-renewal of B permits, lapse
through foreign absence, revocation grounds, public-order analysis, and SEM approval mechanics. They
also make clear where the law is framed as discretion or practice rather than entitlement.

For EU/EFTA cases, the most useful official document is SEM’s factsheet on residing in Switzerland
without gainful activity. It states the continuing requirements of sufficient means and adequate
insurance, the automatic renewal principle while those requirements remain met, and the possibility of
revocation or non-renewal if resources become inadequate.

SEM’s English permit pages and integration pages are also relevant for longer-term planning. They
confirm that cantonal migration authorities are responsible for granting, renewing and revoking
permits, that integration criteria matter in permit decisions under the FNIA framework, and that
ordinary naturalisation requires a C permit plus ten years’ residence.

## Cantonal practice notes

The most useful canton-specific material located in this research is from Geneva. Geneva’s official page
on how to benefit from expenditure-based taxation states that the taxpayer should proceed in parallel
with the cantonal population and migration office and the cantonal tax administration, and that Geneva
uses its own form for both newcomers and renewal agreements. Geneva’s official publication record
then identifies a specific “newcomers and renewals” form. This is not black-letter federal law, but it is a
reliable official indicator that Geneva treats the “agreement” as a living administrative instrument
subject to renewal.

Geneva’s English renewal/newcomer form is especially useful as evidence of compliance expectations. It
asks about family members and dependants, accommodation in Switzerland, gainful activity abroad,
gainful activity in Switzerland, Swiss board positions and whether they are remunerated, lifestyle

expenditure, resources and net worth, and it contains a signed undertaking to report without delay any
significant change in situation. It also authorises communication of useful information to the cantonal
migration office and includes an honour certification that the taxpayer does not carry on gainful activity
in Switzerland and from Switzerland. This is plainly administrative practice rather than a federal statute,
but it is strong evidence of what a renewal review may actually test in Geneva.

Geneva also publishes a current page on annual indexation of expenditure-tax agreements, stating
that legal minimums for cantonal/communal and federal tax are adjusted separately and annually. Its
examples for 2026 show an IFD minimum of CHF 435,000 and Geneva cantonal/communal minimum
figures including the cantonal wealth-tax uplift. This is a canton-specific administrative explanation, not
a substitute for the federal ordinance itself, but it is a reliable official source showing that the financial
floor of an existing agreement is not static.

From Valais, the official cantonal tax page shows a formal coordination point between the tax
administration and the migration service, and the downloadable joint form is expressly designed “for
the tax administration and the population and migration service for the purpose of obtaining a
residence permit”. The form captures worldwide annual expenditure, former tax domicile, proposed
commune of residence, prior Swiss tax residence in the last ten years, and whether gainful activity is
planned in Switzerland. This is primarily an admission-phase document, but it illustrates how some
cantons integrate tax and migration fact-finding.

Reliable official renewal/compliance materials were not found in this session for every canton retaining
the regime. Later automation should therefore avoid treating Geneva practice, or the Valais joint form,
as if they were federal law or universally identical canton practice.

## Practical legal analysis

The first practical point is that maintaining the tax regime and maintaining immigration status are
connected but distinct exercises. The tax side is driven by annual declaration, proof and re-testing
under the expenditure/control-calculation framework. The immigration side is driven by permit validity,
residence substance, absence rules, conditions tied to the admission ground, and broader public-order
or integration considerations. A person can therefore satisfy one side and still be exposed on the other.
For example, a taxpayer may still be able to pay a large annual amount, but if the factual centre of life
moves abroad or the person breaches permit conditions, the residence permit may lapse or not be
renewed. Conversely, a person may still hold a permit, but if Swiss gainful activity has been taken up or
Swiss nationality acquired, the expenditure-tax regime ends.

Secondly, the “agreement” or “ruling” should not be mistaken for a final fixed-status instrument.
Federal law operates through annual taxation, and the federal return and circular both show that each
tax period requires complete information and proof. Geneva’s official practice reinforces this by using a
newcomer/renewal form, by requiring the reporting of significant changes without delay, and by
publishing annual indexation of minimums. In other words, the agreement is an administrative
framework around an annually assessed tax method, not a one-off purchase of status.

Thirdly, absence and centre-of-life evidence are legally central. SEM guidance does not reduce the
question to a simple “183-day rule”. Instead, it ties lapse and continuity to actual abandonment of
residence, transfer of the centre of life, and - as a rule - majority presence in Switzerland during the
year. For internationally mobile wealthy clients, that means diaries, travel records, accommodation
evidence, family-location evidence, schooling evidence, and other centre-of-life indicators remain
important, notwithstanding a large tax payment. This is especially true because short visits back to
Switzerland do not preserve a permit once the real centre of life has moved abroad.

Fourthly, activity boundaries need constant review. Tax law excludes Swiss gainful activity. SEM
guidance for the fiscal-interest route says any gainful activity can only be carried out abroad, except
own-asset management. Geneva’s renewal form goes further by asking directly about Swiss gainful
activity, foreign gainful activity, Swiss board positions, remuneration and participation rights. The
practical lesson is that private wealth management is not the same as carrying on Swiss gainful activity,
and clients with Swiss board seats, family-office structures, or active Swiss operational roles need case-
by-case analysis before renewal, not after.

Fifthly, tax compliance failures can migrate into immigration risk. Circular No. 44 says false facts
may lead to tax-offence proceedings. SEM guidance says false statements in the permit process are a
revocation ground and that public-order breaches can include non-payment of taxes and debt
accumulation. Accordingly, tax arrears, incomplete disclosures, or inconsistent statements between tax
and migration files are not merely accounting problems; they can become residence-status problems.

Sixthly, EU/EFTA cases should not be flattened into the third-country fiscal-interest route. EU/EFTA
economically inactive permit holders have a treaty-based residence route with sufficient-means and
insurance tests and automatic renewal while conditions continue. Third-country fiscal-interest cases, by
contrast, remain discretionary and tied to a more expressly monitored residence-substance and public-
interest framework. Both can coexist with expenditure-based taxation on the tax side, but the
immigration compliance logic is different.

Finally, long-term planning must account for the end-state of the tax regime. If the client’s aim is
eventual Swiss citizenship, the relevant federal rule is ordinary naturalisation after ten years’ residence
with a C permit. But expenditure-based taxation ceases upon acquisition of Swiss nationality. The route
may therefore support residence and long-horizon migration planning, yet it does not produce a
permanent tax status that survives becoming Swiss. There is no official legal basis in the materials
reviewed for calling this a Swiss “golden visa”; rather, that label is best understood as a non-legal
marketing shorthand, and using it in internal legal drafting would be misleading. The latter point is an
inference from the official structure, not a quoted statutory phrase.

## Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe lump-sum taxation as a residence status. It is a tax method; the residence
permit is separate.
Do not suggest that an initial tax agreement fixes the tax indefinitely. Federal filing and factual
proof continue annually, and cantonal agreement minimums may be indexed.
Do not state that renewal is automatic for non-EU/EFTA fiscal-interest cases. The SEM guidance
frames the route as discretionary.
Do not reduce residence compliance to a single 183-day formula. SEM guidance focuses on
actual abandonment, centre of life, and - as a rule - majority presence in Switzerland.
Do not omit the duty to report material factual changes to the tax authority. Federal guidance
makes this explicit, and Geneva practice reinforces it.
Do not treat Swiss board roles, remunerated mandates, or family-office arrangements as
automatically harmless. Official Geneva renewal materials specifically ask about them.
Do not assume that tax arrears are only a tax issue. SEM guidance links non-payment of taxes to
public-order risk.
Do not state that lump-sum taxation survives Swiss citizenship. Circular No. 44 says the right
ceases when Swiss nationality is acquired.
Do not flatten EU/EFTA and third-country cases into one renewal analysis. The permit logic is
different even where the tax regime is the same.
Do not present Geneva or Valais administrative forms as federal law. They are useful practice
evidence only.

## Secondary material

No non-official secondary material has been relied on for substantive propositions in this pack. Where

## Open questions and limitations

This pack is high-confidence on the core maintenance, renewal and loss-of-status architecture, but a few
article-level items should still be checked directly in Fedlex before turning the content into automated
drafting rules. In particular, the current consolidated wording of the cantonal-harmonisation
counterpart to article 14 LIFD, the precise article-level AFMP/Annex I cross-reference most useful for
economically inactive residence, and the exact ZV-EJPD mapping for SEM approval in specific lump-sum
renewal cases were not directly retrievable through the official web parser used here. In addition,
canton-specific renewal/compliance materials were reliably found mainly for Geneva and, more

indirectly, Valais; other cantons should not be assumed to follow identical documentation or review
practices without separate official checking.
