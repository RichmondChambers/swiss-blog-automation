# Swiss lump-sum taxation tax eligibility and conditions authority pack

## Executive legal summary

A. Executive legal summary. Swiss lump-sum taxation, more precisely taxation according to
expenditure, is a tax-law regime rather than an immigration status. At federal level, the core personal
eligibility rule is found in Article 14 of the Federal Act on Direct Federal Taxation, as summarised by the
Swiss Federal Tax Administration in Circular No. 44: a natural person may be taxed according to
expenditure instead of ordinary income tax if the person has no Swiss citizenship, becomes subject to
unlimited Swiss tax liability for the first time or after an interruption of at least ten years, and exercises
no gainful activity in Switzerland. For spouses living in a legally and factually unseparated marriage,
both spouses must satisfy those conditions. The same Circular states expressly that only foreign
nationals may apply, that Swiss dual nationals are excluded, and that acquisition of Swiss citizenship
ends the regime for the whole tax period in which naturalisation occurs.

The route therefore presupposes Swiss tax residence, not merely exposure to Swiss-source taxation.
ESTV publications on subjective tax liability and on income tax for natural persons explain that unlimited
tax liability arises when a person has Swiss tax domicile or tax stay. As a rule, unlimited tax liability
begins on the day a person takes up residence in Switzerland or starts a tax-relevant stay there. For
natural persons, tax stay exists after at least 30 days in Switzerland with gainful activity, or after at least
90 days in Switzerland without gainful activity. Persons who remain only limited taxpayers because of
economic links to Switzerland do not satisfy the route’s core “first-time or returning unlimited tax
liability” gateway.

The “no gainful activity in Switzerland” condition is wider than a ban on Swiss-sourced salary alone.
Circular No. 44 says that disqualifying gainful activity exists where a person pursues any main or
secondary occupation in Switzerland and thereby earns income either in Switzerland or abroad. The
Circular gives artists, scientists, inventors, sportspeople and directors personally active in Switzerland as
examples. It also addresses several important cessation and re-entry issues. If one spouse in an intact
marriage takes up gainful activity in Switzerland, or if one spouse acquires Swiss citizenship, both
spouses lose the regime for the whole tax period. The ESTV further states that a person previously
taxed according to expenditure who later returns to Switzerland may, notwithstanding the ten-year
rule, again be taxed according to expenditure; by contrast, a person who voluntarily abandons the
regime and becomes ordinarily taxed can generally not return to it. Those latter two points are official
administrative guidance, but they are not stated in the same explicit form in the current statutory
wording and should therefore be treated with some caution in future drafting.

At tax-law level, the eligibility criteria do not distinguish between EU/EFTA nationals and other foreign
nationals. The nationality distinction appears on the immigration side. SEM materials state that EU/EFTA
nationals without economic activity have a treaty-based right of residence if they have sufficient
financial resources and comprehensive health and accident insurance; the corresponding EU/EFTA B
permit is then issued and renewed so long as those conditions remain fulfilled. For non-EU/EFTA
nationals, by contrast, SEM’s AIG directives treat permits based on important public interests, including
significant cantonal fiscal interests, as discretionary. The directives say the applicant must show that the
centre of life will move to Switzerland and that the person will stay in Switzerland for the majority of the

time; where admission is based on significant cantonal fiscal interests, any gainful activity may be
exercised only abroad, while management of one’s own assets remains permissible. Tax eligibility and
immigration admissibility therefore overlap in practice but remain legally distinct.

Cantonal materials matter chiefly for evidence, timing and interpretation. Geneva’s current public
guidance repeats the foreign-national and no-gainful-activity logic, says the request should be made on
arrival or before the first assessment becomes final, and states that beneficiaries may not work in or
from Switzerland; it also says private wealth management is admissible within usual jurisprudential
limits, while a remunerated director role is not accepted by the canton. Valais publishes a combined tax-
and-migration form asking for nationalities, prior Swiss unlimited tax liability during the last ten years,
intended Swiss gainful activity, family composition and worldwide living expenses; that form is expressly
addressed to both the cantonal tax administration and the population and migration service. These are
useful and reliable canton-specific materials, but they are practice points, not substitutes for the federal
statutory conditions.

## Structural overview and table of legal propositions

B. Structural overview of tax eligibility conditions. The core architecture is comparatively clear. First,
Swiss expenditure-based taxation is available only to qualifying foreign natural persons; Swiss
citizenship is a bright-line exclusion, including where the person also holds another nationality.
Secondly, the person must become subject to unlimited Swiss tax liability for the first time, or again
after a ten-year interruption, which makes tax domicile or tax stay foundational rather than incidental.
Thirdly, the person must not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland, and the ESTV interprets that
condition broadly enough to catch remunerated work physically carried on in Switzerland even where
payment may arise abroad. Fourthly, spouses in a legally and factually unseparated marriage are
treated as a unit for this purpose: both must qualify, and a failure by one spouse destroys the regime
for both. Fifthly, the federal conditions are harmonised across cantons, but cantonal administrations
control evidence, rulings, forms and local practice. Finally, tax-law eligibility is separate from
immigration-law admissibility: EU/EFTA nationals may have a treaty-based residence position if
financially self-sufficient, whereas non-EU/EFTA nationals remain dependent on separate and
discretionary migration-law pathways.

## Primary legal texts

D. Primary legal texts. The close paraphrases below follow the official ESTV and SEM materials cited,
together with current official cantonal statutory texts where those are easier to retrieve in machine-
readable form.

Article 14 of the Federal Act on Direct Federal Taxation. Official title: Bundesgesetz vom 14. Dezember
1990 über die direkte Bundessteuer (DBG; Federal Act on Direct Federal Taxation). Close paraphrase: a
natural person may pay tax according to expenditure instead of ordinary income tax if the person has
no Swiss citizenship, becomes subject to unlimited Swiss tax liability for the first time or after an
interruption of at least ten years, and exercises no gainful activity in Switzerland; spouses living
together in legal and factual union must both satisfy those conditions. This is the federal core of
personal eligibility.

Article 6 of the Federal Act on the Harmonisation of Direct Taxes of the Cantons and Communes.
Official title: Bundesgesetz vom 14. Dezember 1990 über die Harmonisierung der direkten Steuern der
Kantone und Gemeinden (StHG/LHID). Close paraphrase: the cantonal and communal expenditure-based

regime is the harmonised cantonal counterpart to Article 14 DBG. Current cantonal statutes such as
Geneva’s LIPP and Ticino’s Legge tributaria reproduce the same principal conditions: no Swiss
citizenship, first-time unlimited liability or return after a ten-year interruption, no gainful activity in
Switzerland, and a spouse rule for couples living together. This matters because it shows that cantons
cannot freely redesign the core personal conditions, even though they control local procedure and
practice.

Articles 3 and 8 DBG, together with Article 3 StHG. Close paraphrase: unlimited Swiss tax liability
arises through personal attachment, namely Swiss tax domicile or Swiss tax stay; tax stay exists after at
least 30 days with gainful activity or 90 days without gainful activity; unlimited tax liability generally
begins on the day Swiss residence or tax-relevant stay begins. These provisions matter because
expenditure taxation is available only once personal Swiss tax liability exists. A person who is taxable
only because of Swiss-source economic attachments is in a different category.

Civil Code domicile concept as incorporated into tax law. The ESTV’s income-tax publication
expressly says that natural persons who live or stay in Switzerland “within the meaning of the Swiss Civil
Code” and the tax statutes are, in principle, taxed on their worldwide income. For present purposes, the
key point is that Swiss tax domicile draws on the underlying civil-law concept of domicile, and that the

tax-residence inquiry is therefore substantive and fact-sensitive rather than a mere registration
formality.

Article 30 paragraph 1 letter b AIG and Article 32 VZAE. Official titles: Bundesgesetz über die
Ausländerinnen und Ausländer und über die Integration (AIG/FNIA) and Verordnung über Zulassung,
Aufenthalt und Erwerbstätigkeit (VZAE/OASA). Close paraphrase from SEM’s directives: derogations from
ordinary admission requirements may be granted for important public interests, including significant
cantonal fiscal interests, but the authority has a broad discretion; applicants must show transfer of the
centre of life to Switzerland and majority stay in Switzerland; and in admissions based on significant
cantonal fiscal interests, any gainful activity may only be exercised abroad, except for management of
one’s own assets. These provisions matter here only because tax eligibility does not itself answer the
immigration question.

Annex I Article 24 of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, together with the
implementing free-movement ordinance. Close paraphrase from SEM’s factsheet and permit
guidance: EU/EFTA nationals without economic activity have a right of residence if they have sufficient
resources not to become a burden on Swiss social assistance and have comprehensive health and
accident insurance; the EU/EFTA B permit is then issued and renewed if those conditions continue to be
met. This matters because EU/EFTA residence architecture differs fundamentally from the discretionary
non-EU/EFTA route, even though tax-law eligibility may be the same.

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

E. Official guidance / administrative materials. The single most important federal administrative
source for tax eligibility is ESTV Circular No. 44 of 24 July 2018. It sets out the subjective conditions for
expenditure-based taxation, explains the spouse rule, excludes Swiss dual nationals, treats Swiss gainful
activity broadly, deals with the start of the right, and records the ESTV’s positions on re-entry after a
previous expenditure-tax period and on the practical impossibility of returning once the regime has
been voluntarily abandoned in favour of ordinary taxation. For any draft dealing with personal
eligibility, this Circular should be the anchoring federal interpretive source.

The ESTV’s broader publications on subjective tax liability and on income tax for natural persons are
important supporting sources because Article 14 DBG presupposes unlimited tax liability. Those
publications explain when unlimited liability begins, how domicile and stay work, and how limited
liability by economic attachment differs from full Swiss tax residence. They are not lump-sum-tax notes,
but they are legally relevant to determining whether Article 14’s gateway condition is even engaged.

At federal migration level, the SEM’s AIG directives are the key source for distinguishing tax eligibility
from immigration admissibility in non-EU/EFTA cases. The directives emphasise that permits based on
important public interests are discretionary, describe significant cantonal fiscal interests as one such
ground, require proof that the centre of life will move to Switzerland and that the person will be present
there most of the time, and state that gainful activity may be exercised only abroad in fiscal-interest
cases, except for management of one’s own assets. The directives therefore supply the immigration-side
qualification that tax-law materials do not.

For EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM factsheet on residence in Switzerland without gainful activity and the
SEM B EU/EFTA permit page are the most relevant official materials. They summarise the free-
movement position for financially self-sufficient non-active EU/EFTA nationals, including sufficient
resources, health and accident insurance, and renewal of permit status while the conditions remain

satisfied. Those materials are essential whenever content needs to explain why EU/EFTA applicants
should not be folded into the third-country fiscal-interest framework.

At the federal background level, the Federal Department of Finance’s page on lump-sum taxation
provides a concise official overview of the regime, confirms that it exists in federal and cantonal law, and
notes the post-2011 reform tightening of the rules. It is useful as a high-level official orientation source,
but it is not a substitute for Circular No. 44 on eligibility questions.

The ESTV also publishes a dedicated direct-federal-tax return for taxpayers taxed according to
expenditure, together with a specific guide. Those materials are procedural rather than interpretive, but
they confirm that the regime is administered through dedicated tax-return machinery and not through
a once-for-all tax status granted independently of annual compliance.

F. Cantonal practice notes. Reliable canton-level material was found for Geneva, Valais and Ticino.

In Geneva, the cantonal tax administration’s public guidance says that expenditure-based taxation is
intended for foreign nationals resident in Switzerland without gainful activity there, that persons with
multiple nationalities including Swiss nationality cannot benefit, and that on departure and
reinstallation the absence must have lasted at least ten years. It also says that beneficiaries may not
exercise gainful activity “in Switzerland or from Switzerland,” that private wealth management is
permitted within the usual limits fixed by case law, and that a remunerated director function is not
accepted by the canton. A separate Geneva page states that the request should be made in parallel with
population-office steps on installation in Switzerland and, at the latest, before the first tax assessment
becomes final; it also describes the local documents demanded. These are reliable canton-specific
practice points and procedural expectations, but they should not be promoted to federal law. They also
sit somewhat uneasily beside the ESTV Circular’s returnee nuance for former lump-sum taxpayers,
which Geneva’s public page does not mention.

In Valais, the cantonal tax administration’s expenditure-tax page links directly to a one-page form
addressed jointly to the cantonal tax administration and the population and migration service for the
purpose of obtaining a residence permit. The form asks about nationality, family status, minor children,
planned municipality of residence in Valais, whether the applicant has been subject to unlimited Swiss
taxation in the previous ten years, whether the applicant plans to exercise gainful activity in Switzerland,
and the applicant’s worldwide annual living expenses. That material is especially useful because it
shows that, at least in Valais, tax-eligibility screening and migration processing are procedurally
intertwined from the outset. The point is procedural and evidential, not a free-standing source of
substantive federal law.

In Ticino, the currently published cantonal Legge tributaria reproduces the harmonised core content by
providing that natural persons may be taxed according to expenditure if they do not have Swiss
citizenship, are for the first time or after an interruption of at least ten years subject to unlimited
taxation, and do not exercise gainful activity in the canton; the same source also sets out the cantonal
tax-domicile and tax-stay rules. This is mainly a legal mirror of the federal harmonised structure rather
than a discretionary practice note.

## Practical legal analysis

G. Practical legal analysis. For future drafting, the most important discipline is to keep three questions
separate. The first is whether the person can obtain Swiss unlimited tax liability through domicile or
stay. The second is whether, once in that position, the person satisfies the tax-law conditions for

expenditure-based taxation. The third is whether the person can lawfully reside in Switzerland under
immigration law. These questions overlap in practice, but they do not collapse into one another. A
person can satisfy the tax criteria in principle yet still fail on immigration discretion, especially if the
person is a non-EU/EFTA national relying on important public interest or cantonal fiscal-interest
reasoning. Conversely, an EU/EFTA national may have a residence right as a financially self-sufficient
person without that fact alone deciding whether the tax-law conditions for expenditure taxation are
met.

The tax-law core itself is mostly harmonised and conservative. The federal conditions are narrow: no
Swiss citizenship, first-time or ten-year-return unlimited liability, and no Swiss gainful activity. Cantonal
statutes broadly mirror those requirements rather than reinvent them. That means that the real room
for canton-by-canton divergence is usually not in the existence of the headline conditions, but in the
depth of disclosure demanded, the local interpretation of grey areas, and the way tax and migration
authorities coordinate at the application stage. Geneva’s public guidance on private wealth
management and paid directorships, and Valais’s combined tax-migration form, are good examples of
that practical layer.

The no-gainful-activity condition deserves unusually careful handling in later content generation.
Circular No. 44 shows that the issue is not confined to formal Swiss employment contracts. Work
physically carried out in Switzerland for remuneration can disqualify even if the income is booked or
paid abroad. The Circular’s examples, and Geneva’s still stricter public wording about activity “from
Switzerland,” show that this is fact-sensitive territory. Private wealth management is not automatically
treated the same way as gainful activity, but the scope of what counts as mere private wealth
management is ultimately an interpretive question, and Geneva’s page correctly frames it by reference
to jurisprudential limits. Drafts should therefore avoid abstract assurances that a client may “continue
foreign business as normal from Switzerland” without separate analysis.

The spouse rule is another point on which legal content often drifts into inaccuracy. Under the federal
rule, spouses living together in legal and factual union must both qualify. The disqualification of one
spouse by Swiss citizenship or Swiss gainful activity contaminates the position of the other spouse for
the whole tax period. This is not merely a paperwork issue. It means that household-level nationality
planning, work planning and timing of any naturalisation matter directly to continuation of the regime.
Future drafting should treat this as a structural feature, not a marginal exception.

A further drafting trap concerns returnees. The statute and many public cantonal summaries present
the route as requiring first-time unlimited liability or a return after at least ten years abroad. Yet the
ESTV Circular adds a specific administrative position for a person who was formerly taxed according to
expenditure and then returns to Switzerland: such a person may, according to the Circular, re-enter the
regime regardless of the ten-year period. Geneva’s public page does not mention that nuance and
instead states the ten-year absence rule in simpler form. The safe approach is therefore to describe the
ten-year rule as the statutory baseline and the former-beneficiary returnee point as an ESTV
administrative interpretation that should be checked cantonally before any filing or public-facing
assertion.

Finally, the immigration contrast remains crucial. SEM’s materials make plain that EU/EFTA financially
self-sufficient residence is a treaty-based framework, while non-EU/EFTA admissions linked to fiscal
interest remain heavily discretionary, reputation-sensitive and presence-sensitive. The SEM directives
expressly require transfer of the centre of life to Switzerland and majority presence there, and they
emphasise case-by-case proportionality. That means later content should not imply that satisfying tax
conditions yields a residence entitlement, or that the route is a Swiss “golden visa.” On the present

sources, it is better understood as a possible alignment of a tightly defined tax regime with separately
tested residence-permit grounds.

## Drafting cautions and source list

H. Drafting cautions.

Do not say that Swiss citizens can qualify, and do not forget that Swiss dual nationals are also
excluded.
Do not reduce the gateway condition to “having a Swiss address”; the regime presupposes
unlimited Swiss tax liability through domicile or qualifying stay.
Do not describe the ten-year rule as the whole story without qualification; the ESTV recognises a
narrower returnee exception for former expenditure-tax taxpayers, but canton-level public

•

•

•

materials may omit it.
Do not treat the no-gainful-activity rule as limited to Swiss-sourced employment income; the
ESTV treats remunerated work personally carried out in Switzerland as problematic even if
income is earned abroad.
Do not ignore the spouse rule; both spouses in an intact marriage must qualify, and failure by
one spouse can terminate the regime for both for the whole tax period.
Do not collapse tax eligibility into immigration admissibility. EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA nationals
are not split in the same way on the tax side, but they are split sharply on the migration side.
Do not present Geneva’s “in or from Switzerland,” private-wealth-management and director-
function positions as nationwide federal law; they are reliable Geneva practice points.
Do not suggest that a tax convention or ruling automatically secures residence. Cantonal
materials such as the Valais joint form show coordination with migration authorities, not legal
merger of the two regimes.
Do not assume that once a person chooses ordinary taxation they can freely return later to
expenditure taxation; ESTV guidance says that is generally not possible.
