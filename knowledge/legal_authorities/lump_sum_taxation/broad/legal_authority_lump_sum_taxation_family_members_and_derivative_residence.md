# Swiss Lump-Sum Taxation Family Members and Derivative Residence

## Executive legal summary

In Swiss lump-sum taxation cases, the family analysis must be split between tax law and immigration
law. The tax regime is the federal and cantonal system of taxation according to expenditure under
Article 14 of the Federal Direct Tax Act and the harmonised cantonal rules; the immigration position is
governed separately by the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, its ordinances, and, for EU/EFTA
nationals, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. A favourable tax ruling therefore does not
itself create family residence rights, and a derivative residence permit does not itself preserve the tax
regime if the tax conditions cease to be met.

For the principal taxpayer’s spouse, Swiss federal tax guidance is unusually strict: where spouses live
together and are jointly assessed, both spouses must satisfy the expenditure-tax conditions. ESTV
Circular No. 44 states that if one spouse acquires Swiss nationality or takes up gainful activity in
Switzerland, the right to lump-sum taxation is lost for both spouses for the whole tax period. By
contrast, the same federal tax materials found in this research do not state an equivalent automatic rule
for children; instead, the tax base is calculated by reference to the living expenses of the taxpayer and
the persons maintained by that taxpayer.

On the immigration side for non-EU/EFTA families, the ordinary derivative route is family
reunification under the FNIA. Where the principal holds a B permit, Article 44 FNIA provides that the
foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18 may be granted residence permits if they live with the
principal, suitable housing is available, they are not dependent on social assistance, the adult spouse
satisfies the language requirement or registers for language tuition, and the sponsor is not receiving
supplementary benefits or rendered eligible for them by the reunification. SEM’s 2026 directives
emphasise that Article 44 is a discretionary (“may”) provision, not an entitlement, and that cantons
retain room to apply stricter criteria within the limits of proportionality and the prohibition of
arbitrariness.

For third-country spouses and children who are admitted, Article 46 FNIA states that the spouse and
children of a Swiss national or a person with a settlement or residence permit may work on a salaried or
self-employed basis anywhere in Switzerland. That immigration work right must, however, be read
alongside the tax rule above: in a lump-sum case, a spouse’s Swiss work may be immigration-lawful yet
still terminate the couple’s tax treatment according to expenditure. That is one of the most important
route-specific family coordination points.

For children, the third-country family-reunification category is limited to unmarried children under 18.
SEM’s 2026 directives further state that, for age-limit purposes, the decisive date is the date of filing,
and the claim does not lapse merely because the child turns 18 during the procedure. Late child
reunification after the statutory deadline is possible only for important family reasons and is to be
applied restrictively, with the child’s welfare as the central test; children over 14 may be heard in the
process where necessary.

For EU/EFTA principals, the route architecture is materially different. Economically inactive EU/EFTA
citizens may reside in Switzerland if they have sufficient means and full health insurance; their family
members benefit from the AFMP regime rather than the third-country family-reunification rules. Under
Annex I Article 3 AFMP, family members include the spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent
descendants, dependent ascendants, and, for students, the spouse and dependent children; the family
member’s residence title has the same validity as the principal’s, and the spouse and children under 21
or dependent children have access to economic activity regardless of nationality. Economically inactive
EU/EFTA residents receive a five-year B permit if the conditions are met, renewable while those
conditions continue.

Accommodation, health insurance, and children’s actual schooling matter practically because they
evidence a real family base in Switzerland rather than a nominal address. For newcomers resident in
Switzerland, each family member must in principle obtain Swiss health insurance within three months
of taking residence, and compulsory schooling lasts 11 years under the cantonal school system. Those
facts are not unique to lump-sum cases, but they are important compliance markers in high-net-worth
residence cases where the authorities may scrutinise whether the family truly resides in Switzerland.

A final caution is needed on post-separation rights. The accessible English statutory text located in this
research reflects Article 50 FNIA as preserving post-breakdown rights for spouses and children whose
residence derived under Articles 42 and 43. SEM’s 2026 directives, however, describe Article 50 as
continuing to apply after the dissolution of the marriage or family household also in the case of persons
with a residence permit. Because lump-sum cases typically involve a third-country principal with a B
permit, this discrepancy is material and should be checked against the current German, French or
Italian consolidated FNIA text before downstream drafting uses categorical wording.

## Structural overview of family-member issues

Legal architecture

Family treatment in Swiss lump-sum residence cases is built on two separate legal layers. The first is tax
law, which determines whether the principal and, where relevant, the spouse remain eligible for
taxation according to expenditure. The second is immigration law, which determines whether family
members may enter, reside, renew, work, and eventually qualify for settlement in their own right or as
derivatives. These layers interact in practice, but they are not the same legal question.

Third-country families

For non-EU/EFTA lump-sum cases, the spouse and unmarried children under 18 usually sit under the
FNIA family-reunification framework. If the principal has a B permit, Article 44 FNIA is the core provision;
it is discretionary and contains conditions on cohabitation, housing, financial self-sufficiency,
supplementary benefits, and language for the adult spouse. Children under 18 are exempt from the
language requirement. SEM’s 2026 guidance adds that cantons may apply stricter requirements in
Article 44 cases.

EU and EFTA families

For EU/EFTA economically inactive residents, the AFMP route is structurally different and usually more
favourable. The principal’s right depends on sufficient means and comprehensive health insurance,
while Annex I Article 3 defines family members more broadly than the third-country rules and expressly

grants them co-residence rights, same-validity residence documents, and labour-market access for the
spouse and relevant children.

Tax-side family conditions

The clearest tax-side family rule is the treatment of spouses. ESTV guidance states that spouses living
together must both satisfy the expenditure-tax conditions, and if one spouse acquires Swiss nationality
or engages in gainful activity in Switzerland, the regime is lost for both. The tax base, however, is
computed by reference to the living expenses of the taxpayer and the persons maintained by that
taxpayer, which is why children and dependants still matter even where they are not themselves
“qualifying taxpayers”.

Compliance points

The family case is maintained not only by legal status but also by factual compliance. Real cohabitation,
adequate accommodation, no social assistance, health insurance, school attendance where relevant,
and the absence of sham or nominal family arrangements are all important. For late child reunification,
welfare of the child becomes central; for separated residences, the family relationship must genuinely
continue for the cohabitation exception to apply.

## Primary legal texts

Federal Direct Tax Act Article 14

Article 14 DBG, as reproduced in the ESTV’s 2025 official tax brochure, gives natural persons the right to
be taxed according to expenditure instead of income tax if they are not Swiss citizens, become
unlimited taxpayers in Switzerland for the first time or after at least ten years abroad, and do not
exercise gainful activity in Switzerland. Paragraph 2 states that spouses living together must both
satisfy the conditions. Paragraph 3 measures the tax by reference to the living expenses of the taxpayer
and the persons maintained by that taxpayer, subject to statutory minimums and the control
calculation. This matters to family analysis because it makes the spouse a direct eligibility issue and
makes children/dependants part of the tax base even though they are not the principal applicants.

Foreign Nationals and Integration Act family-reunification provisions

Article 44 FNIA provides that the foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18 of a person with a
residence permit may be granted a residence permit if they live with the sponsor, suitable housing

exists, they do not depend on social assistance, the adult spouse can communicate in the local national
language, and the sponsor is not receiving or rendered eligible for supplementary benefits. Article 46
states that the spouse and children of a Swiss national or a person with a settlement or residence
permit may work anywhere in Switzerland on a salaried or self-employed basis. Article 47 sets the filing
deadlines: five years in general, and for children over 12 a 12-month period, with later reunification
allowed only for important family reasons. These are the central third-country derivative provisions in
lump-sum cases.

Cohabitation, language waiver, post-breakdown rights and expiry

Article 49 FNIA relaxes the cohabitation requirement only where there are good reasons for separate
residences and the family household continues to exist. Article 49a permits waiver of the language-
proof requirement for good cause such as disability or illness. Article 51 governs expiry of family-
reunification rights in cases of abuse and revocation grounds. Article 52 extends the spouse rules to
registered partnerships. Article 50 is the key post-breakdown provision, but a drafting caution is
required: the accessible English statutory wording located in this research reflects continuation rights
for families under Articles 42 and 43, while SEM’s 2026 directives describe the provision as also applying
after breakdown to residence-permit-holder families. That tension must be resolved on the current
consolidated text before relying on Article 50 in B-permit lump-sum family content.

Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons

Article 6 AFMP grants residence rights to persons not engaged in gainful activity according to Annex I.
Article 7 recognises, among other things, family residence rights and the right of family members to
economic activity. Annex I Article 3 defines family members, gives them the right to reside with the
principal, limits documentary demands, gives the derivative residence title the same validity as the
principal’s, grants the spouse and children under 21 or dependent children access to economic activity
regardless of nationality, and protects children’s access to education. Annex I Article 24 requires
economically inactive persons to show sufficient financial means for themselves and their family
members and comprehensive sickness insurance. These treaty rules are the core primary texts for EU/
EFTA family members in lump-sum residence situations.

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

## Official guidance and administrative materials

ESTV Circular No. 44 is the most important federal tax guidance for family treatment in expenditure-
based taxation. It confirms that both spouses must meet the conditions; that one spouse’s Swiss work
or acquisition of Swiss nationality ends the regime for both; and that the worldwide living expenses of
the taxpayer and maintained persons are included in the expenditure concept. It is therefore essential
for any family drafting that touches spouse activity, school costs, support obligations, or dependency.

SEM’s 2026 AIG Directives are the most important immigration guidance for third-country derivative
permits. They state expressly that Article 44 and Article 45 are discretionary rather than entitlement-
based; explain suitable housing, minimum financial means, supplementary-benefit risk, and language
requirements; confirm that children under 18 are exempt from the language condition; state that future

income may exceptionally be taken into account where very likely to be earned; explain that B-permit
family-reunification permits are initially issued for one year and may then be extended for two years;
and emphasise that abuse, revocation grounds, or non-compliance can lead to refusal, revocation or
non-renewal.

SEM’s December 2025 directive on third-country family-reunification procedure clarifies process rather
than entitlement. It explains that the family-reunification process under Articles 42-45 FNIA normally
consists of both a residence-permit application and, where needed, a D-visa application; it usually starts
at the Swiss mission responsible for the applicant’s lawful residence, though in some cases it can begin
with a cantonal filing. The cantonal migration authority is the decision-maker, while SEM approval
remains reserved in certain categories under the approval procedure. This matters in lump-sum cases
because families often move at or near the same time as the principal, and sequencing errors are
common.

SEM’s official factsheet on economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals states that those persons may reside
in Switzerland if they have sufficient financial means and full health insurance, that they have a right to
family reunification, and that a five-year B EU/EFTA permit is issued and automatically renewed while
the conditions continue. This is the key official federal summary for EU/EFTA family architecture.

The FOPH states that anyone settling in Switzerland must take out health insurance within three
months after taking up residence, and that each member of the family, adult and child, must be insured
individually. For family compliance, this is a hard administrative requirement rather than an optional
evidential point.

Official federal and intercantonal schooling materials state that compulsory education in Switzerland
lasts 11 years and that the cantons are responsible for the system. In practice, children’s schooling is a
significant factual element in demonstrating that the family genuinely resides in Switzerland.

## Cantonal practice notes

No reliable current cantonal official material specifically addressing family members in Swiss
lump-sum taxation residence cases was identified in this research run. Family-member questions
therefore appear to be governed predominantly by federal tax guidance, federal immigration law, AFMP
rules where relevant, and canton-by-canton application of general family-reunification practice. That
absence itself is a drafting point: downstream content should not attribute a route-specific family rule
to a canton unless the canton has expressly published it.

## Practical legal analysis

The practical family problem in a lump-sum case is that the spouse often sits in two legal roles at once.
In tax law, the spouse is not merely a dependant: if the couple is married and living together, both
spouses must satisfy the eligibility conditions for expenditure-based taxation, and Swiss gainful activity
by one spouse collapses the regime for both. In immigration law, however, the spouse in a third-country
case may be admitted under Article 44 and may, once admitted, work under Article 46. That means the
immigration position may allow a spouse to do something that the tax structure economically punishes.
Any internal drafting that ignores that mismatch will be wrong in substance.

For non-EU/EFTA applicants, the family route is also more discretionary than many market-facing
summaries suggest. The principal’s B permit linked to a lump-sum case does not generate an automatic
family right equivalent to the position of a Swiss national or many C-permit holders. SEM’s guidance is

explicit that Article 44 is discretionary and that cantons may apply stricter conditions. This is especially
important where the family is moving simultaneously, where there are older teenage children, where
the accommodation evidence is weak, or where there is any concern about social-assistance exposure,
documentary reliability, or the genuineness of the family life relied on.

Children present a different set of issues. For third-country cases, the legal category is narrow:
unmarried under 18. Yet the practical assessment is often more textured. The filing-date rule helps with
age-out risk during processing, but late teenage applications after the statutory deadline are
scrutinised closely and require important family reasons. Once in Switzerland, schooling is not only a
welfare consideration but also evidence of authentic residence substance. In high-mobility families, a
child formally registered in Switzerland but educated and mainly living elsewhere may become part of a
wider credibility problem about the family’s true residence.

EU/EFTA families are materially different. The AFMP regime is broader both in personal scope and in
rights. It covers dependent ascendants as well as the spouse and younger or dependent descendants,
gives derivative permits the same validity as the principal’s, and grants labour-market access for the
spouse and relevant children regardless of nationality. It is therefore incorrect to describe an EU/EFTA
lump-sum family case using only third-country family-reunification language, just as it is incorrect to
import AFMP family-member definitions into a third-country case.

Post-breakdown planning requires the greatest drafting care. The materials located in this research do
not allow a fully categorical statement on whether Article 50 now unequivocally covers spouses and
children whose residence derived from a B-permit holder under Article 44, because the accessible
statutory wording and the 2026 SEM directives do not perfectly align. The safe internal position is that
separation, divorce, widowhood, and related changes in family circumstances require a fresh analysis of
the then-current FNIA text, SEM directives, any Article 8 ECHR considerations, and, where necessary,
hardship-permit possibilities under Article 30.

A further practical point is that family members are not simply “included in the lump-sum permit”. That
phrase is legally sloppy. The spouse and children may be included in the factual and economic picture of
the tax case, but their immigration status is still derivative or independent under the relevant
immigration framework. Their permits have their own conditions, renewal logic, and pathway to
settlement. Any content system output should therefore distinguish: principal tax status; principal
immigration status; derivative family immigration status; and the tax effects of family members’
conduct.

## Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe family members as automatically obtaining Swiss residence because the principal has a
lump-sum tax ruling. The tax ruling and the derivative permit are distinct legal acts, and Article 44 FNIA
is discretionary.

Do not state that a spouse may work “without consequence”. Immigration law may permit work once
the spouse is admitted, but the tax regime for a cohabiting married couple may be lost if the spouse
works in Switzerland.

Do not use the EU/EFTA family-member definition in a third-country case. The treaty covers a broader
group, including dependent ascendants and descendants under 21 or dependent descendants.

Do not omit the adult-spouse language requirement in Article 44 cases, or the possibility of satisfying
it initially by registration for language support, or the waiver for good cause. Also do not extend that
requirement to minor children.

Do not say that third-country children qualify until 21. For Article 44 FNIA, the class is unmarried
children under 18, subject to the filing-date rule and late-reunification restrictions.

Do not state post-separation residence rights for B-permit families categorically without checking the
current consolidated FNIA text in German, French or Italian. The materials located in this research show
a live interpretive uncertainty on Article 50.

Do not ignore accommodation, health insurance, and school attendance. They are routine compliance
points and also practical evidence of a real Swiss family base.

## Open questions and limitations

The most important unresolved point is the current exact scope of Article 50 FNIA for families of B-
permit holders after separation or divorce. The accessible English statutory text located during this
research and the 2026 SEM directives point in different directions. That issue should be checked directly
on the current consolidated German, French or Italian FNIA text before system-wide drafting is
finalised.

I did not identify route-specific current official cantonal publications dealing specifically with family
members in lump-sum taxation residence cases. Any cantonal family “practice” therefore needs to be
verified canton by canton before being operationalised.
