# Swiss Residence Routes for High-Net-Worth Individuals Compared

## Executive legal summary

Swiss “residence by lump-sum taxation” is not a standalone residence category. It is a combination of
two distinct legal elements that may, in suitable cases, be made to work together: first, expenditure-
based taxation under Swiss tax law; and second, a residence permit under Swiss immigration law. The
tax side is governed principally by Article 14 of the Federal Direct Tax Act and Article 6 of the Tax
Harmonisation Act, as explained by the Federal Department of Finance and the Federal Tax
Administration. Those provisions allow qualifying foreign nationals who become Swiss tax residents for
the first time, or after at least ten years abroad, and who are not gainfully employed in Switzerland, to
be assessed on the basis of expenditure rather than ordinary worldwide income and wealth. The
immigration side is separate. For EU/EFTA nationals, the immigration basis is usually the Agreement on
the Free Movement of Persons for economically inactive persons with sufficient means and insurance.
For third-country nationals, the route is generally discretionary, either under the retiree provisions or
under the “important public interests” framework, including significant cantonal fiscal interests. A tax
ruling does not itself create a right to residence.

For EU/EFTA nationals, Switzerland offers a rights-based residence architecture that is materially
different from the third-country route. Economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals are entitled to a B
permit if they have sufficient financial means and adequate health and accident insurance; the permit is
generally valid for five years and renewable if the conditions continue to be met. That route is not, in
itself, a tax route. A qualifying EU/EFTA national may separately seek expenditure-based taxation if the
tax-law conditions are met, but immigration entitlement and tax treatment remain analytically distinct.
By contrast, no equivalent general third-country “financially self-sufficient” category was found in the
official sources reviewed. Instead, third-country HNWI cases tend to fall either into the retiree route
under Article 28 FNIA and Article 25 OASA/VZAE, or into the exceptional public-interest route under
Article 30 FNIA and Article 32 OASA/VZAE. The retiree route requires age 55+, special personal ties to
Switzerland, sufficient means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad other than management
of one’s own assets. The fiscal-interest route is broader in age terms but expressly discretionary.

Entrepreneur and work routes are legally different again. For third-country nationals, work-based
admission is limited to highly qualified persons, subject to overall economic interest, quotas, labour-
market precedence, and salary and employment-condition controls. Third-country self-employment or
business-establishment cases are assessed under Article 19 FNIA, require proof of financial and
operational capacity, and must show a sustainable positive effect on the Swiss labour market; the SEM’s
directives require a business plan, company-formation documents, and evidence of market, staffing and
financial viability. Initial approvals for new businesses are typically time-limited, and extension depends
on whether the business plan’s promised economic effects are actually delivered. EU/EFTA nationals
who are self-employed sit in a different position again: the AFMP framework allows a B permit if an
effective self-employed activity is shown.

Accordingly, Swiss law should not be described as containing a general “golden visa” or passive-
investment residence permit. I found no official federal source creating a residence category granted
merely because an applicant invests capital, buys property, or agrees to pay a nominal fee. The better

legal description is that Switzerland has a closed set of residence frameworks - AFMP rights for EU/
EFTA nationals, ordinary work and self-employment admissions, the retiree route, and exceptional
permits under important public interests - within which certain HNWI fact patterns can be structured.
The specially Swiss feature is not a visa category but the possibility of pairing a non-working residence
basis with expenditure-based taxation, particularly where a canton considers the fiscal contribution
sufficiently significant. That route is often preferable for non-working HNWIs who do not wish to access
the Swiss labour market; it is often unsuitable for applicants who want to work in Switzerland, run a
Swiss business operationally, or obtain a more straightforward labour-market authorisation.

Tax outcomes also differ sharply by route. Expenditure-based taxation is a special assessment method;
it does not replace ordinary tax rates, and it is subject to statutory minimums, control-calculation rules,
and treaty complications. Ordinary worker, entrepreneur and most retiree/self-sufficient permit holders
are ordinarily taxed under the standard Swiss rules unless they separately qualify for expenditure-based
taxation. Starting Swiss gainful activity in Switzerland ends the right to lump-sum taxation. Treaty
access may also be narrower than many commercial summaries imply: ESTV Circular 44 explains both
the control calculation for treaty-relief income and the “modified” expenditure-based taxation required
under certain treaties.

From a planning perspective, the route choice also affects family and long-term residence strategy. EU/
EFTA economically inactive residence carries treaty-based family rights. Third-country retiree and public-
interest cases remain more discretionary and are handled through cantonal migration authorities, with
SEM involvement where applicable. Settlement permits remain governed by the ordinary settlement
framework rather than by any HNWI-specific privilege; for EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM’s own guidance
notes that the AFMP itself does not regulate settlement permits. This reinforces the key drafting point:
Swiss lump-sum taxation residence may be an important HNWI pathway, but it is not a separate Swiss
investment-immigration regime.

## Primary legal texts

The following are the principal federal texts that matter for comparing lump-sum residence with other
Swiss HNWI routes. Where I was unable in this run to retrieve a stable official consolidated-line extract
directly from Fedlex, I give a close paraphrase based on official EFD, ESTV and SEM materials that
expressly cite the provision.

Federal Direct Tax Act, Article 14. The official federal tax guidance and ESTV Circular 44 describe
Article 14 as giving qualifying natural persons the right to pay tax according to expenditure instead of
ordinary income tax if they do not hold Swiss citizenship, become subject to unlimited Swiss tax for the
first time or after at least ten years abroad, and are not gainfully employed in Switzerland. The same
guidance explains that the regime ends if Swiss citizenship is acquired or Swiss gainful activity begins.
For comparison purposes, this is the black-letter tax rule that makes lump-sum taxation fundamentally
incompatible with ordinary Swiss work routes.

Tax Harmonisation Act, Article 6. Cantonal implementation of expenditure-based taxation is
harmonised at framework level through Article 6 StHG/FTHA, but the EFD notes that the procedure is
implemented differently from canton to canton. For comparative analysis, this matters because the tax

regime is federally framed yet cantonally administered, so the practical distinctiveness of the lump-sum
route lies partly in cantonal handling and minimums.

Ordinance on Expenditure-Based Taxation for Direct Federal Tax. ESTV Circular 44 cites the
ordinance and explains, among other things, the permitted deductions in the treaty/control-calculation
context and the unavailability of social deductions under the direct federal tax expenditure regime. It
also explains the modified regime applicable for certain treaties. For this comparison pack, the
ordinance matters because it shows that lump-sum taxation is not a flat-fee immigration device; it is a
technical tax-assessment regime with detailed federal implementing rules.

Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, Articles 18 to 23. SEM’s official work-admission material
summarises these provisions as the backbone of third-country worker and entrepreneur admission:
overall economic interest, quotas, labour-market precedence, salary and employment-condition
controls, and personal qualifications. These provisions matter because they define the ordinary active-
economy routes that compete with, and usually exclude, lump-sum taxation.

Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, Article 28. SEM’s AIG directives summarise Article 28 as the
retiree route for third-country nationals: minimum age 55, special personal ties to Switzerland,
necessary financial means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad except own asset
management. This is the most important comparator to the tax-linked fiscal-interest route for older
HNWIs.

Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, Article 30 paragraph 1 letter b. SEM’s directives describe this
as the legal base for exceptions from ordinary admission requirements in cases of important public
interests. SEM further stresses that these are discretionary provisions, not rights-granting provisions.
This article is the key immigration anchor for non-EU/EFTA lump-sum residence cases based on cantonal
fiscal interest.

Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment, Article 25. SEM’s retiree guidance uses
Article 25 together with Article 28 FNIA to spell out retiree conditions. The provision matters in this
comparison because the retiree route is not simply “being wealthy and non-working”: it has specific age
and tie requirements not found in the fiscal-interest route.

Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment, Article 32. SEM’s directives explain Article
32 as giving content to “important public interests”, specifically including cultural, state-political and
significant fiscal interests. They add that applicants must move their centre of life to Switzerland and
stay here for the majority of the time, and that where admission is based on significant cantonal fiscal
interests, gainful activity may only be pursued abroad, except for management of one’s own assets. This
is the central immigration text for under-55 and non-retiree HNWI cases.

Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, Annex I Article 24, together with VFP Articles 16 to
20. SEM’s AFMP directives and factsheet explain that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals and their
families have a right of residence if they have sufficient means and comprehensive health and accident
insurance, and that this route is not subject to quotas or the restrictive third-country admission rules.
These provisions matter because they create the main rights-based comparator to Swiss lump-sum
residence.

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

At federal level, four bodies of official guidance are especially important. First, the EFD’s lump-sum
taxation page gives the most concise official explanation of the tax regime’s architecture, its personal
conditions, the fact that ordinary tax rates still apply, and the abolition of the regime in certain cantons.
Secondly, ESTV Circular 44 remains the key administrative source on federal expenditure-based taxation
mechanics, especially treaty-relief income, control-calculation logic, modified expenditure-based
taxation for certain treaties, and procedural obligations to demonstrate continuing eligibility. Thirdly,
the SEM AIG directives are the main federal administrative source on third-country retiree admission,
important public interests, centre-of-life expectations, and activity restrictions for permits based on
cantonal fiscal interests. Fourthly, the SEM AFMP directives and factsheets explain the EU/EFTA
architecture for economically inactive persons, workers and self-employed persons.

The most useful official canton document located for route architecture is the Appenzell Innerrhoden
joint tax-and-permit note, “Besteuerung nach dem Aufwand & Bewilligungsprozess”. It is not federal
law, but it is a reliable official illustration of how one canton treats the route: it expressly distinguishes
EU/EFTA economically inactive residence from third-country cases; identifies Article 32 OASA/VZAE as
the basis for third-country applicants under 55 for important fiscal reasons; identifies Article 28 FNIA for
third-country retirees over 55; requires centre-of-life and no-work confirmations; and describes a
sequence in which the tax administration reviews the dossier, economic-development structures decide,
and the migration dossier then goes to the canton and, for third-country nationals, to SEM. It is best
treated as a procedural and evidential practice note, not as a source of federal black-letter law.

An official Obwalden tax note provides a second useful canton-level illustration. It states that foreign
nationals without Swiss citizenship who meet the first-time-or-ten-year return condition and do not
work in Switzerland may apply for expenditure-based taxation; it then adds canton-specific explanatory
positions, including that a directorship role may be permitted if the board fee is insignificant plus
expenses, and that foreign gainful activity is permitted with the place-of-work principle in mind.
Because these statements come from a cantonal tax note rather than federal migration guidance, they
should be treated cautiously as canton-specific explanatory practice, especially on the boundaries of
“gainful activity”.

A French-language official Valais page is also useful at a high level because it shows institutional
coupling between tax and migration authorities: the canton’s “Impôt sur la dépense” page offers
information and a form specifically intended for tax and migration authorities “in view of obtaining a
residence permit”. That does not alter the federal division between tax and immigration, but it shows
how some cantons operationally integrate the two streams in HNWI files.

## Practical legal analysis

The main comparison point is that “lump-sum residence” is legally hybrid. Nothing in the official
materials suggests that Swiss law grants residence merely because a person is wealthy or agrees a tax
amount. The person must fit an immigration basis that independently allows residence, and the tax
office must independently accept expenditure-based taxation. For EU/EFTA nationals, that can be
relatively straightforward because the underlying residence right for economically inactive persons is
treaty-based. For third-country nationals, the position is much less secure because the relevant
immigration gateways are discretionary and cantonal, even where a tax ruling in principle looks viable.

This is why the third-country fiscal-interest route is best understood as an exception-based residence
architecture, not an entitlement route. SEM’s current directives deliberately describe “important public
interests” as an indeterminate concept that must not be interpreted too widely; they also emphasise the
wide discretion of the authorities and the need to balance cantonal fiscal interests against Switzerland’s
broader public interests and reputation. That makes the route materially different from AFMP residence
for EU/EFTA nationals and from ordinary work routes with codified admission criteria. In practical
drafting, this means that even a strong fiscal file should be presented as supporting an exceptional
admission under Article 30/Article 32, not as generating a legal entitlement.

The retiree route should likewise not be collapsed into the lump-sum route. It is a distinct legal pathway
with its own logic: age threshold, special personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means, and a stricter
no-work rule extending even to foreign gainful activity, apart from own asset management. For some
older HNWIs with genuine historic links to Switzerland, it may be viable without relying on fiscal-interest
reasoning. For many internationally active HNWIs, however, it will be unsuitable because it is both
narrower and less compatible with ongoing executive or advisory work abroad than the fiscal-interest
route under Article 32 OASA/VZAE.

Entrepreneur and business-establishment routes are preferable only where an applicant genuinely
intends to operate in the Swiss economy. Official SEM material makes clear that third-country self-
employment is not granted because capital is available or because a company can be bought or
incorporated on paper. The authorities look for a real self-employed role, financial and operational
viability, market opportunity, staffing plans, and sustainable positive labour-market effects. Initial
permits are limited, and extension depends on actual performance against the business plan. For an
HNWI who wants passive residence and tax structuring, this is normally the wrong route. For an HNWI
who wants to build and run a Swiss company, it may be the correct route - but then lump-sum taxation
will usually cease to be available because Swiss gainful activity is incompatible with the tax regime.

Ordinary work-based admission is also a poor substitute for lump-sum residence unless the factual case
is truly a Swiss executive or specialist hire. Official SEM material states plainly that third-country workers
must be highly qualified and are admitted only within the framework of economic interest, quotas and
precedence. That is a very different logic from the non-working HNWI route. Conversely, for EU/EFTA
nationals the worker and self-employed routes are liberal and rights-based under the AFMP, so the
comparative choice is often not “can the person live in Switzerland?” but “does the person want to
remain economically inactive in Switzerland so as to preserve access to expenditure-based taxation?”

The tax comparison is often oversimplified in commercial literature. Official federal sources make clear
that expenditure-based taxation is not a negotiated flat annual payment detached from statute. It uses
ordinary tax rates, statutory minimums, control calculations and treaty-related adjustments. It may
therefore be attractive compared with ordinary taxation for some internationally mobile HNWIs, but its
net advantage depends on the person’s asset mix, treaty claims and income profile. In particular, ESTV
Circular 44 shows that seeking treaty benefits can force inclusion of additional foreign income and, for
certain treaty states, a modified regime approximating ordinary taxation on the relevant treaty-state
income. This is one reason why the route can be preferable from a residence perspective yet less
advantageous than expected from a tax perspective.

Family and long-term planning also differ by route. EU/EFTA economically inactive persons enjoy treaty-
based family residence mechanics. Third-country retirees are directed by the SEM to the ordinary family-
reunification rules, and third-country public-interest cases remain embedded in the broader AIG
system. For settlement planning, the SEM states that the AFMP itself does not regulate settlement
permits; C permits follow the ordinary settlement framework and, for some nationalities, settlement

treaties. The practical consequence is that no HNWI route should be described as a privileged
settlement or citizenship category in itself. It is a route into ordinary Swiss residence law, after which the
normal long-term rules reassert themselves.

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss lump-sum taxation as a Swiss “golden visa” in legal terms. The official
sources reviewed identify tax rules, AFMP residence rights, retiree permits, work permits and
exceptional public-interest permits - not a separate investor-visa category. Treat the “no golden
visa” point as a legal inference from the official architecture, not as a quoted statutory label.
Do not say that agreeing a lump-sum tax ruling automatically gives a right to residence. The tax
and migration decisions are distinct, and Article 30/Article 32 cases remain discretionary.
Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA cases into one route. EU/EFTA economically inactive

## Open questions and limitations

This comparison pack is intentionally architecture-focused. It does not provide a country-by-country
treaty analysis or a canton-by-canton inventory of every HNWI practice point. In this research run, some
statutory provisions were retrieved most reliably through official EFD, ESTV and SEM guidance rather
than through stable line-addressable Fedlex extracts, so the “Primary legal texts” section gives close
paraphrases grounded in those official administrative materials. Treaty-specific or canton-specific
drafting should therefore be supplemented by the relevant treaty text and current canton practice
before publication-quality advice is generated.
