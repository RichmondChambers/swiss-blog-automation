# Swiss Lump-Sum Taxation No Gainful Activity Foreign Business Interests and Board Roles Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

Swiss lump-sum taxation, or taxation according to expenditure, is not an open-ended privilege for wealthy foreign residents to carry on business from Switzerland. At federal tax level, the regime is available only to foreign nationals who are taking up Swiss tax residence for the first time,or after the relevant absence, and who **do not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland.** The Federal Tax Administration's Circular No. 44 treats this condition seriously: a disqualifying Swiss activity exists where the person pursues any main or secondary occupation in Switzerland and derives income from it, whether the income arises in Switzerland or abroad. The circular expressly says that this applies in particular to artists, scientists, inventors, athletes and **board** **members** who are personally active in Switzerland for gainful purposes. If one spouse in an unseparated marriage works in Switzerland, both spouses lose the right to lump-sum taxation for that tax period. The right also ends if Swiss citizenship is acquired. 1

A critical distinction must be kept in view between **tax** **law** **and** **immigration** **law.** The tax rule is framed territorially: the official interpretive materials reviewed for this pack treat the relevant question as whether gainful activity is exercised **in** **Switzerland.** By contrast, immigration law uses a broader concept of gainfuI activity. SEM guidance states that gainful activity includes any dependent or independent activity that is usually performed for remuneration, **even** if it **is** in **fact** **unpaid.** SEM also distinguishes work with and without taking up a post: self-employment, and work for a foreign employer carried out in Switzerland, are still gainful activity and may require separate work authorisation. This means that an arrangement which is arguable on the tax side can still create immigration risk. 2

Official materials support the proposition that **private** **management** of **one's** **own** **assets** is not, in itself, the target of the prohibition. For immigration purposes, SEM's retiree guidance under Article 28AIG expressly permits "own asset management" while otherwise requiring no gainful activity in or outside Switzerland. Separately, ESTV Circular No. 36 on professional securities trading distinguishes private asset management from self-employment and preserves room for ordinary, even relatively active, management of a private securities portfolio. That said, the more the facts resemble an organised business, remunerated service activity, or self-employment, the less safe the position becomes. 3

Foreign business interests therefore raise fewer problems when the person remains a **passive** **investor** than when he or she becomes operationally involved. The official and quasi-official materialsreviewed here support a territorial "work-location" analysis for tax purposes: foreign gainful activity physically exercised abroad is not automatically incompatible with expenditure-based taxation. But that does not produce blanket safety. The facts must show that the person's real executive or operational work is carried out abroad, not in Switzerland, and immigration conditions may still be stricter depending on the permit basis used.4

Swiss board mandates are the most sensitive area. The black-letter administrative position in Circular No. 44 is adverse to Swiss board activity pursued for gain. Federal Council statementsin parliamentary business then qualify that position: in some constellations a board mandate may still be compatible with lump-sum taxation, especially where it is **honorary** or only a reasonable expense reimbursement is paid, and where the person is not additionally running the company's business. An official cantonal note from Obwalden adopts a similar practice point, allowing a board role if the honorarium remains only a minor amount plus expenses. However, an official legal opinion commissioned by the Swiss Federal Audit Office warns that these cases are not straightforward, especially where the taxpayer is also a shareholder: a waiver of remuneration may still economically enrich the person through the increased value of the shares, making the role harder to characterise as non-gainful. That part is not statutory text and should be treated as informed secondary analysis rather than settled black-letter law. 5

For internal drafting, the safest synthesis is this. Lump-sum taxation tolerates **passive investment and** may tolerate some activity **physically** **carried** **out abroad.** It does **not** safely tolerate ordinary Swiss employment, Swiss self-employment, or Swiss operational work for a Swiss or foreign business.Swiss board roles are dangerous unless they fit within a narrow honorary or expense-only practice position, and even then they require separate immigration analysis because immigration law can treat usually remunerated activity as gainful even if unpaid. Family-office structures and permanent-establishment style risks are not expressly addressed in the official lump-sum materials reviewed for this pack; any comfort there is inferentialI and fact-specific, not a stated federal safe harbour. 6

## Structural overview of activity restrictions

## Tax-law rule

The tax-law starting point is short but strict: the lump-sum regime is available only if the foreign national "does not exercise gainful activity in Swvitzerland". ESTV Circular No. 44 records the rule under Article 14 DDBG and applies it to any main or side occupation in Switzerland that yields income, whether the income is sourced in Switzerland or abroad. It then lists board members among the paradigmatic examples.7

## Immigration-law overlay

Immigration law is not coextensive with the tax rule. SEM guidance explains that gainful activity includes any activity ordinarily performed for remuneration, even when carried out unpaid, and it captures both employment and self-employment. SEM also treats activity for a foreign employer carried out in Switzerland as "gainful activity without taking up a post". As a result, a tax argument that an activity is non-gainful does not automatically solve the immigration question. 8

## Private asset management

Own-asset management is the clearest permitted category in the official materials reviewed. SEM's retiree guidance expressly carves out "own asset management" from the no-work requirement for Article 28 AIG cases, and ESTV Circular No. 36 preserves a distinction between private asset management and self-employment in securities trading. The official guidance does not, however, create a lump-sum-specific safe harbour for complex family-office or quasi-commercial structures.3

## Foreign business interests and executive roles abroad

The reviewed materials support a territorial test for the tax condition: activity that is physically exercised abroad is not necessarily harmful for expenditure-based taxation. The Federal Council has said that remunerated board activity performed abroad need not prevent lump-sum taxation, and the official legal opinion commissioned by the Federal Audit Office likewise treats the place of personal performance as central. But these sources do not license Swiss-based de facto management of foreign businesses. 9

## Swiss board mandates

Paid Swiss board mandates are generally difficult to reconcile with the regime. Circular No. 44 identifies Swiss board activity as a disqualifying example. Federal Council statements and some cantonal practice materials qualify that outcome for honorary mandates or modest expense-only arrangements. The most conservative internal position is that Swiss board roles are presumptively problematic, and any exception requires careful fact development on remuneration, ownership, operational influence and immigration treatment. 10

### Spouses, compliance and cessation

The rule is collective for spouses living together: both must satisfy the conditions, and Swiss gainful activity by either spouse ends the regime for both. Circular No. 44 also says that the assessment authority must verify the conditions before each assessment. If prohibited Swiss work is taken up, ordinary taxation applies for the whole tax period. On the immigration side, work can require prior authorisation and breaches can engage the permit-enforcement and sanctions framework. ⑪

## Primary legJal texts

## Tax-law texts

**Article 14 DBG - Federal Act of 14 December 1990 on Direct Federal Taxation.** ESTV Circular No. 44sets out the operative conditions in paraphrase: a natural person may pay tax according to expenditure instead of ordinary income tax if the person is not a Swiss citizen, is becoming subject to unlimited tax liability for the first time or after at least ten years' interruption, and does not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland. The circular also records that both spouses in an unseparated marriage must satisfy the conditions. For this pack, Article 14 DBG is the core source because it is the federal legal anchor for the no-gainful-activity requirement. 7

**Article 6 StHG - Federal Act of 14 December 1990 on the Harmonisation of Direct Taxes of** **Cantons** **and** **Municipalities.** The reviewed official materials treat the cantonal tax rule as harmonised to the same subjective conditions. Circular No. 44 records that the 2012 reform amended both DBG and StHG, and Appenzell Innerrhoden's official note cites Article 6 StHG alongside Article 14 DBG as the federal basis for cantonal expenditure-based taxation. For present purposes, Article 6 StHG matters because the activity restriction is not merely a federal direct tax point: it is imported into cantonal income and wealth tax treatment as well. 18

## Immigration-law texts

**Article 11 AIG - Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration.** SEM's chapter **4** guidance paraphrases Article 11 AIG by stating that gainful activity includes any dependent or independent activity that is usually performed for remuneration, even if it is unpaid. This provision matters because immigration law can classify an ostensibly "honorary" or unpaid function as work requiring separate analysis. 12

<!-- 6 -->

**Articles 1 to 4 VZAE - Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Gainful Employment. SEM's** guidance describes chapter 4 as the interpretation of Articles 1 to 4 VZAE and uses those provisions to distinguish gainful activity, including gainful activity without taking up a post, from non-gainful short business meetings. This matters because it is the operative immigration framework for analysing whether Swiss-based executive work, cross-border services or self-employment triggers work-authorisation requirements. 19

**Article** **28** **AIG** **and** **Article** 25 **VZAE-Retirees.** SEM states that retirees must meet cumulative requirements including age, special personal ties to Switzerland, sufficient means, and no gainful activity in Switzerland or abroad other than own asset management. For this pack, these provisions matter because many lump-sum cases rely on a residence basis for persons not working; immigration law here is in some respects **stricter** than tax law. 14

**Article 14 VZAE and Article 17 VZAE - Gainful activity without taking up a post.** SEM states that self-employment or work for a foreign employer in Switzerland is gainful activity "without taking up a post", that it is only permit-free up to limited short thresholds in certain cases, and that the decision must be awaited before the activity begins. This matters because a foreign executive who remains employed by an overseas company may still be regarded as working in Switzerland if the activity is performed from Swiss territory. 13

**Article 32 VZAE - Admission for important public interests.** Appenzell Innerrhoden's official note states that, for third-country nationals under 55, Article 32 paragraph 1 letter c VZAE allows the cantons to grant a residence permit for important fiscal reasons. This provision matters indirectly to this pack because it explains why immigration authorities may insist on a strict non-work fact pattern where the residence case is fiscally motivated. 20

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

### Federal tax guidance

The most important federal tax guidance is **ESTV** **Circular** **No.** 44 **of** 24 July2018.For activity restrictions it does three essential things. It states the subjective conditions under Article 14 DBG; it gives a relatively strict administrative meaning to harmful Swiss ggainful activity; and it confirms that the tax authority must verify the conditions before every assessment. For later drafting, Circular No. 44should be treated as the primary administrative source for the proposition that Swiss paid work, including Swiss board activity pursued for gain, is incompatible with the regime. 7

The EFD's 2024 official overview page is less detailed but useful as a high-level federal reference. It confirms that the regime is a simplified method of assessing income and wealth for certain foreign nationals, that it ends when Swiss citizenship is acquired or gainful activity is exercised, and that cantonal implementation differs. It is especially useful for concise explanatory drafting, though not as a substitutefor Circular No. 44. 21

**ESTV Circular No. 36 on professional securities trading** is not a lump-sum circular, but it is an official and relevant source for the boundary between permitted private portfolio management and self-employment. It makes clear that there is no bright line except in safer cases and that the overall facts determine whether activity remains private asset management or becomes self-employed trading. For family-office analysis, it is the best official federal source identified on that boundary. 22

### Federal migration guidance

The principal migration materials are the **SEM** **AIG** **guidance** and the **SEM** **factsheet** **on** **residence** **without** **gainful activity.** SEM chapter 4 is essential for the immigration definition of work, including unpaid work that is usually remunerated and work for foreign employers carried out in Switzerland. SEM chapter 5 is especially useful for Article 28 AIG retiree cases, where the guidance expressly permits only own asset management and requires the centre of life to be in Switzerland. The EU/EFTA factsheet is useful in confirming that EU/EFTA non-workers can obtain B permits if they have sufficient means and comprehensive health insurance, but it does not relax the tax-law activity conditions. 23

### Federal Council statements in parliamentary materials

Parliamentary materials are not primary legislation, but the official Federal Council answers reviewed here are valuable for the board-mandate issue. In business **20.3850,** the Federal Council stated that a paid Swiss board function is generally incompatible with expenditure-based taxation, but that compatibility may remain where the role is honorary or only reasonable expense reimbursement is paid; it also said that remunerated board activity physically performed abroad does not necessarily prevent the regime. These statements are particularly useful because they qualify the otherwise strict tone of Circular No. 44, while still stopping well short of a general permission for Swiss board work. 24

## Cantonal practice notes

**Obwalden.** Obwalden's official note is unusually explicit. It states that a person must noot exercise gainful activity in Switzerland, but that a board role is permitted if the board honorarium does not exceed a minor amount plus expense reimbursement; it also states that gainful activity abroad is permitted and that the decisive factor is the **place** **of** **personal performance.** This is best treated as a canton-level practice statement, not a federal rule. 25

**Appenzell Innerrhoden.** The joint tax-and-business-office note is more useful for route architecture than for board roles. It repeats the no-work condition, ties the tax route to the permit process, and states that under-55 third-country nationals may rely on Article 32 VZAE for important fiscal reasons, while over-55 applicants may use Article 28 AIG. This is a procedural and explanatory practice note rather than black-letter federal law. 26

**Bern.** Bern's official TaxInfo page restates the core subjective requirements, including absence of gainful activity in Switzerland, but it does not add the same level of nuance on board mandates as the Obwalden note. It is useful as confirmation that the general rule is applied cantonally in the expected way.27

## Officially commissioned secondary material

The most important secondary source identified is the **official legal** **opinion** **commissioned** **by** **the** **Swiss** **Federal Audit Office.** It is not primary law and should not be quoted as though it were, but it is highly useful because it works through the statutory materials, case law and literature on exactly the questions that create drafting difficulty: honorary board roles, indirect compensation, activity physically carried out abroad, and the distinction between private asset management and operational work. Its analysis is particularly valuable where primary law and public circulars stop short of detailed examples. 28

## Practical legal analysis

The central analytical mistake to avoid is treating the "no gainful activity" condition as a single concept across all branches of Swiss law. It is not. For tax purposes, the rule in Article 14 DBG, as interpreted by ESTV Circular No. 44, is aimed at gainful activity **in** **Switzerland.** For immigration purposes, SEM interprets "gainful activity" more broadly, including activity that is usually remunerated even if no remuneration is actually paid, and including self-employment or work for a foreign employer performed from Switzerland. Internal drafting should therefore always separate the **tax** **question** from the **permit** **question** and then test the same facts against both. 29

This distinction is why **honorary** **Swiss board** **mandates** are so awkward. On the tax side, the Federal Council has accepted that a genuinely honorary mandate or one with only reasonable expense reimbursement may, in principle, remain compatible with lump-sum taxation; one canton, Obwalden, states a similar practice in express terms. On the immigration side, however, the general SEM definition of gainful activity does not rely entirely on whether remuneration was in fact paid; it asks whether the activity is of a kind usually performed for remuneration. A careful internal writer should therefore not assume that a tax-tolerated honorary Swiss board mandate is automatically immigration-safe.That conclusion is an inference from the official materials reviewed, but it is a strong and prudent one. 30

Foreign executive roles are a different problem. The reviewed official materials accept, at least for tax purposes, that work physically exercised abroad does not necessarily destroy lump-sum taxation. That opens room for foreign board mandates and foreign executive responsibilities if **the** **real** **work** is **done** **abroad.** But the evidential burden is factual. Travel patterns, meeting records, signature practice, office location, support staff and the locus of day-to-day decision-making all matter. If the executive function migrates to Switzerland in substance, the position becomes much harder on both tax and immigration analysis. 31

The distinction between **private wealth management and business activity** is also crucial. Official materials support the proposition that a person may manage his or her own assets without disqualifying the residence route, and SEM's retiree guidance says so expressly. But there is no reviewed official federal lump-sum source dealing specifically with family-office structures, advisory entities, or centralised management of multi-entity family assets. The conservative internal approach is therefore inferential: the more a Swiss-based structure uses Swiss premises, staff, routine services, fees, or operational control over bousinesses, the more it resembles self-employment or a Swiss operating platform rather than mere own-asset management. That is not an express lump-sum safe-harbour analysis; it is a prudent inference from the official divide between own-asset management, private asset management and self-employment. 32

Shareholding level matters indirectly rather than directly. The statute disqualifies **activity,** not mere ownership. A minority or controlling investment is not, by itself, the legal problem. The legal difficulty arises when ownership is paired with a Swiss board or executive role. The EFK legal opinion is especially useful here: it argues that a shareholder-board member who "waives" compensation may still benefit economically through increased share value, making the role harder to present as non-gainful. That reasoning is not codified law, but it is a significant warning against treating a controlling shareholder's Swiss board presidency as safely honorary. 33

Spousal activity is another point on which later drafting often goes wrong. The reviewed federal tax guidance does not merely look at the principal applicant. It requires both spouses in an unseparated marriage to satisfy the conditions, and Swiss gainful activity by either spouse destroys the regime for both.That makes spouse employment, spouse self-employment and spouse board participation a direct tax-eligibility issue, not just a family planning detail. The reviewed official materials did not identify an equivalent lump-sum-specific federal rule for adult children, so later drafting should not casually extend the spouse rule beyond the categories actually covered.7

Finally, compliance risk is annual and cumulative. Circular No. 44 states that the tax authority must re-check the conditions before each assessment. SEM guidance makes clear that work authorisation may be needed before Swiss-based activity begins and that immigration authorities expect the permit holder to remain within the relevant non-work category. The practical lesson is that activity restrictions are not a one-off entry condition; they are a continuing status condition. An arrangement that was unobjectionable at the time of arrival can cease to be safe if the resident later accepts a Swiss board chairmanship, begins directing a group company from Switzerland, or turns a private investment operation into a staffed Swiss service platform. 11

## Drafting cautions

·Do not state that lump-sum taxation requires a total ban on all gainful activity anywhere in the world. The tax-law rule is framed around Swiss gainful activity, while some immigration bases, especially Article 28 AIG retiree cases, are stricter. 34

·Do not say that any Swiss board mandate is automatically disqualifying in all circumstances. The black-letter administrative tax position is strict, but Federal Council statements and some cantonal practice accept narrow honorary or expense-only cases. 35

·Do **not** say that an honorary board role is automatically safe. Where the individual is also a shareholder, an unpaid role may still be seen as economically compensated through value accretion. Treat that as a risk point, not a settled rule. 17

·Do **not** collapse tax-law and immigration-law analysis into a single sentence. Immigration law can classify usually remunerated activity as gainful even when unpaid. 12

·Do not present private asset management as limitless. Official guidance preserves own-asset management, but business-like trading or Swiss-based service activity can move the facts towards self-employment. 3

·Do **not** assume that a foreign employer structure solves the Swiss work issue. Work for a foreign employer performed from Switzerland can still be gainful activity in Switzerland for immigration purposes. 13

·Do not ignore spouse activity. Swiss gainful activity by either spouse in an unseparated marriage ends the regime for both spouses. 7

·Do **not** treat canton-level notes, such as Obwalden's tolerance for modest board honoraria, as though they were federal law. Present them expressly as cantonal practice points. 36

·Do not write that family-office structures are expressly accepted by official federal lump-sum guidance. No such reviewed source was identified for this pack; any comfort must be presented as inferential and fact-sensitive.
