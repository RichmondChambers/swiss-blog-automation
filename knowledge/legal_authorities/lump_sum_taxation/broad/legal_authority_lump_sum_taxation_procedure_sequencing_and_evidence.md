# Swiss Lump Sum Taxation Residence Procedure Sequencing and Evidence Pack

A Executive legal summary

Swiss “residence by lump-sum taxation” is not a single codified residence category. In practice it is a
two-track process: first, a tax-side application or ruling request to be taxed according to expenditure
under the federal and cantonal tax framework; second, a residence-permit application under
immigration law, handled primarily at cantonal level and, in relevant third-country cases, subject to
federal approval by the State Secretariat for Migration (SEM). Official federal materials describe
expenditure-based taxation as a special way of assessing income and wealth for certain foreign
nationals, while the SEM’s immigration guidance treats wealthy non-EU/EFTA cases as part of the
broader discretionary “important public interests” framework rather than as a standalone visa route.
There is therefore no autonomous Swiss “golden visa” in the legal sense.

For non-EU/EFTA applicants, current SEM guidance places fiscally motivated residence cases under
Article 30 paragraph 1 letter b FNIA and Article 32 OASA: permits are discretionary, the authorities have
a wide margin of appreciation, significant cantonal fiscal interests may count as an important public
interest, and the applicant must show that they will transfer their centre of life to Switzerland and spend
the majority of the year there. Where admission is justified by significant cantonal fiscal interests, any
gainful activity may be pursued only abroad, apart from managing the applicant’s own assets. Geneva’s
official lump-sum permit page mirrors this approach and expressly requires a major fiscal interest
attested by the cantonal tax administration, transfer of the centre of interests to Switzerland, no gainful
activity in Switzerland beyond private wealth management, filing before entry, and SEM approval.

For EU/EFTA nationals, the residence architecture is different. Their right to reside without gainful
activity flows from the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons route for economically inactive
persons, not from lump-sum taxation itself. SEM’s factsheet states that economically inactive EU/EFTA
nationals must have sufficient financial means and comprehensive health insurance including accident
cover; if they stay for more than three months, they register with the cantonal migration office and are
normally issued a five-year B EU/EFTA permit. Taxation according to expenditure may still be relevant on
the tax side, but it is not the source of the residence right.

The process is canton-led and canton-sensitive from the outset. The Federal Department of Finance
states that expenditure-based taxation is implemented differently from canton to canton. That makes
canton selection a genuine legal and procedural first step, not just a rate-comparison exercise. Official
cantonal tax materials show that the tax-side application is typically addressed to the cantonal tax
administration and is evidence-heavy. Obwalden requires an application with a brief introduction to the
applicant and a rough presentation of the financial situation. Schwyz requires a detailed written request
covering identity, past and future Swiss residence, family composition, present and future activities,
business and board roles, assets, income, treaty-relief claims, worldwide living expenses, and
supporting documents such as passports and lease or purchase contracts.

The immigration-side dossier is likewise evidence-heavy and not standardised nationally. Geneva’s
official page for third-country applicants seeking a permit “with forfait fiscal” requires, among other

things, the cantonal form, a valid passport copy, housing evidence or purchase intentions, the tax
convention with the Geneva tax administration, a criminal record from the country of origin, a
motivation letter, a written undertaking not to work in Switzerland and to transfer the centre of
interests to Switzerland while spending the majority of the year there, a CV, and civil-status documents
for accompanying family members. Federal migration guidance separately confirms that, before
granting a permit to a person expected to stay in Switzerland for a longer period, the authorities may
request a criminal-record extract from the home state.

As to sequencing, official federal guidance states that a permit for a stay without gainful activity of more
than three months must, in principle, be applied for before entry at the authority for the intended place
of residence. Visa-required third-country nationals need a national D visa for such stays, and the FDFA
states that the D visa is issued subject to authorisation from the competent cantonal migration
authority. SEM’s entry guidance also states that, for stays over 90 days, the residence-permit application
should in principle be filed before entry even by nationals exempt from the visa requirement for long
stays. After arrival, commune registration is generally expected within 14 days, and health insurance
must be taken out within three months after taking up residence.

There is no single national processing timeline. The FDFA warns that a national visa process can take
several weeks or even months. Canton Geneva publishes only indicative timings; its nearest published
category for extra-European “other permits without gainful activity” is eight months, with biometric
appointments taking a further two to four weeks. Those figures are canton-specific practice points, not
federal rules.

B Structural overview of procedure and sequencing

Legal character of the process. The route is structurally bifurcated. The tax-side question is whether
the person may be assessed under expenditure-based taxation; the immigration-side question is
whether the person may lawfully reside in Switzerland. Federal finance guidance describes lump-sum
taxation as a special assessment method for certain foreign nationals, while SEM guidance treats non-
EU/EFTA wealthy-person admissions as discretionary permits under the “important public interests”
exception. A favourable tax position therefore supports, but does not itself confer, residence
permission.

Initial screening and canton selection. The opening stage is normally a dual eligibility check: tax
eligibility for expenditure-based taxation and immigration eligibility for residence without Swiss gainful
activity. Because the federal finance authorities expressly note that implementation differs by canton,
and because tax applications are made to cantonal tax authorities, canton selection is logically
upstream of formal filings. In practice, one should expect canton-specific document expectations and
different interfaces between tax and migration offices.

Tax-side pre-filing and ruling phase. No single federal source prescribes a uniform “pre-ruling
negotiation” stage for lump-sum cases. Official cantonal materials instead speak in terms of an
application, a convention or a request addressed to the cantonal tax administration. Geneva requires
the “convention fiscale” from the cantonal tax administration as part of the migration file. Obwalden
requires an application to the cantonal tax administration with a short introduction and an outline of
finances. Schwyz requires a detailed written application with substantial factual and financial disclosure.
The safest characterisation is therefore that tax pre-clearance is a canton-level administrative practice,
not a separately codified federal step.

Migration filing and approval. For third-country nationals wishing to stay for more than three months
without gainful activity, the permit application must in principle be lodged before entry with the
authority for the intended place of residence. For fiscally motivated third-country cases, Geneva
expressly says the file is submitted to SEM for approval. SEM’s directives explain the approval system
more generally: the SEM may approve, refuse, time-limit, or attach conditions in cases subject to
approval, and the canton may issue the permit only after SEM consent where approval is required.

Entry, visa, registration and issuance. Visa-required third-country nationals need a national D visa for
long stays, and the FDFA states that such a visa is issued only after cantonal migration authorisation.
Even visa-exempt nationals are told by SEM that stays of more than 90 days require a residence-permit
filing in principle before entry. After arrival, commune registration is generally expected within 14 days.
Geneva publishes an additional biometric step, with appointments typically taking two to four weeks.

EU/EFTA procedural distinction. EU/EFTA nationals do not use the discretionary fiscal-interest route as
their legal basis of admission. Rather, SEM’s AFMP factsheet says that economically inactive EU/EFTA
persons register with the cantonal migration office if staying longer than three months and obtain a B
EU/EFTA permit if they show sufficient means and insurance. Any tax application for expenditure-based
taxation is therefore ancillary to, not constitutive of, the residence right.

Ongoing compliance and renewal. After grant of expenditure-based taxation, official cantonal tax
materials require ongoing annual filing. Obwalden says the taxpayer must submit an annual tax return
for lump-sum taxation, also used for cantonal and communal tax. Schwyz requires the annual tax return
for expenditure-based taxation together with securities and cash schedules and a list of Swiss real
estate. On the migration side, changes in resources, activity, family facts, domicile reality or permit
conditions can affect renewal; Geneva’s service-times page separately shows a three-month indicative
timeframe for permit renewals without change of circumstances.

D Primary legal texts

The Federal Act on Direct Federal Taxation and the Federal Act on the Harmonisation of Direct
Taxation at Cantonal and Communal Levels supply the tax-side basis for expenditure-based taxation.
Official federal finance guidance states that the regime is available to foreign nationals who make
Switzerland their tax domicile for the first time or after at least ten years abroad and who are not
gainfully employed in Switzerland; the assessment is based on annual living expenses, subject to
statutory minimums and a control calculation. The ESTV’s Circular No. 44 explains the implementing
framework for direct federal tax and refers expressly to Article 14 DFTA and the 2013 ordinance on
expenditure-based taxation.

On the immigration side, Article 10 FNIA is procedurally central. SEM’s directives state that a person
intending to stay in Switzerland without gainful activity for more than three months needs a permit and
must apply for it before entry at the authority responsible for the intended place of residence. The same
chapter explains that visa-required nationals need a national D visa for such a stay and that, without
prior assurance of the permit, cantonal authorities may require the person to await the decision abroad.

For wealthy non-EU/EFTA cases linked to lump-sum taxation, the operative legal basis is the
combination of Article 30 paragraph 1 letter b FNIA and Article 32 OASA. SEM’s current directives say
that “important public interests” is an open legal concept that must not be interpreted too broadly; that
Article 30(1) FNIA creates discretionary, not mandatory, power; that permits may be granted where
significant cantonal fiscal interests exist; that the applicant must prove relocation of the centre of life
and majority presence in Switzerland; and that, in fiscal-interest cases, gainful activity may only be
exercised abroad, save for management of the applicant’s own assets.

The federal approval framework also matters procedurally. SEM’s directives explain that, under Article
99 FNIA and Article 85 OASA, certain cantonal decisions must be submitted to SEM for approval; SEM
may refuse approval, set time limits, or attach conditions; and where approval is required the canton
may issue the permit only after SEM has agreed. Although the specific approval-ordinance text was not
separately extracted from Fedlex in this research run, the SEM directive text is explicit as to the
existence and effect of the approval regime.

For EU/EFTA nationals, the relevant residence basis is Annex I Article 24 AFMP, as reflected in SEM’s
official factsheet and B-permit page. Those materials state that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals
have the right to enter and stay without taking up gainful employment if they have sufficient financial
means and full health insurance including accident cover; for stays beyond three months they register
with the canton and are issued a B EU/EFTA permit, usually for five years.

Two further provisions are procedurally relevant. First, SEM’s directives cite Article 13 paragraph 2
FNIA as the basis on which the authorities may request a criminal-record extract from the country of
origin before granting, or at the latest before renewing, a permit for a person expected to stay long-
term. Secondly, the Federal Office of Public Health states that anyone settling in Switzerland must take
out compulsory health insurance within three months after taking up residence, which becomes a post-
entry compliance step even where insurance evidence is already needed in the permit process.

E Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal tax guidance located was ESTV Circular No. 44 on expenditure-based
taxation for direct federal tax. It links the tax regime to the amended DFTA and DTHA framework
enacted after the 2012 reform, and it confirms the role of the 2013 ordinance on expenditure-based
taxation. For a procedural pack, its main value is that it shows the regime is administered through
ordinary tax assessment mechanics rather than by a separate immigration statute, and that treaty-relief
income and the control calculation remain embedded in the annual tax file.

The Federal Department of Finance dossier on lump-sum taxation is useful as an official structural
overview. It states that expenditure-based taxation is implemented differently from canton to canton, is
reserved to certain foreign nationals who are not gainfully employed in Switzerland, and ceases upon
Swiss citizenship or the taking up of gainful activity in Switzerland. That relatively short statement is
significant because it officially confirms both cantonal procedural variation and the need to analyse tax
and immigration timing by canton.

On the immigration side, the key federal source is the SEM AIG directives. For this topic they do three
things. They explain the approval architecture between cantons and SEM; they set out the general rule
that permits for more-than-three-month stays without gainful activity must be sought before entry; and
they describe wealthy-person admissions under “important public interests”, including significant
cantonal fiscal interests, centre-of-life evidence, majority presence and the activity restriction.

For EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM AFMP factsheet on residing in Switzerland without gainful activity
and the SEM B EU/EFTA permit page are the clearest official procedural sources found. They state that
economically inactive EU/EFTA applicants must prove sufficient financial means and health/accident
insurance, must register with the cantonal migration office for stays over three months, and will
normally receive a five-year B permit.

The federal information platform ch.ch fills several procedural gaps. Its page on living in Switzerland
without gainful employment states that third-country nationals who wish to settle without working
must apply to the cantonal immigration and labour-market authorities before arrival and, depending on
nationality, also obtain a visa. Its moving-registration page states that people are generally expected to
register with the new commune within 14 days. Its permits page confirms that permit holders should
approach the cantonal migration or employment authorities and shows the general renewal interface.

The FDFA visa guidance is the best official source located for the long-stay entry step. It states that the
national type D visa is for stays of more than 90 days and is issued subject to authorisation from the
cantonal migration authority responsible for the intended place of residence; it also warns that
processing can take several weeks or months. The FOPH/BAG health-insurance page confirms the
post-arrival obligation to take out Swiss health insurance within three months of taking residence.

F Cantonal practice notes

Geneva. Geneva’s official third-country “permit with forfait fiscal” page is the most complete canton-
level procedural source located. It requires the application to be filed before entry; sets substantive
conditions of transferring the centre of interests to Switzerland, spending the majority of the year there,
not exercising gainful activity in Switzerland other than managing one’s own wealth, and representing a
major fiscal interest for the canton attested by the Geneva tax administration; and says the file is
submitted to SEM for approval. Its checklist requires the cantonal form, passport, housing evidence or
purchase intention, the tax convention with the cantonal tax administration, criminal record, motivation
letter, written no-work/centre-of-life undertaking, CV, and civil-status documents for family members.
Geneva also publishes indicative timings: eight months for extra-European “other permits without
gainful activity”, and two to four weeks for biometric appointments. These are procedural and evidential
practice points, not federal black-letter law.

Schwyz. Schwyz’s official tax-administration note on expenditure-based taxation has unusually detailed
evidential value. It says the application should be sent to the cantonal tax administration; that the tax
administration reviews the request and makes a proposal to the cantonal government, which decides;
and that, after approval, the taxpayer must file the special expenditure-based tax return together with a
securities-and-cash schedule and a list of Swiss real estate. The annexed checklist asks for a detailed
personal and financial profile, including present and future Swiss address, date of entry, municipality of
move-in, previous Swiss stays, civil status, children, former and future professional activities, board or
organ roles, Swiss dividends, Swiss and foreign bank assets, participations, foreign and Swiss real
estate, trust interests, Swiss and foreign income, treaty-relief claims, worldwide living-expense
estimates, passport copies, any residence permit already available, powers of attorney, and property

purchase or lease contracts. This is tax-administration practice under cantonal law rather than a
national immigration checklist.

Obwalden. Obwalden’s official information sheet is less granular but still useful. It states that taxpayers
claiming lump-sum taxation must submit an application to the cantonal tax administration, together
with a brief introduction to the applicant and a rough representation of the financial situation, and that
the head of the Department of Finance makes the final decision. It also confirms annual filing after
grant. This is again a canton-level administrative practice point, but it clearly supports the proposition
that the tax-side file usually precedes or accompanies migration planning and requires financial
disclosure.

G Practical legal analysis

The procedural core of a Swiss lump-sum residence case is coordination, not mere document collection.
The tax application and the immigration application are linked by facts, but they are not the same legal
act and they are not decided by the same legal test. A tax convention or positive tax-side ruling is useful
because it evidences the fiscal interest, the likely tax base and the seriousness of the move, but official
materials do not turn it into an automatic immigration entitlement. The immigration decision remains
discretionary in non-EU/EFTA cases and is tested against public-interest, reputational and centre-of-life
considerations under SEM’s framework. That is why Geneva requires an AFC tax convention in the
migration file but still says the case goes to SEM for approval.

This also explains why “pre-ruling” is best described as a practice step rather than a freestanding legal
stage. The official cantonal sources located do not prescribe a nationally uniform negotiation protocol.
They instead evidence a canton-specific tax-administration application practice: in some cantons it
appears as a convention; in others as a written application with detailed supporting schedules. Later
drafting should therefore avoid implying a universal federal pre-ruling mechanism or a standardised
national evidence bundle.

In third-country cases, residence substance is built into the procedure from the start. SEM’s directives
require proof that the applicant will move the centre of life to Switzerland and stay there for most of the
year. Geneva operationalises that abstract standard by demanding a written undertaking, housing
evidence and motivation materials. This means that apparently “soft” documents such as a lease,
property-intention explanation, CV and motivation letter are not cosmetic; they are part of the legal
evidential case on domicile reality, admissibility and the sincerity of the intended move.

Activity analysis also belongs early in the procedural sequence, not as an afterthought. Because SEM’s
fiscal-interest guidance forbids Swiss gainful activity and permits only foreign activity plus management
of one’s own assets, the file must be internally coherent on business roles, board memberships,
remuneration and the location where functions are actually exercised. Schwyz’s official checklist is
particularly revealing here because it asks about future activity after moving, organ roles, Swiss board
activity and dividends. That is a strong signal that the tax and immigration authorities expect these
issues to be surfaced before the permit is granted, not during later compliance disputes.

Evidence of means and the sustainability of funds is another area where later drafting must stay
restrained. At federal level, EU/EFTA non-active residence clearly requires sufficient resources and
insurance. For third-country fiscally motivated cases, no single nationwide official “source of funds”
checklist was found in this research run. What the official canton materials do show is that the
authorities expect a full picture of assets, income flows, treaty-relief claims and worldwide living costs
on the tax side, and in Geneva at least a tax convention plus a broader immigration dossier on the

migration side. It is therefore safer to say that financial profile evidence is routinely and materially
relevant, but the exact contents are canton- and case-specific.

Post-approval steps should likewise be described with care. Official sources support a broad sequence:
permit filing before entry; D visa for visa-required nationals once cantonal authorisation exists; entry;
commune registration within the local deadline; biometrics and card issuance; Swiss health-insurance
enrolment within three months after taking residence; then ongoing tax and permit compliance. What
they do not support is a single national service timetable or a single card-issuance workflow across all
cantons. Geneva’s published timings are useful only as an example of canton-specific practice.

Finally, EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA procedure must be kept distinct. EU/EFTA nationals use the AFMP
economically inactive route: sufficient means, insurance, registration after three months, and a
generally five-year B permit. Non-EU/EFTA applicants rely, in the lump-sum context, on a discretionary
public-interest route involving a stricter evidential case and, where applicable, SEM approval. The same
tax regime may be relevant to both groups, but the legal architecture of admission is not the same.

H Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss lump-sum taxation as a standalone “golden visa” or investment-route
category in legal terms; official sources present it as a tax regime plus a separate residence-
permit process.
Do not state that a tax ruling, tax convention or favourable lump-sum position automatically
entitles the applicant to a residence permit. In non-EU/EFTA cases the immigration decision is
discretionary and may require SEM approval.
Do not present the non-EU/EFTA route as a uniform federal checklist. The strongest official
evidence shows canton-specific applications and evidence bundles.
Do not omit centre-of-life and majority-presence evidence. These are explicit in SEM guidance
and in Geneva’s official checklist.
Do not omit the no-gainful-activity position, and do not assume that business roles are harmless.
Official sources require scrutiny of foreign-versus-Swiss activity and preserve only management
of one’s own assets as an express exception in fiscal-interest cases.
Do not say that all applicants can arrive first and regularise later. Official federal guidance says
long-stay non-work permits should in principle be sought before entry, and visa-required
nationals need a D visa.
Do not promise fixed timeframes. Official material supports only indicative, canton-specific
timings.
Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA procedure into a single route. The former is an AFMP
non-active-person pathway; the latter is, in lump-sum cases, a discretionary public-interest/fiscal-
interest pathway.

## Open questions / limitations

A few official items were not fully extracted in this research run. In particular, the exact current text of
the EJPD approval ordinance was not separately quoted from Fedlex because of access limitations,
although SEM’s own directives clearly describe the approval system and Geneva expressly states that its
third-country lump-sum permit cases are submitted to SEM for approval. No single federal checklist
specifically labelled “source of funds” for Article 30/32 lump-sum cases was located; the evidence picture
is therefore drawn from official cantonal tax and migration guidance and from general federal non-
work residence materials.
