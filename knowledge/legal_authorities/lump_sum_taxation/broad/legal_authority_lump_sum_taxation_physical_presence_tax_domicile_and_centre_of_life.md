# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Physical Presence Tax Domicile and Centre of Life in Lump-Sum Taxation Residence Cases

## Executive legal summary

Swiss residence by lump-sum taxation does not rest on a free-standing “investor residence” rule. On the
tax side, the person must become tax resident in Switzerland under the ordinary federal rules on
personal attachment, which are built around tax domicile or qualifying tax stay. The Swiss Federal Tax
Administration explains that natural persons are unlimitedly tax liable if they have their tax domicile or
tax residence in Switzerland; tax domicile is aligned with the civil-law concept of domicile and is where
the person stays with the intention of remaining, meaning where the centre of personal and economic
interests lies. Separately, a person who remains in Switzerland for at least 30 days while exercising
gainful activity, or at least 90 days without gainful activity, is treated as resident by presence. ESTV
Circular No. 44 on expenditure-based taxation confirms that foreign nationals seeking lump-sum
taxation must take Swiss tax domicile or tax stay under Article 3 DBG, and that the regime presupposes
residence in Switzerland without Swiss gainful activity.

That means residence substance matters. The lump-sum regime changes how income tax is assessed; it
does not relax the underlying residence facts needed to establish Swiss tax liability in the first place.
Civil-law domicile remains relevant because Article 23 of the Civil Code defines domicile as the place
where a person stays with the intention of permanent residence, and it adds that domicile is not
created merely by being somewhere for education or institutional placement. Article 24 further provides
that an existing domicile continues until a new one is acquired. Those principles are reflected in the
ESTV’s explanation that the relevant place is the centre of personal and economic interests, usually
tested through family, social and economic connections rather than simple counting of nights alone.

On the immigration side, the residence permit is legally distinct from the tax position. Federal migration
guidance states that non-working third-country nationals need a permit for a stay of more than three
months, while short non-working stays of up to 90 days in 180 days are permit-free. Registration is
made with the authority responsible for the intended place of residence, and the SEM’s directives state
that the competent municipality is the one where the person’s “centre of life” is located, namely the
focus of family, social and private relationships. The same directives explain that a permit lapses not
only on deregistration but also on actual abandonment of residence, and in principle after more than
six months abroad; short return visits do not preserve the permit if the centre of life has moved abroad.

For EU/EFTA nationals, residence architecture is different but the factual residence point remains
important. SEM guidance on free movement states that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals are
entitled to a B permit if they show sufficient financial means and adequate health insurance. The same
guidance also makes clear that permits granted under the Agreement on the Free Movement of
Persons can be withdrawn or not renewed if the conditions cease to be met, and that L and B EU/EFTA
permits lapse after an uninterrupted six-month stay abroad.

The commonly repeated “183-day rule” is therefore misleading in Swiss lump-sum residence work. In
the federal materials reviewed, the operative Swiss rules are not a single 183-day test. Domestic tax law
turns on domicile and the 30/90-day tax stay thresholds; immigration law uses the 90-days-in-180-days
permit-free threshold and, for maintaining permits, a six-month abroad rule combined with an actual-
centre-of-life analysis. Treaty residence is different again: official Swiss treaty materials show the
standard approach under double tax treaties, beginning with residence under domestic law and then,
for dual residents, moving to tie-breakers such as permanent home and the state with closer personal
and economic relations. Treaty text must be checked case by case.

For lump-sum taxpayers, split-living patterns therefore create real legal risk. Federal tax and migration
guidance both emphasise the centre of life, not just a formal Swiss address or tax payment. SEM
guidance on week-residence states that neither the professional or study location nor the place where a
person happens to spend the majority of time is automatically decisive; the key question is where the
centre of domestic life lies. Recent case references cited by SEM show that authorities may also examine
objective signs of nominal residence, such as sporadic occupation of the purported Swiss home, very
low electricity consumption, minimal clothing or personal effects, and the absence of ordinary living
indicators. For internal drafting, the safest position is that a lump-sum taxed person needs a genuine
Swiss residential base and credible evidence that Switzerland is not merely a postal, tax-ruling or
permit-holding location.

## Structural overview of residence and centre-of-life issues

Tax domicile under Swiss domestic law

Under Article 3 DBG and Article 3 StHG, unlimited tax liability arises from personal attachment, namely
Swiss tax domicile or tax residence. The ESTV explains that tax domicile follows the civil-law idea of
domicile and sits where the person stays with the intention of permanent residence, meaning the
centre of personal and economic interests. Independently of domicile, a tax residence can also arise by
presence: at least 30 days in Switzerland with gainful activity, or at least 90 days without gainful activity,
ignoring short interruptions. For lump-sum taxation, Circular No. 44 expressly ties eligibility to taking
Swiss tax domicile or tax residence under Article 3 DBG.

Civil-law domicile concepts

Article 23 of the Civil Code states that a person’s domicile is where they stay with the intention of
permanent residence, and it adds that presence for education, or placement in a care institution,
hospital or penal institution, does not by itself create domicile. Article 24 adds continuity: once
established, domicile remains until a new one is acquired. Those civil-law rules matter because the ESTV
expressly reads tax domicile in light of them.

Immigration residence and actual residence expectations

For migration purposes, third-country nationals staying without gainful activity for more than three
months require a permit, and a person who needs a permit must register with the authority responsible
for their intended place of residence. SEM guidance states that the competent municipality is the one
where the person’s centre of life is located. The same guidance says that week-residence status is
acceptable only where the centre of life remains in the permit canton, and that the decisive place is the
centre of domestic life, meaning the focus of family, social and private relations. Permits lapse on
deregistration or actual abandonment of residence, generally after more than six months abroad, and
short visits back to Switzerland do not preserve the permit if the centre of life has already moved
abroad.

EU and EFTA residence and continuity

Economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals can obtain a residence permit if they have sufficient financial
means and adequate health and accident insurance. But entitlement under free movement does not

eliminate residence-substance issues. SEM guidance states that permits can be revoked or not renewed
when the conditions are no longer met, and that EU/EFTA L and B permits lapse after an uninterrupted
six-month period abroad.

Treaty residence and split-living

Domestic Swiss tax residence, immigration residence, and treaty residence are not the same question.
The ESTV notes that treaty rules can displace domestic residence consequences in international cases,
and SIF explains that double tax treaties allocate residence and taxing rights in cross-border settings.

Official Swiss treaty materials illustrate the standard tie-breaker sequence where a person is resident in
both states under domestic law: permanent home, then closer personal and economic relations,
followed by further treaty tests if necessary. Because treaty wording is state-specific, later drafting
should never assume treaty residence from Swiss domestic residence alone.

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal tax guidance for this subtopic is the ESTV’s Dossier Steuerinformationen -
Subjektive Steuerpflicht. It explains tax domicile, the centre of personal and economic interests, the
30-day and 90-day physical-presence rules, the start and end of unlimited tax liability, and the treatment
of divided living arrangements such as week-residence. It is not a substitute for statute, but it is a
reliable official synthesis of the federal framework and the leading case law patterns.

For lump-sum taxation specifically, the key tax guidance is ESTV Circular No. 44 of 24 July 2018. For
present purposes, its importance lies less in the calculation mechanics and more in its confirmation that
expenditure-based taxation applies only where the taxpayer is a qualifying foreign national who takes
Swiss tax domicile or tax stay under Article 3 DBG and performs no Swiss gainful activity. That guidance
therefore assumes, rather than replaces, ordinary domestic residence analysis.

On migration matters, the most important source is the SEM Weisungen und Erläuterungen I.
Ausländerbereich. The material reviewed clarifies that registration is tied to the municipality where the
centre of life lies; that week-resident treatment depends on retaining that centre of life; and that
permits lapse when Swiss residence is actually given up, usually including cases where the centre of life
moves abroad and the person is no longer predominantly based in Switzerland over the course of the
year. The same guidance also states that brief visits back to Switzerland do not interrupt the six-month
rule if the centre of life has moved abroad.

For EU/EFTA nationals, the central source is the SEM Weisungen VFP together with SEM’s public pages
on the B permit EU/EFTA and the FAQ on non-working residence. These materials explain sufficient
means and insurance, the Swiss-wide effect of EU/EFTA permits, and the six-month lapse rule for
uninterrupted absence abroad. The VFP guidance also records recent case-law based indicators of sham
or merely sporadic Swiss residence, such as low electricity use and minimal personal belongings.

For treaty residence, the official administrative anchor is the SIF DBA overview page, which explains
that treaties allocate residence and taxing rights where international connecting factors exist. In
addition, official Swiss treaty materials reviewed here show the standard structure of residence articles
and tie-breakers. That matters because a person may be domestically tax resident in Switzerland and
yet still face a separate treaty-residence analysis.

## Cantonal practice notes

Reliable canton-specific official material on physical presence, split-living and centre-of-life
standards in lump-sum cases was limited in the reviewed sources. No official cantonal source was
identified that clearly relaxes the federal domicile and actual-residence framework for lump-sum
taxpayers. That itself is an important drafting point: later content should not suggest that canton
practice overrides the federal architecture on residence substance.

Geneva. The Geneva official pages on imposition d’après la dépense explain the local tax-base approach,
confirm that the regime is intended for foreigners staying in Switzerland without gainful activity, and
state that the request should be made on arrival in Switzerland or, at the latest, before the first
assessment becomes final. Geneva also publishes indexed minimums for 2026. These materials are
useful as procedural and explanatory evidence, but they do not create a special Geneva-only rule on
what counts as a sufficient Swiss centre of life.

Vaud. A Vaud State Council publication states that Vaud applies expenditure-based taxation strictly
within the federal and cantonal legal framework. That is helpful as a practice signal, but it is not a
detailed doctrinal source on domicile or split-living in this route. No official Vaud material was identified
in the reviewed sources establishing a simplified “183-day” test or a lighter residence-substance
threshold for lump-sum taxpayers.

## Practical legal analysis

In substance, the tax and immigration systems are asking related but distinct questions. Tax law asks
whether the person is resident for Swiss tax purposes through domicile or qualifying stay. Immigration
law asks whether the person has, and maintains, a lawful basis for residence in Switzerland. Neither
system is displaced by the fact that the taxpayer may pay substantial lump-sum tax. Circular No. 44
assumes the person has become Swiss-resident under Article 3 DBG; SEM’s migration directives require
registration where the centre of life is located and treat actual abandonment of Swiss residence as
permit-ending conduct. A tax ruling therefore does not eliminate the need for residence facts; it sits on
top of them.

That is why the familiar “183 days” shorthand is a poor drafting shortcut. Swiss domestic tax residence
in the reviewed materials does not turn on one annual day-count. Instead, federal tax law looks first to

domicile and then to the 30-day/90-day presence thresholds. Migration law uses a separate 90-days-
in-180-days rule for permit-free stays, and a different six-month abroad rule for permit maintenance.
Those are distinct tests serving distinct legal purposes. A person may therefore satisfy one threshold yet
still fail another, particularly if the underlying centre of life remains abroad.

Split-living cases are especially sensitive. Both ESTV and SEM emphasise the centre of life or the centre
of personal and economic interests. ESTV states that when work and family are in different places, the
decisive domicile is generally where the person’s stronger personal, family, social or economic ties are
located; for married taxpayers who regularly return to the family home, family ties generally prevail,
while special facts can shift the analysis. SEM’s week-residence guidance similarly says that profession,
study or even the place where the person spends most time is not automatically decisive; the decisive
place is where the domestic centre of life lies.

In practice, that means a lump-sum taxpayer’s evidence file matters. The legal sources repeatedly point
back to family, social and private relations. From those official tests, one can safely infer that authorities
will care about where the spouse and minor children actually live, where children attend school,
whether there is a real and lived-in Swiss home rather than serviced accommodation used sporadically,
how often the person actually returns there, and whether the person’s day-to-day private life is
genuinely organised from Switzerland. This is not a new legal test; it is an evidential inference from the
statutory and administrative focus on the centre of life.

For that reason, Switzerland being used merely as a nominal base is dangerous. SEM’s VFP guidance,
citing recent case law, records concrete indicators of an asserted home being only sporadically used:
low electricity consumption, very little clothing or few personal belongings, and the lack of ordinary
perishable food. Those examples are highly relevant for lump-sum taxpayers with multiple residences.
They show that authorities may test not only legal paperwork but also whether the Swiss home looks
like a real place of life.

Business links require equally careful treatment. Even where gainful activity itself is excluded or tightly
limited in a lump-sum structure, the centre-of-life inquiry still takes account of where economic
interests are mainly organised. The ESTV states that tax domicile is where both personal and economic
interests are centred. If the taxpayer’s operative business life, management routines and core
professional presence remain abroad while Switzerland functions only as an address plus tax
assessment, the domestic and treaty-residence analysis becomes more fragile. That is particularly true
if daily life indicators also point abroad.

Treaty residence needs a separate step. A person may be Swiss-resident domestically for tax purposes
and still have dual residence under foreign domestic law. SIF’s official treaty materials show the
standard way treaties then resolve the problem: they typically start with permanent home and then ask
where the closer personal and economic relations lie. In later drafting, “Swiss tax resident” should
therefore never be used as if it automatically answers treaty residence. The treaty question may
produce a different answer, or at least require its own tie-breaker analysis.

For internal content generation, the safest synthesis is therefore this: expenditure taxation does not
lower the legal standard for genuine residence substance. The person still needs a credible Swiss tax
domicile or qualifying Swiss tax stay; a migration file that supports the claimed Swiss centre of life; and,
where the person keeps a strong foreign footprint, a separate treaty-residence analysis. Where
Switzerland is only nominally used, the system is vulnerable on both tax and immigration sides.

## Drafting cautions

Do not write as though Swiss lump-sum taxation creates residence automatically. The tax regime
presupposes ordinary Swiss tax residence under Article 3 DBG rather than replacing it.
Do not describe “183 days” as the legal Swiss test for this route. The reviewed federal sources
instead use domicile, 30/90-day tax-stay thresholds, 90-days-in-180 permit-free stay, and six-
month permit-lapse rules.
Do not equate tax residence, immigration residence and treaty residence. They are legally
distinct inquiries and can diverge.
Do not imply that owning or renting Swiss property is enough by itself. Civil-law and tax-domicile
rules focus on intention to remain and the centre of personal and economic interests.
Do not ignore family, social and private ties. SEM and ESTV both treat the centre of life or centre
of interests as the decisive framework.
Do not present week-residence, split living or heavy travel as inherently harmless. The legal
question is where the domestic centre of life truly remains.
Do not say that brief return trips to Switzerland preserve a permit if the person’s centre of life
has already shifted abroad. SEM guidance says they do not.
Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA analysis. The legal route differs, but EU/EFTA permits
still have ongoing means, insurance and continuity conditions.
Do not state treaty residence outcomes without checking the actual treaty. Swiss official treaty
materials show the general tie-breaker logic, but treaty text remains state-specific.
Do not understate evidential risk where Switzerland seems only notional. Official migration
guidance records objective warning signs such as sporadic occupancy, low electricity use and
minimal personal effects.
