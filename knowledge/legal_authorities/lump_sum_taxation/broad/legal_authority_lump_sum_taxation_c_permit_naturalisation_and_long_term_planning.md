# Swiss Lump-Sum Taxation Residence Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

For a person who has obtained, or plans to obtain, Swiss residence linked to expenditure-based taxation, the long-term legal pathway is not a separate “investment immigration” or “golden visa” track. The tax regime and the immigration status remain distinct. On the immigration side, the person ordinarily holds a residence permit rather than a settlement permit at the outset; under Swiss immigration law, the residence permit is purpose-linked, time-limited and renewable, whereas the settlement permit is the later, more stable status. On the citizenship side, ordinary naturalisation is a further and separate step which requires a settlement permit, residence periods and successful integration. The statutory and official materials reviewed do not disclose any fast-track citizenship route based merely on wealth, tax contribution or lump-sum taxation.<sup>1</sup>

For non-EU/EFTA nationals, this matters because lump-sum cases typically begin with a B permit obtained under immigration rules that are discretionary and separate from the tax ruling. Long-term planning should therefore assume that the person must maintain a genuine Swiss residence, satisfy renewal requirements and later meet the conditions for settlement. For EU/EFTA nationals, the architecture is different: economically inactive persons can obtain a B EU/EFTA permit if they show sufficient financial means and adequate health and accident insurance, and the permit is normally valid for five years and renewed if the conditions continue to be met. However, even for EU/EFTA nationals, the settlement permit is not created by the Free Movement of Persons Agreement itself; the SEM expressly states that settlement permits for EU/EFTA nationals are governed by the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and settlement treaties or reciprocal arrangements, not by the AFMP.<sup>2</sup>

A C permit is possible, but not automatic. The core federal rule is ten years’ residence in Switzerland with a short-stay or residence permit, with the last five years held continuously with a residence permit, no relevant revocation grounds, and integration. There is also an early-settlement possibility after five uninterrupted years with a residence permit where the person satisfies the same public-order and integration criteria and can demonstrate a higher language level. Official materials show that integration is not reduced to paid employment: participation in economic life can be satisfied through income, assets or legally enforceable third-party support. For lump-sum taxpayers who are deliberately not working in Switzerland, that is an important point. But wealth does not displace the other requirements: public order, constitutional values, language and real residence still matter.<sup>3</sup>

Ordinary naturalisation is again a separate statutory step. The applicant must hold a C permit at the time of filing and must show ten years of residence in Switzerland, with three of those years falling within the five years before the application. Time spent in Switzerland between ages eight and eighteen counts double, with a minimum actual residence of six years. Federal law then requires successful integration, familiarity with Swiss ways of life and no threat to Switzerland’s internal or external security. The citizenship ordinance sets a higher federal language threshold for ordinary naturalisation than for early settlement: B1 oral and A2 written in a national language. It also requires participation in economic life or education, although, again, that requirement can be met through assets rather than employment. Cantons may add further integration criteria, and cantonal law must impose a local residence requirement of between two and five years.<sup>4</sup>

For long-term tax planning, the critical point is that settlement does not, by itself, terminate expenditure-based taxation. The federal tax rules focus on nationality, first-time or qualifying return residence, and the absence of Swiss gainful activity. Nothing in the tax provisions reviewed makes the tax regime incompatible with a C permit as such. By contrast, Swiss citizenship is fatal to lump-sum taxation eligibility: the Federal Tax Administration’s circular states that only foreign nationals can claim the regime and that the right ends when the taxpayer acquires Swiss citizenship, with ordinary income taxation applying for the entire tax period in which naturalisation occurs. The circular also states that in an intact marriage, if one spouse acquires Swiss citizenship or one spouse takes up gainful activity in Switzerland, the right to expenditure-based taxation is lost for both spouses.<sup>5</sup>

Residence continuity and factual residence substance remain central throughout. A residence permit lapses on deregistration abroad or at expiry; if the foreign national leaves Switzerland without deregistering, a B permit lapses after six months abroad and a C permit also lapses after six months abroad, although the C permit can be maintained for up to four years on request. Temporary visits back to Switzerland do not interrupt that six-month period. At the citizenship stage, local residence requirements can become quite practical and restrictive: for example, Zurich’s official guidance makes the process municipality-sensitive and states that moving too early can end the pending process, while Zug imposes specific cantonal and communal residence periods and, from January 2025, stricter cantonal citizenship standards than the federal minimum. For lump-sum taxpayers who are internationally mobile, that makes genuine Swiss centre-of-life planning a legal necessity rather than a presentational nicety.<sup>6</sup>

## Structural overview of long-term planning

### Starting point under the lump-sum route

A classic lump-sum taxation case starts with Swiss tax eligibility under expenditure-based taxation and a time-limited residence permission, not with settlement or citizenship. Swiss immigration law treats the residence permit as a permit for stays of more than one year, issued for a specific purpose and renewable if no revocation grounds exist. Ordinary naturalisation, by contrast, is only open later to a foreign national who already holds a C permit.<sup>7</sup>

### Settlement permit planning

The general federal settlement rule is ten years’ residence in Switzerland, including the last five years continuously with a residence permit, together with integration and the absence of relevant revocation grounds. Early settlement after five uninterrupted years is possible, but only where the integration criteria are met and the applicant can show stronger language competence. Official immigration materials reviewed for this pack therefore support a planning model in which a lump-sum taxpayer should expect B-permit years first, with C-permit eligibility assessed later rather than presumed from the outset.<sup>8</sup>

### Nationality distinctions

For EU/EFTA nationals without gainful activity, the legal architecture is materially easier at the residence stage: sufficient means and insurance support a B EU/EFTA permit, and certain EU/EFTA nationalities benefit from five-year settlement timing under settlement treaties or reciprocity. The SEM also states that the AFMP itself does not regulate settlement permits. For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the public official materials reviewed do not offer an equivalent treaty-based public summary for five-year settlement in lump-sum cases, so planning should conservatively assume the ordinary AIG framework unless a nationality-specific official basis is separately verified.<sup>9</sup>

### Integration and language

Integration is a legal criterion both for settlement and for ordinary naturalisation. Under the AIG, it includes public order, constitutional values, language and participation in economic life or education. Under the citizenship legislation, it also includes familiarity with Swiss life and support for the integration of close family members. Language thresholds tighten as one moves from early settlement to naturalisation.<sup>10</sup>

### Residence continuity and centre of life

Long-term planning is vulnerable to interruption by absences and mobility. A B or C permit can lapse after time abroad, and ordinary naturalisation also requires residence counting rules that depend on permit type and actual residence history. Cantonal naturalisation procedures can further depend on stable local residence, including at municipal level.<sup>11</sup>

### Tax transition planning

Obtaining a C permit does not, on the materials reviewed, in itself end lump-sum taxation. Acquiring Swiss citizenship does. A lump-sum taxpayer who ultimately naturalises must therefore plan for a transition to ordinary taxation in the tax year of naturalisation. Separate trigger events can also end the regime earlier, including Swiss gainful activity or a spouse’s acquisition of Swiss citizenship in an intact marriage.<sup>5</sup>

## Primary legal texts

### Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and implementing ordinance

**Article 33 AIG, Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration.** This provision defines the residence permit for stays of more than one year, states that it is issued for a specific purpose, and confirms that it is time-limited and renewable if no revocation grounds exist. For lump-sum taxpayers, this is the legal starting point for longer-term residence planning before settlement becomes available.<sup>23</sup>

**Article 34 AIG.** This is the central settlement-permit provision. It states that the settlement permit is granted for an unlimited period and without conditions; that it may be granted after ten years’ residence with the last five years held continuously on a residence permit, absent relevant revocation grounds and with integration; and that it may be granted earlier after five uninterrupted years where the same conditions are satisfied and the person can communicate well in the local national language. It also contains a clause allowing grant after a shorter stay for important reasons, but the public official materials reviewed do not clearly operationalise that clause for lump-sum taxpayers, so it should be handled cautiously.<sup>13</sup>

**Article 58a AIG.** This provision codifies the immigration-law integration criteria: respect for public safety and order, respect for constitutional values, language competence, and participation in economic life or education. For long-term planning, it is the hinge between B-permit renewals, C-permit eligibility and potential downgrading risk.<sup>15</sup>

**Articles 61-63 AIG.** Article 61 provides for lapse of permits, including on deregistration abroad and, if the person leaves Switzerland without deregistering, after three months for short-stay permits and six months for residence and settlement permits; on request, a C permit may be maintained for up to four years. Articles 62 and 63 govern revocation of B and C permits, including for serious public-order breaches, non-compliance with conditions, and social assistance dependence; Article 63(2) also allows downgrading from C to B if integration criteria are no longer met. These provisions matter because a lump-sum taxpayer’s long-term plan can fail even after obtaining settlement if residence continuity or integration breaks down.<sup>24</sup>

**Article 62 VZAE.** The Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment sets the detailed threshold for early settlement: integration criteria must be met, the applicant must prove at least B1 oral and A1 written in the local national language, and the integration degree of family members over twelve is also considered.<sup>25</sup>

**Article 77d VZAE and Article 77e VZAE.** Article 77d regulates how language competence is proved for immigration purposes. Article 77e is critical for non-working HNWI cases because it provides that participation in economic life exists where the person covers living costs and maintenance obligations through income, assets or enforceable third-party benefits. That allows wealthy non-working residents to satisfy the “economic participation” limb without Swiss employment.<sup>26</sup>

**Article 79 VZAE.** This ordinance provision clarifies that the six-month period for lapse of a settlement permit after departure is not interrupted by temporary visit, tourism or business stays in Switzerland, and that the request to maintain the C permit must be filed before the six-month period expires.<sup>27</sup>

### Swiss Citizenship Act and Citizenship Ordinance

**Article 9 BüG, Federal Act on Swiss Citizenship.** Ordinary naturalisation at federal level requires a C permit at filing and ten years’ residence in Switzerland, three of them in the five years before the application. Residence between ages eight and eighteen counts double, subject to a six-year minimum actual stay. This provision is the core gatekeeping rule for lump-sum taxpayers considering citizenship.<sup>28</sup>

**Articles 11 and 12 BüG.** These are the principal substantive naturalisation criteria. They require successful integration, familiarity with Swiss ways of life and no threat to internal or external security. Article 12 then spells out the integration indicators, including public order, constitutional values, language, economic participation or education, and support for family integration. Cantons may legislate additional integration criteria.<sup>18</sup>

**Article 13 BüG.** This provision shows that ordinary naturalisation is a multi-level process. The canton designates the filing authority; after cantonal review, the file is sent to SEM; and SEM issues the federal naturalisation authorisation if the statutory conditions are fulfilled. This is one reason why a strong tax contribution does not itself create a citizenship entitlement.<sup>29</sup>

**Article 18 BüG.** Federal law requires cantonal legislation to impose a cantonal and communal residence requirement of between two and five years. This is why canton choice at the residence-permit stage can affect eventual naturalisation timing and mobility constraints.<sup>20</sup>

**Articles 2, 4, 6, 7, 8 and 9 BüV, Citizenship Ordinance.** Article 2 explains what “familiarity with Swiss life” means in practice. Article 4 defines failures to respect public order. Article 6 sets the federal naturalisation language threshold at B1 oral and A2 written. Article 7 states that participation in economic life can be met through income, assets or enforceable support and denies the criterion where social assistance has been received in the three years before filing or during the procedure unless fully repaid. Article 8 concerns support for the integration of family members. Article 9 allows accommodation of compelling personal circumstances. Together, these provisions show that a lump-sum taxpayer can satisfy the economic-participation criterion without employment, but not without language, public-order compliance and broader integration.<sup>30</sup>

### Direct Federal Tax Act and Tax Harmonisation Act

**Article 14 DBG, Federal Act on Direct Federal Tax.** This is the federal expenditure-based taxation provision. It allows natural persons to pay tax by reference to expenditure rather than ordinary income tax if they are not Swiss citizens, are becoming Swiss tax resident for the first time or after at least ten years’ absence, and do not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland. It also sets the federal minimum framework for the tax base and treaty-related modified calculation. For long-term planning, the key point is that the conditions concern nationality and activity, not permit category.<sup>31</sup>

**Article 6 StHG, Tax Harmonisation Act.** This provision harmonises the cantonal regime. It allows cantons to grant expenditure-based taxation in place of income and wealth tax to foreign nationals meeting the same basic eligibility criteria, while leaving cantons to determine the minimum base and the way wealth tax is absorbed. It matters because a person can remain a lump-sum taxpayer at cantonal level only while still meeting the foreign-nationality condition.<sup>32</sup>

### EU/EFTA treaty architecture

An AFMP treaty provision relevant to economically inactive persons is part of the route architecture for EU/EFTA nationals, but the exact treaty wording was not separately extracted for this pack. What the official SEM materials do state clearly is that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals can obtain a B EU/EFTA permit if they have sufficient means and insurance, while settlement permits for EU/EFTA nationals are governed by the AIG and settlement treaties or reciprocity, because the AFMP itself contains no settlement-permit rules. That distinction is sufficient for the present pack’s long-term-planning purpose.<sup>33</sup>

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

### Official federal guidance and administrative materials

**SEM guidance on integration requirements and language.** The SEM’s integration page restates the four AIG integration criteria and notes that cantonal migration authorities decide on granting, renewing and revoking permits. The SEM’s language page states that language proof is required for residence permits, settlement permits and ordinary and early naturalisation, and that the federal levels are minimum requirements.<sup>34</sup>

**SEM guidance on ordinary naturalisation.** The SEM’s ordinary-naturalisation page and FAQ confirm the need for a C permit, ten years’ residence, and the counting rules for B, C, F, N and L permits. The FAQ also summarises the practical federal integration expectations, including B1 oral / A2 written language, public-order compliance, paid taxes, no social assistance in the prior three years unless repaid, and social and cultural participation.<sup>35</sup>

**SEM citizenship handbook material.** The SEM’s handbook appendix describes ordinary naturalisation as the “last integration step” and explains that the C permit is required in order to ensure coherence between nationality law and immigration law. It also notes the interaction between EU/EFTA settlement practice and immigration law.<sup>36</sup>

**SEM residence guidance for EU/EFTA nationals.** The SEM’s official pages on B EU/EFTA and C EU/EFTA permits clarify that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals may hold a five-year B permit if they can show sufficient means and insurance, and that certain listed EU/EFTA nationalities may receive C permits after five years under settlement treaties or reciprocity.<sup>9</sup>

**ESTV Circular No. 44 on expenditure-based taxation.** This is the main official tax-administrative guidance used in this pack. It confirms that only foreign nationals may claim the regime, that the right ends when Swiss citizenship is acquired, that ordinary taxation applies for the whole tax year of naturalisation, and that in an intact marriage a spouse’s Swiss citizenship or Swiss gainful activity can end the regime for both spouses. It also confirms that a person who voluntarily abandons expenditure-based taxation and returns to ordinary taxation generally cannot later revert to lump-sum taxation.<sup>37</sup>

### Cantonal practice notes

**Canton of Zurich.** Zurich’s official ordinary-naturalisation page states that an applicant must have lived at least two years in the same municipality before filing. It also states that the overall process takes around two years and that, if the applicant moves before the municipal investigation is complete, the pending process stops and a new application can be filed only after two years in the new municipality. This is a procedural and evidential point, not a federal rule, but it is highly relevant for globally mobile lump-sum taxpayers.<sup>38</sup>

**Canton of Zug.** Zug’s official page states that, for ordinary naturalisation, the applicant must have a C permit, ten years in Switzerland, five years in the canton and three uninterrupted years in the municipality. It also states that, for applications filed from 1 January 2025, Zug has tightened its cantonal criteria, including a five-year look-back for social assistance and language levels of B2 oral / B1 written. These are cantonal citizenship rules going beyond the federal floor and should not be presented as Swiss-wide law.<sup>39</sup>

## Practical legal analysis

The central planning point is that a residence-by-lump-sum-taxation case matures through ordinary Swiss immigration and nationality law rather than through a bespoke HNWI citizenship route. The tax ruling helps to support the broader residence project, but it does not convert into settlement or citizenship by itself. In legal terms, the resident moves from a B permit, if it is granted and renewed, to a possible C permit, and only then to possible ordinary naturalisation. Each stage has its own statutory tests and its own authority structure.<sup>40</sup>

For non-working HNWI residents, the most useful statutory point is that Swiss immigration and nationality law do not insist on salaried work as the only way to demonstrate participation in economic life. Official ordinance text on both the immigration and citizenship side recognises that living costs and maintenance obligations can be met through assets. That means a genuine lump-sum taxpayer is not structurally excluded from C-permit or naturalisation planning simply because he or she does not work in Switzerland. But that advantage should not be overstated. The same laws also require real integration, language acquisition, public-order compliance and stable residence history. A passive financial profile is not enough on its own.<sup>41</sup>

From a status-planning perspective, the C permit is the decisive threshold. It is the immigration system’s most stable status and the citizenship system’s express gateway. Yet it remains vulnerable. It can lapse through prolonged absence from Switzerland and can be revoked or downgraded for serious public-order issues, heavy social-assistance dependence or integration failure. For internationally mobile families, prolonged foreign residence, deregistration or “nominal” Swiss residence can therefore undermine the entire long-term plan even if the person remains fiscally attractive.<sup>42</sup>

Naturalisation planning then adds a second layer of difficulty. Federal law sets the minimum: C permit, residence duration, integration, language and familiarity with Swiss life. But cantons and communes remain central to procedure and may impose stricter local criteria or local residence periods. The Zurich and Zug materials illustrate the risk. Initial canton choice is therefore not only a tax and residence-permit question; it can materially affect later naturalisation timing, mobility flexibility, language expectations and the administrative burden of the citizenship process.<sup>43</sup>

On the tax side, the decisive transition point is Swiss citizenship, not settlement. A foreign national may, in principle, continue expenditure-based taxation after obtaining a C permit if the tax-law conditions still hold, because the federal tax provisions are framed around foreign nationality, qualifying residence history and no Swiss gainful activity, not around permit category. By contrast, once Swiss citizenship is acquired, expenditure-based taxation falls away. In practice, that means sophisticated long-term planning should model the tax cost of ordinary taxation before filing for naturalisation, rather than treating citizenship as a purely immigration milestone.<sup>21</sup>

The same is true for family planning and life changes. The FTA’s circular makes clear that in an intact marriage, a spouse’s Swiss citizenship or Swiss gainful activity can terminate expenditure-based taxation for both spouses. The circular also states that a voluntary exit from lump-sum taxation will generally prevent later return to it. So the move from residence planning to settlement planning and then to naturalisation planning can have irreversible tax consequences. This is not a route that becomes more flexible with time; in some respects it becomes less reversible.<sup>37</sup>

The route should therefore be described, internally and externally, as follows: Swiss lump-sum taxation may support Swiss residence for qualifying persons, but longer-term progression to C permit and citizenship depends on ordinary settlement and naturalisation law, on real residence and integration, and on a deliberate tax transition strategy. That is materially different from a residence-for-investment or citizenship-for-investment programme.<sup>44</sup>

## Open questions and limitations

The official sources reviewed clearly support the EU/EFTA settlement-treaty framework listed by SEM, but they do not safely support a public statement in this pack about current operational five-year C-permit treatment for all historically treaty-linked non-EU nationalities. That point should be checked separately if a future draft needs to address a specific nationality such as a US or Canadian citizen.

The AFMP route for economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals was addressed here through current SEM and ch.ch official materials. The exact treaty wording of the relevant AFMP annex provision was not separately extracted in this pack.

No official cantonal materials were found that directly explain long-term C-permit or naturalisation planning specifically for lump-sum taxpayers as a category. The cantonal notes therefore focus on general citizenship practice in selected cantons, which is still highly relevant for HNWI planning.

## Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss lump-sum taxation residence as a Swiss “golden visa” or as a citizenship-by-investment route. The reviewed legal framework points instead to ordinary immigration and nationality pathways layered on top of a distinct tax regime.<sup>7</sup>

Do not imply that a tax ruling, high tax payment or substantial private wealth creates a legal entitlement to a C permit or to Swiss citizenship. Settlement and naturalisation remain governed by statutory residence, integration and procedural requirements.<sup>45</sup>

Do not say that obtaining a C permit automatically ends lump-sum taxation. On the materials reviewed, that is not the rule; Swiss citizenship, by contrast, clearly ends the regime.<sup>5</sup>

Do not ignore language and integration. For many lump-sum taxpayers, the real bottleneck is not assets but the accumulation of language, local ties and evidentially credible integration over time.<sup>46</sup>

Do not collapse EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA cases into one pathway. Economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals benefit from a treaty-based residence architecture, while settlement for EU/EFTA nationals is expressly said by SEM not to arise from the AFMP itself; non-EU/EFTA cases should be analysed conservatively under the AIG unless a nationality-specific official basis is separately verified.<sup>33</sup>

Do not assume that ten years in Switzerland automatically means immediate naturalisation. A C permit is required at filing, cantonal and communal residence periods apply, and cantons may impose stricter local standards.<sup>47</sup>

Do not understate absence and mobility risks. Permit lapse after time abroad and local residence rules in cantonal naturalisation procedures can derail an otherwise strong HNWI profile.<sup>48</sup>

Do not forget the tax-year consequence of naturalisation. Once Swiss citizenship is acquired, ordinary taxation applies for the entire tax period in which naturalisation occurs.<sup>37</sup>
