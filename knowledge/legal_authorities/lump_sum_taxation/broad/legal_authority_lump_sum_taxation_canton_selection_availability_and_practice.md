# Swiss Lump Sum Taxation Canton Selection Availability and Cantonal Practice Pack

This pack is confined to canton selection, cantonal availability, and canton-level practice for Swiss residence cases linked to expenditure-based taxation. It is written for internal legal-content use, not client publication. Amounts and practice points are tax-year specific. Several cantons publish indexed amounts annually, and some summary pages lag behind the latest indexed figures; where that occurs, this pack prioritises the most current official canton sheet, indexation page, ordinance, or federal tariff update located in this research session.

## Executive legal summary

Swiss expenditure-based taxation is not a single national “residence by investment” regime. At federal level, the Direct Federal Tax Act provides a regime of taxation according to expenditure for qualifying foreign nationals who are newly tax resident in Switzerland and do not exercise gainful activity in Switzerland; for tax year 2026, the indexed federal minimum amount under the federal rule is CHF 435,000. At cantonal and communal level, however, the legal position depends on cantonal implementation under the Tax Harmonisation Act and the canton’s own legislation, regulations and practice. The Federal Department of Finance expressly states that the regime is implemented differently by canton and identifies five cantons that have abolished it at cantonal level: Zurich, Schaffhausen, Appenzell Ausserrhoden, Basel-Landschaft and Basel-Stadt.

Canton choice therefore matters in at least four separate ways. First, it determines whether the classic cantonal and communal expenditure-tax route is available at all. Secondly, even where available, the published cantonal floors differ materially. Current official materials located in this session show, for example, CHF 250,000 in Fribourg and Valais, CHF 400,000 in Bern, CHF 415,000 in Vaud, CHF 426,357 in Geneva for cantonal and communal tax in 2026, CHF 434,700 in Ticino, CHF 500,000 in Zug, CHF 600,000 in Schwyz, and CHF 647,100 in Lucerne; Thurgau publishes a different model centred on agreed factors, lifestyle costs, a control calculation, and a minimum annual tax burden of CHF 150,000. These figures are not perfectly comparable because some are minimum tax bases, some include a wealth-tax loading, and some are minimum annual tax liabilities rather than bases.

Thirdly, canton choice affects the municipal layer. The Federal Tax Administration notes that most cantons levy tax through a system of tax rates and tax multipliers or tax feet, and that cantons and municipalities have their own rates and multipliers. Several cantonal materials also show that expenditure-based taxation is not merely a cantonal tax issue but a cantonal-and-communal tax issue: Ticino refers expressly to the cantonal coefficient and commune taxes; Valais describes separate cantonal and communal levies; Geneva publishes separate indexed cantonal and communal minima; and Vaud’s system operates with cantonal and communal taxation under ordinary-rate mechanics. In practice, this means that commune selection inside a viable canton can materially alter the overall burden.

Fourthly, canton choice affects administrative handling and migration viability. The tax regime and the residence permit are distinct, but some cantons have built coordinated procedures around them. Geneva instructs incoming applicants to pursue the tax request in parallel with the cantonal population and migration office, and requires a written request, a proposed taxable amount, evidence of accommodation, and a lifestyle form. Valais publishes a form expressly for “information for the tax and migration authorities with a view to obtaining a residence permit”. Appenzell Innerrhoden publishes a combined leaflet on “taxation according to expenditure and permit process”, with a staged file review involving the tax administration, the Office for Economic Affairs, the economic promotion commission, the cantonal interior authority and, for third-country nationals, SEM. Lucerne publicly states that, for non-EU/EFTA persons, a tax burden of at least CHF 400,000 across direct federal, cantonal and communal taxes is treated in that canton as quantifying a substantial fiscal interest for immigration purposes.

For non-EU/EFTA cases, canton selection must also be read against SEM guidance on Article 32 VZAE. The SEM guidance states that these are discretionary “may” cases, that applicants must show they are relocating their centre of life to Switzerland and will reside in Switzerland for the majority of the time, and that in cases admitted because of significant cantonal fiscal interests any gainful activity may be carried on only abroad, save for management of one’s own assets. The same SEM materials also emphasise that fiscal interests must be weighed against the public interest in Switzerland’s reputation. That makes “easy” or purely nominal canton selection legally unsafe.

The principal drafting consequence is that later content must never discuss Swiss lump-sum taxation as if every canton were available or as if a single national minimum applied. The legal route is a federal-cantonal architecture. Availability, current indexed amounts, whether the published number is a tax base or tax burden, the commune multiplier, the formality of the ruling process, and the canton’s migration posture all have to be checked canton by canton.

## Framework and propositions

### Structural overview of cantonal availability and practice

Under the federal framework, expenditure-based taxation remains part of Swiss federal tax law and sits within a harmonised cantonal architecture. The Federal Tax Administration’s cantonal gazette page explains that cantonal gazettes focus on special tax provisions and the associated cantonal legal rules that fall within cantonal competence under the Tax Harmonisation Act. In other words, federal law sets the framework, but cantons retain important implementation space.

That implementation space has been used differently. The Federal Department of Finance states that the procedure is implemented differently by canton and names five cantons that have abolished the regime at cantonal level: Zurich, Schaffhausen, Appenzell Ausserrhoden, Basel-Landschaft and Basel-Stadt. The same federal source notes that Bern, St. Gallen, Lucerne and Thurgau chose to retain the regime but tighten it. For internal drafting, that means the first canton-selection question is binary: available or not available at cantonal and communal level.

Among available cantons, the second question is what the published entry point actually is. Current official sources show large divergences: Fribourg and Valais publish CHF 250,000 cantonal minima; Bern publishes CHF 400,000 for cantonal and communal tax; Vaud publishes CHF 415,000 including a 15% loading covering wealth tax; Geneva publishes an indexed 2026 cantonal and communal minimum of CHF 426,357 and a federal minimum of CHF 435,000; Ticino publishes CHF 434,700; Zug publishes CHF 500,000; Schwyz publishes CHF 600,000; Lucerne publishes CHF 647,100; and Thurgau publishes a practice model using agreed factors together with a minimum annual tax burden of CHF 150,000 instead of a simple published floor. These numbers must not be compared without first identifying whether they describe a base, a loaded base, or a minimum tax bill.

The third question is the commune. The FTA states that cantons and municipalities maintain their own rates and tax multipliers. Ticino’s gazette explains that the cantonal base tax is used for the application of the cantonal coefficient and for computation of commune taxes; Valais levies a separate communal tax under the ordinary tariffs on an average-rate basis; Vaud and Geneva likewise treat the cantonal and communal sphere together in the expenditure-tax framework. Strategic canton selection therefore requires commune selection as a second-stage exercise.

The fourth question is procedural and migratory rather than purely fiscal. Some cantons publish only black-letter tax rules; others publish administrative paths that show how the tax and migration files interact. Geneva, Valais, Lucerne and Appenzell Innerrhoden all publish material that materially informs route planning. Where no public current procedural note was located in this session, later drafting should avoid presenting hidden local practice as if it were settled law.

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

### Official guidance and administrative materials

At federal level, the most useful current official overview is the EFD dossier on taxation according to expenditure. It confirms the nature of the regime, says implementation differs by canton, lists the cantons that abolished it, records the legislative tightening that entered into force at cantonal level in 2014 and federal direct-tax level in 2016, and notes that persons already under the regime at the time of reform were subject to transitional rules until the end of 2020.

The key federal interpretive text remains ESTV Circular No. 44 on direct federal tax. Its current searchable extract states the federal legal basis, repeats the core eligibility conditions, confirms the spouse rule, and notes the end of the transition period. The ESTV’s form-and-guidance materials for the federal expenditure-tax return are also useful for operational points: they describe the federal legal basis, the requirement to file a specific return, the annual declaration of living costs, and the control-calculation structure.

For cantonal differentiation, the FTA’s cantonal gazettes are particularly valuable. The FTA states that these gazettes focus on special tax provisions falling within cantonal competence under the Tax Harmonisation Act, and the FTA announced in March 2026 that the gazettes had been updated to reflect the position as at 1 January 2026. For this pack, those gazettes were especially useful for Geneva, Vaud, Valais, Ticino, Fribourg and Zug.

For commune effects, the FTA page on Swiss tax feet, deductions and tariffs explains that most cantons compute taxes using tax rates and tax feet and that cantons and municipalities each have their own. That general federal point is confirmed at canton level by Ticino’s explanation of the cantonal coefficient and calculation of commune taxes, and by Valais’s separate treatment of communal tax on an average-rate basis.

On the immigration side, the critical federal administrative text is the SEM’s AIG guidance as at 1 January 2026. The guidance states that Article 30(1)(b) AIG and Article 32 VZAE are “may” provisions and therefore confer a wide administrative discretion; that applicants relying on cultural, state-political or significant fiscal interests must relocate their centre of life to Switzerland and stay mainly in Switzerland; that in cases admitted because of significant cantonal fiscal interests any employment may only be abroad, except for management of one’s own assets; and that Switzerland’s reputational interests can outweigh pure fiscal considerations.

## Practical legal analysis

The legal architecture makes canton selection unavoidable. The federal regime under the Direct Federal Tax Act is nationwide, but cantonal and communal implementation depends on the canton’s use of its harmonised competence. That is why the federal government can simultaneously state that taxation according to expenditure exists in Swiss law and that it is implemented differently by canton, with five cantons having abolished it at cantonal level. Any later content that describes “Swiss lump-sum taxation” without immediately qualifying the canton is therefore structurally incomplete.

For abolished cantons, the correct approach is caution. The federal and cantonal legal bases are separate, so a cantonal abolition does not itself repeal the federal direct-tax rule. At the same time, the classic residence-by-lump-sum-tax narrative depends in practice on a viable cantonal and communal framework, because the cantonal fiscal contribution, commune tax layer, and cantonal migration handling typically carry much of the route’s real-world significance. The practical consequence is that abolished cantons are poor candidates for the classic combined pathway, but the exact filing consequences should still be checked against current destination-canton practice before any operational drafting. That is a principled legal inference from the dual federal-cantonal structure rather than a proposition expressly stated in one official FAQ.

Even among available cantons, published “minimums” are heterogeneous. Vaud’s CHF 415,000 minimum already includes a 15% uplift covering wealth tax. Geneva publishes separate current indexed amounts for cantonal and communal tax and for direct federal tax. Schwyz, Zug and Lucerne publish higher minimum taxable bases. Thurgau does not foreground a simple statutory minimum base at all, but an administrative model that compares agreed factors, actual lifestyle costs, control calculation, and a minimum annual tax burden. Lucerne then adds a separate immigration-side threshold of CHF 400,000 total tax load for a third-country case. The only safe internal drafting method is to translate each published figure into a common category before comparing cantons: minimum tax base, minimum wealth proxy, minimum annual tax liability, or immigration-side fiscal-interest threshold.

Official public materials also show how easy it is to be misled by stale or incomplete guidance. Geneva’s public summary page still states a CHF 400,000 minimum and was last updated in 2021, but Geneva’s dedicated indexation page and 2026 cantonal gazette show that the indexed 2026 cantonal and communal amount is CHF 426,357 and the federal amount is CHF 435,000. This is not merely a Geneva problem; several cantons spread the relevant information across legislation, gazettes, leaflets, forms and practice pages. The drafting lesson is that public “overview” pages should not be treated as the final amount source if a more current indexation page, gazette or tariff update exists.

Commune choice is also not a cosmetic extra. The FTA’s own explanation of Swiss tax feet states that cantons and municipalities have their own rates and tax multipliers. Ticino’s official sheet says the cantonal base tax forms the basis for application of the cantonal coefficient and computation of commune taxes. Valais treats communal tax separately, albeit on an average-rate basis for the expenditure-tax scheme. In practical route design, that means there can be a two-stage fiscal analysis: first selecting a viable canton from a legal and procedural perspective, and then selecting the commune that best aligns with the intended tax profile. Content that only compares cantons but not communes is often directionally right but economically incomplete.

The procedural layer is equally important. Some cantons publish a relatively sophisticated administrative path. Geneva openly links the tax request to the migration process and specifies the documents expected by the tax administration. Valais hosts a form that is explicitly framed as information for tax and migration authorities for residence-permit purposes. Appenzell Innerrhoden goes further by mapping the whole file flow across tax administration, economic-promotion bodies, the interior authority and SEM. Bern and Lucerne publish clear application routes to the tax administration. Those differences matter when advising on feasibility, timeframe, documentary burden, and the risk of later inconsistency between the tax file and the migration file.

For non-EU/EFTA applicants, canton selection cannot be reduced to “lowest tax floor wins”. SEM’s guidance is explicit that Article 30(1)(b) AIG and Article 32 VZAE are discretionary provisions, that the cantonal authorities have a wide margin, and that applicants must relocate their centre of life to Switzerland and reside there for the majority of the time. The guidance also states that in fiscally motivated admissions any gainful activity may only be carried on abroad, save for management of one’s own assets. That means a canton with a low published tax floor is not automatically the easiest canton if the canton’s migration practice is restrictive, if the canton lacks a transparent coordinated procedure, or if the applicant’s factual centre of life, family pattern or activity profile fits poorly with that canton.

Conversely, a canton with a higher published threshold may be more workable in practice if its rules are transparent and its migration interface is clearly articulated. Lucerne is the clearest example in the sources reviewed here. It publishes a high tax minimum base, but it also tells the reader what the tax application must contain, how annual review works, what financial changes must be disclosed, and how the canton quantifies substantial fiscal interest for non-EU/EFTA residence purposes. That kind of transparent public guidance can make route architecture more predictable than a lower-threshold canton whose real practice remains unpublished.

A further planning point concerns future canton changes. Schwyz’s official leaflet expressly states that where a person moves before year-end into another canton, taxation for the entire period is determined in the destination canton, and whether the person is taxed ordinarily or according to expenditure is governed exclusively by that destination canton’s law. That is a strong warning against “entry canton now, optimal canton later” strategies if the later canton has different rules, a higher minimum, or no regime at all. Medium-term canton planning should therefore be built into the first filing strategy rather than postponed.

Finally, canton selection must be framed honestly in later drafting. Swiss expenditure taxation is neither uniform across the country nor legally equivalent to a golden-visa programme. What the official materials show is a layered system: federal law, cantonal availability, cantonal minimums, commune-level tax multipliers, canton-specific tax-administration practice, and a distinct residence-permit assessment that in third-country cases remains discretionary and fact-sensitive. Strong tax contribution helps, but it operates inside a broader legal and administrative architecture that remains canton-specific.

## Drafting cautions

- Do not write as though Swiss lump-sum taxation is available on the same terms in every canton; the EFD expressly says implementation differs by canton and lists five cantons that have abolished the regime at cantonal level.
- Do not compare cantons using a single “minimum” figure unless you first identify whether the figure is a minimum taxable base, a loaded base, or a minimum annual tax liability. Thurgau and Lucerne are especially easy to misread on this point.
- Do not cite Geneva’s static CHF 400,000 overview page as the current amount without checking Geneva’s current indexation table or the latest cantonal gazette.
- Do not quote the federal minimum and ignore the municipal layer; the FTA confirms that municipalities and cantons have separate tax multipliers and several cantonal sheets show communal taxation as part of the regime.
- Do not present a tax ruling or tax application as creating an immigration entitlement. The SEM guidance emphasises discretion, centre-of-life relocation and majority presence in Switzerland.
- Do not describe Lucerne’s CHF 400,000 figure as Lucerne’s tax base; on the official page it is the canton’s immigration-side quantification of substantial fiscal interest for certain third-country cases.
- Do not present Thurgau’s CHF 150,000 figure as a Swiss-wide rule; it is an official Thurgau practice point within that canton’s own comparison model.
- Do not omit commune selection when discussing canton strategy, especially in cantons where official materials show commune taxes or commune coefficients feed directly into the final burden.
- Do not describe low published tax floors as making residence straightforward for non-EU/EFTA persons; SEM requires a broader public-interest and proportionality assessment, and some cantons quantify or channel fiscal-interest cases differently.
- Do not assume a later move to another canton preserves the same treatment; Schwyz’s official leaflet specifically warns that the destination canton’s law governs after the move.
- Do not treat current public canton pages as exhaustive statements of practice where no detailed public practice note was found. Absence of a public note is not evidence of national uniformity.
