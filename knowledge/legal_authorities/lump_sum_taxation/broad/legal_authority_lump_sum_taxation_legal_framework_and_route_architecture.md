# Swiss Lump-Sum Taxation Residence Legal Framework and Route Architecture

## Executive legal summary

Swiss “lump-sum taxation” is not a separate immigration category. In Swiss tax law it is a special
method of assessing tax for certain foreign nationals who become Swiss tax resident without carrying
on gainful activity in Switzerland. The regime is grounded federally in the rules on taxation according to
expenditure, and it uses ordinary tax rates applied to a deemed tax base derived from worldwide living
expenses, subject to statutory minimums and a control calculation. It is therefore not a flat annual fee,
not a nominal payment, and not a residence permit in itself. The key federal tax architecture is set out in
the Federal Direct Tax Act and the Tax Harmonisation Act as summarised by the Federal Department of
Finance and the Swiss Federal Tax Administration, with detailed implementation in ESTV Circular No. 44.

At immigration level, the route for third-country nationals is legally distinct from the tax regime. Public
Swiss migration materials do not identify a standalone federal “golden visa” or pure residence-by-
investment category. Instead, the non-EU/EFTA architecture normally relies on the discretionary
derogation power for “important public interests” under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, read
with the Admission, Period of Stay and Employment Ordinance, which expressly lists significant
cantonal fiscal interests as a relevant public-interest ground. Current SEM directives emphasise that this
is a can-provision, not an entitlement, that the concept must not be interpreted too broadly, and that
applicants must show that they are moving their centre of life to Switzerland and will spend the majority
of their time there. The same directives state that, where admission is based on cantonal fiscal interests,
any gainful activity may be carried on only abroad, with management of one’s own assets excluded
from the ban.

The institutional architecture is tripartite. The cantonal tax authority handles the expenditure-taxation
side, including advance agreements or conventions where the canton uses them. The cantonal
migration authority handles the residence-permit file. The Confederation, through SEM, remains
competent for derogations from admission requirements and for approval procedures in the cases
specified by federal law and ordinance. Geneva’s public materials make this interaction especially clear:
the tax request is filed with the cantonal tax administration, while the third-country “forfait fiscal”
residence request is filed with the migration office; Geneva’s migration office requires the AFC tax
convention as part of the immigration file and states that the application is submitted to SEM for
approval. Vaud’s public guidance likewise treats major cantonal fiscal interests as a derogation route
under federal law and says the file goes to SEM if the canton accepts it.

EU/EFTA nationals sit in a different legal architecture. Their residence without gainful activity is based
primarily on the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its annexes, not on the discretionary
fiscal-interest route used for third-country nationals. Official SEM material states that EU/EFTA nationals
without gainful employment are entitled to a B permit if they can show sufficient financial means and
adequate health and accident insurance. The AFMP text likewise provides a residence right for non-
active persons who can support themselves and their family and hold comprehensive sickness
insurance. In practice, an EU/EFTA national may still seek expenditure-based taxation on the tax side if
the tax-law conditions are met, but the tax status is not the source of the immigration right.

Canton choice matters legally and practically. Tax law is partly harmonised but still cantonal in
implementation, and the official federal tax system materials note that the regime has been abolished
in Zurich, Basel-Stadt, Schaffhausen and Appenzell Ausserrhoden, while Basel-Landschaft effectively
limits it to the year of arrival. Public canton materials also show meaningful divergence in procedure,
evidential requirements and the language used to express fiscal-interest thresholds or expectations.
That matters because there is no single national permit product called “lump-sum residence”; there is a
tax regime and there are canton-run permit processes applying federal immigration law.

As a matter of legal characterisation, Swiss lump-sum taxation residence should therefore be described
conservatively as a combined tax-and-immigration pathway, not a statutory “golden visa”. For third-
country nationals it is highly discretionary and fact-sensitive; for EU/EFTA nationals it is usually better
understood as ordinary non-working residence under free movement rules, potentially paired with
expenditure-based taxation on the tax side. The absence of gainful activity, the reality of Swiss
residence, the centre-of-life evidence, the canton’s fiscal interest, and successful coordination between
tax and migration filings are all part of the route architecture.

## Structural overview of the legal framework

Legal nature of expenditure-based taxation. Federal official guidance describes taxation according to
expenditure as a special way of assessing income and, at cantonal level, wealth for certain foreign
nationals living in Switzerland without gainful activity there. The tax is computed using ordinary rates,
but on a deemed expenditure basis rather than ordinary net income and wealth, and it is subject to
minimum bases and a control calculation. This is tax architecture, not immigration status.

Relationship between tax ruling and residence permit. The tax ruling or convention is handled by
the tax administration; the residence permit is handled by migration authorities under foreign-nationals
law. Geneva’s official materials expressly require parallel dealings with the cantonal tax administration
and the cantonal population and migration office, while the immigration file for third-country “forfait
fiscal” cases requires the tax convention and is submitted to SEM for approval. This supports the core
distinction: interaction in practice, but no automatic conversion of tax approval into residence
authorisation.

Federal and cantonal tax bases. The federal tax regime is anchored in Art. 14 DBG and Art. 6 StHG as
explained in ESTV Circular No. 44, the EFD dossier, and cantonal implementing notes. The calculation
starts from worldwide living expenses, but the tax base cannot fall below the statutory minimums, the
seven-times-rent or rental-value rule (or three times the pension price for hotel/pension situations), and
the control calculation on specified Swiss-source and treaty-relief income. Cantons retain important
implementation powers and may set their own cantonal minima and wealth-tax treatment within the
harmonised framework.

Immigration basis and nationality distinctions. For third-country nationals, the architecture usually
runs through the derogation for important public interests in AIG Art. 30 and OASA Art. 32, including
major cantonal fiscal interests. SEM’s current directives treat this as a discretionary exception. For EU/
EFTA nationals, by contrast, non-working residence sits under the free movement framework: sufficient
means and comprehensive insurance generate a right to residence, so the immigration basis is
different even if expenditure-based taxation is later sought on the tax side.

Role of cantons and SEM. AIG Art. 40 allocates permit-granting authority to the cantons, while
reserving federal competence for derogations from admission requirements and for the approval
procedure. Current SEM guidance explains the approval mechanism under AIG Art. 99 and OASA Arts

85-86: SEM can refuse approval, time-limit it, or impose conditions. Geneva and Vaud both publicly
state that major-fiscal-interest files go to SEM after cantonal acceptance.

Residence, centre of life and presence expectations. The 2026 SEM directives state that applicants
under the public-interest route must prove they are relocating their centre of life to Switzerland and will
spend the majority of their time there. Geneva’s public migration guidance mirrors this almost exactly.
These residence expectations are immigration-law compliance points; they are related to, but
analytically distinct from, tax residence concepts and treaty residence.

Activity restrictions. Both the tax regime and the immigration route are built around the absence of
Swiss gainful activity. ESTV Circular No. 44 treats Swiss gainful activity as incompatible with expenditure-
based taxation. SEM’s directives say that where admission rests on cantonal fiscal interests, any gainful
activity may be exercised only abroad, except for management of one’s own assets. SEM’s annex on the
concept of gainful activity shows that some short business meetings and negotiations may fall outside
gainful employment, whereas project work, on-the-job training, implementation work, performances
and management tasks can amount to gainful activity.

Alternative routes and limits of the architecture. Third-country retirees use a separate legal route
under AIG Art. 28 and OASA Art. 25, which require age, personal ties to Switzerland and sufficient
means. Entrepreneur and investor cases belong to the work/admission-for-economic-interest side of
AIG, including investor/entrepreneur admissions that preserve or create jobs. OASA Art. 6 also states
that mere acts such as buying property, renting housing, signing a work contract, founding a company
or taking a stake in one do not by themselves justify a permit claim. These rules are why the lump-sum
route must not be conflated with a general investment visa.

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

Federal guidance and administrative materials. The EFD’s official dossier explains the tax regime at a
high level and stresses that it is a simplified method of assessing income and wealth for certain foreign
nationals, not a separate tax rate. ESTV Circular No. 44 is the most important federal administrative text
for the tax side: it sets out the subjective conditions, the worldwide expenditure basis, the minimum
bases, the control calculation, treatment of treaty-relief income, procedural obligations, and the effect
of Swiss citizenship or Swiss gainful activity. Current SEM directives are the principal federal
administrative text for the immigration side: they characterise the public-interest derogation as a
narrow, discretionary can-provision, require transfer of the centre of life and majority presence in
Switzerland, and explain the consequence of admission based on cantonal fiscal interests. SEM’s
separate annex on the concept of gainful activity is useful for borderline cases because it distinguishes
non-gainful meetings and negotiations from productive or managerial activities that qualify as gainful
work.

## Practical legal analysis

The central analytical mistake to avoid is collapsing the tax regime into the immigration process. The
two are functionally linked but legally separate. Tax authorities care about whether the person qualifies
for expenditure-based taxation and what the assessed basis will be; migration authorities care about
admission, purpose of stay, compliance with the no-work condition, and whether a lawful permit basis
exists. Geneva’s public materials demonstrate the separation particularly well: the tax convention is
requested from the tax administration, while the immigration application is filed with the migration
office and must then go to SEM for approval in third-country fiscal-interest cases. Federal law confirms
the separation institutionally: AIG Art. 40 leaves permit issuance to the cantons but reserves federal
competence for derogations and approvals.

That separation also explains why a tax ruling does not create an immigration entitlement. Third-

country fiscal-interest cases sit on AIG Art. 30 and OASA Art. 32, and SEM’s current directives say those
provisions are discretionary can-provisions with a wide margin of judgment. Even a strong fiscal file can
therefore fail if the authorities are not satisfied on residence reality, compliance risk, reputation
concerns, or other public-interest considerations. SEM’s 2026 directives expressly warn against an over-
broad reading of “important public interests” and discuss the need to weigh fiscal interest against
broader public interests, including Switzerland’s reputation.

Canton choice is legally decisive for two reasons. First, expenditure-based taxation is cantonal in
implementation even though federally harmonised in outline, so availability and cantonal minima differ.
Official federal tax-system material states that the system has been abolished in Zurich, Basel-Stadt,
Schaffhausen and Appenzell Ausserrhoden, while Basel-Landschaft has only a residual year-of-arrival
regime. Secondly, even among available cantons, public evidence shows materially different practices in
how tax conventions are handled, what fiscal interest threshold or evidential case is expected, and how
migration offices frame the public-interest route. Genève’s public documents are more explicit than
many others about majority-of-year presence, SEM approval and the tax convention’s role in the
immigration file.

EU/EFTA cases must be analysed on a different track. Their primary residence basis is the AFMP rule for
persons not pursuing an economic activity, which creates a right to residence when sufficient means
and comprehensive insurance are shown. For them, expenditure-based taxation may be relevant on the
tax side, but it is not the legal source of the right to stay. Treating EU/EFTA cases as though they
depended on discretionary “major fiscal interest” is therefore legally inaccurate. The practical advantage
of the third-country lump-sum route-cantonal fiscal interest as a public-interest exception-simply
addresses a different legal problem.

The route also differs sharply from both entrepreneur/investor and retiree routes. Swiss law does
recognise investor and entrepreneur admissions in the work-related chapter, but those are admissions
with gainful activity and macroeconomic/job-creation logic, not non-working HNWI residence.
Separately, Swiss law recognises a retiree route for older applicants with close personal ties to
Switzerland and sufficient means. Public Vaud guidance rightly mentions that some fiscally attractive
non-working applicants aged 55+ with Swiss ties could also consider the retiree basis. These are
different legal categories with different evidence packages and different risks.

The “no golden visa” point follows from the legal architecture. Swiss materials do not identify a
freestanding federal residence category called a golden visa, and OASA expressly states that acts often
associated with investor-visa marketing-buying property, renting a dwelling, signing an employment
contract, founding a company or merely taking a participation in one-do not themselves justify a

permit claim. The practical route marketed internationally as “Swiss residency by lump-sum tax” is
therefore better understood as a combination of existing tax rules and a canton-led, SEM-supervised
discretionary immigration process. That is a legal characterisation, not a marketing description.

Activity restrictions remain one of the most legally sensitive areas. ESTV Circular No. 44 treats Swiss
gainful activity as incompatible with expenditure-based taxation, and current migration guidance is
similarly restrictive for fiscal-interest residence. Yet some conduct sits near the boundary. SEM’s annex
shows that high-level representative visits, contract negotiations and certain meetings can be non-
gainful, whereas project implementation, supervision involving productive activity, artistic
performances, on-the-job training and recurring personnel-management functions can amount to
gainful activity. Geneva’s own practice note goes further and says a remunerated board seat is not
permitted. Any drafting system built from this pack should therefore flag board mandates, Swiss
management functions, and hands-on roles in Swiss businesses as high-risk rather than describing
them as generally allowed.

Finally, the route does not shortcut long-term migration law. AIG Art. 34 sets the ordinary framework for
settlement permits, and SEM’s naturalisation page states that ordinary naturalisation requires ten years’
residence and a C permit. Neither the expenditure-taxation regime nor a large cantonal fiscal
contribution displaces those general rules. A person may of course build lawful residence time under
the route, but longer-term planning still has to be done through ordinary settlement and citizenship
law, with attention to integration and permit history.

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss lump-sum taxation as a Swiss “golden visa” as though that were a federal
legal category; the safest formulation is that this is a combination of expenditure-based taxation
and existing residence-permit rules, often discretionary for third-country nationals.
Do not imply that obtaining a tax ruling or tax convention automatically entitles the applicant to
a residence permit; Geneva and Vaud both show a separate migration file and SEM approval
layer for third-country fiscal-interest cases.
Do not describe the tax as a flat fee or nominal annual payment; official federal guidance says
ordinary rates apply to a deemed expenditure base with statutory minima and a control
calculation.
Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA cases into a single route architecture; EU/EFTA non-
working residence is treaty-based, while third-country fiscal-interest residence is discretionary
under AIG/OASA.
Do not suggest that property purchase, company formation, or passive shareholding alone
creates a Swiss residence claim; OASA says the opposite.
Do not omit centre-of-life and physical-presence analysis; current SEM guidance and Geneva
public practice both require the centre of interests to move to Switzerland and the applicant to
spend the majority of the year there.
Do not understate activity restrictions. Management of private wealth may be allowed, but Swiss
gainful activity remains incompatible with the route and borderline business functions require
fact-specific analysis.
Do not present cantonal practice as federal law. Geneva’s statements on five-year conventions,
remunerated board functions, and published threshold indexation are useful, but they are
canton-specific practice points.
Do not conflate the non-working fiscal-interest route with the separate retiree route or with
investor/entrepreneur work routes.
Do not imply that large tax payments bypass ordinary C-permit or citizenship rules. Settlement
and naturalisation remain governed by ordinary federal law.

## Open questions and limitations

The public federal and cantonal materials are strong on the overall architecture, but public canton-by-
canton migration practice is uneven. Geneva and Vaud publish comparatively clear guidance; other
cantons may have additional internal practices that are not publicly documented to the same level. Also,
for some tax-law provisions the analysis relies on official ESTV/EFD guidance and official cantonal
summaries rather than directly scraped current Fedlex article text, because the official web retrieval
environment did not consistently expose the latest consolidated text for all tax provisions. The legal
propositions above have therefore been drafted conservatively and anchored in the most authoritative
official materials that were accessible.
