# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Artists,Performers and Cultural Workers

Suggested internal filename:

## Executive summaryA Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not treat artistic, performance, musical, entertainment, or cultural status as an immigration exemption. The starting point is functional rather than sectoral: if the person is carrying out an activity in Switzerland that is normally performed for remuneration, Swiss law treats that as gainful activity even if the individual is unpaid in the particular case. That principle is broad enough to capture performers, musicians, readers, film crews, photographers, cultural technicians, and similar production roles when they are working in Switzerland. 1

For **EU/EFTA nationals** and certain **posted workers / self-employed service providers** established in the EU/EFTA, the key dividing line is usually the **90-day** **notification** **regime** under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its Swiss implementing rules. Short-term work is not automatically exempt: some categories must be notified, some require a permit, and some activities are outside the notification concept because they are treated as non-productive attendance or meetings. Attendance at a conference without speaking, contract negotiations, and internal strategy meetings are treated as non-notifiable in the SEM's Annex 5 examples; by contrast, speaking at a conference, client-facing advice, project work, filming, photography, and artistic performances are treated as notifiable productive activity. 2

**For non-EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the default rule is materially stricter. Gainful activity in Switzerland requires a work authorisation regardless of duration, and dependent employment requires an employer application and satisfaction of the usual admission conditions, including economic interest, priority to the domestic / EU-EFTA labour market, local salary and working conditions, and personal qualifications. There is, however, a specific short-stay regulatory category for certain third-country artists under **Article** **19** **paragraph** **4** **letter** **b** **VZAE,** allowing short-stay admission without counting against the short-stay quotas for artists in music, literature, performing art, visual art, and circus / variety, up to **eight** **months** **within** **twelve** **months.** That category is **not** a permit exemption, and the ordinary labour-market conditions still matter. 3

The classification question is often driven less by artistic label and more by the **legal architecture** of **the** **engagement**: who is the employer, whether there is subordination to a Swiss establishment, whether the Swiss party is merely an organiser / venue / client, whether the individual is genuinely self-employed, whether the work is part of a foreign service contract, and whether the person is only attending meetings or is actually producing cultural output in Switzerland. Zurich's official artist instruction is especially useful on this point: where the Swiss venue controls appearances and modalities, the canton treats the matter as employment with a Swiss employer; where a public concert organiser merely provides infrastructure and the band controls the musical programme, the canton treats the case as cross-border service provision without Swiss "job start". 4

Official federal material is strongest on the **general immigration framework, the EU/EFTA** **notification route,** and the **narrow third-country artist category.** Official material is thinner on some edge cases such as unpaid amateur artistic participation, mixed promotional appearances, or multi-canton touring logistics. In those areas, the safest drafting position is conservative: if the person is rehearsing, performing, filming, installing, reading, or otherwise producing cultural work in Switzerland, classify the case as work unless a specific official source clearly says otherwise. 5

### D Primary legal texts

The core statutory architecture is built from the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** **Act** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment.** The Act provides the broad definition of gainful activity, the permit requirement for work, the employer-application rule for dependent employment, the labour-market admission conditions, and the split of competence between cantons and the SEM. The Ordinance adds several operational points that matter directly in cultural cases: the eight-day rule for cross-border work, the quota-exempt short-stay category for certain third-country artists up to eight months in twelve months, registration rules specific to artists, and the document package needed to prove salary, conditions and service arrangements. 25

<!-- 6 -->

For EU/EFTA nationals and service provision from the EU/EFTA, the treaty basis is the **Agreement** on **the** **Free Movement of Persons,** especially **Article 5** and **Annex I Articles 17, 20, 21 and 22.** Those provisions liberalise certain short cross-border services up to 90 actual working days per calendar year, remove the residence-permit requirement for certain service providers foor stays up to 90 days, and preserve domestic rules on employment and working conditions for posted workers. 20

**The** **Posted** **Workers** **framework** matters whenever a foreign company sends workers into Switzerland for a service contract. Swiss official guidance states that foreign employers must guarantee at least the Swiss minimum labour and pay standards required by federal law, ordinances, generally binding collective agreements and standard employment contracts, with sanctions including administrative fines and service bans for violations. 26

### E Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful federal admninistrative text for arts cases is the SEM's **2025** **guide** **for** **musicians and** **artists under Article 19 paragraph 4 letter b VZAE in clubs / bars / restaurants.** It is expressly narrow and should not be over-generalised, but it gives high-value evidence on the union of immigration andI labour-market scrutiny in the entertainment field.It says that the usual third-country principles still apply, that the artist route is available for up to eight months within twelve months without quota counting, that the labour-market requirements in AIG arts. 18 and 21-23 remain cumulative, that short performances without "job start" lasting no more than eight days in a calendar year are not permit-required in that specific setting, and that cantons may demand detailed business and payment evidence, including accounts, advertising materials, payroll evidennce, and proof of social-security and withholdingg-tax payments. 27

The SEM's **notification** **guidance** is the key operational source for EU/EFTA and AFMP-based cases. It identifies the persons who may use the notification route, allocates who must file the notification, explains the day-before rule for short-term Swiss-employer hires and the eight-day prior rule for posted workers and self-employed providers, and confirms the sectoral first-day rule and the cumulative counting rules. 21

The SEM's **VFP** **Annex** **5** is the most useful official source for the boundary between a mere visit and productive work. It is especially important for cultural content drafting because it gives examples that map neatly onto arts practice: simple attendance is not notifiable, but public-facing intellectual or creative delivery usually is. The same annex specifically lists filming, photographic work, author readings, opera tours and band performances as notifiable artistic activity. 23

SECO's material is strongest on **posted workers, self-employed service providers, and compliance** **evidence.** Its pages confirm that self-employed providers must prove their independence with a Swiss-official-language document set, and that foreign employers posting staff into Switzerland are subject to Swiss labour-conditions controls and sanctions. 28

### F Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** has the strongest publicly accessible artist-specific cantonal material located during this research. The Zurich instruction of 15 February 2022 states that artistic activity is gainful activity even if unpaid; distinguishes Swiss employment from service provision by reference to subordination and the role of the Swiss venue; says the eight-day rule only applies to work without Swiss job start; requires advance filing of the original signed application and contract; confirms registration before work unless the eight-day rule applies; states that stays above three months and up to eight months require a biometric permit plus work confirmation; and requires evidence on qualifications, contracts and wages. It also says DJs from third countries are restricted to those at the very top of the field,which is a strong indication of restrictive cantonal practice in that niche. 29

<!-- 7 -->

**Geneva** does not appear to publish a dedicated artist instruction equivalent to Zurich's, but its official work-permit pages still contain relevant practice signals. Geneva states that third-country salaried work includes artists, and that a Swiss company hiring a third-country national must usually apply around six to eight weeks in advance while satisfying economic-interest, labour-priority,qualification and salary tests. GGeneva also says that EU/EFTA frontier work authorisation covers **any** **activity,** **including** **unpaid** **internships,** which is a useful cantonal indication that lack of pay does not by itself remove the need for labour authorisation. Its service-provider pages also list the evidential package expected for longer assignments, including service contracts, work contracts, motivation letters, CVs and diplomas. 30

**Ticino** publishes useful official guidance on short-duration notification practice. Its pages confirm the split between Swiss-employer hires, posted workers and self-employed providers; the day-before rule for short Swiss-employer notifications; the eight-day prior rule for cross-border service providers; the 90-day cap; the separate permit route for third-country nationals; the requirement that third-country nationals posted from EU/EFTA undertakings must have been permanently admitted to the EU/EFTA labour market for at least twelve months; and the obligation for posted-worker notifications to include a declaration regarding minimum Swiss labour conditions. It also states that workers who will be employed directly in Ticino do not fall under the cross-border service-provider procedure. 31

Official cantonal material outside Zurich is therefore helpful but relatively general. No other canton located in this research published a similarly detailed public artist memo on the same level as Zurich's. 32

## Practical analysis

## G Practical legal analysis

The safest Swiss classification rule is that **performing, rehearsing for performance, filming,** **photographing, reading publicly, installing or producing cultural content in Switzerland should** **usually** **be** **treated** **as** **work.** The legal reason is the breadth of the gainful-activity definition and the SEM's AFMP examples treating artistic activities as productive activity. Official material is thinner on artist-specific "rehearsal" language than on performances themselves, so the most conservative internal drafting position is to treat rehearsals that are connected to a Swiss engagement as part of the work package unless a canton or the SEM confirms a narrower view. 33

By contrast, **attendance-only** activity is materially different. The SEM's Annex 5 examples place conference attendance without presenting, pure workshop attendance, representative visits, contractual negotiations, and internal strategy meetings on the non-notifiable side of the line. That is the best official anchor for distinguishing a true business visit from performance work. For arts content, this means that merely attending a launch, meeting a promoter, discussing a contract, or observing a rehearsal is legally safer than speaking, performing, teaching, coaching on the job, or carrying out production. 34

For **EU/EFTA nationals working directly for a Swiss employer,** the practical short-term rule is straightforward: up to three months / 90 days can usually be handled through the notification system, but the notification must be filed by the employer and normally no later than the day before the first day of work. The eight-day notification-free period does not apply to job starts with a Swiss employer. If the engagement continues beyond ninety days or three months in the calendar year, the matter moves into the permit system. 35

<!-- 8 -->

For **posted workers and self-employed service providers from the EU/EFTA,** the key practical issue is whether the engagement falls within the AFMP notification corridor. Up to ninety actual working days can generally be notified rather than permitted, but the eight-day rule, the first-day sectors, and the cumulative counting rules must be checked carefully. Because hospitality is a first-day sector in the federal guidance, performances supplied into hotels, restaurants or comparable hospitality venues require special caution: the official sources do not discuss artists expressly in that sectoral rule, but the safest reading is that the sector rule may apply where the service is performed in that sector. 26

**For** **third-country** **nationals,** the default is more restrictive. The work authorisation analysis does not start from the duration of the engagement but fromn the existence of gainful activity. The permit application must usually be organised in advance by the Swiss employer in employment cases, or by the independent provider in some service cases, and the file must satisfy the ordinary admission tests. The narrow short-stay artist category in VZAE Article 19 paragraph 4 letter b is significant because it allows certain artists to avoid quota counting, but it does not remove the employer-application logic, the wage controls, the labour-priority review, or the qualifications scrutiny. 9

The **employer** **/ organiser** / **venue** **/ client** / **agent** structure often decides the route. If a Swiss venue or Swiss employer controls appearances, timing or working modalities, Zurich treats that as employment. If a Swiss organiser merely provides infrastructure and ticketing while the foreign band or troupe remains responsible for the artistic programme, Zurich treats the case as cross-border service provision. That distinction is valuable for promoters, festivals and touring acts, but it is practice-based rather than a universal statutory formula. Internal drafting should therefore describe the test as one of control, subordination, and service-recipient structure rather than as a bright-line "venue rule". 11

The **evidence** **file** should be built around the route. For permit cases, Swiss rules and cantonal practice point to the need for signed contracts, a clear itinerary, exact dates, the workplace or venue, the Swiss recipient or employer, fee or salary data, and evidence of qualifications where the person is being admitted on a skilled or artist route. Zurich and the SEM musician guide add original signatures, signed agency involvement where relevant, business and advertising material, income statements, payroll evidence, proof of social contributions, and documentary evidence of artistic standing. Geneva likewise expects service contracts, work contracts, a motivation letter stating duration and place of work,and CVs / diplomas for longer service-provider assignments. 36

**The** **salary** **and** **working-conditions** layer should never be omitted from arts content. Under the AIG and VZAE, admission depends on locality-, profession-and sector-typical conditions. Under the posting framework, foreign employers must also respect Swiss minimumn standards and can face fines or service bans if they do not. In the narrow entertainment-music guide, the SEM goes even further and points cantons to sectoral guidance tariffs and supporting payroll / contribution evidence. Any internal content saying that artist engagements are exempt from salary review would therefore be legally unsafe. 37

**A** **visa** is a separate issue from labour authorisation. Zurich's artist instruction expressly says that visa-required nationals still need a visa for entry even in no-Swiss-job-start cases, and it distinguishes that from the underlying work classification. The same instruction also says that Swiss-employer artist cases require a visa or assurance of the residence authorisation for entry. Internal content should therefore keep four questions separate: entry visa, notification, work permit, and salary / labour-condition compliance. 38

<!-- 9 -->

The principal **compliance** **risks** are predictable. They include starting work before the required notification or permit; using "bousiness meeting" language for productive artistic work; misclassifying dependent work as self-employment; overlooking cumulative day counting for tours; assuming that lack of pay removes the need for authorisation; and filing weak evidence on artistic qualifications, itinerary, or Swiss labour conditions. Federal and cantonal guidance also underline sanctions for misrepresentation and for labour-conditions breaches. 39

### Drafting cautions

## H Drafting cautions

Future internal content should avoid five recurrent drafting errors.

First, do **not** write that "artists are exempt", "performances under eight days are exempt", or "festivals do not need work authorisation". Swiss law does not recognise a general arts exemption. The legal route depends on nationality, whether there is Swiss employment or cross-border service provision, whether the AFMP applies, whether the wvork falls within the eight-day / ninety-day notification rules, and whether the case is really just attendance rather than productive work. 40

Secondly, do **not** treat "unpaid" as synonymous with "non-work". The statutory definition of gainful activity and the cantonal practice materials point the other way. If the activity is normally remunerated, or if the person is productively contributing to a cultural output in Switzerland, the safer drafting position is that labour authorisation is engaged. 41

Thirdly, keep **attendance** separate from **delivery.** "Attending a conference" is not the same as "speaking at a conference"; "meeting a promoter" is not the same as "performing a showcase"; and "observing" is not the same as "training on the job" or "running a workshop". The SEM's Annex 5examples are especiallyuseful here and should inform future content language. 42

Fourthly, do **not** collapse the roles of employer, promoter, venue, agent and Swiss client. In Swiss practice, those roles can change the classification entirely. Internal templates should therefore require the drafter to identify: the contracting employer; the legal recipient of the service; the venue or venues; whether there is subordination to a Swiss entity; whether there is a foreign service contract;and whether the individual is genuinely self-employed under Swiss standards. 43

Fifthly, label narrow sources accurately. The SEM musician/ artist guide is **sector-specific** and aimed at Article 19 paragraph 4 letter b VZAE cases in clubs / bars / restaurants. Zurich's artist instruction is **cantonal** **practice,** not national black-letter law. Those sources are very useful, but they should be cited as such and not universalised beyond their scope. 44

### Open questions and limitations

Official Swiss material is relatively rich on the general work-authorisation framework, EU/EFTA notification procedure, and the narrow third-country artist route, but it is thinner on several arts-specific edge cases: unpaid amateur cultural participation, rehearsals as a standalone category, visual-art exhibition installation, and multi-canton touring administration outside the Zurich practice note. No dedicated federal case law turned out to be necessary to state the baseline rules, and none was relied on as a primary authority in this pack. In those thin areas, the legally safer content position is conservative and should be framed as such. 49
