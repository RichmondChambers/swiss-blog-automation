# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Domestic Workers and Private Household Staff

## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law treats private household work broadly as **gainful** **employment.** In SEM's official guidance, an activity is treated as gainful employment where it is normally performed for remuneration, even if the individual case is unpaid or only compensated through subsistence benefits such as food and accommodation. In other words, a domestic arrangement does not fall outside work-authorisation rules merely because it is informal, family-like, low-paid, or live-in. 1

For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** access to the Swiss labour market is materially easier because of free movement. Official SEM materials state that the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons lifts labour-market restrictions for EU/EFTA citizens, and that eligible short-term work can in appropriate cases be handled through the notification procedure for up to three months or 90 working days per calendar year. If the stay exceeds three months, the relevant residence oor work permit must be applied for before work starts. 2

For **third-country** **nationals,** private household need is not, by itself, a sufficient immigration ground. SEM's official labour-market guidance states that ordinary admission of non-EU/EFTA workers is limited **to a restricted number of highly qualified workers,** subject to overall economic interest, quotas, precedence for the Swiss and EU/EFTA labour force, region-and sector-appropriate salary and employment conditions, and suitable accommodation. That ordinary framework sits uneasily with many domestic-staff roles such as cleaner, nanny, housekeeper, cook, chauffeur or live-in hnelper. 3

There is, however, a **narrow** **officiaI SEM** **route** **for** **"house** **staff"** in household tasks, childcare, and in some cases care of disabled or seriously ill persons. The relevant SEM chapter makes clear that this is an exceptional category, largely framed around cumulatively satisfied conditions,primarily short-stay permits, and employer-bound permission with no permitted job change. This is the key special-employment authority for third-country domestic staff in private households. 4

Domestic-household files are also **compliance-sensitive** because of dependency, isolation and exploitation risk. Official Swiss materials therefore insist on a detailed written contract, translation into a language the worker understands, proper rules on accommodation and rest time, and in the au pair context mandatory placement through a recognised Swiss organisation because of trafficking and exploitation concerns. Live-in care guidance further stresses that one person cannot lawfully provide round-the-clock care and that working and rest times must be documented. 5

## Structural overview of domestic-worker and private-household authorisation

A Swiss private household can, in principole, be the **employer-applicant** in a work-authorisation file. SEM's procedure pages state that the employer submits the application to the cantonal employment or immigration authority, and SEM's guidance defines "employment with taking up a post" as work for an employer based in Switzerland. Nothing in the official material excludes a private family from being that employer. The critical point is not whether the emnployer is a company or a household, but whether the chosen immigration route can lawfully accommodate the role. ⁶

The first structural category is **ordinary Swiss employment in a private household.** If the worker will be employed by a household in Switzerland, the case is a Swviss employment case, not an informal family arrangement. For EU/EFTA nationals this is usually analysed through free movement, notification or permit rules depending on duration. For third-country nationals the ordinary admission conditions apply unless a specific exception can be shown. 7

The second category is the **SEM** **house-staff exception.** SEM's household chapter allows, in exceptional cases, admission of house staff for household tasks, childcare, and care of disabled or sick poersons where cumulative conditions are met. This is the main federal administrative authority forprivate-household domestic staff from third countries. It is not a broad consumer hiring route; it is a tightly controlled exception. 4

The third category is **caregiving** **and** **live-in** **support.** Swiss official guidance distinguishes carefully between household support and regulated nursing. Household support in a private home may fall under housework or caregiving rules, but actual nursing or medical care needs professional qualifications and, in general, cantonal health authorisation. SEM further states that direct recruitment from abroad of non-EU/EFTA nationals for 24-hour care is not possible. 8

The fourth category is au **pair,** which must not be collapsed into nannying or ordinary household service. SEM describes au pair as a temporary cultural-exchange arrangement. Third-state au pairs aged 18 to 25 may be admitted for up to 12 months, they are not subject to precedence, and because of the increased protection risk the arrangement must go through a recognised Swiss organisation. The role is limited to light household work and childcare linked to language learning and cultural exchange. 9

The fifth category is **family support, volunteering, posting and accompaniment.** SEM states that work between relatives is in principle still gainful employment. Narrow exceptions exist for some ascendants or descendants during short visitor stays, but only where the arrangement remains a genuine social courtesy and does not enable labour-market participation or cost savings. Separately, if the employer remains abroad, the case may move into cross-border service or posting analysis rather than Swiss household emnployment. Those categories must not be conflated in drafting. 10

### Primary legal texts

The core federal instruments are the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment,** which SEM itself identifies as the governing texts for employment admission. SEM's official labour-market summary links those instruments directly to the criteria of economic interest, quotas, precedence, salary and employment conditions, personal requirements, and accommodation. For EU/EFTA cases, SEM's free-movement pages identify the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons** as the source of easier labour-market access. 18

Within that framework, the provisions that matter most for domestic-household cases are the rules on **gainful employment, admission conditions, exceptions, accommodation, and employer duties.In** the source set used here, those provisions are most accessibly and concretely operationalised through SEM's chapter 4 directives, which repeatedly anchor domestic-household analysis in the AIG/VZAE structure and then explain how the federal criteria are applied in practice. 19

For domestic-household cases specifically, SEM also relies on **ILO** **Convention** **No. 189** **on** **decent work** **for domestic** **workers.** The household chapter expressly says Swiss admission practice for hoouse staff is based not only on the AIG requirements but also on the relevant ILO Convention, which Switzerland ratified in 2014 and brought into force in 2015. That is legally and interpretively important because it explains the contract, accommodation, information and dignity safeguards found in the SEM guidance. 12

On the salary side, the central labour-market benchmark is the **federal NAV** **Hauswirtschaft** together with applicable **cantonal** **NAV/CTT** **texts.** SEM's househoId contract section expressly directs decision-makers to the federal domestic-work NAV and any cantonal domestic-work NAV rules, subject to the principle of the more favourable standard where legal texts collide. SECO's January 2026 information sheet then gives the current federal minimum-wage categories and rates for domestic work in private households. 20

For short-term EU/EFTA work and certain cross-border situations, the legally relevant text family also includes the **notification** **procedure** **rules** under the free-movement regime. Official SEM materials explain that this system can cover EU/EFTA nationals taking up short Swiss employment for up to three months or 90 days, as well as posted workers and self-employed service providers in the defined categories. 21

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important administrative source is **SEM's** **AIG** **directives, chapter** 4, especially the sections on the concept of gainful employment, local salary and working conditions, the household chapter, family-childcare by relatives, care of disabled or seriously ill persons, au pairs, and employer duties. For internal authority work, this chapter is indispensable because it is the only official federal source in the research set that deals with private-household employment in substantial operational detail. 22

SEM's public-facing English pages are also highly useful as **orientation** **and** **evidential** **checklists.** The labour-market basis page summarises the federal admission criteria for third-country workers and expressly points users back to the chapter 4 directives for sector-specific rules. SEM's procedure page and application-document checklist then show what the authorities expect in practice: full forms, passport copy, precedence evidence, CV and qualifications, reasons for the vacancy, job description,and a binding employment contract or transfer confirmation. 23

On the labour-market compliance side, **SECO's materials** are essential. The January 2026 NAV guidance gives current domestic-work minimum rates and confirms that federal and cantonal domestic-work NAV rules apply in combination. SECO's caregiver sheet clarifies residence conditions for foreign caregivers, makes clear that this home-care work is performed in an employment relationship rather than freelance form,and states that foreign nationals cannot be directly employed from non-EU/EFTA states in that model. SECO's live-in care page further states that one individual cannot legally provide 24-hour care and that working-time and rest-time documentation is required in the personal-leasing model. 24

SECO's **model contract materials for private employers** are not immigration lawv, but they are still relevant evidentially. They show the state's expected contractual structure for household employment and reinforce that household employers are treated as real employers with documentary obligations. For immigration drafting, they are best used to support what a compliant evidential bundle should look like, not as a source of independent immigration entitlement. 25

### Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** publishes a particularly useful practice note from the secretariat of the Tripartite Commission, distinguishing **au** **pair** from **nanny/household** **help.** The leaflet says that if the criteria for au pair are not fully met, the relationship is treated as household work and falls under the domestic-work NAV. It also expects language-study elements, caps au pair working time at 30 hours a week, requires a free day,and expects an own room in the host family. At the same time, the Zurich leaflet uses age and duration criteria that are looser than SEM's third-state au pair rule, so it should be read as a **cantonal** **labour-market classification** aid, not as a substitute for federal immigration rules. 26

**Geneva** publicly empohasises that private households employing house staff-cleaners, childcarers, carers, gardeners, cooks and private drivers included-must comply with the domestic-economy contract setting minimum wages. Genevas official third-country salaried-work page also shows the canton's broader labour-market posture: OCPM and OCIRT review the file, the employer must prove Swiss/EU-EFTA recruitment efforts, the salary and working conditions must be sector-appropriate, the employment is expected to be full time, and conditions may be attached to the permit. That page is not domestic-staff-specific, but it is a reliable public illustration of how a canton scrutinises third-country employment cases. 27

**Ticino** publicly confirms that domestic work in private households is governed by both the federal domestic-work CNL/NAV and the cantonal domestic-work contract for the other working conditions. Ticino's foreigner pages also show a practical frontier-worker angle that can matter for live-in or weekly-resident situations: a frontier worker who does not return daily must notify presence to the Swiss municipality where they stay overnight. That is not a domestic-staff rule as such, but it is relevant where frontier caregiving or household support is considered. 28

**Vaud** is less explicit in public immigration guidance on domestic staff as a category, but its official salaried-permit form is still instructive. The form asks for working week, gross salary, duration, and specifically whether deductions will be made for food or lodging, which shows that those issues are part of cantonal permit scrutiny. Vaud also maintains a cantonal private-household CTT. Public cantonal guidance in this field is therefore strongest on wage/working-condition control and evidential inputs, and thinner on bespoke domestic-staff migration routes. 29

### Practical legal analysis

A Swiss household can plainly be an employer, but that does not create a separate immigration principle of "private need sponsorship". Once the worker will be employed by a household in Switzerland, the file is a Swiss employment case and must be analysed through the ordinary federal categories: EU/EFTA free movement, third-country ordinary admission, or an expressly recognised exception such as the SEM house-staff route. That is why household cases must be drafted with the same discipline as corporate work-permit cases, albeit with additional vulnerability issues. 6

**For third-country** **nationals,** the ordinary route is often the first major difficulty. SEM's public summary states that ordinary admission is confined to managers, specialists and other qualified workers. Domestic roles such as ordinary cleaner, nanny, cook, driver or housekeeper will often sit awkwardly within that logic. Internal content should therefore avoid any generic suggestion that a household can simply sponsor a non-EU nanny or domestic helper in the same way that a company sponsors a specialist employee. In many cases, the key question is whether the file can genuinely fit the special house-staff exception rather than the ordinary skilled-worker route. 30

The **house-staff exception** is factually demanding. SEM says that household-task and childcare staff are treated as "qualified" either where they have already worked in a regular employment relationship for at least two years for the same family that is moving to or settling in Switzerland, or-if newly hired -where they can show at least five years' specific experience plus at least five years' lawful labour-market admission and residence in an EU/EFTA state. Failed recruitment efforts in Switzerland and EU/ EFTA states must also be shown. In practice, this means that "we now wish to recruit a domestic worker from outside Europe to join our household in Switzerland" is usually too simplistic and legally unsafe. 12

**The** **caregiving** **branch** is even narrower. For home-based care of disabled, seriously ill orcare-dependent persons, SEM requires medical evidence that constant home care is necessary and that other solutions, such as Spitex, are not possible; failed Swiss/EU-EFTA recruitment; specific training and experience in care; and a stable EU/EFTA residence history. Where the role includes actual nursing or medical-type care, SEM and SECO both add a further layer: the worker needs the relevant professional qualifications and, generally, cantonal health authorisation. SEM expressly states that direct recruitment from abroad of non-EU/EFTA nationals for 24-hour care is not possible. 31

Salary and living arrangements need especially careful treatment in domestic files. SEM's official position is not merely that some contractual salary must be paid, but that **local, professional and** **sectoral salary** **and** **employment conditions** must be met. Only contractually guaranteed pay elements count. In the house-staff chapter, SEM then adds a dense set of worker-protection rules: nature-of-pay deductions for accommodation and food must stay within official social-security maxima; a live-in worker may not be required to remain in the household during rest periods or holidays; and, if there is no shared household, rent should not exceed one-third of net salary. The federal NAV Hauswirtschaft minimums and the applicable cantonal NAV/CTT rules therefore matter directly to immigration compliance. 32

<!-- 8 -->

Role differentiation is **critical. A nanny, housekeeper, domestic manager, cook, cleanner,private** **driver, companion-carer** **and** **au** **pair** are not a single category in Swiss official materials. Au pair is a cultural-exchange route with age, duration, placement and language conditions, and the work must remain light household work/childcare tied to exchange and language learning. Zurich's own practice note reinforces the same point: if the au pair profile is not truly met, the relationship is treated as nannying or household help under the domestic-wvork regime. Internal content should therefore describe the actual role with precision and avoid relabelling ordinary paid care or domestic work as au pair. 33

Family support also needs precise handling. SEM is explicit that work between relatives is, in principle, still gainful employment. The narrow exception for ascendants or descendants on a short visitor stay exists only where the activity remains a social courtesy-typically temporary support that does not enable the parents to take up or increase employment and does not save necessary childcare costs. Once the support allows the Swiss-based household to work more or avoid paid care costs, the activity moves back into the work-permit sphere. For siblings, cousins and other collateral relatives, SEM says the permission requirement applies from the first day. 14

Private-household employment must also be distinguished from **posting** **or** **accompaniment.** Official SEM guidance says that work for a Swiss employer is "employment with taking up a post"; work for an employer seated abroad falls under the foreign-employer/cross-border service side. Under the free-movement framework, up to 90 days of posted work or short EU/EFTA employment may be handled through notification for eligible categories, and third-country posted workers under that route must already have durable access to an EU/EFTA labour market. By contrast, if a household in Switzerland hires the worker directly, this is not a posting case. Public official material directly on **private** **staff** **accompanying** **a** **non-working** **employer** was thin in the accessible federal sources used here; secondary current statutory mirrors, corroborated by an earlier official text, indicate that Art. 13 VZAE addresses private staff accompanying a non-working employer during a permit-free stay, but that point should be checked directly on live Fedlex before external publication. 34

Refusal and compliance risks are correspondingly high. Authorities are likely to be sceptical where the role is unskilled but framed as exceptional, where the labour-market search looks perfunctory,where the contract is vague or untranslated, where the live-in arrangement suggests excessive dependency, or where the role quietly combines housework and regulated nursing without proper authorisation. SEM also states that no business or **household** may employ a foreign worker, or use cross-border services, without verifying the right to work first. Geneva's official page underlines that employing foreign nationals without work authorisation is a criminal offence. 35

From an evidential perspective, a robust domestic-staff file should normally include: the signed employment contract; a precise job-description separating household duties, childcare and any care or nursing; salary, allowances and deductions; working time and rest-time arrangements; accommodation details; proof that the worker understands the contract; passport, CV and qualification evidence; recruitment proof; and, where relevant, a medical report, health-authorisation materials, proof of prior employment with the same family, or proof of the worker's EU/EFTA labour-market residence history. Proof of the private employer's identity and practical ability to honour salary, accommodation and

### Drafting cautions

Do not write as if a private household has a general right to sponsor a non-EU domestic worker. The ordinary third-country regime remains restrictive, and the relevant domestic-household route is exceptional and cumulative. 30

Do **not** imply that live-in status, room and board, or "being treated like one of the family" can replace salary and local working-condition compliance. Official Swiss material goes in the opposite direction: it tightens contract, accommodation, rest-time and deduction controls precisely because of dependency and exploitation risk. 32

Do **not** collapse au pair, paid nannying, domestic service and caregiving into one category. Au pair is a protected cultural-exchange model; household work is ordinary employment; home care may trigger additional professional authorisation; and 24-hour care for a single live-in worker is legally sensitive and cannot be presented as an unrestricted one-person model. 37

Do **not** assume unpaid or family-based help is automatically permission-free. SEM's position is that relatives' work is generally gainful employment, subject only to a narrow social-courtesy exception for some ascendants or descendants in short visitor situations. 38

**Do** **not** present posted work, accompanying foreign employers and direct Swiss household employment as interchangeable. The applicable route depends on who the employer is, where that employer is established, the worker's citizenship and status, and the duration and place of work. 39

### Source list

The principal federal instruments identified by official federal pages are the **Federal Act** **on** **Foreign** **Nationals and Integration,** the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment,the** **Agreement** on **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** for EU/EFTA access, and the domestic-work wage floor instruments under the **NAV** **Hauswirtschaft.** For domestic-household practice specifically, SEM also grounds its guidance in **ILO** **Convention** **No.** 189. 40

The key federal administrative materials are: **SEM's** **AIG** **directives chapter** 4, especially the concept of gainful employment, salary and employment conditions, household, family childcare, care roles, au pair and employer-duty sections; **SEM's labour-market basis page; SEM's procedure page;** and **SEM's** **application-document checklist.** 41

The most relevant SECO materials are: **Informationen** **zum** **NAV** **Hauswirtschaft** dated 1 January 2026; **Important information for caregivers; Die Frage nach der Regelung der Live-In Betreuung;** and SECO's **model contract** materials for private employers. 42

Open questions and limitations should be kept in mind. The current wording of **Art.** 13 **VZAE** on private staff accompanying a non-working employer was not directly verifiable through accessible live official federal text in this research set; secondary current statutory mirrors and an earlier official text indicate the rule's continuing existence, but any external-facing publication on that point should be checked directly on live Fedlex.
