# Swiss legal authority pack on academic, research and teaching work authorisation

Jurisdiction: Switzerland

**Purpose**: Internal immigration-law authority pack for content systems

**Scope**:Academic, research, scientific, university, teaching, visiting scholar, lecturer, fellowship, postdoctoral, and research-institution roles

## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law looks first at the **substance** **of the** **activity,** not the academic label attached to it. Under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, anyone who wishes to work in Switzerland requires authorisation regardless of the duration of the stay, and "gainful employment" includes any salaried or self-employed activity that is **normally carried out for payment, whether or not payment is actually** **made.** The Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Gainful Employment further provides that the cantonal labour-market authority decides whether a foreign national's activity counts as gainful employment, with doubtful cases referred to SEM. That matters in academic settings, because SEM's 2026 directives place academic guests, doctoral candidates, postdoctoral researchers and scholarship holders inside specific work-authorisation frameworks even where there is no Swiss salary or only a reduced allowance. 1

**For non-EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the starting point is the ordinary third-country work-admission regime: overall economic interest, employer-led application, quotas, labour-market precedence,local salary and working conditions, personal qualifications and accommodation. The Act also says that Switzerland's cultural and scientific needs must be taken appropriately into account, and it allows a derogation for recognised persons fromn the world of science. But these provisions do **not** create a general academic exemption from work authorisation; they operate inside the ordinary admission structure or within specific derogation routes. 2

The main Swiss **academic-specific authorisation** **channels** sit in the art. 30(1)(g) FNIA / art. 40 OASA space for education, continuing education and scientific specialisation, and in art. 41 OASA for organised international exchangJe. SEM's 2026 directives expressly treat doctoral candidates, postdoctoral researchers, MAS participants, scholarship holders and academic guests as identifiable categories within art. 40 OASA, with specific conditions on scientific nexus, host institution, duration and evidence. These are structured immigration routes, not casual visitor carve-outs. 3

**For** **Swiss-degree** **holders** **from** **third** **states,** FNIA art. 21(3) is the key study-to-work rule. It removes the labour-market precedence requirement where the proposed work is of high academic or economic interest, but SEM stresses that the role must still fit the person's completed Swiss degree and that the remaining admission requirements continue to apply. Separate from that, SEM also recognises a six-month short-stay permit to look for qualified work after completion of a Swiss university degree, subject to sufficient funds, adequate accommodation, and only limited ancillary work. 4

For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the AFMP framework is easier butstill classification-sensitive. Employment with a Swiss employer for up to three months per calendar year is dealt with by electronic notification rather than a residence permit. Cross-border service provision or posting from an EU/EFTA base runs through a separate 90-day notification framework, usually triggered once work exceeds eight days in a calendar year and always subject to procedural timing rules before work starts. Beyond 90 days, permit rules apply; for services beyond that ceiling, the official SEM user guide states there is no legal entitlement to a permit. 5

## Structural overview of academic research and teaching authorisation

The decisive Swiss questions are usually these: **who is the employer or principal, where is the work** **performed, what exactly is being done, is the activity one normally remunerated in the labour** **market, is the stay fundamentally educational or productive, is there a Swiss host institution,and** is **the** **person** **EU/EFTA** **or** **third-country?** OASA assigns classification of gainful employment to the cantonal labour-market authority, and the FNIA / OASA scheme then routes the person into either employment, study/training, self-employment, or cross-border service provisions. 6

**Swiss employment contract with a university or research institution.** If **a** Swiss university, ETH-domain body, or research institute employs the person to teach, research, supervise, or deliver other productive academic services, the default route is salaried employment under FNIA arts.18-25.For third-country nationals, that means the ordinary labour-market conditions apply unless a more specific derogation route can be justified. For EU/EFTA nationals, the permit form depends mainly on duration: up to three months is notification-based; longer periods require L or B residence documentation. The permit label follows the duration and purpose of stay, not the prestige of the academic title. 7

**Continuing-education and research-specialisation route under art. 40 OASA.** SEM places doctoral candidates, postdoctoral researchers, MAS participants, scholarship holders and academic guests inside this route where the stay is anchored in a university or university-of-applied-sciences setting and the activity remains tied to the person's scientific specialism or continuing academic formation. That makes art. 40 OASA especially important for fellows, sabbatical visitors, university-based research training and certain thesis-linked roles. 8

**International exchange** **under** **art.** 41 **OASA.** Where the stay sits within a bilateral or multilateral training, continuing-education orexchange programme, SEM treats art. 41 OASA as a possible basis. The exchange must be the principal purpose of the stay; reciprocity and temporariness are central; private projects are only exceptionally acceptable; and permits are generally short-stay permits unless a multi-year stay is justified from the outset. This basis is potentially relevant to organised teacher exchanges, visiting teaching fellowships or structured academic-mobility programmes, but not to ordinary Swiss labour-market recruitment disguised as "exchange". 9

**True** **non-gainful academic** **presence.** Swiss law does leave room for limited academic activity without gainful-employment treatment. The FNIA allows permit-free stay without gainful employment for up to three months, subject to any shorter visa limit, and SEM's directives give examples of non-gainful situations such as self-funded library research, theoretical familiarisation with equipment, or one-off exploratory contacts. But that safe space is narrow. Once the person is productively teaching, researching, supervising or otherwise performing work normally remunerated, the "academic" label stops being decisive. 10

**Unpaid work is not automatically immigration-neutral.** The same core rule that defines gainful employment irrespective of actual payment is reflected in SEM's academic-guest guidance: academic guests may participate in teaching and research in Switzerland even though, as a rule, they receive no Swiss salary. SEM also states more generally that even volunteer activity can fall within gainful-activity rules unless it meets a narrow ideological/social/environmental volunteer exception with low-hour limits.For academic content drafting, "unpaid" should therefore never be treated as a substitute for legal analysis. 11

**Self-employment and cross-border services are separate routes.** An independent lecturer,trainer, researcher or consultant is not a student or visitor merely because the engagement is short or intellectually focused. Third-country self-employment runs through FNIA art. 19. Cross-border academic work carried out for a foreign employer or principal is analysed as service provision or posting, not local Swiss emnployment, and the AFMP / notification rules then matter for EU/EFTA cases while FNIA art. 26and the third-country regime matter outside that sphere. 12

### Primary legal texts and official guidance

The primary statutory framework is **the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act** and the **Ordinance on** **Admission, Stay** **and** **Gainful Employment.** For this pack, the most important FNIA provisions are arts. 3, 10,11,18-27,30,32 and 33. The English FNIA text used here is expressly identified as an **informational** **translation** **without** **legal force,** so the binding law remains the official German, French and Italian consolidated texts. For OASA, the relevant provisions are arts. 1a-4, 12, 22, 31 and 38-41;the article wording consulted here came from a German consolidated mirror, while current SEM 2026directives confirm that those provisions remain the operative framework for theacademic categories under discussion. 23

The **EU/EFTA** **layer** sits under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the parallel EFTA regime. EDA's current overview describes the AFMP as granting Swiss and EU citizens the right to choose place of work and residence in the territory of the contracting parties, but only subject to conditions such as a valid employment contract for employed persons and proof of effective self-employment for the self-employed. EDA and SEM also emphasise that the AFMP simplifies access to the labour market, yet the service-provision branch is limited rather than fully liberalised in the way known inside the EU internal market. 24

For day-to-day academic immigration analysis, the **SEM directives** are the most useful official administrative materials. The 2026 chapter on gainful employment supplies the decisive operational rules for doctoral candidates, postdocs, academic guests, scholarship holders, MAS students, Swiss-degree graduate recruitment and international exchange; the companion 2026 directives outside chapter 4 deal with the six-month graduate job-search stay, student ancillary work and the approval practice for initial non-gainful permits for students, doctoral/postdoctoral researchers and academic guests from listed higher-risk states. 25

<!-- 6 -->

For **EU/EFTA** **practical compliance,** the key official administrative sources are SEM's factsheets on employment and cross-border service provision, SEM's 2026 user guide to the notification procedure, and SECO's pages on employer notification and permit duties. Together they distinguish Swiss employment from posting and self-employed service provision, explain the three-month and 90-day thresholds, fix the one-day and eight-day notification rules, and clarify when work must instead move into the permit system. 26

### Cantonal practice notes

Official **cantonal academic-specific guidance is comparatively thin.** SEM expressly notes that cantons are responsible for issuing permits, and its graduate-transition guidance leaves it to the cantons to regulate the practical conditions for the six-month Swiss-graduate job-search permit. In practice, most cantonal public materials do not create distinct legal categories for visiting scholars or researchers; they operationalise the federal rules through document checklists, portal instructions and labour-market evidentiary expectations. 27

The **Canton** **of Zurich** is the most useful published cantonal source consulted for third-country work-permit practice. Zurich states that third-country nationals require a permit from the first day,that there is no legal right to a specific permit or extension, and that applications are examined individually. For local employment, Zurich asks for the usual federal documentary package:employer application, concrete grounds, vacancy-posting proof where required, signed employment contract, qualificatioons, CV and passport copy. For cross-border assignments, Zurich treats the case as service provision under FNIA art. 26, waives precedence because the assignee is only temporarily in Switzerland, and requires assignment/service/project documentation plus salary and expense details. 28

The **Canton** **of Lucerne** provides a narrower but useful academic note for students from third states. It confirms that study-related work is permit-based, that ordinary ancillary work is capped at 15 hours per week, and that exceptions exist for third-country nationals undertaking continuing education at a university or university of applied sciences who work in their scienntific specialism. Lucerne's checklist also shows the kind of evidence cantons expect in practice: employer form, employment contract at local/sector pay, matriculation confirmation, school confirmation and passport copy, with work starting only after approval. 29

### Practical legal analysis

**University and research-institution employment.** **If** the person is being hired by a Swiss university, faculty, laboratory or research institute under a Swiss employment relationship, the core analysis is usually ordinary salaried employment. That means the third-country tests in FNJIA arts. 18-24 remain the default: overall economic interest, employer request, quotas, precedence, salary/work conditions, qualifications annd accommodation. SEM's own public admission summary applies those criteria across sectors and notes that only limited numbers of highly qualified non-EU/EFTA workers are admitted. Internal content should therefore avoid language suggesting that a professorial or research title, by itself, displaces the ordinary third-country scheme. 15

**Scientific interest** **and** **recognised** **scientists.** The Swiss statute expressly requires scientific needs to be taken into account, and art. 23(3)(b) allows admission by derogation for recognised persons from the world of science. SEM's own approval summary, however, indicates that ordinary "universities, science

and research" cases are generally handled under art. 23(1) FNIA or art. 30(1)(g) FNIA, wwith art. 23(3)(b) operating as an exception rather than the standard route. In practice, this means internationally prominent or unusually specialised scientific figures may be argued under the science derogation, but most university recruitment still needs a full employment-law and immigration-law analysis. 30

**Visiting academics, visiting scholars, fellows and guest lecturers.** SEM's academic-guestguidance is the clearest authority. It treats visiting professors or scientists on sabbatical who temporarily participate in the teaching and research activities of a Swiss federal or cantonal higher-education institution as cases under art. 40 OASA. As a rule, they do not receive salary in Switzerland, though they may receive a lower allowance than a permanent appointee, and where they come from the private sector they may continue to be remunerated by the previous employer if the Swiss university activity is closely connected to the earlier work. The application must include the Swiss university invitation, the secondment or sabbatical letter from the home university or previous employer, and remuneration data. The standard duration is up to one year, exceptionally two. This is exactly the sort of authority that blocks over-simplified claims that a"visiting" or "unpaid" academic stay is immigration-neutral. 17

**Research** **stays** **without** **Swiss** **employment.** There is no single answer. If the person is merely present in Switzerland without gainful employment for up to three months, FNIA art. 10 leaves room for a permit-free stay, and SEM's interpretive examples include self-funded library research and purely theoretical familiarisation with equipment. But once the person actively contributes teaching,research, supervision or other productive services normally remunerated in the labour market, the case moves back toward work-authorisation analysis under FNIA art. 11, art. 40 OASA, or a service/posting route. In other words, "no Swiss employment contract" does not mean "no immigration issue"; the legal question is whether the Swiss-facing activity is productive work, continuing education, or merely non-gainful presence. 31

**Doctoral candidates.** SEM's 2026 directives treat doctoral candidates as a defined art. 40 OASA category. The permitted structures include thesis-linked work at the academic institution, external wwork linked to the thesis where there is a written cooperation agreement between the university and private employer and the doctoral candidate's copyright position is protected, and certain limited outside work of up to 15 hours weekly where the activity does not delay thesis completion. SEM says the doctoral status is limited to the dissertation period, ordinarily three or four years and at the outside eight years, with the authorities expected to ensure that the dissertation can be completed within the available period. That makes doctoral roles a poor fit for loose "student visitor" drafting. 32

**Postdoctoral roles.** SEM handles postdocs under the same art. 40 OASA umbrella but with tighter sequencing rules. The person must hold a Swiss or foreign doctorate and continue training through research connected to earlier study and work; the activity may be supplemented by teaching as an assistant. The maximum stay is eight years from award of the doctorate, counting earlier doctoral stays, and the postdoctoral stay must begin before two years have elapsed fromn the end of the dissertation, subject only to specific-purpose derogations. These details are too specific to be inferred from generic university webpages and should be lifted only from SEM-level authority. 33

**Scholarship** **holders** **and** **funded fellows.** SEM explicitly distinguishes scholarship holders as persons with an academic degree or equivalent higher-technical diploma who have obtained funding from a Swiss, foreign or international body in order to acquire a specialism or continue research. The status runs only for the duration of the scholarship as shown by supporting evidence. The legal point is that a stipend or grant is not irrelevant: it can be the very basis for a structured academic status under art. 40OASA. At the same time, funding status does not by itself decide whether the person is outside gainful-employment rules, because the activity itself still matters. 17

**Paid teaching, lectures, seminars, workshops and supervision.** Swiss law does not create a special migration-free zone for academnic teaching. If the activity is done for a Swiss employer, it is treated as employment. If it is performed for a foreign university or another non-Swiss principal, it may instead be a cross-border service or posting. OASA defines cross-border service as the provision of a temporary service in Switzerland under a contractual relationship by a person or undertaking based abroad. Zurich's published practice then shows the evidence expected in such assignment cases: a service or project contract, project description, salary data and expense assumptions, while labour-market precedence is waived because the assignment istemporary. The classification is therefore factual and contractual, not terminological. 34

**Short academic** **engagements.** For third-country nationals, Swiss law remains strict: work authorisation is required from day one, regardless of howw short the academic engagement may be. OASA then distinguishes a short-stay framework under which a person with entry authorisation for gainful employment or cross-border services for a total of up to four months in twelve months need not register after arrival, but that is not the same as being permit-free. By contrast, an EU/EFTA national taking up employment with a Swiss employer for up to three months per calendar year can work through electronic notification, while EU/EFTA service providers or posted workers can ordinarily use the 90-day notification regime, subject to the eight-day rule and the AFMP's service limits. Beyond those ceilings, permit rules apply. 35

**Swiss** **graduate** **transitions.** SEM's guidance is comparatively favourable but still disciplined. "Swiss university degree" covers recognised Swiss universities and universities of applied sciences and also includes cases where the person completed only a Swiss master's or doctorate. The guidance expressly mentions institutions in the Swiss university sector and gives examples oof federally supported research institutions such as EMPA, PSI and EAWAG when explaining the university landscape. For art. 21(3), SEM interprets "science" broadly in light of academic freedom and international competition for talent, but it excludes generic roles lacking a qualified link to the completed studies, giving administrative work outside the field as an exammple. The six-month search permit is available once the person has successfully completed the studies, on proof of sufficient funds and adequate accommodation, and a school statement is enough to prove completion. 36

**Self-employment and independent academic activity.** An academic who enters Switzerland to provide independent lecturing, training or research consultancy is not in a study or guest route merely because the services are intellectual or educational. Third-country self-employment is governed by FNIA art. 19 and requires the necessary financial and operational conditions as well as an adequate and independent source of incomne. EU/EFTA nationals have a simpler path: SEM's own factsheet says they will initially receive a B EU/EFTA permit if they can show an effective self-employed activity when applying. Internal content should therefore separate salaried academic work from genuine independent professional activity. 37

**Evidence and** **refusal** risk. The official materials consistently point to the same recurring evidential themes: signed employment or assignment contract, host invitation, sabbatical or secondment confirmation, remuneration data, CV, diplomas and translations, project description, scholarship proof, matriculation or school letter, proof of means and proof of accommodation. Zurich and Lucerne both show how seriously cantonal authorities treat documentary completeness. Refusal or compliance risk rises where the case is misclassified as "study" or "visitor" despite productive work, where the role does not match the persoon's training, where labour-market or posting evidence is missing, where notification thresholds are exceeded, or where long serial academic stays appear to be circumventing the structure of temporary study/postdoctoral residence. 38

#### Drafting cautions

Do not write that academic purpose, visiting status, fellowship status, or absence of Swiss salary automatically removes work-authorisation requirements. The core statute says gainful employment exists even where payment is not made, and SEM's own academic-guest guidance proves that unpaid or externally funded teaching and research can still sit inside a specific authorisation route. 39

**Do** **separate** **the** **route labels carefully.** Content should distinguish: Swiss salaried employment; foreign-employer posting or cross-border service; study/training without gainful employment; art.40OASA continuing-education/scientific-specialism cases; art. 41 OASA exchange cases; and genuine self-employment. Collapsing these into one basket called "academic mobility" would be legally unsafe. 40

Do **not** equate stipend, grant, allowance and expenses. SEM treats scholarship funding as a formal basis for academic status in some cases; academic guests may have no Swiss salary but still need authorisation; and for posted workers Swiss law distinguishes expense compensation for travel, board and lodging from salary. These distinctions matter for classification, wage review and documentary evidence. 41

Do **not** oversell the Swiss-graduate route. FNIA art. 21(3) removes only precedence. SEM expressly says the remaining admission requirements still apply, interprets science broadly but not infinitely, and excludes generic roles without a qualified connection to the completed Swiss studies. The six-month search permit is also temporary, non-extendable, and linked to funds and accommodation. 4

**Do flag cantonal points as practice, not black-letter law.** Cantons issue permits and publish useful operational guidance, but the controlling legal framework remains federal law, the OASA and SEM guidance. Cantonal sources should therefore be used to evidence documentation habits, processing expectations and locally published interpretations, not to override federal texts. 42
