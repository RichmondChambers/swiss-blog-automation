## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law treats **gainful activity** broadly. As reflected in the SEM's current directives, any activity that goes beyond a mere favour and is **normally** **performed** **for** **remuneration** is potentially gainful activity, even if in the concrete case it is unpaid or paid only modestly, and evenn if it is only a side activity. The **duration** of the activity and the fact that it is described as "secondary", "seasonal" or "short-term" do not take it outside wvork-authorisation analysis. This matters acutely in sport, where accommodation,meals, transport or other support may be enough to move an arrangement into the immigration-law concept of gainful activity. 1

The SEM's sports-specific guidance draws a three-way distinction that should anchor internal analysis. First, some sports activity is treated as **not gainful activity,** including participation in certain **international** **championships,** training outside competition and without engagement by a Swiss employer, and genuinely unpaid amateur sport. Secondly, **tests, trial** **matches, friendlies** **and** **related** **training** can still count as **gainful activity without taking employment** and are channelled through the rules on activity "without taking employment", notably Article 14 OASA/VZAE. Thirdly, where a foreign athlete is **engaged by a Swiss sports club to participate in a championship,** SEM treats that as **gainful** **activity** **with** **taking** **employment,** which brings the ordinary permit or notification rules into play from the outset. 2

The label **"amateur"** **is** **not determinative.** The SEM expressly states that paid amateur sport is permit-relevant gainful activity, and that the same is true where the athlete receives another financial benefit such as **sponsorship** **income** or **substantial prize** **money.** Very small expenses or insignificant prizes may remain tolerable, but admission cannot be built around unpaid amateur sport in its own right, and if a person has no other right to stay in Switzerland, unpaid amateur participation cannot be invoked to obtain a residence or work permission. 3

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the key split is between employment by a Swiss employer, posting by an EU/ EFTA employer, and EU/EFTA self-employed service provision. A Swiss clubor sports organisation may employ an EU/EFTA national for up to **three** **months** **/** 90 **days** **per** **calendar year** by way ofthe **notification** **procedure;** beyond that, an L, B **or** G **EU/EFTA** **permit** is required depending on contract duration and living arrangements. Posted workers and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers may also use notification for up to **90 effective** **working** **days,** subject to the detailed rules on day-counting and prior notification. 4

For **third-country** **nationals,** the sports-specificroute is narrow and evidence-heavy. The SEM's current guidance requires, in essence, a **professional-level** **dossier**: usually a club in one of the **top** **two** **Swiss** **leagues,** an athlete with **solid multi-year top-level experience,** or a coach with a **recognised diploma** **and** **Swiss** **federation** **recognition;** the sporting role should in principle be **full-time;** remuneration must meet Swiss **local, occupational and** **sectoral standards;** and cantonal pre-decisions for professional athletes and coaches are subject to **SEM** **approval.** The SEM says thhat sports cases should usually be handled first through a **short-stay permit,** that **a trial engagement cannot be approved,** and that an ordinary residence route requires a **multi-year** **contract.** 5

The most important drafting risk is over-simplification. Internal content should **not** say that sport is outside work-authorisation rules merely because it is **seasonal, short-term, friendly, semi-professional** or labelled **amateur.** The official materials instead require a factual analysis of **who** **employs whom, what the person actually does in Switzerland, whether anything of value is** **received,which competition level is involved, what the contract says,** and **which nationality / free-movement route is available.** 6

## Structural overview and legal propositions

**Structural overview.** In practice, Swiss sports immigration analysis works best when split into four questions. The first is whether the case involves **employment** **by** **a** **Swiss** **club** **or** **Swiss** **sports** **organisation.** The second is whether the activity is instead a **cross-border** **service** performed for a **foreign** **employer** or by a **self-employed** provider. The third is whether the facts fall into the narrow class of activity that SEM treats as **not** **gainful activity** at all, such as certain international championship participation. The fourth is whether the person is a **professional athlete/coach,** an apparently "amateur" participant receiving value, or other staff such as technical, medical or administrative personnel, for whom the ordinary worker/service-provider framework usually governs. 7

**For Swiss-employer** **cases,** nationality is decisive. EU/EFTA nationals normally use the notification procedure up to three months / 90 days, then move to L/B/G EU/EFTA permits. Third-country nationals require a permit route from the outset, and the sports-specific SEM filters for professional athletes and coaches then matter. For **foreign-employer / no-take-up-of-employment cases,** the key distinction is between the AFMP notification regime for qualifying EU/EFTA postings or EU/EFTA self-employed services, and the more restrictive Article 14 OASA/VZAE route for non-free-movement cases. 8

**For semi-professional** sport, the official materials do not create a separate immigration category. Instead, they split sport into fully professional leagues, sports with a **limited** **professional status,** and sports that are **mainly** **amateur.** Even in mainly amateur sports, a paid role remains permit-relevant, and SEM's sports salary annex allows only a narrow side-employment model with a **60/40** **income** **split** and only **one such permit per club per season.** 9

## Primary legal texts

The governing legal framework is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (AIG / LEI /** **LStrI)** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment (VZAE / OASA / OASA).** In the official SEM material reviewed, the decisive provisions for this topic are: the **concept** of **gainful activity** and its broad scope; the distinction between activity **with** and **without** **taking** **employment;** the third-country labour-market admission rules on Swiss economic interest, quotas, salary/working conditions and qualifications; and the implementing provisions for **short-stay, residence, wage** **checking,** and the decision-making competence of the cantons and SEM. The sports-specific rules found are not in a separate sports immigration act; they are applications of the general AIG/OASA system to sports professionals. 27

<!-- 7 -->

**For EU/EFTA nationals,** the decisive instruments are the **Agreement on the Free Movement of** **Persons (AFMP / FZA / ALCP)** and the Swiss **Ordinance on Free Movement of Persons (VFP / OLCP),** together with the SEM and SECO materials applying them. Those materials treat **notification** **and** **permit** as distinct mechanisms: notification is tied mainly to a short-term activity, whereas the permit is tied mainly to the person and the longer-term residence/employment constellation. The AFMP/VFP materials also clarify the 90-day service liberalisation, the different treatment of Swiss-employer hires, postings and self-employed services, and the narrow cases in which notification and permit may coexist in the same calendar year. 28

A further instrument in practice is the **DFJP** **approval ordinance** cited in the SEM directives as **OA-DFJP** **/** **ZV-EJPD.** The current SEM sports chapter states that cantonal pre-decisions admitting **professional** **athletes** **and** **coaches** are subject to federal approval because these cases are treated as involving **special professional knowledge** **or abilities.** That approval point is important for internal systems becauuse it confirms that sports dossiers are not merely routine cantonal filings. 12

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

**The single most important authority set** for this topic is the SEM's current **Chapter 4 "Stay with** **gainful employment",** in its German and French versions dated **state** **1** **January** **2026,** together with the SEM annexes specifically titled **"Professional athletes and coaches"** and **"Salary and wvorking** **conditions** of **professional athletes".** Those documents contain the operative administrative rules on what counts as gainful sporting activity, which leagues qualify, the evidence expected, the treatment of lower leagues and juniors, and the reference wage floors used by SEM in approval practice. Although the sports salaryannex itself dates from **2016,** it is still listed by SEM on the current 2026 chapter page as the active sports-specific annex. 29

**For EU/EFTA and short-term cross-border activity,** the central official materials are the SEM's **notification procedure** pages and user guides, the SEM's **VFP annex on notification and permits,** and SECO's pages on the **Meldeverfahren** and on employing foreign workers in Switzerland. Taken together, these sources are the safest basis for statements about the 90-day system, the 8-day threshold for service providers, who may file the notification, who still needs a permit, and the role of cantonal authorities in processing notifications and permit applications. 30

**On** **cantonal** **practice,** the clearest athlete-specific source found was the **Canton** **of Zurich** page on work permits for individual sectors. Zurich essentially mirrors the SEM sports chapter but adds useful operational detail for internal drafting: permits for third-country professional athletes are described as **full-time only, limited to at most one year, tied to the post,** and supported by a concise evidence set including the application reasoning, signed contract, passport copy, palmarès, diplomas/licences and, for trainers, Swiss federation recognition. 31

Other cantonal material located was mainly **general rather** **than** **sport-specific,** but still useful for process notes. **Geneva** states in general third-country salaried cases that the employer must apply, that the canton examines Swiss economic interest, labour-market priority, qualifications, quotas, salary/ working conditions and **full-time** **employment,** and that work may start only once the relevant authorisation is in hand unless the person holds an entry authorisation with taking employment. **Ticino** gives practical short-term-notification instructions and explicitly states that EU/EFTA hires by Swiss employers must be notified **by** **the** **day** **before** **work** **starts,** while third-country hires remain subject to the permit route and labour-market examination; Ticino also requires new activity or a job change to be notified before the new employment begins. These cantonal pages are best used as **practice** **notes,** not as a substitute for the SEM sports chapter. 32

## Practical legal analysis

**Professional and semi-professional sport as gainful activity.** The internal system should assume that professional sport, semi-professional sport and any sport ordinarily done for value are within Swiss work-authorisation analysis unless a clearly defined exception applies. This remains true even where the cash salary is modest or absent, because Swiss immigration law looks to whether the activity is one normally performed for remuneration, and the SEM expressly says that accommodation, meals or similar basic support do not stop it being gainful activity. This is why "volunteer", "semi-pro", "expenses only" and "academy support package" are risky descriptors if the underlying sporting performance has labour-market value. 33

**Amateur sport.** The official rule is not "amateurs do not need permits"; it is narrower. A person who already has a lawful stay status in Switzerland may practise sport as an amateur if there is no **remuneration** **and** **no** **other** **financial benefit,** save for minor expenses or negligible prize money,and if amateur sport is **not the** **main** **purpose** **of** **stay.** But if the same activity is carried on for money or in-kind value, it becomes gainful activity evenif federation rules still call it amateur sport. Internal drafting should therefore avoid treating the amateur/professional divide as a federation-labelling question; the decisive issue is **immigration-law remuneration and labour-market impact.** 34

**Contracts, salary, seasonality** **and** **side** **work.** Swiss practice does not create a separate immigration category for "season contracts". Instead, the relevant questions are contract duration, whether the sporting role is genuinely professional, and whether the salary and working conditions meet the SEM/ cantonal thresholds. For third-country sports professionals, the SEM expects the sporting role to be full-**time,** says remuneration must come **through** **the** **club,** and says thatfor coaches and player-coaches a side occupation is in **principle** **excluded.** The sports salary annex then adds concrete minimum reference wages and a tightly controlled side-job model for sports that remain largely amateur in Switzerland. This means a "seasonal" sports contract is not permit-free; it is more likely to be analysed as a **short-stay** **sports** **employment,** and if the club wants a longer residence route, the SEM requires a **multi-year contract.** 35

**Short competitions, tournaments, triaIs, demonstrations and events.** The SEM materials require very careful categorisation. Participation in some **internationalI championships** is treated as non-gainful. By contrast, **aptitude tests, trial games, friendlies and related training** are treated as **gainful activity** **without taking** **employment** and pushed into Article 14 OASA/VZAE logic. Actual **engagement by a Swiss club for championship participation** is gainful activity with taking employment. That structure means a short sports visit can fall into three wholly different legal analyses depending on the facts. Official material is relatively thin on "demonstrations" and "exhibition events" as such, so internal content should analogise conservatively: if the person is **actively** **performing** **or** **supplying** **sporting** **services** **in** **Switzerland,** treat the activity as potentially work-regulated unless the championship/non-gainful category clearly applies. 2

**Notification versus permit in sports cases.** For an **EU/EFTA athlete or coach employed directly by a** **Swiss** **club** for up to three months, the Swiss employer should use the **notification** **procedure;over** that threshold, the person needs the relevant EU/EFTA permit route. For **posted** **foreign** **staff** or **EU/** **EFTA** **self-employed** **service** **poroviders** coming in for an event, training block or support assignment, the 90-day notification route may be available, and the 8-day notification-free threshold may still operate outside the published first-day sectors. For **third-country** **nationals,** the position is more restrictive: a Swiss-employer sports case is permit-based from the start; a foreign-employer/no-employment service case can use Article 14 OASA/VZAE only within its limits; and third-country workers posted from EU/EFTA employers can use the AFMP route only if they have already been integrated into the EU/EFTA labour market forat least 12 months. 36

<!-- 9 -->

**Coaches, trainers, technical staff, medical staff and club staff.** The SEM sports chapter has a genuine sports-specific regime for **professional athletes and** **coaches.** It does **not** provide an equally detailed separate sports-specific chapter for technical analysts, medical personnel, kit staff, logistics staff or ordinary club administrators. The safest readingis therefore that coaches use the sports chapter, while other sports staff are analysed under the ordinary rules for **employees, posted** **workers,** **self-employed** **service** **providers** or, if relevant, regulated professions. Internal content should not assume that all club-related staff benefit from the athlete/coach rules; in many cases the ordinary labour-market and service-provider framework will be the correct one. That is an inference from the scope of the published guidance and should be stated as such. 37

**Evidence and** **decision-making.** In sports files, the evidence package matters as much as the legal classification. The SEM wants a binding written contract or at least binding contract offer setting out the essential termms, and the sports chapter adds a **palmarès** showing the level at which the athlete has actually competed, plus salary detail and any in-kind benefits. Coaches also need the relevant **diploma** and **Swiss** **federation** **recognition.** Zurich mirrors those requirements in its online branch notes.In doubtful classification cases, the current SEM directives say the canton decides first, but doubtful cases should be submitted to the SEM with the relevant facts annd a reasoned cantonal position.38

**Refusal and** **compliance** **risk.** The clearest high-risk patterns in the official materials are: lower-league third-country placements; juniors/seniors presented as professional admissions; loans to lower leagues; trial engagements dressed up as short permits; unpaid or underpaid "amateur" arrangements that in truth include club support, sponsor money or prize values; academy/student routes being used as holding patterns for future pro careers; and work starting before notificatioon or authorisation is properly in place. The official materials also warn against assuming that notification and permit routes can be mixed freely within the same calendar year, and they emphasise that unauthorised employment can trigger penal and administrativeconsequences. 39

A final point for internal content: I did **not** locate a current official SEM sports note expressly waiving the ordinary **third-country** **labour-market-priority** logic. The general SEM and cantonal third-country materials still present labour-market priority as part of the default framework, while the sports annexes emphasise league level, sporting profile and wages. The prudent drafting position is therefore to avoid saying that third-country athletes or coaches enjoy a blanket labour-market-priority exemption unless a current canton-specific orSEM source confirms that proposition for the type of case being described. 26

## Drafting cautions

Do not write that "amateur sport is outside work-permit law". The official position is narrower:only genuinely unpaid amateur activity, with no other meaningful financial benefit and not as the main purpose of stay, may sit outside gainful-activity analysis; once money, sponsorship, material support or meaningful prize value appears, the work-authorisation question reopens. 34

Do **not** write that "short-term" or "seasonal" sports work is permit-free. Swiss law makes duration largely irrelevant to whether something is gainful activity. The real questions are nationality, employer location, whether the case is one of employment or service provision, and whether an EU/EFTA notification route exists. 40

<!-- 10 -->

**Do** **not** let the content system collapse distinct buckets into one. It should separately recognise: **international championship participation, non-employment tests/friendlies, Swiss-club** **championship employment, EU/EFTA notification cases, posted-worker/self-employed service** **cases, and third-country permit cases.** 41

Do **not** assume that all sports-adjacent staff fall under "athlete" rules. Coaches are specifically regulated in the SEM sports chapter; medical, technical and administrative staff are not, andshould usually be analysed under the ordinary employment, posting or self-employment rules. 42

Do **not** rely on student, academy or "sports school" framing for third-country youth cases without a separate immigration analysis. The SEM's express warning on "sport students" is strong, and future professionalisation after schooling is treated as a refusal risk if the sports-admission criteria are not met. 25

Do **not** present the SEM sports wage figures as a complete Swiss salary statement. They function as **administrative** **reference** **minima** within the current SEM annex, but Article 22 wage scrutiny still looks to law,collective or standard contracts, same-employer comparators and statistical wage material. Where the local market commands more, the higher figure may still matter. 9
