# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Religious Workers and Mission Roles

Suggested filename:

legalauthorityspecialemploymentreligiousworkersandmissionrolespack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law does not treat religious purpose as an automatic exemption from work authorisation. The State Secretariat for Migration (SEM) interprets "gainful activity" broadly: an activity counts if it is **normally** **remunerated** **on** **the** **labour** **market,** even where the individual case is unpaid or only minimally supported. SEM's directives expressly state that religious activities, including work as a religious caregiver and as a missionary, are to be treated as gainful activity when they are "normally gainful"; the 20)23 SEM circular on short stays reiterates that the same broad approach applies to religious caregivers, missionaries and volunteers. 1

For **third-country** **nationals,** the default framework remains the ordinary Swiss labour-admnission regime: overall economic interest, quotas, precedence of workers already entitled to the Swiss labour market, local salary and employment conditions, personal qualifications and suitable accommodation. SEM's guidance on the labour market confirms that these Articles 18-24 criteria apply across sectors, and the religious-caregiver rules in the FNIA directives state that those ordinary labour-market rules must be met **cumulatively** with the special religious-caregiver integration rules in Article 26a AIG. 2

Swiss law does recognise a **special admission track for "religious caregivers"** under Article 26a AIG, but that category is narrow. SEM defines it as people who, for their faith community, are the **central** **spiritual and** **ritual point** **of** **reference.** SEM gives examples suchas priests, pastors, rabbis, imams, monks and nuns in certain traditions. The same guidance expressly says that **missionary** **activity** **oriented** **outside** **the** **faith** **community** is **not** within that narrow religious-caregiver circle, nor are functions that are primarily ritual rather than central pastoral/spiritual roles. That distinction matters: it means that an applicant may be engaged in religious or mission-based work for immigration purposes, yet still fall **outside** the special Article 26a religious-caregiver route. 3

For religious caregivers who do fit the Article 26a route, SEM's published criteria are demanding. In addition to Articles 18-24 AIG, the person must satisfy **integration-related** **conditions,** including familiarity with the Swiss legal and social value system and language ability at B1 **oral** / A1 **written** in the local official language, subject to specified evidential alternatives and a short-stay exception. SEM also requires that the religious community and the caregiver recognise Swiss legal norms, comply with the Constitution and laws in theory and practice, require the same of members, and condemn misconduct. 4

Unpaid, stipend-based or maintenance-supported roles are **not automatically** **outside** **immigration** **control.** SEM states that an activity can still be gainful even if it is fully unpaid or if payment only covers the person's basic subsistence, including food and accommodation. For religgious caregivers, SEM expects ordinary remuneration reflecting qualifications and responsibility, save for a limited exception **where a historically rooted religious vow of poverty** is proved and the community undertakes full maintenance and related costs. 5

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the analysis changes because of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. SEM states that EU/EFTA nationals employed in Switzerland for up to three months or 90 days per calendar year can use the **notification** **procedure,** and longer employment normally moves into the usual EU/EFTA residence-permit system. Posted workers and self-employed service providers from the EU/EFTA may also use notification up to 90 working days if they satisfy the relevant conditions; for posted **third-country** workers from an EU/EFTA undertaking, SEM requires prior long-term admission to that EU/EFTA labour market, generally for at least 12 months. 6

Short religious events require careful role analysis. SEM's 2023 circular says that if a person at a religious festival or within a religious community is actively **performing** music, liturgy, sermons or comparable tasks, and is not merely present as a visitor, that will **generally** be treated as gainful activity. By contrast, SEM's annex on the concept of gainful activity says that **passive** **participation** in religious conferences or events, especially without givingJ a presentation, is not treated as gainful activity. For third-country cross-border service, SEM's circular points to the general rule that authorisation is required once activity exceeds **eight assignment days** in a year in most sectors; direct employment is permit-based from the outset. 7

The safest drafting position for internal immigration content is therefore this: Swiss law contains a **narrow and highly regulated** route for third-country **religious caregivers,** but it does **not** create a general religious exemption, and the sources reviewed do **not** disclose aseparate federal "missionary permit" category. Missionaries, volunteers, visiting clergy, charitable staff and administrative personnel all need role-by-role analysis under the ordinary immigration and labour-market rules, with only limited and highly specific exemptions. 8

## Structural overview of religious-worker and mission-role authorisation

The Swiss framework works best if organised around the **nature** **of the** **activity, the** **nationality** **category,** and whether the role is **inside** **or** **outside** the narrow Article 26a religious-caregiver definition. The structure below reflects the official materials reviewed. 9

**·Private religious activity with no labour-market element.** Worship, prayer and comparable private observance are outside the work-authorisation problem unless the stay itself requires another immigration basis. SEM's materials also preserve a narrow monastic exception for contemplative orders wvhere the person does not carry out specific preaching or pastoral work and the life form is not a labour-market activity. 10

**·Passive attendance** **at religious** **events.** SEM's annex states that passive participation in religious congresses or events, especially without holding a presentation, is not treated as gainful activity. This is the safer category for ordinary visitors, retreat attendees as participants, or conference delegates who do not deliver pastoral or performative content. 11

**·Active religious work by a third-country national for a Swiss religious organisation.** This falls within the ordinary third-country labour-admission structure. If the role is that of a narrowly defined **religious** **caregiver,** Articles 18-24 AIG and Article 26a AIG both apply, together with SEM approval of the cantonal preliminary decision. If the role is religious but not a "religious

caregiver", the official materials still treat it as gainful activity but do not provide the same special route. 12

**·Missionary, volunteer, charitable, community or administrative roles.** SEM's sources make two points clearly: missionary and volunteer activity can still be gainful activity; and missionary roles are not within the narrow "religious caregiver" class. Beyond that, the federal materials are thin. The conservative reading is that these roles default to ordinary immigration analysis, unless some other specific rule applies. That is an inference from the official material, not an express separate code for each role. 13

**·Short-term active visits by clergy, musicians or speakers.** SEM's 2023 circular says active religious functions at festivals or within communities usually count as gainful activity. For third-country cross-border service, the ordinary eight-day threshold is relevant in most sectors; for direct employment, authorisation is required from the first day. For exceptional short assignments of up to four months, the directives allow aspecial short-stay route where the assignment is justified, for example by a major religious festival or replacement needs. 14

**·EU/EFTA nationals and eligible service providers.** EU/EFTA nationals have easier market access under free movement rules. Employment in Switzerland for up to three months can use the notification procedure; longer work moves into the EU/EFTA permit system. Posted workers and self-employed EU/EFTA service providers can also use notification up to 90 effective working days, subject to the SEM rules. 15

### Primary legal texts and official guidance

The core federal legal framework is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration** (AIG / FNIA, SR 142.20) and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment** (VZAE / OASA, SR 142.201), both identified on SEM's official legal-bases page. For this topic, the most relevant AIG provisions are the provisions on gainful activity and labour admission, especially Articles 11, 18-24, 26a, 62 letter d, and the general discretionary and public-order provisions referred to in the official guidance; the most relevant VZAE provisions are the provisions on gainful activity and labour-market procedure, especially Articles 1a-4, 14, 19, 20, 22b and the language / integration evidential provisions referenced by SEM. 30

The most important administrative authority is the **SEM** **FNIA** **directives,** especially chapter 4 on stay with gainful activity. These directives are the central operational source for this subject. They define gainful activity broadly; create the detailed branch rule on **religious** **activities;** explain Article 26a admissions for religious caregivers; specify language and values-based integration evidence; describe the short-stay route for special religious occasions; and set out the volunteer-work guidance and the need for SEM approval in Article 26a cases. 31

The key short-stay authority is SEM's **circular** **of** **5** **October 2023** on persons entering Switzerland for religious activities for less than 90 days. It confirms that religious caregivers, volunteers, missionaries and artists can all fall within gainful activity; says active religious performance at festivals or within communities usually counts as work; coonfirms that cross-border service is generally permit-based after more than eight days in a year in most sectors; and instructs the employer or inviting community to file the relevant canton-level application before visa issuance where work authorisation is needed. 25

A further official SEM annex on the **concept** of **gainful activity** is especially useful for the visitor / speaker / retreat distinction. It states that passive participation in religious congresses or events without a presentation is not gainful activity, while pastoral work appears in the positive examples of work-like activity. In practice, this is one of the clearest official materials for distinguishing a non-working religious guest from a performing or preaching religious visitor. 11

For integration and public-order-linked evidence, SEM publishes a one-page **declaration** **on** **familiarity** **with** **the** **Swiss** **social and** **legal value** **system** for Article 26a cases. The form requires the signer to confirm familiarity with Swiss norms, public security and order, the constitutional value system and Swiss law, and notes that breach can lead to revocation under Article 62 letter d AIG. 32

<!-- 7 -->

For general labour-market evidence, SEM's official pages on the admission of third-state workers and application documents remain important even in religious cases. They summarise the overall economic interest test, quotas, precedence, salary and employment conditions, accommodation, personal qualifications, translation requirements and the minimum application bundle. 33

The international framework for EU/EFTA nationals is the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** as described on SEM's free-movement pages and implemented through the SEM notification-procedure materials. Those materials explain the three-month / 90-day notification route, the posted-worker and self-employed-service routes, and the special rule for third-country workers posted from an EU/EFTA undertaking who have already been integrated into that EU/EFTA laboour market for at least 12months. 6

SECO becomes relevant on two points. First, vacancy-notice obligations may apply where the relevant occupation falls within sectors with sufficiently high unemployment; second, SECO's illegal-work materials frame undeclared work as non-compliance with work-related notification and authorisation duties under, among other things, foreigner law, and explain the sanctions architecture under the federal black-work legislation. 34

## Cantonal practice notes

Public cantonal guidance is uneven. In the sources reviewed, the **Canton** **of Zurich** provides the clearest religion-specific page. Zurich mirrors the SEM position that religious caregivers are key persons for integration, requires recognition of Swiss legal norms and constitutional order, limits the category to central spiritual and ritual leaders, excludes missionary roles oriented outside the faith community, requires a financially sustainable community, theological training and experience, full-time exclusive pastoral work, B1 oral / A1 written language proof, evidence of unsuccessful domestic / EU/EFTA recruitment, and a values-system declaration. Zurich also lists a concrete document bundle for applications. 35

The **Canton** **of Geneva** publishes a general page for third-state employment that is unusually useful for this topic because it states expressly that work-authorisation applications are required in principle for **all gainful activity,** including activityas a trainee, apprentice, volunteer, social worker, artist or **missionary.** Geneva also describes proof of Swiss / EU labour-market searches, qualification level, quotas, salary compliance, full-time work expectations, possible SEM approval, and criminal liability for employing a foreign worker without the required authorisation. 36

Geneva's public **EU/EFTA** page aligns with the federal free-movement structure. It says that EU/AELE nationals with Swiss employment of more than three months must apply for residence authorisation with work, while any gainful activity of less than three months must be handled through the SEM online notification procedure. It also sets out when L or B EU/EFTA permits are issued and preserves the 120-day/up-to-four-month route. 37

**The** **Canton** **of Ticino** and the **Canton** **of Graubünden** publish general short-stay labour-guidance pages that confirm the ordinary 90-day notification route for EU/EFTA nationals and posted workers, and the ordinary requirement that third-state workers need authorisation from the first day. However, in the public materials located for this research, those cantons do **not** publish religion-specific admission criteria comparable to Zurich's more detailed page. 38

A useful official federal background point is that the Federal Council has itself acknowledged that the special admission rules for religious caregivers cover only **a small share** of the religious caregivers

That helps explain why cantonal public material is often sparse and why practice must often be reconstructed from general labour-market sources plus the specific Article 26a rules. 39

## Practical legal analysis

**Religious** **work** **as** **gainful activity.** The safest starting point is to ask whether the role is one that is **normally carried out for remuneration on the labour market.** If yes, Swiss immigration law can treat it as gainful activity even where the church, mosque, temple, mmission or religious association pays nothing or only covers subsistence. That is why sermons, pastoral care, missionary work, event performance and organised volunteer placements can all still trigger immigration controls. 16

**Private devotion and contemplative monastic life remain distinct.** The official materials preserve a narrow carve-out for contemplative religious life in a monastery where there is no specific preaching or pastoral function and the life form is not one ordinarily offered on the labour market. SEM ties this directly to BGE 118 Ib 81. This point should not be overstated: once a monastic or religious person takes on specific preaching, pastoral, missionary or comparable outward-facing functions, the analysis moves back into gainful-activity territory. 10

**The special Swiss route is for "religious caregivers", not for all faith-based workers.** Article 26a AIG, as implemented by SEM, is designed around people who occupy a recognised **bridge** **function** in integration questions and who are the **central spiritual and** **ritual** **reference** **point** within an existing faith community. Official examples point to priests, pastors, rabbis and imams, not to broad mission or support staff. This matters for pastoral assistants, community coordinators, charity workers, youth workers and administrators: unless they can honestly be characterised as the narrow Article 26a category, the official materials do not give them a dedicated religion-specific admission route. That conclusion is an inference from the sources reviewed, but it is the conservative one. 40

**Missionaries are the hardest category to overstate.** The official sources clearly say that missionary work is gainful activity when it is normally remunerated, but the same official sources exclude "missionary activities" from the narrow circle of religious caregivers. I did not locate an official federal source creating a separate long-stay missionary category with its own settled criteria. The better internal drafting position is therefore to say that missionaries may require work authorisation under the ordinary labour-market regime, but that the precise route depends on the concrete role, duration, nationality and whether some other legally recognised category is genuinely available. 13

**Unpaid and volunteer work requires special caution.** The guidance on volunteer work is not a general invitation for religgious institutions to bring in unpaid foreign workers. SEM's volunteer exemption is limited to certain people already holding protected statuses in Switzerland and is tied to unpaid activity of an ideal, social or charitable nature that remains modest in weekly time commitment. SEM then says that all deployments of persons **newly** **entering** **from** **abroad** for that purpose, or other voluntary placements outside those limits, remain permit- or notification-relevant. That is especially important for mission teams, church volunteers, charity outreach workers and faith-based "interns". 19

**Salary, maintenance, accommodation and stipends do not displace immigration control.** Swiss authorities are concerned with theobjective labour-market character of the activity, not merely with whether the institution labels the support as a stipend, honorarium, maintenance package or room and board. SEM expressly says that food and accommodation alone can still fall within gainful activity where the underlying role is normally remunerated. At the same time, the religious-caregiver guidance insists on ordinary remuneration reflecting qualifications and responsibility, save for a narrow, historically rooted poverty-vow exception backed by an undertaking from the community to meet maintenance and additional costs. 5

<!-- 9 -->

**Integration and values evidence is real, formal and enforceable.** For Article 26a cases, language and values are not optional soft factors. SEM requires B1 oral and A1 written command of the relevant official language, subject to the alternatives in the ordinance. It also requires a signed values declaration confirming familiarity with Swiss legal and social normms, public order and constitutional values, and notes revocation risk if the condition is not respected. Short, justified religious assignments can obtain some relief from the integration side, but that is an exception, not the ordinary position. 41

**Evidence of the institution and the role matters as much as evidence about the person. SEM** focuses on whether the community has institutional structures, fixed worship premises and significance beyond a purely local niche, and whether it has the financial capacity to pay the role properlyor maintain the person lawfully if a valid poverty-vow case applies. Zurich's public guidance adds the practical evidential layer: a reasoned submission on the community and candidate, proof of labour-market search, a dated signed contract, CV, diplomas, language certificate and the values declaration. As a matter of drafting, future internal content should present these as an **evidence** **bundle,** not as optional extras. 42

**Short religious events require a visitor-versus-worker distinction that must be expressed clearly.** On the official material, a person who attendds a religious conference, retreat or festival as **a** **passive** **attendee** is not doing gainful activity. A person who leads prayers, preaches, celebrates liturgy, performs religious music, or undertakes comparable active functions is, in principle, doing gainful activity. For retreats specifically, the official materials do not use that exact label, so the safest wording is analogical: a retreat participant with no active role is close to passive attendance; a retreat leader or preacher is close to active religious work. That is an inference from the SEM materials and should be signalled as such in future drafting. 43

**Nationality changes the route but not the need for analysis.** EU/EFTA nationals benefit from free movement, so a Swiss religious institution can use the notification route for short employment and ordinary EU/EFTA permits for longer engagements. Eligible EU/EFTA service providers and posted workers have the 90-day notification regime. That ddoes not mean "no controls": there are still procedural steps, posting conditions, day-counting rules and, for service beyond 90 days, a permit requirement with no general legal entitlement. Third-country nationals posted from an EU/EFTA undertaking also have to satisfy the prior labour-market-integration rule in the sending state. 44

**Refusal and** **compliance** **risks** **are** **predictable.** Risks include mislabelling active religious work as a private visit, festival attendance or volunteering; trying to fit a missionary or ritual helper into the narrow religious-caregiver category; failing to show proper recruitment efforts in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA where required; inadequate salary or weak evidence of community finances; missing language or values evidence; and non-compliance with notification and authorisation rules. SECO's black-work materials also show that non-compliance can trigger wider labour-market sanctions alongside immigration consequences. 45

## Drafting cautions

Do not say or imply that a religious purpose, pastoral purpose or charitable purpose removes the need for a Swiss work authorisation. Swiss officiaI sources take the opposite starting point and treat religious work broadly asgainful activity when it is ordinarily remunerated. 17

Do **not** say or imply that unpaid work, room-and-board support, stipends,subsistence-only maintenance, donations or "volunteer" labels automatically take a role outside immigration control. Swiss guidance is explicit that they do not. 46

**Do not** collapse the categories of **private religious activity, passive attendance, religious** **caregiving, missionary work, volunteering, charitable work and administrative employment** into one concept. The official sources distinguish them, and different consequences follow from those distinctions. 47

Do **not** present "religious caregiver" as a synonym for all clergy or all mission staff. The category is narrow and role-specific, and missionary activities are expressly excluded from it in SEM's guidance. 3

Do **not** portray the language, values-system and public-order aspects as discretionary soft factors. For Article 26a cases they are part of the admission structure itself, backed by formal evidence and possible revocation. 48

**Do not** state that Switzerland has a distinct official "missionary permit" unless a later official source squarely creates one. The present official materials reviewed for this pack do not do so. 26

Where content discusses short visits by clergy, speakers or worship teams, always separate **mere** **attendance** from **active preaching / liturgy / music / leadership** and then state the relevant permit or notification consequences conservatively. 43

Where content refers to cantonal practice, say expressly that public cantonal guidance is **thin** **and** **uneven,** and that Zurich currently provides the clearest published religion-specific practicenote among the sources reviewed here. 49

The sources reviewed do not publish a separate settled federal "missionary permit" pathway. Public cantonal guidance is also uneven and may not reflect unpublished case-handling practice. Future internal content should therefore remain conservative and present such cases as requiring careful role-specific analysis under the ordinary Swiss work-authorisation framework.
