## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law does **not** treat "unpaid" as a safe harbour. The core statutory rule is that gainful employment includes any salaried oor self-employed activity that is normally carried out for payment, whether or not payment is actually made. The implementing ordinance then makes the point even more concrete by stating that salaried gainful activity includes, among others, work as an apprentice, intern, trainee, volunteer, social helper, missionary, religious care worker, artist and au pair. In other words, the label "volunteer", "intern", "trainee" or "unpaid" does not decide the immigration outcome. 1

The immigration analysis is therefore functional and objective. Official SEM guidance states that gainful activity may exist even where the person works free of charge or where the only "remuneration" is coverage of elementary living needs such as food and lodging. The same guidance says the distinction rests on **objective rather** **than** **subjective** criteria. That is a strong warning against arguments based solely on goodwill, charity, learning intentions, or the parties' wording. 2

At federal level, the main legal dividing line is between activity that is genuinely non-productive and activity that is integrated into a host's operations, labour process or output. SEM's official annex on the "notion of gainful activity" treats purely theoretical or technical training without integration into work/ production, passive attendance at conferences, ordinary business meetings, and preparatory visits as outside gainful employment.By contrast, on-the-job induction or learning that is integrated into work and production, stages/training stays, project assignments, maintenance work and similar operational activity are treated as gainful employment. 3

Trial work is a narrow exception, not a general loophole. SEM's FNIA directives, citing Federal Supreme Court case law, say trial work may be done without authorisation or notification only where it does not exceed **half** a **day,** exceptionally a **full** day, and is genuinely limited to assessing suitability for a specific role. Longer engagements are subject to authorisation and notification rules. The Court's published summary likewise states that job applications and recruitment discussions do not require authorisation, but work performed on a trial basis in contemplation of employment does. 4

Internships and traineeships are generally treated as work, not as an automatic "study" category. OASA expressly regulates mandatory internships during studies, work during higher education, international exchange, and "stagiaire" programmes. For third-country nationals, official federal and cantonal materiaIs require permit analysis even where the internship is unpaid. Geneva's current OCPM guidance states expressly that an internship application must be filed **whether** **the internship** is **paid** **or unpaid,** and Zurich's guidance for third-country students requires local salary and working conditions to be respected for eligible internships. 5

For EU/EFTA nationals, the issue is not whether work-authorisation disappears, but whether the case falls within the **notification** **procedure** or requires a permit. SEM's official notification page states that short-term employment with a Swiss employer of up to three months, and cross-border services of up to 90 working days per calendar year, can in principle be handled by notification; beyond that, a permit is required. Those regimes still presuppose that the activity is treated as work in the first place. 6

Official federal guidance on **generic volunteering by incoming foreign nationals** is comparatively thin. The clearest express SEM wording appears in the FNIA directives in the asylum/protection context, where "voluntary work" without authorisation/notification is confined to unpaid activity pursuing an ideal, social, charitable or ecological purpose and averaging no more than six hours per week over a year; the same guidance says missions undertaken by persons who come from abroad for that purpose, and other forms of volunteering that donot meet those criteria, are subject to authorisation and notification. That is best used conservatively as a **practice** **indicator,** not as a universal statutory safe harbour for all migrants. 7

### D. Primary legal texts

The primary federal texts are the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment.** The most important provisions for this topic are FNIA arts. 10-11 (permit requirement and definition of gainful employment), 18-24 (admission for salaried work and labour-market conditions), 27 and 30 (study/training and derogations), and OASA arts. 1a and 4 (definition and decision-maker), 12 and 14 (short-term work and cross-border work), 19-20a (short-stay quota architecture), 22 (salary and working conditions), and 38-42 (student work, mandatory internships, exchange and stagiaire schemes). 17

For EU/EFTA nationals, the federal framework must be read together with the **AFMP** / **free** **movement** **regime** and the **notification** **procedure.** SEM's current official notification guidance states that the AFMP liberalises covered services for up to 90 effective working days per calendar year through prior notification, and also allows Swiss employers to use notification for EU/EFTA workers engaged for up to three months; beyond those thresholds, a permit is required. 6

### E. Official guidance / administrative materials

The most important administrative materials are: the **SEM** **FNIA** **directives, chapter** 4; the separate **SEM annex on the notion of gainful activity;** the **SEM FAQ on working in Switzerland;** the **SEM** **notification procedure guidance** for AFMP cases; the **SEM young professionals instructions;** and, for salary/working-condition control in certain reporting contexts, the **SECO** **guidance** **on** **employment** **of** **persons** **in** **the** **asylum** **domain.** Together, these sources provide the clearest official treatment of unpaid work, trial work, volunteering, internships, study-related activity, and notification obligations. 18

### F. Cantonal practice notes

The cantonal picture is useful but should be treated as implementation practice, not as an override of federal law. Zurich's official guidance for third-country students confirms that internships are admissible only in specific cases and that local salary and working conditions must be respected; it also requires educational evidence for mandatory internships. Geneva's OCPM states that an applicatioon must be filed **before** a study-related internship starts, that the internship must be an integral part of the studies, that its duration must be less than half the full course, and that the filing obligation applies **whether** **the** **internship** **is remunerated** or **not.** Ticino's official page requires notification of student internships and distinguishes between ordinary ancillary work and compulsory study-related placements, while third-country nationals may only begin after authorisation is received. 19

<!-- 6 -->

A particularly useful Zurich labour-market document from the cantonal tripartite commission distinguishes **Schnuppern** (career observation), **Probearbeit** (trial work), and **Praktikum** (internship).It says Schnuppern is not about performing work, must be limited to a maximum of five days, and should mainly involve watching, trying things out and getting to know the team; Probearbeit in a recruitment process is limited to two days and likewise should not involve independent work; internships, by contrast, are structured practical placements, usually with a training character and often a limited duration. This is not an immigration rule as such, but it is a valuable cantonal indicator of how Swiss authorities distinguish observation from productive work. 20

## Practical legal analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis

The safest internal working assumption is this: if the foreign national will be doing something that a Swiss business, organisation, charity or institution would ordinarily have to staff, supervise, schedule or value as labour, the case should be analysed as **gainful employment** even if no salary is paid. That conclusion flows directly from FNIA art. 11(2), from OASA's inclusion of interns and volunteers within salaried gainful activity, and from SEM's statement that even food-and-lodging-only arrangements may still be gainful employment. 21

The most important factual distinction is between **productive** **contribution** and **non-productive** **presence.** SEM's official annex treats non-productive theoretical training, ordinary business meetings, passive attendance and preparatory visits as outside gainful activity, because the visitor is not integrated into the host's work process. The same annex treats learning that takes place **inside** **work** and production, and operational assignments delivered for the host, as gainful employment. This is why "shadowing" cannot be analysed by label alone: observation-only may fall outside work, but shadowing that turns into handling tasks, seeing clients, supporting deliveries, processing files, or contributing to operational output will usually fall back into work-authorisation territory. 8

The same logic explains why **unpaid** **internships** remain immigration-sensitive. Swiss law has formal routes for internships precisely because they are treated as work. For students, the key questions are whether the placement is genuinely built into the course, whether there is school confirmation, whether there is a defined programme, whether the internship is limited in duration, and whether local wage and working conditions are respected. Geneva's explicit requirement to file a stage application even where the internship is unpaid is especially useful authority for internal drafting. 5

**For** **traineeships** **/** **stagiaire** **programmes,** Swiss law again points away from informal unpaid arrangements and towards formal work-authorised structures. OASA art. 42 sends stagiaire cases into specific agreements and administrative arrangements, while SEM's current young professionals instructions require a written contract, a training programme, local wage norms, a defined duration, and a role linked to the person's learned profession. That is a strong indication that Swiss authorities

<!-- 7 -->

expect true training placements to be documented and labour-market compliant, not disguised as free labour. 15

**Volunteer activity** needs especially careful drafting. The existence of altruistic motives does not, by itself, take an activity outside immigration law. The clearest express SEM criteria for volunteer work appear in the FNIA directives' asylum/protectionsection: ideal/social/charitable/ecological purpose, no remuneration, and average duration not exceeding six hours a week overa year; the same guidance says that assignments undertaken by persons who come from abroad for that purpose, and other volunteering that does not meet those criteria, are subject to authorisation and notification.That wording should be treated as a **narrow** **administrative** **practice** **point,** not as a general federal exemption for all foreign volunteers. Where a person comes to Switzerland specifically to work for a host organisation, especially on regular duties or for longer periods, the safer immigration viewis that work-authorisation analysis is required.7

**On** **trial work** **and** **trial** **days,** the law is deliberately narrow. SEM's guidance allows a half-day, exceptionally a full day, where the purpose is only to test suitability for a specific post. The activity must remain part of evaluation and contract negotiation; it is not meant to provide the employer with ordinary labour. Any longer or more operational arrangement should be treated as work. Internal content should therefore avoid any wording suggesting that several unpaid trial days, a trial week, or "just helping out before the contract starts" is automatically permissible.4

**The role of remuneration, expenses and benefits in kind** should be described conservatively. Swiss authorities do care about local salary and working conditions in work-authorisation analysis, but payment is not the threshold issue for deciding whether something is work. The threshold issue is whether the activity is of a kind normally performed for remuneration. Once that threshold is crossed, low remuneration, expense-only payment, free meals, accommodation, stipends or "pocket money" do not resolve the immigratioon problem; if anything, they may reinforce that the arrangement has an organised economic structure. SEM's guidance is explicit that even activity performed free of charge or for basic subsistence only may still be gainful employment. In the religious context, SEM recognises a narrow historical vow-of-poverty exception to ordinary remuneration principles, but even there the community must support the person's living costs and other expenses. 22

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the right question is often "notification or permit?" rather than "permit or nothing?". If the activity is gainful employment with a Swiss employer for up to three months, or an AFMP-covered service for up to 90 days, the notification route may be available; beyond that, a permit is needed. But AFMP residence-right analysis can still be more nuanced because "worker" status depends on whether the activity is real and effective rather than purely marginal and ancillary. Low pay or short duration may still qualify in some cases, but they may also show that the person is not a "worker" for AFMP residence purposes. That does not mean the activity is automatically outside domestic work-authorisation analysis. 23

**For** **third-country** **nationals,** the default is stricter. SEM's procedure page states that even short stays of up to four months for gainful employmment require an employer-led application to the cantonal authority,canton-level screening, and SEM approval where applicable, with work starting only after the required steps are complete. Where an "unpaid" arrangement is objectively productive, the fact it lasts only a few days or weeks does not remove the need to analyse the work-authorisation route. 24

The best evidence set for difficult cases is therefore documentary and factual, not rhetorical. Internal case assessment should obtain: a precise duty list; who benefits from the activity; the degree of supervision; whether the person will produce output or only observe; duration and schedule; whether the placement is compulsory under an educational programme; confirmation from the school; a written contract or placement letter; a training plan; who pays salary, stipend, meals, accommodation, travel and insurance; whether the person will be integrated into ordinary staffing; and, for AFMP cases, whether the situation fits notification thresholds or requires a permit. Those evidential expectations are reflected across SEM's annex, the SEM young-professionals instructions, and current cantonal internship forms and guidance. 25

<!-- 8 -->

**Open** **questions** **/** **limitations.** Official Swiss materials are strong on the general concept of gainful activity, trial work, internships, student work and certain volunteer or religious settings, but thinner on a single general federal rule for all forms of "volunteering", "shadowing" and "pocket-money/ stipend" arrangements across all migration categories. For those topics, the most defensible approach is to rely on the statutory definition of gainful activity, the SEM annex on productive integration into work, the narrow trial-work rule, and clearly identified canton-specific or status-specific guidance. 26

## Drafting cautions

## H. Drafting cautions

Internal content should not say or imply that unpaid activity is automatically outside Swiss work-authorisation rules. The legally accurate starting point is the opposite: if the activity is of a type normally performed for remuneration, it may be gainful employment even when unpaid. 27

Internal content should also avoid saying that laboels such as volunteer, intern, trainee, observer, shadow or trial day are determinative. Swiss law and guidance look to the objective substance of the arrangement, and OASA expressly includes several of those labelled categories within salaried gainful activity. 28

Where content discusses **observation,** it should state that only genuinely non-productive observation is low-risk. If the person starts handling tasks, covering staffing needs, contributing to a client-facing process, or receiving on-the-job training integrated into production, work-authorisation analysis is likely required. 8

Where content discusses **internships,** it should distinguish at least four categories: internship integral to studies; pre-study internship; post-study stagiaire / young professional route; and informal work-experience placement. The first and third may fit recognised work-authorised routes; the others are much riskier and should not be described casually as "just study" or "just experience". 29

Where content discusses **volunteering,** it should say expressly that official Swiss guidance is relatively fact-sensitive and, outside narrow published settings, not especially generous. It is safer to analyse by duty content, frequency, host benefit, and whether the person is coming from abroad specifically to perform the activity. 30

Finally, internal content should keep the immigration analysis separate from employment-law, wage, social-security, tax, education and charity-law questions. Those regimes may overlap, but they do not control whether an activity is "gainful employmment" under Swiss migration law. SEM says expressly that definitions used in other legal fields are not decisive for immigration purposes. 31
