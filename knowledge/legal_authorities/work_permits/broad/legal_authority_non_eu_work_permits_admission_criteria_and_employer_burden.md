# Swiss non-EU work permits authority pack

**Suggested filename:** `legal_authority_non_eu_work_permits_admission_criteria_and_employer_burden_pack.pdf`

## Executive legal summary

Switzerland applies a dual admission system to foreign labour. EU/EFTA workers benefit from the free movement framework, while non-EU/non-EFTA nationals are admitted only on a complementary and restrictive basis. For standard salaried employment, the core federal framework is built around overall economic interest, quota control, precedence for the domestic and EU/EFTA labour market, compliance with local salary and working conditions, and the candidate’s personal qualifications and integration prospects. In practice, the system is designed for managers, specialists, and other qualified workers rather than routine or easily substitutable hires.<sup>1</sup>

The employer carries most of the evidential burden. Swiss authorities expect an employer-led filing that explains why the hire serves the Swiss economy or the Swiss business in a legally relevant way, why no suitable priority worker could be recruited in Switzerland or the EU/EFTA area, why the salary and employment terms are locally customary, and why the individual candidate is professionally suitable for the specific role. SEM’s published application guidance shows that this burden is operationalised through compulsory evidence such as RAV/EURES advertising proof, sectoral recruitment evidence, applicant/rejection records, CVs, diplomas, references, a signed employment contract, and an employer explanation of why the vacancy must be filled.<sup>2</sup>

Labour-market testing is substantive, not cosmetic. SEM states that employers must show a genuine search in terms of time, geographic scope, and methods used, and that contact with third-country nationals should only occur after the search for Swiss/EU/EFTA candidates has failed. In occupations with pronounced structural shortages, SEM’s directives allow some easing of the proof burden, but not an automatic exemption, and the vacancy-notification duty still applies where the statutory threshold is met.<sup>3</sup>

From a filing strategy perspective, the strongest Swiss third-country work permit files are coherent rather than merely document-heavy. The role description, recruitment history, business rationale, salary benchmark, and candidate profile all need to point in the same direction. Weak files typically fail because one of those elements looks artificial, under-developed, or internally inconsistent. Swiss and cantonal guidance also makes timing important: the process is canton-first, then federal, and authorities repeatedly warn against assuming that filing itself permits entry or work.<sup>4</sup>

## Primary legal texts

The core primary-law framework for standard non-EU salaried work permit cases is the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act. For internal content purposes, the key provisions are Art. 18 on salaried

employment, Art. 20 on limitation measures and quotas, Art. 21 on precedence, Art. 21a on vacancy notification, Art. 22 on salary and employment conditions, Art. 23 on personal requirements, and Art. 30 on statutory exceptions. Those provisions supply the main legal hooks for virtually all mainstream admission arguments.<sup>33</sup>

The Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment gives operational detail. For present purposes, the most important ordinance material is the quota machinery and the rule that local, professional, and sector-standard salary and working conditions are determined by legal rules, collective/normal employment contracts, same-employer or sector comparators, and statistical wage data. SEM’s FAQ also points to OASA/ASEO Art. 55 for changes of job, which matters for renewals and post-grant variations.<sup>34</sup>

The vacancy-notification regime is separately implemented through the unemployment insurance framework referenced by SEM, especially the provisions cited in the SEM directives as AVV Arts. 53a-e. For admission drafting, the crucial point is that job-registration is not the same thing as labour-market precedence: it is an additional compliance layer triggered by the applicable unemployment threshold and monitored by cantonal authorities.<sup>35</sup>

SEM’s sectoral guidance also confirms that some important exceptions are embedded in the statutory structure itself, notably management or specialist transfers in international groups and the Swiss- university-graduate exception. A content system should therefore keep a separate exception layer rather than treating all third-country work cases as standard Art. 18 cases.<sup>36</sup>

## Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM’s “Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market” page is the most useful federal overview because it brings together the operative tests under Arts. 18, 20, 21, 21a, 22, 23 and 30 FNIA in one place. It also exists in German, French, and Italian, which is particularly useful where an internal system needs alignment across source languages.<sup>37</sup>

SEM’s procedural materials are highly practical. The procedure page confirms the employer-led filing route, the canton-to-SEM workflow, and the fact that SEM issues the federal approval decision after cantonal pre-screening. The application-documents page then itemises the minimum evidential package: RAV/EURES confirmation, adverts, other Swiss/EU/EFTA search efforts, applicant/rejection information, CV, qualification proof, employer reasons for the hire, job description, company details, project evidence, start-up business plans, and the signed employment contract.<sup>38</sup>

The SEM FAQ is especially valuable for strategic points that are often mishandled in practice. It states that a Swiss job offer by itself does not make a third-country national eligible to work; precedence, personal qualifications, salary and work conditions, and quota availability must still be shown. It also gives a practical federal processing expectation of roughly six to eight weeks, notes the six-month job- search and precedence derogation for certain Swiss graduates, and identifies some exception categories and job-change rules.<sup>39</sup>

SEM’s Chapter 4 directives on “Aufenthalt mit Erwerbstätigkeit”, updated to 1 January 2026, are the most important administrative authority for substantive argumentation. They explain what “overall economic interest” means in practice, stress that the system is not meant to preserve low-skilled structures through cheap labour or support particular interests, explain how the precedence burden is proven, describe eased treatment for pronounced shortage occupations, clarify how salary evidence is

assessed, and add important nuances on language skills, sector-specific rules, and the evidential use of statistical wage tools.<sup>40</sup>

SECO and the Federal Statistical Office supply the main official benchmarking tools that interact with work permit practice. SECO’s Check-Up 2026 shows whether a vacancy is subject to job-registration as of 1 January 2026. SECO’s national wage calculator and the FSO’s Salarium are official statistical tools based on Swiss earnings data, but both expressly say that their outputs are not wage recommendations. That makes them useful evidential aids, but not safe-harbour figures.<sup>41</sup>

## Cantonal practice notes

Zurich. Zurich’s public guidance is one of the clearest German-language practice notes. It repeats the cumulative federal conditions and then, in a dedicated 2024 explanatory PDF, gives concrete evidential expectations: a specific statement of reasons, and where relevant a business plan and financial statements for the overall-interest showing; proof of vacancy notification where required; RAV/EURES publication, dated adverts, and an applicant list with nationality, qualifications, date of application, and rejection reason for precedence; a current signed contract for salary/work conditions; and CV, diplomas, references, and passport copy for personal requirements. Zurich also states that in pronounced shortage occupations the employer may be able to dispense with full advert and candidate-list evidence if the shortage is explained, and that non-academic shortage roles can still qualify with a vocational qualification or at least five years’ experience.<sup>42</sup>

Geneva. Geneva’s current French-language page adds several practical points that are useful for drafting. It says employers should file around six to eight weeks before entry, that the job must be notified to the cantonal employment office as quickly as possible regardless of the branch unemployment rate, that the employer must prove thorough Swiss/EU/EFTA recruitment and inability to train a locally available worker within a reasonable time, and that only executives, specialists, and qualified workers are admissible. Geneva also highlights local quota availability, usual branch salary, the expectation of full-time employment in its standard route, and the requirement for an employer motivation letter describing the situation, projects, and reasons for the hire, together with detailed search evidence. It further notes that OCIRT may impose conditions, including time limits and renewal conditions tied to the objectives announced by the employer.<sup>43</sup>

Bern, Basel-Landschaft, and Lucerne. Bern’s official guidance requires filing at least eight weeks before the proposed start and lists the common core bundle: justification letter, CV, diplomas, employment contract, passport copy, and proof of recruitment attempts in Switzerland and the EU-27/ EFTA. Basel-Landschaft’s official information sheet goes further into evidential and compliance detail: it requires an “economic necessity” explanation, proof of all reasonable domestic/EU search efforts, adverts and/or RAV posting, an explanation why available labour could not be trained in time, and warns that unsuccessful RAV referrals do not create any right to a permit. It also states that any later deterioration in working conditions requires renewed approval. Lucerne expressly states that there is no legal entitlement to a particular permit or to its extension.<sup>44</sup>

Ticino. Ticino’s public pages are less elaborated on substantive labour-market testing, but they are still useful for an internal content system because they show the Swiss-Italian administrative pattern: the canton routes users directly to the SEM’s federal materials on admission grounds and the foreign- nationals directives, signalling that the federal framework remains the primary substantive authority even in cantonal practice.<sup>45</sup>

## Practical legal analysis

The Swiss test is best understood as a cumulative burden with one policy core: third-country hiring is allowed where the file shows genuine Swiss labour-market need without wage dumping or displacement of priority workers. SEM’s directives make that policy purpose explicit by saying that admissions should not preserve structures through low-qualified labour on low wages, back particular interests, or create undesirable competition with the resident workforce through lower salaries and poorer conditions. That means a file framed merely around employer preference or convenience is legally weak even if the candidate is impressive.<sup>46</sup>

There is no freestanding statutory “commercial necessity” test by that name, but Swiss practice effectively requires one. SEM’s application guidance requires the employer to explain why the vacancy must be filled, what the duties are, and, for project work, to provide contract/order evidence, a project description, and an implementation plan. For business start-ups it requires a deed of foundation, commercial-register entry, and a three-year business plan with staffing, financing, and market analysis. Cantonal practice is even more explicit: Zurich expects a specific justification and may ask for business plans and accounts; Basel-Landschaft asks for an explanation of economic necessity and intended deployment; Geneva asks for an employer letter describing the company’s situation, projects, and the reasons for engagement. A strong internal drafting template should therefore force the employer to explain the Swiss-side need with evidence, not just assertion.<sup>47</sup>

Labour-market testing in Switzerland has two distinct layers that should never be collapsed into one. The first is precedence under Art. 21 FNIA: the employer must show a genuine, sensible, and unsuccessful search for priority workers in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area. The second is vacancy notification under Art. 21a FNIA: in occupations or regions above the unemployment threshold, the vacancy must also be registered through the public system unless an exception applies. SEM’s directives and public guidance are clear that search efforts must be timely, sequential, and real; candidate contact with third-country nationals should come only after the priority search has failed; and irrelevant filters should not be used to rule out priority workers. In practice, a file should therefore show chronology, media used, search geography, applicant outcomes, and-where relevant-proof of official vacancy notification.<sup>48</sup>

Suitability is also more exacting than a generic “highly skilled” claim. The candidate must fit the specific Swiss role. SEM and cantonal practice want the qualifications to correspond to the intended activity, with evidence in the form of CV, education certificates, references, and, where necessary, translations or explanatory curriculum materials. SEM’s directives also show that language operates contextually: good English may be enough for highly specialised multinational or research environments, whereas local- language capacity may be decisive in sectors such as healthcare or hospitality. For shortage occupations, the framework is more flexible: a non-academic role can still qualify if the person has a vocational qualification or usually at least five years’ professional experience, but the employer still needs to explain why the occupation truly falls within a pronounced shortage category.<sup>49</sup>

Salary evidence is not a side issue; it is part of the admission merits. OASA/VZAE and the SEM directives show that authorities compare the proposed package against legal norms, collective or normal employment contracts, actual conditions for comparable work in the same employer or sector, and statistical wage data. The SEM directives also say that, as a rule, only remuneration components to which there is a contractual entitlement count, while start-up equity-style components are treated cautiously and only where direct financial means remain sufficient and the equity remains proportionate to the basic wage. SECO’s wage calculator and Salarium are useful official supports, but

both tools caution that their outputs are not wage recommendations. A prudent filing therefore uses several layers of wage evidence rather than one screenshot.<sup>50</sup>

Quota sensitivity should be treated as real but not absolute. At national level, the Federal Council left 2026 third-country quotas unchanged at 8,500 and reported that quotas had not been fully used in recent years. Even so, quotas remain finite, are managed administratively, and sit on top of a discretionary admission system. Geneva’s cantonal guidance also frames quota availability as a practical local constraint. The right strategic conclusion is not that quotas always block strong cases, but that employers should file early, expect scrutiny over permit category and timing, and avoid presenting the case as if approval is automatic once the legal tests are facially met.<sup>51</sup>

A strong Swiss filing strategy is therefore to delay filing until the evidential record is genuinely ready, but not longer. Use a conditional employment contract, preserve a clean chronology of search efforts, build an employer statement that explains Swiss value-add and inability to recruit or train locally within a reasonable period, benchmark salary with official and sectoral sources, and make sure the candidate’s qualifications match the actual duties rather than a general seniority narrative. If the case rests on a shortage occupation, say so expressly and attach evidence. If it rests on a Swiss graduation exception or an intra-group transfer, structure the application around that exception rather than forcing the case into a generic labour-market-test narrative.<sup>52</sup>
