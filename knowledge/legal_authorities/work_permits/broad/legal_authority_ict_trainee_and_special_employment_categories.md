# Swiss legal authority pack for ICT, trainee and special employment categories Suggested filename: legal_authority_ict_trainee_and_special_employment_categories_pack.pdf

Executive legal summary A. Executive legal summary. For Swiss work authorisation purposes, the core federal framework for intra-company transfers and training/exchange-based work sits in the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Gainful Employment. The key provisions are FNIA article 30(1)(h), which allows derogations to simplify the transfer of senior management staff and essential specialists within internationally active companies, and OASA/VZAE article 46, which operationalises that transfer route. Training and exchange routes sit principally under FNIA article 30(1)(g) and OASA/VZAE articles 39 to 42, covering compulsory internships, work during certain further education, international exchange and stagiaires/young professionals. Read together, these materials show that Switzerland does recognise an ICT-type route, but not as a standalone EU-ICT-directive permit; rather, it is handled through Swiss derogation provisions, quota rules and SEM guidance, with a separate GATS overlay for qualifying transfers. 1

The transfer-purpose logic is functional and restrictive. Article 46 VZAE is aimed at executives, senior management and indispensable specialists in internationally active companies, while SEM guidance says the route is intended for temporary or fixed-term recruitment without applying the ordinary domestic-worker priority. For GATS-covered intra-corporate transferees, SEM further requires at least twelve months’ prior employment in the foreign group company, while keeping quotas, salary conditions and geographic/professional constraints in place. 2

The training-purpose logic is equally controlled. For the bilateral young professionals / stagiaires route, SEM states that the person must be from a contracting state, generally aged 18 to 35, have completed vocational or higher education, work in the profession in which they were trained, receive going local starter-level pay, and hold a written employment contract with an integrated training programme. The route is capped at 18 months, prohibits part-time work, self-employment and staff leasing in principle, and limits multiple trainees to 5% of the workforce. For compulsory academic internships, article 39 VZAE requires the internship to be part of a full-time course and not exceed half of the total training period. 3

Swiss authorities are alert to disguised ordinary employment. Article 23 VZAE states that educational stays do not satisfy the personal requirements if the circumstances indicate that the supposed education or training merely serves to circumvent the general admission and residence rules. Article 54 VZAE separately requires a new permit if a holder changes from one purpose-specific admission ground to another. Cantonal materials reinforce the same logic: Geneva only recognises certain defined training/insertion internships; Ticino’s labour-market guidance treats a “stage” as a training/ reorientation device rather than an ordinary job and says it does not apply to employment

programmes; Basel-Stadt states that internships for third-country nationals are limited to intra-group or compulsory university internships. 4

B. Structural overview. For internal content-system purposes, the Swiss authority structure is best understood in four operational buckets. First, intra-company transfer authorisations under FNIA article 30(1)(h), VZAE article 46 and, where pleaded, the SEM’s GATS guidance. Secondly, training/exchange routes under FNIA article 30(1)(g) and VZAE articles 39 to 42, especially compulsory internships, international exchange and young professionals/stagiaires. Thirdly, adjacent special categories such as au pairs and special international functions, which exist in the same derogation chapter but are usually peripheral to corporate immigration analysis. Fourthly, cantonal labour-market practice, which shapes evidence, sequencing, anti-chaining rules and scrutiny of whether a case is genuinely a transfer or a training placement rather than a normal hire. 5

Primary legal texts D. Primary legal texts. The essential statutory backbone is the FNIA/AIG, especially article 18 on salaried employment, article 21 on domestic-worker priority, article 22 on salary and employment conditions, article 23 on personal requirements, and article 30 on the derogation grounds that matter here: international exchange/ training in article 30(1)(g) and intra-company transfers in article 30(1)(h). Those provisions frame the normal admission model, its exceptions, and the conditions that continue to apply even where the ordinary route is relaxed. 15

The implementing ordinance is the VZAE/OASA. Several provisions matter repeatedly in this topic: article 3 defines cross-border service provision; article 4 allocates decisions on whether an activity counts as gainful employment and sends doubtful cases to SEM; articles 19 to 22a set the short-stay quota logic, market salary rules, documentary requirements and compensation rules for posted workers and operational transfers; articles 39 to 42 create the main internship/exchange/stagiaire routes; article 46 sets the international corporate transfer rule; and articles 54 to 57 regulate purpose changes, job changes and anti-chaining of short permits. 16

For trainee exchange agreements, article 42 VZAE is crucial because it expressly says that procedure and permit issuance follow the stagiaires agreements and intergovernmental administrative arrangements, and that SEM may issue and extend stagiaire approvals up to 18 months. SEM’s current Young Professionals instruction then identifies the contracting countries and the operative prerequisites. 10

For GATS-based corporate transfers and related service-provider admission, the most practically useful authority is not a statute but SEM’s explanation of how Switzerland gives effect to its WTO commitments. SEM states that for qualifying cadre transfers and other essential persons, the ordinary

domestic-worker priority is lifted, but quotas, salary conditions and mobility limits remain, and SEM approval is still required. 8

E. Official guidance and administrative materials. The single most important administrative source is SEM, Weisungen AIG, Chapter 4, Aufenthalt mit Erwerbstätigkeit. It explains that international exchange under article 41 only works where exchange remains the main purpose and the stay is temporary with return; that stagiaires are persons aged 18 to 30/35 with completed training who expand their professional and linguistic knowledge for a limited period in the learned profession; that their pay must be locally and professionally customary; that, as a rule, trainees should not exceed 5% of a firm’s staff; and that the training programme should be recorded in the employment contract. It also states that a profession change for stagiaires is not allowed, job changes require a reasoned written request to SEM, and repeat stagiaire stays are exceptional within the overall 18‑month ceiling.

The SEM Young Professionals instructions are equally important because they provide an operational checklist. They set out the current participating countries, age limits, training thresholds, occupation- match requirement, starter-level pay expectation, maximum duration, ban on part-time/self- employment, the rule against staff leasing, the need for a written employment contract with an integrated training programme, the application documents, the rule that the application is submitted through the competent authority in the home country, and the rule that applicants may not enter Switzerland before approval. 11

SEM’s chapter on student and post-study internships is especially useful where the case is not a classic stagiaire agreement case. SEM says that mandatory internships require a letter from the foreign university confirming enrolment and that the internship is an integral part of the studies. For internships after graduation, SEM identifies two recognised patterns: a stagiaire route under article 42, and an internship within an internationally active group with a view to later deployment within the group abroad or at an important client abroad.

Official guidance and cantonal practice F. Cantonal practice notes. Cantonal practice matters because labour-market scrutiny and sequencing are cantonal even where SEM approval is also required. Zurich’s guidance for third-country employment cases states that an initial labour-market pre-decision is required before a first L or B work permit is issued, and it applies the federal reissuance and anti-chaining rules expressly. It warns against “hidden permanent stays”, says stagiaire permits are ordinarily single-use, and notes that a four-month permit can, in some cases, be followed immediately by an article 19(1) short-stay permit or a stagiaire permit only by counting the earlier period into the total allowed stay. Zurich also says that requests to extend stagiaire permits must be filed with SEM. 17

Basel-Stadt publishes comparatively blunt practice notes. Its internship page states that for third- country nationals, work authorisation for internships is available only for intra-group internships or compulsory university internships, and that the application is channelled through IAESTE or AIESEC. Basel-Stadt also maintains a separate stagiaire page for people aged 18 to 35 and directs them to the SEM stagiaire programme. 18

Geneva’s labour-market authority does not publish an immigration-law ICT note, but its internship guidance is still highly relevant to the disguised-employment question. Geneva recognises only internships anchored in school or professional training under federal/cantonal legislation and a set of

internship categories approved by the labour-market supervisory council; it also says that the employment conditions for a training or insertion internship should be assessed by reference to the framework set by the training institution. That is strong official support for requiring an objective, external training framework rather than a purely employer-defined “internship” label. 19

Ticino’s June 2024 guidance on the criteria for defining a stage is similarly useful. It treats the stage primarily as a completion of a training path, a professional-orientation measure or a requalification device, expressly says the document does not apply to employment programmes, and then uses concrete indicators such as a tripartite contract with the educational institution, reinsertion/new orientation with a maximum six-month duration, end-of-study placements, institute-required stages and holiday stages for students below a specified age. Although this guidance is labour-market focused rather than an immigration permit manual, it is highly valuable when drafting evidence on whether a proposed “internship” is genuine.

Practical legal analysis G. Practical legal analysis. The first legal question in almost every case is classification. If the person will come to Switzerland to perform services under a foreign service contract, article 3 VZAE defines that as a cross-border service, and article 22 VZAE requires the service contract and posting confirmation to be filed with terms on duration, employment conditions and salary. If, instead, the person will be integrated into the Swiss employer’s organisation for a group-driven temporary transfer, article 46 VZAE is the better fit. If the core purpose is training or exchange, articles 39 to 42 and the SEM training materials should drive the analysis. Misclassification is dangerous because Swiss law is purpose-specific, and article 54 VZAE requires a new authorisation for a new purpose. 20

For an ICT case, the persuasive burden is not merely to show a group relationship; it is to show why the Swiss entity needs this person on a temporary transfer basis. The strongest files therefore include a group structure chart; evidence of the foreign and Swiss entities’ link; a role description showing senior managerial authority or genuinely indispensable specialist knowledge; a project or business narrative explaining why the transfer is temporary and necessary; salary and benefits evidence at Swiss market level; accommodation details; and, where GATS is relied on, proof of at least twelve months’ prior employment abroad in the group company immediately before entry. That evidential approach is not listed as one single statutory checklist, but it follows directly from article 46 VZAE and SEM’s instruction that the supporting documents must establish the decisive business and employee facts and justify the non-application of domestic-worker priority. 21

For a young professionals / stagiaire case, the legal logic is different. The strongest evidential package is one that looks unmistakably like a structured training placement: proof of nationality within the participating-country list, age compliance, completed vocational or higher education, occupation match, a written contract with an integrated training programme, starter-level market pay, and confirmation that the individual will not be self-employed, leased out or used as undifferentiated labour. Because the route depends on intergovernmental procedure, the file should also be prepared for filing from the person’s home country and not framed as a Swiss status-switch or in-country conversion. 11

For compulsory or curriculum-based internships, the decisive issue is whether the internship exists independently of the employer’s staffing needs. Article 39 VZAE requires the internship to sit within a full-time course and not exceed half of the overall training duration. SEM’s internship guidance goes further by requiring a university letter confirming that the intern is enrolled and that the internship is an integral part of the studies. Basel-Stadt’s public practice and Geneva’s labour-market guidance move

in the same direction by recognising only defined training-linked categories for third-country interns.

The main refusal risks are therefore predictable. They include: trying to use a transfer route for a role that reads like an ordinary local vacancy; failing to show that the employee is senior, highly qualified or indispensable; omitting proof of prior group employment where GATS is invoked; presenting a trainee role that is outside the person’s trained profession; using a generic internship title with no structured programme; salary that does not match local/cantonal expectations; staff-leasing or labour-hire features in a young professionals case; inability to prove a compulsory academic link; and permit- chaining patterns that suggest hidden long-term residence rather than a bounded special-purpose stay. Those are not exhaustive refusal codes, but they are the most likely pressure points when the statutory tests and the published administrative guidance are applied together. 23

H. Drafting cautions. When drafting an ICT application, avoid generic statements such as “specialist” or “key employee” without showing what knowledge is actually scarce, group-specific or commercially indispensable. The Swiss materials are much more comfortable with files that identify the executive chain, business responsibility, project necessity and temporary-assignment rationale in concrete terms. Where GATS is relevant, say so expressly and plead the prior foreign-group employment period and requested permit- duration bucket with care. 24

When drafting a training/internship application, do not mix purposes. A contract that reads partly as a training placement and partly as a normal productive role invites the authority to apply the anti- circumvention principle in article 23 VZAE. The training plan should therefore be specific, dated and measurable, and it should align with the person’s diploma, the proposed Swiss duties and, where applicable, the external educational framework. If the case is a post-study intra-group internship outside the classic stagiaire agreements, the onward deployment logic identified by SEM should be documented explicitly. 25

When drafting around duration and sequencing, pay attention to quotas, renewal restrictions and anti-chaining rules from the outset. Article 56 VZAE generally requires a break before another short-stay permit of the same type, and article 57 VZAE blocks the direct stacking of several short-stay categories, including stagiaire permits, subject only to narrow exceptions. Zurich’s guidance shows that cantons actively police these rules. A file that ignores them can fail even if the substantive role description is otherwise good. 14

Open questions and limitations. This pack is designed as a high-confidence authority set, not as an exhaustive treaty digest. It does not reproduce or analyse each bilateral stagiaire agreement country by country, and public cantonal practice is uneven: some cantons publish detailed labour-market notes, while others publish only short public guidance pages. Where a live matter depends on a specific canton’s unwritten practice, direct confirmation with the competent cantonal labour-market authority remains advisable. The overall federal structure, however, is clear from the sources above. 26
