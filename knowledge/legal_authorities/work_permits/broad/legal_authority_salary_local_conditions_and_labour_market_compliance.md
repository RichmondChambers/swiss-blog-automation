# Swiss legal authority pack on salary, local conditions, and labour market compliance in work permit cases

## Executive legal summary

## A. Executive legal summary

Swiss salary and local-conditions review in work-permit matters is anchored principally in article 22 of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and article 22 of the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Gainful Employment. In substance, a foreign national may be admitted for gainful employment only if the salary and working conditions are customary for the locality, profession and sector; for posted workers, the reimbursement amounts for posting-related expenses must also be customary, and reimbursement of travel, lodging and food is not treated as salary. In long-term postings, the reimbursement duty normally falls away only after more than twelve months of uninterrupted stay in Switzerland, subject to the minimum-wage exception in article 22a OASA. Equivalent article 22 controls also survive in the notification regime for provisionally admitted persons, recognised refugees and stateless persons through article 85a and article 65 OASA.<sup>1</sup>

The official purpose of this review is not merely migrant protection in the abstract. SEM states expressly that article 22 is meant both to protect foreign workers against abusive engagement conditions and to prevent domestic workers from facing competition from cheaper labour. SEM’s directives also explain the methodology: authorities should look first to legal rules and to the salary and working conditions offered for similar work in the same undertaking and sector, while also taking account of collective agreements, standard employment contracts and current wage statistics. SEM further notes that statistical surveys show average wages rather than hard legal minima; in practice, those averages can generally be treated as minimums in cities, while in some other regions somewhat lower figures may be acceptable in individual cases.<sup>2</sup>

Salary compliance is therefore a necessary condition of approval, but it is not the only one. For third- country local hires, SEM’s published admission framework adds cumulative requirements including general economic interest, quotas, priority for Swiss and EU/EFTA workers, vacancy-notification compliance where applicable, and personal qualifications. Geneva’s current public guidance illustrates how cantonal practice overlays the federal framework: OCIRT/OCPM review third-country salaried cases, expect proof of prior Swiss and EU/EFTA recruitment efforts, require customary branch salary conditions, and state that the job must be full-time; Geneva also notes that the authority may grant a different permit than requested or attach conditions and time limits.<sup>3</sup>

The employer’s evidential burden is correspondingly significant. Article 22 OASA requires a work contract or mandate confirmation; for cross-border services, a posting attestation and service contract must be filed, and these documents must state duration, engagement conditions and salary. SEM’s public guidance adds that the employer should attach a written contract signed by both sides and marked to the effect that it is valid only if the authorities grant work authorisation; SEM also says use of industry-standard forms is helpful and notes separate social-insurance and source-tax obligations.

Cantonal materials from Zurich, Geneva and Basel show, in practice, that authorities expect signed contracts, qualifications evidence, labour-market search evidence, and often supporting material showing that the proposed wage is locally customary.<sup>4</sup>

Benchmarking tools exist, but they do not all do the same job. SECO’s national wage calculator was developed in the context of the flanking measures and is expressly designed to indicate customary wages by region, occupation and economic sector; it uses ESS 2022 private-sector data, gives a median plus interquartile wage range, deliberately excludes gender, nationality and residence-status variables because those may carry discrimination potential, and shows narrower geographic outputs only when minimum data-quality thresholds are met. By contrast, the Federal Statistical Office’s Salarium is an informational salary estimator, not a salary recommendation, and it takes all available explanatory factors into account, including gender, nationality and residence status. As a practical inference from those official descriptions, Salarium is better treated as supplementary context than as the sole core exhibit in an immigration file where article 22 alignment must be defended in a locality-, profession- and sector-specific way.<sup>5</sup>

Published search-accessible summaries of Federal Supreme Court decisions also indicate that “customary wage” is not merely a pre-admission administrative concept. In 4A_425/2017, the Court summary states that, where no work authorisation had been sought, the civil judge could determine the customary wage under articles 22 LEtr and 22 OASA; in 4A_365/2021, the search summary indicates that the customary wage was fixed by reference to branch and local practice while taking account of all the circumstances. For internal drafting, that matters because an under-benchmarked work-permit file can create not only permit risk but later wage-claim risk.<sup>6</sup>

## Legal framework and authorities

## B. Structural overview

At federal level, the key architecture is straightforward. Articles 18 to 26 of the Act govern admission for gainful employment, with article 21 covering priority for the domestic and EU/EFTA labour market, article 21a covering vacancy-notification duties, article 22 governing salary and working conditions, article 23 dealing with personal qualifications, article 25 covering frontier workers, and article 26 addressing cross-border service providers. Article 85a then preserves labour-market compliance in notification-based work by certain protected categories.<sup>7</sup>

At ordinance level, article 22 OASA is especially important because it operationalises article 22: it identifies the benchmark sources for determining customary salary and working conditions and imposes the documentary filing duty. Article 22a then deals with the duration of reimbursement obligations in long-term postings, and article 65 OASA sets out the salary-containing notification regime for provisionally admitted persons, refugees and stateless persons, including the employer’s attestation of compliance.<sup>8</sup>

At guidance level, SEM’s chapter 4 directives are the principal administrative authority. Those directives explain the purpose of article 22 review, identify the evidential hierarchy to be used, and include sectoral annexes that show how function, training level, project structure, in-kind benefits and expense reimbursements are analysed in practice. SECO and BFS then supply the main official benchmarking tools, while cantons layer on their own forms, documentary expectations and local labour-market practices.<sup>9</sup>

## D. Primary legal texts

The primary federal texts to keep in the pack are: article 22 of the Act; article 22 of OASA; article 22a OASA for long-term postings; article 85a of the Act for notification-based work by provisionally admitted persons; and article 65 OASA for the detailed contents and legal effect of the employer’s notification. Where the case concerns third-country local hires, the surrounding admission provisions in articles 18, 21, 21a, 23, 25 and 26 also matter because they explain why salary compliance is necessary but not sufficient.<sup>17</sup>

For posted-worker or service-provider cases, the pack should also carry the Posted Workers Act and Posted Workers Ordinance as referred to by SEM, together with article 360a CO where minimum wages under standard employment contracts are relevant to the article 22a exception. SECO’s international wage-comparison page is not itself legislation, but it points directly to the operational law-and-guidance package used in that context.<sup>18</sup>

A practical note on citation hygiene is important. The bilingual article pages on Droit-bilingue are convenient for accessible article wording, but they state on their own face that they are not official publications and that only the publication of the Federal Chancellery is authoritative. For internal drafting, they are serviceable mirrors; for contentious filing, Fedlex/Bundeskanzlei remains the controlling text.<sup>19</sup>

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM’s directives and comments on chapter 4 of the foreigners domain are the centrepiece soft authority. The current PDF version visible through SEM’s site is marked as updated to 1 January 2026. Within that chapter, section 4.3.4 explains the purpose and method of article 22 review; section 4.8.2.3 applies article 22 to cross-border service providers; section 4.8.5 explains the notification regime for

refugees, provisionally admitted persons, stateless persons and protected persons; and sector-specific passages show how salary composition rules differ for professional sport, trainees and hospitality.<sup>20</sup>

One especially useful SEM document is the annex to section 4.3.4 on salary and working conditions. That annex states that, for foreign workers posted into Switzerland, the relevant remuneration includes salary paid abroad plus allowances paid for the Swiss service, but excludes true expense reimbursements such as travel, lodging and food. It also provides a structured model for presenting the remuneration breakdown and requires an employer attestation confirming that housing, food and travel costs are borne by the employer and are not deducted from the amount presented as remuneration.<sup>21</sup>

Another highly practical SEM authority is the two-page annex on project staff and service providers, particularly in IT. It treats project changes as equivalent to job changes requiring fresh permission, explains that many projects should be covered through shorter admission routes depending on genuine duration, warns that a “new” fixed-term direct hire by the Swiss branch amounts to a change of purpose requiring a new admission process, and states that pay must be fixed by reference to regional wage structures, requirement profiles and specialisations. For IT entrants with up to two years’ experience, the annex states that an annual customary salary of at least CHF 75,500 should be paid; for more experienced IT staff, salary should be measured in the light of education, experience, function, degree of specialisation and age, with the usual wage calculators as the minimum reference point.<sup>22</sup>

SECO’s official benchmarking ecosystem is also important. For postings, SECO’s international wage- comparison page explains that foreign employers posting workers to Switzerland must guarantee the working and salary conditions mandatorily prescribed in Switzerland, and it links both to an official calculation example and to the instruction on international wage comparison. SECO also points users to the entsendung.admin.ch platform, which identifies whether a generally binding collective agreement applies in a specific branch and region and what mandatory minimum wages it contains.<sup>23</sup>

For broader benchmarking, the national wage calculator and Salarium need to be distinguished, not conflated. SECO describes the national wage calculator as producing a median and interquartile wage range for a selected profile, based on ESS 2022 private-sector data and a model developed for the flanking measures; the calculator only displays a wage range at the chosen canton, labour-market-area or major-region level when minimum sample-quality tests are passed, otherwise it broadens the geographic level. BFS describes Salarium as an informational statistical salary calculator, not a salary recommendation, and notes that it seeks to reflect ESS salary levels as closely as possible using all available explanatory factors, including gender, nationality and residence status. BFS also publishes a comparison note stating that the two calculators were built in different contexts and pursue different objectives, so their models and outputs differ.<sup>5</sup>

Zurich’s Lohnbuch Schweiz is not legislation, but it is a widely used administrative working tool. The Canton of Zurich states that the 2026 edition provides a comprehensive overview of locally, professionally and sectorally customary wages and minimum wages in Switzerland, that it contains around 9,400 wage indications sourced from collective agreements and recommendations/statistics of Swiss professional and employer associations, and that it includes correlation tables to adapt figures to local norms of Swiss major regions. That makes it a useful corroborative exhibit where article 22 justification needs more nuance than a single calculator output can provide.<sup>24</sup>

## F. Cantonal practice notes

Zurich’s public material is particularly useful because it explains the permit file from the canton’s own labour-market perspective. Its January 2024 explanatory note for third-country nationals states that the employer, not the worker, must apply; that the authority can demand additional documents; that article 22 serves equal treatment of foreign and domestic workers; and that, for local hires, the employer must give the labour-market authority information on duration, employment conditions and remuneration, with a current and complete work contract. For postings, Zurich requires either a posting attestation or a posting contract depending on duration, and the required content includes the worker, the home and host company, function, task area, work location, start and duration, base salary in the home country, salary supplement, separate confirmation that all posting expenses are borne, insurance cover, social- security arrangements, and explicit treatment of holidays and working time or a clause applying Swiss law to unspecified points. Zurich also notes that, in occupations with pronounced skills shortages, some search evidence may be waived, but the shortage must be demonstrated by the applicant and temporary cyclical fluctuations do not suffice.<sup>25</sup>

Geneva’s published practice is both immigration-facing and labour-market-facing. On third-country salaried permits, Geneva states that the employer must have explored the Swiss and EU/EFTA labour markets first, must have announced the vacancy to the cantonal employment office, must respect customary branch salary and work conditions, and must file a detailed evidence bundle including the candidate’s CV, diplomas and work certificates, the employer’s motivation letter, recruitment evidence and a signed employment contract. Geneva also states, at least on the current public page, that the job must be full-time, and that OCIRT may attach limits or conditions to the authorisation. Separately, Geneva’s OCIRT “usages” pages explain that Geneva maintains around thirty sectoral reference documents on customary salaries and social benefits, offers attestations of compliance, and can sanction and publicly list non-compliant firms. Those “usages” materials are not federal immigration law, but they are highly relevant local reference points where Geneva labour-market compliance is in issue.<sup>26</sup>

Basel-Stadt, Vaud, Ticino and St Gallen show the same general pattern with local variations. Basel- Stadt’s third-country permit page links directly to Salarium as a way to calculate the wage owed, and its digital form repeatedly asks for signed contracts together with possible documents showing the locally, professionally and sectorally customary wage; for full third-country cases the same form asks for detailed search evidence, diplomas, a CV and an anonymised applicant list. Vaud’s notification form under article 85a states expressly that filing the notice counts as the employer’s attestation that it knows and will respect customary salary and working conditions; Vaud’s frontier-worker form requires, among other things, a signed work or mission contract and the latest payslip. Ticino’s labour-market office presents its remit in terms that include foreign labour, undeclared work and the risk of wage dumping, and its short-term activity page states that permit issuance remains subject to labour-market review including contingents, domestic priority, wage-and-work-condition control and qualifications. St Gallen’s notification form likewise states that submission counts as a declaration that the employer or third party knows and complies with customary salary and working conditions or with the special conditions of an integration measure.<sup>27</sup>

## Practical legal analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis

The first practical point is that “salary compliance” in Swiss work-permit cases is not a freestanding commercial negotiation issue. It is a statutory admission condition. Where article 22 is not satisfied, lawful admission cannot be granted; but where article 22 is satisfied, the file may still fail on economic- interest, priority, vacancy-notification, qualification or quota grounds. Internal systems therefore should

not treat salary review as either optional or decisive by itself. It is a gatekeeper criterion sitting inside a cumulative test.<sup>28</sup>

The second point is that the authorities look through job titles to functions. That is implicit in article 22 OASA’s insistence on comparators for “the same work” in the same undertaking and branch, and it becomes explicit in the guidance materials. SEM’s IT annex says salary is to be set in light of regional wage structures, requirement profiles and specialisations, and that more experienced IT workers must be assessed by education, experience, function, degree of specialisation and age. SEM’s trainee guidance says pay must reflect function and training, including semesters completed; its hospitality guidance ties remuneration to collective-agreement categories while taking account of training length and experience. Put simply, the stronger the claim that the person is a specialist or senior worker, the more dangerous an under-level salary becomes for both article 22 and article 23 credibility. That last point is an inference from the official materials, but it is a strong one.<sup>29</sup>

The third point is that contract structure is part of salary compliance, not a separate housekeeping matter. OASA article 22 requires the contract or mandate confirmation to state the duration, the engagement conditions and the salary. SEM’s public guidance recommends a signed contract expressly conditional on permit issuance, and its directives say the applicant should use the chosen contract form in the language of the foreign worker. Zurich’s guidance adds that the contract must be current and complete. In posting scenarios, structure is even more important: expense reimbursement must sit outside salary, and the authority expects a posting-specific instrument showing home and host entities, function, duties, place of work and the remuneration split. The SEM labour-leasing guidance adds a further warning: if the workers are subject to the host undertaking’s direction and control, the legal position may be one of labour leasing even if the parties have labelled the arrangement differently.<sup>30</sup>

The fourth point is the distinction between legally acceptable pay and persuasively market-aligned pay. The authorities do not say, in terms, that a salary must be “persuasive”; that is an analytic distinction. But it is useful. Legally acceptable pay means a remuneration package that can be defended under article 22 using the recognised benchmark sources: binding CBA or standard contract where one exists, same-employer comparators, same-branch comparators, and relevant statistics. Persuasive market alignment means going further and showing that the proposed salary coheres with the actual function, seniority, geography and contractual setting of the role. SEM’s own methodology supports that approach because it places emphasis on same-enterprise comparators and current statistical evidence, while also warning that statistical averages are only averages and that their practical use varies by region. For internal systems, that means a file is stronger when it triangulates multiple benchmark sources rather than resting on one generic calculator printout.<sup>31</sup>

The fifth point is that official benchmarking tools need to be deployed with discipline. SECO’s national wage calculator is the more article-22-adjacent tool when the issue is customary wages in the Swiss anti-dumping context: it is built for that context, excludes discriminatory variables, and gives a median plus an interquartile span, broadening geographically when sample quality does not support a narrower local output. Salarium does something different: it estimates salary levels for a selected profile, for information purposes, using all available explanatory factors in the ESS, including gender, nationality and residence status. Neither tool is a binding salary recommendation. As an internal drafting rule, then, the stronger practice is to use SECO’s national wage calculator, sectoral CBA/NAV materials, same-company comparators and canton-specific references as the spine of the benchmark, with Salarium reserved as supporting context rather than the sole legal anchor. That is an inference from the official descriptions, but it is a cautious and defensible one.<sup>5</sup>

The sixth point is that notification-based cases should not be underestimated. In article 85a / article 65 OASA regimes, the notice itself must carry salary, location, working-time and identity information, and

its transmission is the employer’s attestation that it knows and will comply with customary salary and employment conditions. SEM further states that cantonal authorities may seek clarifications and warn of sanctions where the notification duty or its attached conditions are breached, and the salary- compliance standard therefore remains live even where no conventional work-permit decision is issued beforehand. Published court summaries also suggest that, if salary compliance was poor, the issue can later reappear in civil wage litigation through judicial reconstruction of the “customary” wage.<sup>32</sup>

## H. Drafting cautions

When building or auto-generating Swiss work-permit content, the following cautions are the safest ones to hard-code:

- Do not rely on the job title alone. The file should narrate the actual function, reporting line,

duties, skill scarcity, required qualifications, seniority and work location, because the official materials repeatedly tie salary review to the underlying function and comparator universe rather than to labels.<sup>33</sup>

- Use a dated, fully signed, current contract. SEM’s public guidance recommends wording that the

contract is valid only if the authorities issue the work authorisation, and SEM’s directives say the chosen contract form should be in the language of the foreign worker. Zurich’s guidance also stresses that the contract must be current and complete.<sup>34</sup>

- Separate salary from reimbursements. Travel, lodging and food costs in posting cases are not

salary. The remuneration model should show base salary, any posting allowance, and true expense reimbursements separately. Do not “improve” the benchmark by folding expenses into gross pay.<sup>35</sup>

- Be careful with in-kind benefits and variable pay. SEM’s professional-sport guidance allows some

concrete in-kind items to be counted for article 22 review in that specific sector, but excludes result-linked bonuses, expense reimbursements and certain other benefits from the salary calculation. That guidance is sector-specific, so it should be used by analogy only with care.<sup>36</sup>

- Do not paper over a local hire as a posting. SEM’s IT annex says that where a new fixed-term

direct employment relationship is created with the Swiss branch, the matter generally becomes a new Swiss admission procedure. Likewise, if the host undertaking exercises direction and control, labour-leasing rules may apply regardless of the contract title.<sup>37</sup>

- If the proposed salary is at or below the low end of the benchmark range, explain why. SEM’s

statement that somewhat lower figures may sometimes be acceptable outside cities is guidance, not an automatic discount. Internal drafts should therefore explain geography, collective- agreement category, company comparator logic, hours, experience level and any sector-specific features, not merely quote a median.<sup>38</sup>

- Prefer a benchmark hierarchy, not a single tool. Best practice is usually: binding CBA/CTT or

minimum wage first; same-company and same-branch comparators next; SECO national wage calculator and cantonal materials after that; Salarium as supplementary context. This is particularly important because SECO and BFS themselves say the calculators were built for different purposes and use different models.<sup>39</sup>

- For Geneva files, expect extra local framing. Geneva’s published practice currently expects proof

of OCE vacancy notification, broader Swiss and EU/EFTA searches, full-time employment, customary branch salary conditions, and a documentary bundle including recruitment evidence and a signed contract. Geneva’s separate “usages” materials can also affect how local market conformity is perceived.<sup>26</sup>

- For Zurich and Basel files, expect the authority to ask for wage-proofing documents where the

wage is not self-evidently compliant. Zurich and Basel both publish evidence-heavy pathways, and Basel’s digital form expressly asks, where appropriate, for documents showing the locally, professionally and sectorally customary wage.<sup>40</sup>

- Maintain a reusable evidence bundle. The most defensible Swiss file will usually include

recruitment chronology, vacancy-notification proof where relevant, anonymised applicant table, CV and diplomas, signed contract, benchmark printouts, payroll/deduction assumptions, social- insurance and source-tax set-up, and, for notification regimes, the filed notice carrying the salary data and attestation.<sup>41</sup>
