# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Hiring EU/EFTA Nationals into Switzerland Executive legal summary

As at 27 April 2026, the core Swiss framework for hiring EU/EFTA nationals is the Swiss-EU Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the parallel EFTA free-movement framework, implemented in Swiss domestic law through the free-movement ordinance and applied ahead of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act where the treaty regime says otherwise. Swiss official guidance treats EU and EFTA nationals together for operational purposes; Croatia now falls under the unrestricted regime from 1 January 2026, while UK nationals are outside the free-movement framework since 1 January 2021.<sup>1</sup>

What is “simpler” than a non-EU case is substantial but easy to overstate. For ordinary EU/EFTA hires, Swiss guidance does not require the third-country model of highly qualified status, quota availability, and proof that no suitable Swiss or EU/EFTA worker is available. Instead, the ordinary route is based mainly on nationality, a real employment relationship, and the correct procedural channel. Swiss authorities also state that, for EU/EFTA nationals, the residence permit doubles as the work permit.<sup>2</sup>

The simplification is not the same thing as a procedure-free regime. A Swiss employer hiring an EU/EFTA national for up to three months in a calendar year normally uses the online notification procedure rather than a residence permit, and that notification must ordinarily be filed no later than the day before work starts. If the employment lasts longer than three months, the worker must register with the commune within 14 days of arrival and before starting work, presenting identity documents and written proof of employment; the outcome is usually an L permit for employment up to 364 days or a B permit for employment of at least one year or for an indefinite term. Frontier workers use the G permit route.<sup>3</sup>

The main employer misunderstandings are these. First, “EU/EFTA” does not mean “no permit issues at all”; it often means “notification instead of permit” or “registration-driven permit” rather than “substantive labour-market approval.” Second, the eight-day notification-free rule belongs to posted workers and self-employed service providers; it does not displace the day-before notification requirement for short direct hires by Swiss employers. Third, the 90-day service-provider route is limited, excludes certain sectors such as staff leasing and financial services, and over-90-day services outside the treaty’s liberalised scope fall back into the FNIA regime. Fourth, wage and working-condition controls still bite, especially for postings and services.<sup>4</sup>

## Structural overview

This pack is organised for internal content use around the practical routing question that matters most in Swiss free-movement law: is the individual being hired by a Swiss employer, entering Switzerland as a cross-border service provider or posted worker, or commuting from abroad as a frontier worker? Swiss law treats those routes differently, and much of the confusion in employer-facing drafting comes from mixing them together. Primary legal texts. The core legal texts for this topic are the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU, the parallel EFTA framework, the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, and the Ordinance on the Introduction of the Free Movement of Persons. For service cases and wage-protection issues, the Posted Workers Act and Posted Workers Ordinance also matter. SEM’s legal-bases page identifies the AFMP and the free-movement ordinance as the principal sectoral

texts, while FNIA art. 2 confirms the treaty-first structure and expressly names both the EU and EFTA instruments.<sup>22</sup>

Official guidance and administrative materials. In practice, the most operationally useful federal materials are SEM’s AFMP FAQ, the short-term notification page, the permit pages for L/B/G EU/EFTA permits, and the current factsheets on gainful activity, frontier workers, and cross-border service provision. For employer-side compliance outside pure residence law, the key sources are SECO’s flanking-measures materials, work.swiss guidance on the vacancy-notice obligation, and SERI’s recognition guidance for regulated professions.<sup>23</sup>

## Cantonal practice notes

Zurich’s current official pages closely track the federal distinction between direct hires, notifications, and permits, but they add useful operational clarifications. Zurich states that EU/EFTA nationals working in Switzerland for more than 90 days must register personally with the commune within 14 days, while short work up to 90 days uses the notification route. Zurich also makes especially clear that direct hires by Swiss employers must be notified from the first day, whereas the “eight notification-free days” concept is about postings and self-employed service provision. Zurich further warns that self-employed status is assessed under Swiss law, so a person treated as self-employed abroad can be treated as employed in Switzerland.<sup>24</sup>

Geneva’s current employer-facing page illustrates how cantonal process design can be more granular than the federal high-level categories. Geneva states that any EU/AELE national hired by a Swiss company for more than three months needs a residence authorisation with work, but its current e- procedure distinguishes between contracts over four months and “120 days or up to four months” cases. Geneva also says that regulated activity may require an additional professional authorisation, and that certain short-term cases can begin work on entry or once a complete file has been sent. Those are operational practice notes, not universal nationwide rules.<sup>25</sup>

Ticino’s current pages reinforce the same federal architecture but in stricter operational language. For short-term employment by a Swiss employer, Ticino says the Swiss employer must make the online notification at least the day before the work begins. For B-permit applications, Ticino says workers must submit the request before beginning work and warns that late filing may lead to a fine. This is useful for drafting because it shows that a content system should not assume canton-neutral implementation messaging.<sup>26</sup>

Solothurn’s current frontier-worker service page is another good cautionary example. For an EU/EFTA frontier worker, Solothurn states that work may start only once the G permit has been issued. Whether one characterises that as a conservative administrative instruction or simply cantonal workflow, it confirms that commencement-of-work language can vary in canton-facing materials and should be checked where start dates are commercially important.<sup>27</sup>

## Practical legal analysis

For ordinary hires by Swiss employers, the legally safest way to think about the EU/EFTA regime is this: Switzerland has simplified admission, not abolished process. The worker does not need the third- country style pre-entry labour-market approval model, and the residence permit itself serves as the work authorisation. But the employer still needs a real written employment relationship; short hires still need the notification filing; and longer hires still need communal registration and permit issuance.

Once admitted, EU/EFTA workers benefit from much broader mobility than third-country hires, including the ability to change employer, job, and place of work within Switzerland.<sup>28</sup>

The most important doctrinal distinction is between a Swiss employment relationship and cross-border service provision. A Swiss company hiring an EU/EFTA national for up to three months uses the short- hire notification route; beyond that, the case becomes an L/B/G permit question. By contrast, postings and self-employed services sit inside a separate, only partially liberalised service regime: there is usually an eight-day advance notice requirement once the service exceeds eight days in the calendar year, several sectors require notice from the first day, the 90-day ceiling is counted per company and per service provider, and services beyond 90 days outside specially liberalised sectors fall back into the FNIA labour-market and quota system.<sup>29</sup>

Several employer misunderstandings recur in official materials. One is to assume that “no permit required” means “no filing required”; for short Swiss-employer hires, that is wrong because notification still applies. Another is to apply the eight-day notification-free rule to all short-term activity; Zurich and SEM make clear that this rule belongs to postings and self-employed service providers, not to direct hires by Swiss employers. A third is to treat the service route as a general substitute for permits; SEM expressly says it does not cover all services, excludes staffing and certain financial services, and does not create an entitlement for over-90-day services outside the treaty scope.<sup>30</sup>

Employer-side compliance does not stop at immigration filing. In the service and posting context, SECO’s flanking measures and the Posted Workers Act require compliance with Swiss minimum wage and working conditions, and Zurich warns that documentary failures or false self-employment can lead to sanctions. Separately, the domestic vacancy-notice regime may require certain vacancies to be registered first with the public employment service where the occupation’s unemployment rate reaches the statutory threshold; that obligation is independent of the AFMP route and is backed by FNIA sanctions, although jobs lasting no more than 14 days are exempt.<sup>31</sup>

One difficult drafting point deserves special care. The treaty-style text reflected in the EFTA Annex says that completion of permit formalities should not prevent an applicant from immediately taking up contracted employment, but SEM’s operational guidance for EU/EFTA workers says that workers staying longer than three months must register within 14 days and before actually taking up work. Geneva and Solothurn then show still more canton-specific operational messaging. The best internal drafting approach is therefore conservative: state the federal operational rule, then add that canton-specific commencement practice should be checked where timing matters. That conclusion is an inference from the cited materials rather than a single express federal sentence.<sup>32</sup>

## Drafting cautions and source list

Drafting cautions. Content should always distinguish between direct employment by a Swiss employer, frontier commuting, and cross-border service provision. It should avoid phrases such as “EU/EFTA nationals need no permit” unless the sentence immediately explains whether the case is actually notification-based, commune-registration-based, or frontier-permit-based. It should also avoid importing service-provider rules into the direct-hire route, especially the eight-day rule.<sup>33</sup>

Content should ask, or internally classify, at least six routing facts before generating advice: nationality; whether the employer is Swiss or foreign; whether the person will live in Switzerland or abroad; the planned duration in the current calendar year; whether the activity is direct employment, posting, or self-employed service provision; and whether the role is regulated professionally. Without those facts, Swiss free-movement content is prone to category errors.<sup>34</sup>

Where English-language legislation is used internally, the content system should flag that it is informational only. The Refworld-hosted English FNIA text describes itself as an official translation but also states that the English text has no legal force and that the legally authoritative texts are the German, French, and Italian versions. For outward-facing legal drafting, it is safer to cite the official- language Swiss text and use English guidance only as an explanatory aid.<sup>35</sup>

Content should also avoid turning cantonal process pages into nationwide propositions. Geneva, Zurich, Ticino, and Solothurn all reflect the same federal architecture, but they message commencement, filing mechanics, and document requirements differently. Canton-specific drafting should therefore be marked as cantonal practice, not as federal law.<sup>36</sup>

Source list. The core treaty and framework sources used here are SEM’s free-movement overview and legal-bases page, the SEM factsheets on the AFMP, gainful activity, frontier workers, and cross-border services, the EFTA Annex K text for article-level free-movement rules, and FNIA art. 2 as translated in the official non-authentic English text.<sup>37</sup>

The main federal operational guidance sources are SEM’s AFMP FAQ, SEM’s short-term notification page, the SEM pages for L/B/G EU/EFTA permits, SECO’s flanking-measures pages, work.swiss vacancy-notice guidance, and SERI’s regulated-profession recognition pages.<sup>38</sup>

The cantonal practice examples used here are Zurich, Geneva, Ticino, and Solothurn official pages. They are included to show implementation mechanics and drafting risk, not because they alter the federal legal framework.<sup>39</sup>

Open questions / limitations. This pack is strongest on current federal guidance and operational routing. Direct machine access to article-level Fedlex pages was limited in this environment, so the treaty structure and ordinance references were anchored through official SEM legal-bases pages, current SEM factsheets, the EFTA Annex K text, and the official non-authentic FNIA translation. Cantonal practice can change faster than federal law, so start-date messaging and local document lists should be re-checked at canton level before publication or case use.<sup>40</sup>
