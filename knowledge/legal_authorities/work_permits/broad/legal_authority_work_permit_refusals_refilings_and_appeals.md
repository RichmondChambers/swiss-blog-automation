# Swiss Work Permit Refusals, Re-filings and Appeals Authority Pack Suggested filename: legal_authority_work_permit_refusals_refilings_and_appeals_pack.pdf

This pack is designed for internal immigration-content use. It is written in English, but the underlying Swiss authorities searched and used here include German, French, Italian and English official materials. A critical threshold point is that the decisive legal regime for most refusal analysis in this topic is the third-country national regime under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and its implementing ordinance; EU/EFTA workers generally benefit from a materially different, more liberal framework under free movement rules. The federal English versions are useful for orientation, but Swiss official legal force rests in the German, French and Italian texts. 1

## Executive synthesis A. Executive legal summary

Swiss work permit refusal analysis is structurally straightforward, even though the filing practice is highly evidence-heavy. For third-country nationals, the official refusal logic is built mainly around overall economic interest, annual quota availability, labour-market precedence, vacancy-notification duties, local salary and employment conditions, the worker’s qualification profile, and basic admissibility matters such as accommodation and a properly supported employer-led application. Those themes appear consistently in the FNIA statutory scheme, the SEM’s admission-criteria guidance, the SEM’s application-document checklist, and cantonal practice notes. 2

The process is layered. The employer files with the competent cantonal labour-market or migration authority; the canton screens the case and makes a preliminary decision; if the canton approves, the file goes to the State Secretariat for Migration for federal approval; only after that do visa and residence- registration steps follow. That structure matters because it splits refusal strategy into at least two different questions: whether the authority committed a reviewable legal or procedural error, and whether the file was simply too weak on the evidence required by the admission rules. 3

The appeal/re-file distinction should therefore be treated as a legal triage exercise. A refusal is more obviously appeal-worthy where the authority applied the wrong legal framework, ignored or incompletely assessed relevant evidence, used the wrong salary or labour-market benchmark, violated procedural guarantees, or unlawfully delayed issuing a decision. Under the Administrative Procedure Act, federal appeal grounds include violation of federal law, incorrect or incomplete establishment of relevant facts, and—in principle—inadequacy. By contrast, where the authority has simply applied the admission rules to an under-evidenced file, a rebuilt application is usually the more practical route. That conclusion is an inference from the structure of the admitted appeal grounds on the one hand and the very detailed documentary requirements on the other. 4

A separate caution concerns entitlement. Zurich’s official guidance expressly states that there is no legal entitlement to a specific permit, and no entitlement to extension purely as such. At federal Supreme

Court level, the Federal Supreme Court Act excludes the ordinary public-law appeal route for foreign- national decisions concerning permits to which neither federal law nor international law grants a right. In practice, that means higher-level litigation is usually strongest when the challenge is framed as legal, factual or procedural error rather than mere disagreement with an adverse discretionary assessment.

A further procedural split is important for route-mapping. SEM materials indicate that where a canton wishes to refuse the grant, renewal or extension of a permit that would otherwise have required SEM approval, the canton may refuse in its own competence and must issue a decision stating the cantonal remedies. If the canton approves and SEM later refuses federal approval, the SEM’s formal decision becomes the federal act that is normally challenged under the federal appeal structure. That is the key dividing line between cantonal-remedy cases and FAC-bound federal-remedy cases. 6

### B. Structural overview

The operative structure of Swiss work-permit refusal work can be reduced to five questions. First, which admission rule failed: economic interest, quota, labour-market test, vacancy reporting, salary conditions, role design, qualification profile, accommodation, or procedural completeness? Second, which decision-maker refused: canton, SEM, or a cantonal appeal body? Third, is the defect legal/ procedural, or evidential and curable? Fourth, is timing now dominated by a fixed appeal deadline, by quota timing, or by a need to rebuild the file? Fifth, does the case sit in a special sub-category—self- employment, intra-group transfer, posted service provider, frontier worker, short stay under four months, or UK national quota logic—where the evidential package changes materially? 7

## Authority matrix D. Primary legal texts

The central statutory text is the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act. For salaried work, Art. 18 requires overall economic interest, an employer application and satisfaction of Arts. 20–25. For self- employment, Art. 19 adds financial and operational requirements and an adequate independent income source. Art. 21 governs labour-market precedence; the SEM’s own guidance explains that third-

country nationals can be admitted only if no suitable worker from Switzerland or the EU/EFTA space can be recruited first. Art. 22 regulates salary and employment conditions; Art. 23 regulates personal requirements; Art. 24 requires suitable accommodation; and Art. 25 sets a distinct frontier-worker rule under which Arts. 20, 23 and 24 do not apply. 18

The implementing ordinance is the Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment. The SEM identifies it, alongside the FNIA, as the basic legal source for admission criteria. It is also the vehicle through which annual quota numbers are set and adjusted. The current SEM directives page confirms that the third-country work chapter and associated annexes are maintained in German, French and Italian and were updated as of 1 January 2026. 19

For federal remedies, the key procedural text is the Administrative Procedure Act. Under the APA, any ruling is in principle appealable; standing requires participation or denied participation below, specific affectation, and a protectable interest; grounds include violation of federal law, incorrect or incomplete fact-finding, and—in principle—inadequacy; the appeal period is 30 days from notification; and the appeal petition must state the application, grounds, evidence and signature and attach the contested ruling where available. The Federal Administrative Court Act matters because FAC proceedings are generally governed by the APA unless special law provides otherwise. 15

At the next tier, the Federal Supreme Court Act matters mainly for its limits. Its immigration exception rules make the ordinary public-law appeal route unavailable for permit decisions where neither federal law nor international law confers an entitlement. That does not remove the importance of federal litigation entirely, but it does narrow the strategic value of pushing every third-country work permit refusal beyond the FAC stage. 20

A final primary text for route mapping is Art. 99 FNIA, on SEM approval. The statute provides that the Federal Council determines which cantonal permit grants and cantonal labour-market preliminary decisions must be submitted to the SEM for approval, and that the SEM may refuse approval or attach limits, conditions or requirements. This is the statutory hinge between cantonal assessment and federal approval review. 21

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful federal administrative source is the SEM’s page on the basis for admission to the Swiss employment market. It consolidates the official refusal architecture in one place: overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, vacancy registration, salary and employment conditions, personal requirements, exceptions, and accommodation. It also states expressly that applicants must explain and document why the admission is in the overall economic interest, and it gives unusually concrete examples of acceptable labour-market evidence, including RAV and EURES proof, sector- specific advertisements, agency confirmations and a candidate list showing why applicants were unsuitable. 22

The SEM procedure page is the key route source. It confirms the filing sequence: employer to canton, cantonal preliminary decision, SEM review and formal SEM decision, then visa issuance where required, followed by Swiss registration. It also states that the SEM decision does not itself authorise entry, and that federal registration after entry must occur within 14 days. For incomplete files, the SEM’s application documents page is equally important: the SEM and cantonal employment services will only process complete applications, and the minimum bundle includes the form, passport copy, recruitment evidence, CV, qualifications, translated documents where necessary, reasons for the hire, job description, company information and salary documents. 23

The SEM directives and commentaries are current and multilingual. The 2026 directives landing pages confirm the availability of Chapter 4, “Stay with gainful employment”, in German, French and Italian, with annexes dealing inter alia with vacancy-notification rules, salary and employment conditions, project/service-provider cases, specialist cooks, professional athletes and artists. These are especially useful when a refusal depends on a sectoral or sub-category rule rather than the broad FNIA articles alone. 24

On vacancy reporting, the State Secretariat for Economic Affairs and arbeit.swiss remain essential. The SEM’s own guidance states that Art. 21a FNIA requires vacancy notification in certain professions and refers readers to the annually updated list. arbeit.swiss confirms that the 2026 set of occupations subject to the job-registration requirement expanded again compared with 2025 and that all occupations that were reportable in 2025 remain reportable in 2026. 25

For federal litigation procedure, the FAC’s own page on proceedings at the FAC is the best official practice source in this research set. It states that FAC proceedings are generally governed by the APA, that there is no mandatory legal representation, that Art. 52 APA sets the substantive requirements for the appeal petition, and that Art. 49 APA governs the grounds of appeal. 26

### F. Cantonal practice notes

Zurich. Zurich is especially useful for refusal analysis because its official public materials are unusually candid. The canton’s migration page sets out a step-by-step process in which the labour-market authority issues a preliminary employment-market decision, positive cases are sent to the SEM for approval, and the migration office can still refuse the permit on foreign-national grounds if revocation reasons exist. Zurich’s January 2024 explanatory note for third-country work also says there is no legal entitlement to a particular permit or to extension; that applications are assessed individually; that extra documents may be required; that employee-filed applications are inadmissible because the employer must apply; and that shortage occupations may justify relaxing some domestic-recruitment proof if the applicant demonstrates a genuine skilled-labour shortage. It also insists that qualifications must fit the new role and, in shortage occupations, points to vocational qualifications or at least five years’ experience as possible alternatives where no university degree is required. 27

Geneva. Geneva’s official page for salaried employment of a third-country national is valuable because it highlights cantonal intensification of the labour-market test. It advises employers to file roughly six to eight weeks before entry. It states that the OCPM grants the authorisation only if the foreign-labour directorate of the OCIRT accepts the case in advance, and it lists the conditions checked: economic interest, labour-market priority, qualifications, quota availability, salary and working conditions, and— importantly—full-time employment. It also says that, in Geneva, the employer must show the vacancy was notified to the cantonal employment office as quickly as possible regardless of the unemployment rate in the sector, and that wider Swiss/EU/EFTA search efforts were made in time. Geneva additionally notes that employing a foreign national without the required work authorisation is criminally prosecutable. 28

Basel-Stadt. Basel-Stadt’s official third-country work page is shorter but operationally useful. It directs employers to the cantonal permit form; links expressly to the SEM’s admission-criteria page for the central federal tests; points to the official Salarium salary calculator; and lists the core federal legal bases, including the FNIA and OASA. That makes Basel a clean source for the proposition that salary benchmarking should be evidence-led and that the canton expects applicants to anchor salary submissions in objective tools, not assertion alone. 29

Vaud. Vaud’s Form 1350 is instructive because it shows, in a single official document, the fields the canton treats as operationally material: role type (director, cadre, qualified employee, non-qualified employee), current staff numbers, weekly working time, gross pay, deductions, 13th salary, intended start date, permit duration sought, place of filing depending on domicile, and the annexed employment contract. The form also states clearly that employment may not begin until the cantonal authorities have decided, and it contains a field for the prior labour-market decision of the work-market surveillance authority. As evidence of cantonal practice, it is highly useful for identifying exactly what a rebuilt file must make explicit. 30

## Practical analysis G. Practical legal analysis

Refusal grounds that are usually identifiable from the record. In practice, the most common officially identifiable refusal bases are not obscure. They are usually one or more of the following: the employer has not shown overall economic interest; no valid employer-led application was made; quotas are unavailable; the labour-market priority test was not met; vacancy-notification proof is missing where required; salary or working conditions do not match local norms; the role is not a manager/ specialist/other qualified-worker post or the candidate’s profile does not fit it; accommodation is not shown; or the file is simply incomplete. SEM and cantonal materials repeatedly emphasise completeness, specificity and documentary proof. A refusal letter that maps onto one of these themes is often signalling a file-quality issue rather than a hidden legal theory. 31

When an appeal is the stronger response. Use the appeal route first where the refusal appears to rest on legal or procedural error rather than evidential weakness. The clearest examples are these: the authority applied the wrong legal regime or sub-category; it ignored relevant evidence that was already in the file; it established the facts incompletely; it used an incorrect salary benchmark or misunderstood the role classification; or it violated the applicant’s procedural guarantees. The APA expressly recognises these grounds. Appeal is also the right route for unlawful refusal to decide or unlawful delay, because the APA allows that challenge at any time. And where a permit matter has passed through cantonal adjudication and then encounters a problematic SEM approval refusal, the FAC’s 2024 press release in F-2182/2021 shows that approval-layer procedural issues can themselves become strong federal-law points. 32

When a re-file is often stronger than an appeal. Where the refusal is rooted in a weak or incomplete dossier, a rebuilt application will often fit the Swiss framework better than a pure appellate attack. That is especially true where the defects are curable: thin labour-market testing, missing RAV/EURES material, no candidate comparison grid, inadequate explanation of the company’s economic interest, low or unbenchmarked salary, a role description that reads as junior or non-specialist, missing translations, an unsigned or insufficiently conditional contract, or lack of supporting material for projects or start-ups. The authorities’ own checklists are so detailed that a second, materially stronger filing can often present a genuinely different case record rather than a mere repetition of the first. 33

Fresh evidence after an appeal decision. If the case has already reached an appellate authority and subsequently decisive new facts or evidence emerge, Swiss federal procedure does provide a formal review mechanism. APA Art. 66 permits review where relevant new facts or evidence are introduced, where the appellate authority overlooked relevant facts on record or specific applications, or where important procedural rights such as recusal, file inspection or the right to be heard were violated. That is not the same thing as a routine “reapplication”, but it is a meaningful source-grounded point for post- decision strategy. 34

Evidential rebuilding after refusal. A stronger re-file should usually rebuild five evidential pillars. First, the labour-market chronology: dated vacancy notice, RAV and EURES evidence where applicable, sector- standard advertisements, agency confirmations and a candidate list showing objective reasons for rejection. Second, the role architecture: a precise job description, reporting line, specialist need, and explanation why the role serves Swiss economic interests. Third, the remuneration package: salary, hours, 13th salary if any, deductions, allowances and objective benchmarking against official or cantonal tools. Fourth, the personal file: table-form CV, diplomas, references, passport copy and certified translations where needed. Fifth, the company/business evidence: for projects, order confirmation, project description and implementation plan; for start-ups, incorporation documents and a three-year business plan with market and competition analysis. Those are not generic best practices; they are the documentary expectations the authorities themselves publish. 35

Interaction between quotas, labour-market testing, salary and role design. These factors do not operate independently. A role that is weakly designed as a non-specialist or marginally skilled post makes it harder to prove both overall economic interest and the Art. 23 qualification threshold. A salary that sits below local sector norms can independently defeat the case even if recruitment efforts were real. Weak labour-market testing also bleeds into the economic-interest analysis, because the authority will question why the Swiss market genuinely needed the foreign hire. Quotas add a separate constraint: they are not a substitute for merits, but they can change timing strategy even where merits are good. Geneva’s full-time expectation and Zurich’s shortage-occupation practice are especially important here: they show that cantons do not only check whether a candidate is qualified; they also test whether the role itself is credibly built as a third-country admission case. 36

Procedural timing. The filing sequence should be treated as part of merits strategy, not an afterthought. Geneva recommends filing about six to eight weeks before entry. SEM procedure guidance confirms the canton-first, SEM-second sequence and says the SEM decision does not itself authorise entry. At federal level, the ordinary APA appeal period is 30 days from notification. Federal law also allows an appeal at any time for unlawful refusal or delay in issuing a ruling. One point that needs careful drafting is work start. The SEM’s general procedure page says the employee can only begin work after registration in Switzerland, whereas Geneva states that persons holding an entry authorisation with employment or an assurance of residence authorisation may work from entry. That is exactly the kind of canton-specific execution point that should be checked against the actual decision wording before any start date is promised in client-facing text. 37

Challenge-route map. For internal workflow purposes, a practical route map can be stated as follows. If the canton refuses in its own competence, follow the cantonal remedies identified in the refusal and the applicable cantonal time limits. If the canton approves and the SEM refuses approval, the challenge moves into the federal APA/FAC lane. If a cantonal court has already ruled in favour and the SEM still attempts to use approval power to undo that result, F-2182/2021 shows a potentially significant procedural argument about separation of powers and the proper route to the Federal Supreme Court. This route map is not a substitute for reading the legal-remedies notice, but it is a defensible authority- based starting structure. 38

## Drafting cautions H. Drafting cautions

Do not quote English federal translations as though they were the legally authoritative text. Both the FNIA and the APA English versions accessible through official channels are expressly marked as

information-only translations without legal force. Internal drafting may be in English, but the decisive legal wording should be checked in German, French or Italian before publication or litigation use. 39

Do not conflate appeal, review, and re-filing. Under Swiss federal procedure, an appeal attacks a ruling within a deadline and on recognised grounds; review under APA Art. 66 targets an appellate decision based on serious new facts/evidence or procedural defects; a fresh application is a new permit file. These are not interchangeable labels, and the wrong label can distort user guidance. 40

Do not describe every refusal as a legal error. If the employer simply failed to produce RAV/EURES proof, candidate-comparison material, translations, a coherent specialist role description, salary benchmarking or required project/start-up documents, the sources point toward dossier weakness, not necessarily appealable unlawfulness. Internal content should train the system to distinguish “authority got the law wrong” from “the file did not prove the law was met”. 41

Do not promise work start dates casually. Federal SEM guidance and Geneva canton practice are not perfectly identical on when employment may begin after entry. Content should therefore use cautious wording tied to the decision, the canton, visa status and registration requirements, rather than a blanket statement. 42

Do not under-specify the role. Cantonal materials show that role design is scrutinised directly: Vaud’s form asks whether the worker is a director, cadre, qualified or non-qualified employee; Geneva expects a full-time post; Zurich tests whether qualifications align with the actual role and permits relaxation in shortage occupations only if that shortage is itself demonstrated. A vague “skilled worker” label is not good enough for Swiss refusal analysis. 43

Do not omit objective recruitment reasoning. The SEM expressly suggests a list of candidates with brief details of missing qualifications, and Zurich asks for detailed candidate information and rejection reasons. That means internal content should favour neutral, evidence-based candidate-comparison drafting over conclusory language such as “no local candidate was suitable”. 44

Do not assume quota issues and merits issues are the same thing. Quotas are a separate annual gatekeeper under the federal scheme. Internal text should therefore avoid implying that quota pressure proves a failure on salary, labour-market testing or qualifications, or conversely that a merits- strong file automatically succeeds regardless of quota timing. 45

Do not ignore cantonal variation. Zurich, Geneva, Basel-Stadt and Vaud all publicly expose slightly different operational emphases, and those differences are legally important for drafting user-facing guidance on what to fix after refusal. Any content system should therefore tag Swiss work-permit refusal content by canton wherever the authority identified in the refusal makes that possible. 46
