## Executive legal summary and structural overview

## Executive legal summary. Switzerland operates a dual labour-migration framework. For EU/EFTA

nationals, access to the Swiss labour market is principally organised through the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the federal ordinance implementing that regime; in this branch of the system, a Swiss residence permit often functions as the work authorisation, and some short-term work is handled through a notification procedure rather than a permit. For non-EU/EFTA nationals and, since 1 January 2021, most UK nationals, access is principally governed by the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, with admission limited in general to highly qualified workers, subject to labour-market tests, salary/ conditions review, and annual quotas. 1

## Structural overview. In operational terms, Swiss work authorisation is best understood as three

distinct legal mechanisms. First, there are permit-based admission routes, especially for third-country nationals, usually resulting in an L short-stay permit, B residence permit, or in limited frontier cases a G cross-border commuter permit; certain special categories receive Ci status. Secondly, there are residence-permit routes that themselves confer labour-market access, especially for EU/EFTA nationals, for whom the residence permit “also counts as” the work permit, and for holders of settlement status C, whose status is not time-limited. Thirdly, there are notification-based routes, especially the EU/EFTA short-term online notification system, and the simplified reporting regime for recognised refugees, provisionally admitted persons, protected persons and some related statuses. 2

## Primary legal texts. The core federal instruments identified by SEM for this topic are: the Federal Act

on Foreign Nationals and Integration ( AIG / LEI / LStrI, SR 142.20 ); the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment ( VZAE / OASA, SR 142.201 ); the FDJP Approval Ordinance on foreign- national permits and preliminary decisions requiring SEM approval ( SR 142.201.1 ); the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU ( RS 0.142.112.681 ); the implementing free-movement ordinance ( RS 142.203 ); and, for posted-work / labour-condition enforcement, the Posted Workers Act ( SR 823.20 ) and the Posted Workers Ordinance ( SR 823.201 ). SEM’s legal-basis pages for work by non-EU/EFTA nationals and for Swiss–EU/EFTA free movement point expressly to these instruments, and the Federal Council’s 2026 quota press release confirms that the 2026 quota changes were made through amendment of the OASA/VZAE. 17

What each primary text does. In functional terms, the AIG/LEI/LStrI supplies the basic foreign- national admission framework and the key work-admission tests; the VZAE/OASA operationalises those rules, including permit procedure details and annual quota mechanics; the free-movement treaty and ordinance create the EU/EFTA residence-and-work framework and the short-term service/notification regime; the FDJP approval ordinance defines which cantonal decisions or preliminary decisions must

be submitted to SEM; and the Posted Workers Act/Ordinance add the labour-condition compliance layer for posting and service provision. 18

## Practical legal analysis

Residence permit, work authorisation and notification are not the same thing. Swiss materials repeatedly separate these concepts. In the EU/EFTA branch, a residence permit commonly doubles as the work authorisation, or no permit is needed at all for certain very short employments because an online notification suffices. In the third-country branch, labour-market admission is examined first and the residence document is then issued through the cantonal migration chain. In the asylum/protection branch, some statuses are allowed to work on the basis of notification rather than a classic work permit. Any content system that uses “work permit” as a universal label will generate avoidable inaccuracies. 26

## Drafting cautions

Do not import UK sponsor-licence vocabulary. Swiss official sources speak in terms of permit applications, cantonal labour-market review, SEM approval, and notifications. They do not describe a federal sponsor-licence regime. “Employer-sponsored” is acceptable as a functional descriptor if clearly explained, but “sponsor licence” should generally be avoided. 37

Do not treat “work permit” as a universal label. For EU/EFTA nationals, the residence permit is ordinarily the work authorisation; for some short employments, there is no permit at all, only notification; for refugees/F/S categories, work may be notification-based; and for some special groups, the relevant document is instead Ci or G. 38

Do not say all short work under 90 days is permit-free. The framework is more granular. EU/EFTA short Swiss hires use a three-month notification route; EU/EFTA service provision uses a 90-day-per- calendar-year notification regime; some service sectors require notification from day one; and outside the free-movement framework, SEM guidance recognises only a narrow up-to-eight-day exception for certain cross-border activity, again with sector carve-outs. 39

Do not imply that SEM approval alone allows the worker to enter or start work. SEM’s own FAQ says the approval decision does not itself authorise entry; visa and cantonal registration steps may still remain. For EU/EFTA residents taking up work for more than three months, registration must occur within 14 days after arrival and before starting work. 40

Do not overlook the posted-workers / labour-law layer. Immigration permission is not the whole framework. SEM’s non-EU/EFTA legal-basis page and SECO’s posting page both point to the Posted Workers Act, the Posted Workers Ordinance, and wider labour-law instruments as relevant compliance layers, especially on minimum salary and working conditions. 41

Do not assume the same rule-set for UK nationals as for EU/EFTA nationals. SEM’s current UK FAQ states that, from 1 January 2021, UK citizens are generally treated as third-country nationals, subject to the third-country admission framework unless they fall within acquired-rights arrangements. 42

Use official-language texts for authority statements. The Federal Chancellery states expressly that English versions of legislative texts have no legal force. For internal content engineering, English can be used for comprehension, but proposition-level authority storage should default to German, French or Italian citations. 16

## Open questions and limitations. This pack is built on official federal and cantonal materials and is

designed for framework-level internal use. It does not reproduce long article-by-article quotations from every current consolidated Fedlex text, and it therefore should not replace direct consultation of the official German, French or Italian consolidated statutes and ordinances when preparing litigation-grade footnotes or final legal opinions. The propositions above are nevertheless grounded in current official SEM, Federal Council and cantonal authority materials and should be reliable for internal content- system architecture and first-line drafting.
