# Swiss legal authority pack on work permits and employer sponsorship

**Suggested filename:** `legal_authority_work_permits_legal_framework_and_employer_sponsorship_pack.pdf`

### Executive legal summary and structural overview

Executive legal summary. Switzerland operates a dual labour-migration framework. For EU/EFTA nationals, access to the Swiss labour market is principally organised through the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the federal ordinance implementing that regime; in this branch of the system, a Swiss residence permit often functions as the work authorisation, and some short-term work is handled through a notification procedure rather than a permit. For non-EU/EFTA nationals and, since 1 January 2021, most UK nationals, access is principally governed by the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, with admission limited in general to highly qualified workers, subject to labour-market tests, salary/ conditions review, and annual quotas.<sup>1</sup>

Structural overview. In operational terms, Swiss work authorisation is best understood as three distinct legal mechanisms. First, there are permit-based admission routes, especially for third-country nationals, usually resulting in an L short-stay permit, B residence permit, or in limited frontier cases a G cross-border commuter permit; certain special categories receive Ci status. Secondly, there are residence-permit routes that themselves confer labour-market access, especially for EU/EFTA nationals, for whom the residence permit “also counts as” the work permit, and for holders of settlement status C, whose status is not time-limited. Thirdly, there are notification-based routes, especially the EU/EFTA short-term online notification system, and the simplified reporting regime for recognised refugees, provisionally admitted persons, protected persons and some related statuses.<sup>2</sup>

Institutional decision-making. The federal sources reviewed show a layered decision structure. Cantonal authorities remain the main operational entry point: SEM’s directory lists, canton by canton, both migration authorities and employment/labour-market authorities. In the classic third-country procedure, the employer files with the competent cantonal authority; the canton examines the file and makes a preliminary decision; positively screened cases subject to federal approval are transmitted to the State Secretariat for Migration for approval; only after that do the cantonal migration authorities handle visa authorisation where needed and issue the residence document. SEM’s own directives describe the approval procedure as a mechanism through which SEM exercises its supervisory role over uniform execution of federal foreign-national law.<sup>3</sup>

Employer sponsorship in Swiss terms. The official materials reviewed do not describe a UK-style sponsor-licence regime. Instead, they describe an employer-led, case-by-case application and notification framework: the employer is often the applicant or notifier, must evidence labour-market and employment-condition compliance, and may have to interact with both cantonal and federal authorities. That is best characterised as employer sponsorship in a functional sense, but not as a separate federal “sponsor licensing” status. This is an inference from the structure of the legislation and guidance rather than a defined statutory label.<sup>4</sup>

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

Primary legal texts. The core federal instruments identified by SEM for this topic are: the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (AIG / LEI / LStrI, SR 142.20); the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment (VZAE / OASA, SR 142.201); the FDJP Approval Ordinance on foreign- national permits and preliminary decisions requiring SEM approval (SR 142.201.1); the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU (RS 0.142.112.681); the implementing free-movement ordinance (RS 142.203); and, for posted-work / labour-condition enforcement, the Posted Workers Act (SR 823.20) and the Posted Workers Ordinance (SR 823.201). SEM’s legal-basis pages for work by non-EU/EFTA nationals and for Swiss-EU/EFTA free movement point expressly to these instruments, and the Federal Council’s 2026 quota press release confirms that the 2026 quota changes were made through amendment of the OASA/VZAE.<sup>17</sup>

What each primary text does. In functional terms, the AIG/LEI/LStrI supplies the basic foreign- national admission framework and the key work-admission tests; the VZAE/OASA operationalises those rules, including permit procedure details and annual quota mechanics; the free-movement treaty and ordinance create the EU/EFTA residence-and-work framework and the short-term service/notification regime; the FDJP approval ordinance defines which cantonal decisions or preliminary decisions must

be submitted to SEM; and the Posted Workers Act/Ordinance add the labour-condition compliance layer for posting and service provision.<sup>18</sup>

Official guidance and administrative materials. The most important federal administrative materials are SEM’s Directives and Explanations I. Foreign Nationals Sector (including Chapter 4 “Stay with Gainful Employment”) at 1 January 2026, and SEM’s Directives on the Free Movement of Persons / OLCP at 1 January 2026. In addition, SEM’s public guidance pages on Working in Switzerland, EU/EFTA living and working in Switzerland, Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market, Procedure, Notification procedure for short-term work, and Employment for persons seeking or granted asylum are especially valuable because they translate the statutory framework into decision- tree form without displacing the underlying legislation.<sup>19</sup>

Authority weight. For an internal content system, the appropriate hierarchy is: first, the consolidated federal statutes and ordinances in the official languages; secondly, SEM’s directives and official explainer pages; thirdly, official cantonal process guidance. That ordering matches both the user’s stated priority and the structure of the federal materials themselves.<sup>20</sup>

## Cantonal practice notes

General cantonal point. Cantonal practice matters procedurally more than substantively. SEM’s official directory shows that Swiss cantons typically maintain both a migration authority and an employment/labour-market authority, though organisational design varies. Any content engine should therefore treat cantonal material as implementation guidance, not as a substitute for federal legal rules.<sup>21</sup>

Zurich. Zurich’s official pages state that third-country nationals need a permit from the first day, that the permit application may only be made by the employer, and that cumulative requirements include overall economic interest, priority observance and compliance with local/sectoral salary and employment conditions. Zurich also requires work-permit applications to be filed through the cantonal online portal, but states that only persons or companies based in Switzerland can register as applicants there. The same cantonal site also publishes Zurich’s 2026 sub-allocation of federal quotas, illustrating the federal-cantonal quota distribution in practice.<sup>22</sup>

Geneva. Geneva’s official guidance states that any Swiss company wishing to employ a third-country national must in principle file with the OCPM, but that the OCPM issues the authorisation only if the OCIRT’s Directorate for Foreign Labour/MOE accepts the application first. Geneva’s organisation pages then make the division explicit: the MOE/OCIRT decides work authorisations for third-country nationals active in Geneva and for certain posted/service-provider cases, while the OCPM service étrangers is the cantonal authority competent to examine, grant and renew stay and/or work authorisations.<sup>23</sup>

Vaud. Vaud’s official pages likewise show a split model. For employed activity by third-country nationals, the employer must seek work authorisation from the DGEM; Vaud describes the principal third-country categories as L and B, lists the federal admission conditions, and explains that the dossier is submitted via the commune or municipal foreigner’s office and then transmitted to the competent cantonal authorities. Vaud’s more detailed page states that, if the cantonal labour-market review is positive, the employer receives a preliminary decision, the file is sent to the federal authority for approval, and the cantonal population service then establishes the documents allowing the worker to take up employment. Vaud also publishes an official page on the limited third-country G frontier-permit route.

Ticino. Ticino’s official migration pages are useful mainly for organisational mapping. They show that the cantonal migration office handles issuance, renewal and modification of permits, and that within it there is a dedicated “Third Countries and Labour Market” service alongside a separate “Community nationals” service. SEM’s own directory separately lists both Ticino’s migration office and its labour- market surveillance office, again illustrating the federal expectation of a two-track cantonal implementation structure.<sup>25</sup>

## Practical legal analysis

Residence permit, work authorisation and notification are not the same thing. Swiss materials repeatedly separate these concepts. In the EU/EFTA branch, a residence permit commonly doubles as the work authorisation, or no permit is needed at all for certain very short employments because an online notification suffices. In the third-country branch, labour-market admission is examined first and the residence document is then issued through the cantonal migration chain. In the asylum/protection branch, some statuses are allowed to work on the basis of notification rather than a classic work permit. Any content system that uses “work permit” as a universal label will generate avoidable inaccuracies.<sup>26</sup>

Employer role. The employer’s role is strongest in the third-country and notification branches. For third-country hires, SEM’s own material says the future employer must apply and must prove unsuccessful recruitment and compliant salary/working conditions. For EU/EFTA short hires and postings, the employer submits the notification. For F/S/refugee notification routes, the employer reports the start and end of employed activity. The functional picture is therefore not “licensing the employer”, but “placing the employer at the centre of the evidential and procedural chain”.<sup>35</sup>

Decision chain. For classic third-country labour admission, the process should be described in this order: employer filing; cantonal labour-market or migration review; cantonal preliminary decision; SEM approval where required; visa authorisation if needed; cantonal registration and permit issuance; only then commencement of work. SEM also states that a federal approval decision does not by itself authorise entry.<sup>36</sup>

## Drafting cautions

Do not import UK sponsor-licence vocabulary. Swiss official sources speak in terms of permit applications, cantonal labour-market review, SEM approval, and notifications. They do not describe a federal sponsor-licence regime. “Employer-sponsored” is acceptable as a functional descriptor if clearly explained, but “sponsor licence” should generally be avoided.<sup>37</sup>

Do not treat “work permit” as a universal label. For EU/EFTA nationals, the residence permit is ordinarily the work authorisation; for some short employments, there is no permit at all, only notification; for refugees/F/S categories, work may be notification-based; and for some special groups, the relevant document is instead Ci or G.<sup>38</sup>

Do not say all short work under 90 days is permit-free. The framework is more granular. EU/EFTA short Swiss hires use a three-month notification route; EU/EFTA service provision uses a 90-day-per- calendar-year notification regime; some service sectors require notification from day one; and outside the free-movement framework, SEM guidance recognises only a narrow up-to-eight-day exception for certain cross-border activity, again with sector carve-outs.<sup>39</sup>

Do not imply that SEM approval alone allows the worker to enter or start work. SEM’s own FAQ says the approval decision does not itself authorise entry; visa and cantonal registration steps may still remain. For EU/EFTA residents taking up work for more than three months, registration must occur within 14 days after arrival and before starting work.<sup>40</sup>

Do not overlook the posted-workers / labour-law layer. Immigration permission is not the whole framework. SEM’s non-EU/EFTA legal-basis page and SECO’s posting page both point to the Posted Workers Act, the Posted Workers Ordinance, and wider labour-law instruments as relevant compliance layers, especially on minimum salary and working conditions.<sup>41</sup>

Do not assume the same rule-set for UK nationals as for EU/EFTA nationals. SEM’s current UK FAQ states that, from 1 January 2021, UK citizens are generally treated as third-country nationals, subject to the third-country admission framework unless they fall within acquired-rights arrangements.<sup>42</sup>

Use official-language texts for authority statements. The Federal Chancellery states expressly that English versions of legislative texts have no legal force.
