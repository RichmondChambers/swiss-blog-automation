## Scope and key conclusions

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate a single generic “short-term assignment” route. The correct route depends first on whether the activity is legally classified as gainful employment, then on whether the person is: employed by a Swiss employer, posted by a foreign employer, or providing services as an independent service provider; and finally on nationality, establishment state, sector, regulated-profession status, and duration. For EU/EFTA-based service provision, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons only partially liberalises services: it generally covers temporary cross-border services for up to 90 actual working days per calendar year, subject to the notification system rather than a residence/work permit. For direct employment with a Swiss employer, EU/EFTA nationals may also work for up to three months per calendar year through notification alone. Beyond those thresholds, a permit route opens, but for services exceeding 90 days there is generally no treaty entitlement unless a specific bilateral service agreement applies. 1

The notification route is narrower than many internal business users assume. It is in principle available only to: EU/EFTA nationals taking up short-term work with a Swiss employer; posted workers from an undertaking established in an EU/EFTA state; and self-employed EU/EFTA service providers established in an EU/EFTA state. For posted workers of third-country nationality, notification is available only if they were previously integrated long-term into the regular labour market of the EU/EFTA sending state, generally evidenced by at least 12 months with a residence/work right there. The notification route cannot be used for all other categories. 2

Duration is a recurrent trap. For posted workers and independent service providers, Swiss guidance treats the eight notification-free days and the 90-day ceiling as applying both to the sending undertaking and to the individual worker/provider. Simultaneous deployment of several workers does not multiply the company’s day count, but sequential deployment does; and reported periods can consume days unnecessarily because the system can deduct all notified calendar days, including weekends and public holidays, if those dates are entered as part of the assignment period. The practical consequence is that day counting must be tracked centrally at both company and individual level, and notifications should reflect actual working days, not broad calendar ranges. 3

A further trap is the distinction between a genuine business visit and gainful work. SEM guidance states that short business meetings, contract negotiations, representative visits, information-exchange meetings, pre-assignment visits, and passive attendance at conferences are generally not gainful employment. By contrast, on-the-job training integrated into a productive process, internships and trainee placements, repair and maintenance work, project implementation, artistic work, pastoral

activity, and even recurring personnel/feedback discussions in Switzerland are treated as gainful employment. In other words, seemingly “short” or “internal” visits often fall into the work-authorisation regime if they are productive or operational. 4

Posted work also triggers a separate Swiss labour-protection layer. Posted workers, and in part self- employed service providers, are subject to the Posted Workers Act and Ordinance, including compliance with Swiss minimum wage and working conditions, advance notification, and in some cases wage disclosure in the notification form. Foreign staff-leasing from abroad is prohibited, and financial services requiring prior approval remain outside the ordinary liberalised 90-day route. Regulated professions create an additional overlay: EU/EFTA service providers in regulated professions must make a separate declaration to SERI for temporary services of up to 90 working days per year. 5

For non-EU/EFTA nationals outside the EU/EFTA posting logic, the baseline remains the FNIA/OASA permit system. Switzerland summarises this as a dual regime: easier access for EU/EFTA nationals under free movement, and a quota-based, highly qualified admission regime for third-country nationals based on economic interest, precedence, salary/working conditions, and personal qualifications. For stays of up to four months, employers still need to file a permit application with the competent canton, and work may begin only once the process has reached the required approval/registration stage. 6

A final current-point note is the United Kingdom. Since the end of free movement, UK citizens can no longer use the short-term notification route for taking employment with a Swiss employer, but under the temporary Swiss-UK mobility arrangement, posted workers from UK-based companies and self- employed UK service providers based in the UK may still use a 90-day notification-based route broadly similar to the EU/EFTA system, currently through 31 December 2029. 7

## Practical legal analysis

### G. Practical legal analysis

The most important practical point is that “assignment” is not a decisive Swiss legal category. A short intra-group secondment, a posted service contract, a business trip, a local Swiss employment relationship, and a self-employed specialist attending a client site may all be described internally as “short-term assignments”, but Swiss law separates them by legal function. Whether notification suffices, or a permit is needed, depends on who remains the employer, where the undertaking is established, whether the activity is productive, and whether the person is supplying a service into Switzerland or taking up ordinary employment in Switzerland. 48

For direct employment by a Swiss employer, the route is comparatively simple for EU/EFTA nationals: up to three months per calendar year, notification; beyond three months, a permit. That is not a service- provision case at all, even if the worker remains tax- or payroll-linked abroad in some group-internal sense. From a content-system perspective, any Swiss local employer relationship should be routed away from “posted work” logic and into “Swiss employer” logic immediately. 15

For posted work, the critical feature is that the worker remains tied to the foreign employer while entering Switzerland to perform a service, either for a Swiss client or, in substance, for a Swiss group entity within a cross-border service context. Swiss labour-protection law then overlays the immigration route: minimum wage and working conditions must be respected, and foreign employers may have to declare the gross Swiss hourly wage in the notification. This is why a content system should treat posted-work analysis as a dual enquiry: first immigration status, then posted-worker compliance. 49

For independent service provision, Swiss authorities are visibly concerned with fake self-employment. Both SEM and cantonal materials say that self-employed status may have to be proven during on-site checks, usually through the notification/permit, the A1 certificate, and the service contract or client confirmation. Zurich expressly warns that Swiss law governs the classification question, and a person self-employed in the home state may still be treated as non-self-employed in Switzerland. Internal drafting should therefore avoid assuming that a foreign tax or social-security label will decide Swiss treatment. 26

The eight-day rule requires especially careful wording. It is not simply “notify eight days in advance”. Rather, the structure is: in most sectors, service provision becomes notifiable when Swiss work exceeds eight days in the calendar year; but once notification is required, the notice is generally due eight calendar days before work starts. Some sectors require notification from day one, and emergencies can relax the lead time only in tightly defined circumstances. Short staffing or late planning do not qualify as emergencies. This is a classic area where public-facing content often collapses three different ideas into one sentence and becomes wrong. 50

Day counting should be treated as a separate legal subtopic, not a footnote. Federal guidance makes clear that the 90-day ceiling and the eight-day notification-free allowance are tracked against both the undertaking and the posted individual. Example-driven guidance shows that sending three workers at once may cost the company only five days if they all work on the same five days, but sending them one after another can cost fifteen company-days. The same materials warn that if the notification states a continuous calendar period, weekends and public holidays can be deducted too. For internal systems, this means the content should prompt users to ask: “What are the actual work dates, for which worker, and what has the undertaking already used this calendar year?” 3

The business visitor boundary is another recurring weak point. SEM’s examples are extremely useful here. Meetings, strategy sessions limited to information exchange, contract negotiations, representative visits, pre-move familiarisation visits, and passive attendance at conferences are generally outside gainful employment. But the moment the visitor is integrated into a productive process, performs repair or warranty work, implements a project, undertakes work-based training, or carries out an operational function on-site, Swiss law treats the stay as gainful employment. This distinction is more functional than formal; internal labels such as “visit”, “training”, “kick-off”, or “knowledge transfer” are not determinative. 21

Sector-sensitive complications arise in three different ways. First, some sectors are day-one notification sectors because of wage-dumping risk. Second, some activities are not liberalised at all in the ordinary 90-day service regime, notably certain financial services and foreign staff-leasing / private placement. Third, some professions are regulated professions, where an immigration notification is still insufficient without SERI declaration and, in many fields, a profession-specific approval step. Health professions are the clearest cantonal example, but the principle is wider. 51

For third-country nationals, the correct analysis starts with whether they can piggyback on an EU/EFTA or UK service-provision route through lawful long-term integration in the sending labour market. If not, the case moves into the ordinary FNIA third-country regime. Swiss official guidance stresses economic interest, quotas, precedence, salary/working conditions, and personal qualifications, and the short stay under-four-month process still requires a formal application. This is where many internal stakeholders make a category error: a short duration does not itself create a notification route for non-EU/EFTA nationals. 52

On employer compliance, the weak points are predictable. They include late or wrong notifications; miscounted days across multiple Swiss sites; failure to notify day-one sectors; inaccurate classification of self-employed status; use of the wrong route for third-country posted workers; omission of wage data for posted workers; lack of A1 / contract evidence during inspections; and content errors that imply that “under 90 days” is always enough. Cantonal materials also show that changes to existing notifications are procedurally sensitive: wrong site entries generally require a new filing, while some extensions or replacement workers can be handled differently depending on canton and timing. 53

## Drafting cautions and limitations

### H. Drafting cautions

The content system should avoid the shorthand “under 90 days = no permit needed”. That formulation is incomplete and often wrong. The correct formulation is that certain EU/EFTA and UK service providers may rely on the notification route for up to 90 actual working days per calendar year, subject to category restrictions, eight-day/day-one sector rules, and separate regulated-profession requirements. For ordinary Swiss employment, the short threshold is three months, not 90 service days. 54

The system should also avoid equating “assignment” with “posting”. “Assignment” is only a business descriptor. It may refer to a posted worker, a local Swiss hire, a non-gainful visit, or an intra-group transfer that still requires a full permit. Content should ask who the legal employer is, where the employer is established, and whether the worker remains under the foreign employer’s direction while performing a Swiss service. 55

Another drafting error is to describe business visitor activity too broadly. Swiss examples show that the line turns on whether the person is taking part in a productive process or carrying out operational work. “Training” must be split into theory-only training, which may be non-gainful, and on-the-job/production- linked training, which is gainful. “Meetings” must be limited to genuine representation, negotiation or information exchange; recurring Swiss-based management functions can become gainful work. 21

The system should never state that permits above 90 days are automatic for EU/EFTA service providers. Federal guidance says that for services above 90 days outside specific bilateral service agreements, Swiss law applies and there is no general right to obtain the permit. If the content system wants to mention a permit route, it should say “permit application required; outcome depends on the applicable regime” rather than “permit available”. 11

It is also important not to forget the dual compliance character of posted work. Immigration notification and labour-law compliance are separate. A route may be immigration-valid but still non- compliant on wages, working hours, proof of self-employment, A1 documentation, or regulated- profession rules. A good drafting rule is that any “posted work” answer should have a final sentence flagging Swiss labour conditions and, where relevant, regulated-profession checks. 56

Finally, the system should not suggest that UK and EU/EFTA cases are identical without qualification. For service provision, the UK currently has a parallel facilitation regime to 31 December 2029. For direct employment with a Swiss employer, UK nationals are treated under the third-country rules and cannot use the short-term notification route. 7

## Open questions and limitations

This pack is intentionally federal-first and practical. It does not attempt a comprehensive survey of all canton-specific implementation pages or all profession-specific regulators. In particular, some regulated professions have their own licensing bodies and profession laws beyond the immigration/SERI layer. The pack also notes that direct article-by-article retrieval from Fedlex was partially constrained in the browsing interface, so article-level references are cross-checked primarily through current SEM/SECO/ SERI materials and, where necessary, supplementary mirrored statutory snippets. Those points should be kept in mind if the content system later needs a litigation-grade, article-text-only annex.
