# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Cantonal Work Permit Practice and Procedure Suggested internal filename: legal_authority_work_permit_cantonal_practice_and_procedure_pack.pdf

Executive legal summary and structural overview A. Executive legal summary. Swiss work-permit law is federally framed but administratively delivered through cantonal structures. At federal level, Switzerland applies a dual system: EU/EFTA nationals benefit from the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, while nationals of other states are admitted under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act on a selective, quota-limited basis. For standard non-EU/EFTA employment, the employer-led application is first examined by a competent cantonal labour-market or migration authority; if the canton is positive, the file is sent to the State Secretariat for Migration for approval; if a visa is needed, the cantonal migration authority then issues visa authorisation; and the worker must register after entry. The SEM also states that cantons may ask for documents beyond the federal minimum. 1

Swiss “employer sponsorship” is therefore not a single national sponsor-licensing regime. In practice it is an employer-driven application process built on federal admission criteria but channelled through canton-specific filing routes, forms, portals, annexes, and local authority combinations. Official cantonal materials already show meaningful variation: Zurich uses a digital organisational portal and requires a Swiss-domiciled applicant or Swiss representative for foreign employers; Geneva receives the filing through OCPM and routes labour-market review to OCIRT; Vaud uses communes as intake desks in many cases and sends work-authorisation requests for third-state workers to DGEM/DISMAT; Bern directs third-state employer filings to AWI; Zug’s standard form is visibly split between AWA and AFM; and Ticino embeds a “third states and labour market” service inside its migration office. 2

B. Structural overview. For third-state recruitment, the practical sequence is usually: employer assembles the recruitment, qualification, salary, and company dossier; files through the competent cantonal gateway; receives cantonal labour-market screening and (if positive) cantonal pre-decision; the file then goes to SEM for approval; the cantonal migration authority handles visa authorisation or permit issuance; and, after arrival, the worker completes registration and residence formalities. Zurich’s own migration guidance maps that sequence step by step, while SEM describes the same federal approval architecture in general terms. 3

For EU/EFTA work, the route is different and is often the first decisive triage point. SEM states that short- term work for up to three months or 90 working days can often proceed through the federal notification system rather than through a classic permit application. But the detail still matters: for Swiss employers hiring EU/EFTA nationals for short-term employment, notification is due no later than one day before the first day of work; for posted workers and self-employed service provision, the general federal rule is an eight-day advance notification, subject to first-day sectors and emergency exceptions. Once the 90-day or three-month threshold is exceeded, the matter returns to the ordinary permit architecture. 4

Official guidance and cantonal practice notes E. Official guidance and administrative materials. SEM publishes the main federal administrative materials that an internal content system should treat as the first operational layer beneath the statutes and ordinances: current FNIA directives, including chapter 4 on gainful employment; chapter 1 materials on procedure and competences; public pages on admission criteria, procedure and required documents; FAQs; and the separate free-movement directives for AFMP/VFP matters. The directives are current in German, French and Italian, and SEM’s public-facing materials expressly direct users back to cantonal authorities for local document and filing details. 19

SEM’s documents are particularly important because they make the federal–cantonal split explicit. The SEM procedure page says the employer files with the cantonal employment or immigration authority, the canton makes the preliminary decision, positive files go to SEM, and the cantonal migration authority later handles visa authorisation. The SEM application-documents page also says that most

cantons publish their own forms and that some allow online filing, while the FAQ adds that cantons may demand additional evidence. This is the clearest official basis for treating cantonal forms, portals and checklists as legally significant administrative materials rather than mere convenience documents. 20

F. Cantonal practice notes. In Zurich, the employer-side route is highly formalised and digital. The canton says work permits can be submitted online via the cantonal portal; the applicant must be Swiss- domiciled; invoices can only be issued to a Swiss address; and a foreign employer must appoint an authorised representative in Switzerland. Zurich also publishes its own document expectations, including reasons for the application, contract, CV, diplomas and passport copies. On the migration side, Zurich states that AWI issues the labour-market pre-decision and the Migrationsamt then issues the residence authorisation; where the worker lives in another canton, the Zurich Migrationsamt issues consent to work after the labour-market pre-decision. 21

Geneva’s published architecture is different. For a third-state salaried worker, the Swiss employer files with OCPM, online or by post, around six to eight weeks before entry. OCPM then transmits the matter to the foreign labour directorate of OCIRT, which checks the labour-market criteria. Geneva’s guidance is unusually specific: it expects proof that the vacancy was notified to the cantonal employment office at the earliest opportunity, irrespective of the branch unemployment level; proof of Swiss and EU/EFTA recruitment efforts; compliance with standard salary conditions; and, for the standard page concerned, a full-time role. Geneva also states that OCIRT may grant a different authorisation than requested or attach limits and conditions, that some favourable cases then go to SEM for approval, and that both OCPM and OCIRT charge fees. OCPM separately publishes average processing times, including six to eight weeks for a first work-permit application for an extra-European national. 22

Vaud’s public materials show a more municipalised intake structure. The “Bureau des étrangers” aide- mémoire says communal foreign-resident offices act as reception and transmission desks for permit applications to SPOP, and it expressly states that work-authorisation requests on form 1350 from employers on the commune are transmitted for examination and decision to DGEM. The same source says SPOP then deals with the stay decision. Vaud’s form 1350 is itself an important practice source: it routes the filing differently depending on where the worker is already domiciled, records company headcount and permit mix, captures weekly hours and wage details, requires the employment contract as an annex, and shows a labour-market pre-decision box for DISMAT. Vaud also flags a frontier-specific variation: since 2022, frontier applications go directly to SPOP rather than to the commune. 23

Bern’s practice is comparatively direct and timetable-driven. The canton says that a Swiss company hiring from a third state files with AWI and should do so at least eight weeks before the planned start date. Bern also publishes a concrete document list: motivation letter, CV, diplomas, contract, passport copy and evidence of recruitment in Switzerland and the EU-27/EFTA; and it prefers submission by email in a single PDF or by post. For EU/EFTA postings beyond 90 days, Bern instructs a different employer route with at least four weeks’ notice and may require a salary inquiry. These local timetable instructions are important because they are more concrete than the general SEM six-to-eight-week expectation. 24

Basel-Stadt’s published pages show a border-canton variant. For third-state initial employment, the canton directs users to its own application form and federal labour-market criteria page. For EU/EFTA employment, Basel-Stadt states that up to 90 days no permit is needed, only an employer notification no later than one day before the start; beyond 90 days, the case shifts into residence-permit or frontier- permit channels. Its legal-basis page also expressly lists the Franco-Swiss and German cross-border traffic instruments, which is a useful reminder that frontier practice in border cantons may be shaped by more than the core FNIA/OASA layer. 25

Zug’s public materials are especially valuable for documentary practice. The canton’s B2 third-state form is jointly branded for the Office for Economy and Labor and the Office for Migration, signalling a dual- authority process on the face of the form. It asks for prior years’ turnover, profit, taxes and payroll, current workforce composition by permit category, the role description, wage, advertising efforts, and a power of attorney if the employer is represented by a third party. Page 3 then lists the annexes: written justification with labour-market search evidence, signed contract, HR extract, CV, degree, passport copy and, for project-based cases, the client project contract plus project description and implementation plan. The same page contains separate fields for the AWA positive labour-market pre-decision and AFM residence approval. 26

Ticino shows a different institutional shape again. Its migration office says it issues, renews and modifies all permits and authorisations for foreign nationals in the canton, and inside that office sits a “third states and labour market” service handling third-state applications and labour-market matters. For EU/EFTA short-term work, Ticino’s labour-market surveillance office states that a Swiss employer uses online notification at least one day before start, whereas EU/EFTA posted workers and self- employed service providers use the eight-day route. Ticino also publishes a frontier-specific local step: for a new G permit, dependent workers must first attend a Gendarmerie post for identity-document certification, and the certified copy must be attached to the application before work begins. St Gallen’s public guidance gives yet another formulation, stating that the Migration Office issues work permits and that sometimes the AWA must also co-decide. 27

Practical legal analysis G. Practical legal analysis. The safest analytical split is this: federal law governs the substantive admission architecture, while cantonal practice governs a large share of the operational route. The federal side answers who may be admitted, on what criteria, under which quotas, and when SEM approval is required. The cantonal side answers where the employer files, whether the front office is labour-market, migration or communal, what the local form asks for, how the file is packaged, how salary evidence is localised, whether there are separate front-end and back-end fees, and how the case is sequenced from labour-market review into residence issuance. That is why black-letter drafting should be federal, but process drafting should be explicitly framed as current cantonal practice. 28

On authority roles, the key point is not simply that “the canton decides”. Official materials show several institutional models. Zurich and Bern present a classic labour-market-first structure: AWI/AWI receives the employer filing and the migration authority follows after labour-market clearance. Geneva uses a migration-office front door with internal transmission to OCIRT for labour-market examination. Vaud inserts the commune and SPOP into the chain before or alongside DGEM/DISMAT. Ticino integrates third-state and labour-market handling inside the migration office itself. St Gallen tells the public that the Migration Office issues the permit and that the AWA only sometimes co-decides. An internal content system should therefore avoid abstract nouns like “the cantonal authority” where the specific office actually matters. 29

The documentary pattern across the official sources is broadly stable, but the depth of evidence varies sharply. SEM’s federal baseline asks for: the cantonal form; passport copy; recruitment evidence; CV; qualifications; translations where needed; a justification for the hire; job description and company details; project documents where applicable; and the employment contract or posting confirmation. Cantonal materials then add local layers. Geneva asks for a motivation letter from the employer, detailed company staffing and permit information, and in some cases housing and family documents at the arrival stage. Bern asks for a motivation letter and prefers the whole filing in a single PDF. Vaud form 1350 asks for company staffing by permit type and records the intended salary and hours. Zug

goes furthest in the reviewed sample by asking for prior-year corporate financial metrics and workforce composition. 30

For employer-side preparation, the most robust internal workflow is a modular one. The federal core module should always include: a conditional employment contract; a recruitment memo showing priority-compliant search efforts and vacancy-notification compliance where applicable; a salary benchmark tied to the region, sector and relevant collective instruments; the worker’s passport, CV, diplomas, certificates and translations; and a short legal business case on the economic interest, the skill profile and the role. The canton module should then add the local extras, such as a Swiss representative in Zurich, OCPM/OCIRT-facing employer narrative and staffing breakdown in Geneva, commune/DGEM routing in Vaud, single-file formatting and lead-time planning in Bern, detailed company metrics and project annexes in Zug, or frontier-specific identity steps in Ticino. 31

For commercially urgent matters, route selection is usually more decisive than discretionary argument. A short-term EU/EFTA direct hire may be a one-day notification case. A posted-worker or self-employed service case may need eight days. A Bern third-state direct hire should be lodged at least eight weeks ahead. Geneva tells the employer to apply roughly six to eight weeks before entry and publishes six-to- eight-week average handling for first work-permit applications for extra-European nationals. SEM’s own FAQ gives a general expectation of six to eight weeks for a fully processed file and warns that first-time sponsors and more complex cases can take longer. A content system should therefore classify urgency by route family first, and only then by factual complexity. 32

The most difficult cases in practice are those in which category confusion and local procedure collide: project work that might be misfiled as direct employment; postings that actually exceed the notification thresholds; frontier work in border cantons; inter-cantonal work where residence and work canton differ; first-time sponsors with thin corporate history; family-accompanied arrivals that need housing and civil-status documents at the right stage; and third-state roles that are part-time, weakly documented, or hard to align with “manager/specialist/qualified worker” criteria. Geneva’s explicit reference to full-time work for the standard third-state salaried route, Zurich’s requirement for a Swiss representative for foreign employers, and Zug’s unusually searching company-information fields are all examples of local administrative pressure points that may matter more in difficult commercial filings than the federal criterion list alone suggests. 33

Drafting cautions H. Drafting cautions. Do not write Swiss work-permit content as though there were one uniform “sponsorship” pathway. The official sources force at least these distinctions: EU/EFTA residence cases; EU/EFTA short-term notification cases; posted-worker or self-employed service cases; third-state direct- hire cases; frontier cases; UK cases; and special-status cases such as refugees, permit F, permit S, students, trainees and family members. The correct route determines the correct authority, evidence set and start-date logic. 34

Do not collapse labour-market approval, SEM approval, visa authorisation, residence issuance and work start into a single event. SEM expressly says the canton makes a preliminary decision and positive files go to SEM; SEM approval does not itself authorise entry; visa authorisation must follow where needed; and registration is still required. Geneva adds a useful local nuance: only certain categories already holding an entry authorisation with employment or an assurance of residence authorisation may begin work on entry; in other cases the employee works only once the work permit has been received. 35

Do not present any national document list as exhaustive. SEM says cantons may require more, and the sampled cantons prove the point. Zurich wants a structured digital dossier; Geneva requires a local employer letter and staffing data; Vaud’s form routes differently by current residence and demands contract plus company/personal data in a prescribed format; Bern specifies its own document stack; Zug requests extensive financial and workforce data; and Ticino publishes local identity-check steps for some frontier filings. Use wording such as “at minimum” and “current cantonal practice may require additional annexes”. 36

Do not promise uniform timings. SEM gives a general six-to-eight-week expectation for a complete file, but Geneva publishes its own average processing times, Bern publishes lead-time instructions, and notification routes can operate on one-day or eight-day clocks depending on case type. “Fast” and “slow” are therefore route-specific and canton-specific, not purely legal. 37

Do not confuse the 90-day notification route with the eight-day notification-free or eight-day advance- notice rules for specific service scenarios, and do not assume that direct Swiss employment and cross- border service provision are the same thing. SEM, Zug, Zurich and Ticino all present these categories separately, with different thresholds and different procedural consequences. 38

Finally, be careful with form-driven drafting. Published forms often reveal operational expectations that are real even if they are not stated in the statute itself: exact identity details matching the passport, conditional contract language, translations, employment start dates aligned with permit stage, salary formatting, and company headcount/permit breakdowns. These should be treated as current administrative practice notes, not silently upgraded into universal legal propositions. 39
