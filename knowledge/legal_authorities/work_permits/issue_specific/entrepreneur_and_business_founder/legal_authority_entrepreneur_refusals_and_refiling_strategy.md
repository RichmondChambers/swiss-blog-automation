## Executive legal summary

Swiss founder and entrepreneur work-authorisation cases for third-country nationals are primarily governed by the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Empoloyment, as applied through SEM directives and cantonal labour-market procedure. The core federal rule for genuine self-employment is Article 19 FNIA: self-employment may be permitted only where it is expected to have a positive effect on the Swiss labour market, the financial and operational requirements are met, and the personal requirements in Article 23 FNIA are satisfied. For employed founder structures, the ordinary emnployed-work rules remain relevant, including overall economic interest, employer sponsorship, precedence, salary and local conditions, and qualification requirements. Swiss practice therefore depends first on correct legal characterisation: is the founder truly self-employed, or in substance an employee of a Swiss company, even if he or she is a shareholder, director or CEO on paper? 1

The main refusal risk is not a single technical defect but failure to persuade the authorities that the case advances Switzerland's overall economic interest in a concrete, durable and evidenced way. SEM's current directives say the applicant must show that the planned self-employed activity will have a lasting positive impact on the Swiss labour market. That benefit is described in practical terms: contribution to regional or sectoral diversification, retention or creation of jobs for local workers, substantial investment, or generation of new orders for the Swiss economy; for start-ups, innovation and implementationof academic research may also be relevant. Official SEM web guidance and cantonal pages make the same point in simpler language: applicants must explain and document why admission serves the overall economic interest, and Geneva expressly states that third-country entrepreneurs must demonstrate the economic interest of the application. 2

That is why founder cases are often refused for evidential weakness rather than because some narrow rule mechanically bars them. SEM requires a business plan showing intended activities, financing, market opportunities, staffing development and recruitment, planned investments, turnover and profit, together with organisational links to other businesses, external financing commitments, and usually formation documents and a commercial register extract. SEM's general application page adds that incomplete applications are not processed, and documents in other languages usually need certified translation into an official Swiss language or English. Zurich's official materials add a practical viability test:the venture should be expected to generate enough income to cover both business costs and living costs, and sufficient start-up capital should be proven, ordinarily through a business plan or opening balance sheet and, helpfully, leases or credit agreements. 3

A second cluster of refusals concerns founder-role ambiguity. SEM's directives are explicit that titles, an employment contract, or commercial-register entry alone do not decide whether activity is self-employed or employed. Authorities look instead at the real structure: own organisation, ownership or control, absence of a superior body, actual power to direct others, and genuine entrepreneurial risk. This matters because many founder cases sit in a grey zone. If the file presents the applicant as an entrepreneur to avoid employed-work requirements, but the evidence shows subordination to investors, a board or a parent company, the case may fail on the legal classification point before the business merits are even reached. 4

Salary and local-conditions issues also require careful drafting. Article 22 FNIA and Article 22 OASA protect against wage dumping and require local and occupationally customary conditions for admitted foreign workers. SEM's directives say the cantons must examine real local conditions, not just minimum collective-agreement figures, and may use salary statistics. They also note that start-up-style equity participation may count towards remuneration only if there are sufficient direct financial means for subsistence and the participation remains proportionate to base pay. In a pure self-employment file, the better analytical question is not always "salary", but whether the founder has shown a viable independent livelihood; however, where the founder will be employed by a Swiss vehicle, or where the package is largely equity-based, remuneration becomes a live refusal issue. 5

Appeal analysis must therefore separate three things. First, legal or procedural challenge: wrong legal test, misclassification of the role, failure toconsider relevant evidence, or procedural unfairness. Secondly, procedural correction: incomplete papers, translation defects, missing contracts, missing corporate documents, or other curable omissions. Thirdly, substantive evidential rebuilding: weak market evidence, speculative projections, uncommitted funding, unclear operational presence, or unsupported economic-interest claims. The Federal Administrative Court reviews federal administrative decisions under the federal public-law procedure statutes, and official SEM procedure materials show that a positive cantonal assessment in these cases still goes to SEM for federal approval; but official materials reviewed for this pack do not describe any entrepreneur-specific, easy reconsideration route. In weak founder files, a substantially rebuilt re-filing is often more realistic than an appeal that simply re-argues the same thin record. Content written for future publication should therefore avoid suggesting that refusals are routinely or easily overturned. 6

## Structural overview and practical legal analysis

### B. Structural overview

**Legal refusal framework.** Third-country entrepreneur admission sits within Switzerland's labour-market admission system. For genuine self-employment, the key gateway is Article 19 FNIA; for employed founder structures, the Article 18 FNIA track remains relevant. SEM's directives add that self-employment is defined autonomously in migration law and not simply by social-security or corporate-law labelling. Company formnation or participation, by itself, does not create an entitlement in permit proceedings. 7

**Economic-interest refusal** risks. The authorities ask whether the case serves Switzerland's overall economic interest, not merely the applicant's private interest. SEM links that enquiry to labour-market conditions, sustainable economic development, and integration prospects; in self-employment cases it requires a lasting positive effect on the Swiss labour market, such as diversification, job creation or retention, investment, or orders for the Swiss economy. Thin statements about "innovation", "international expansion" or "future hirinng" are risky unless backed by concrete Swiss-facing evidence. 8

**Business-substance** **refusal** risks. A speculative business model, generic slide-deck, or business plan written at a high level without operational proof is vulnerable. SEM expects evidence about activities, market opportunities, staffing, investments, revenue and profit forecasts, financing, corporate links, and formation documents. Zurich adds that enough income should be expected to cover both business and living costs. A file with no Swiss clients, no Swiss premises, no contracts, no licensing position, and no explanation of why Switzerland is the real operating centre is structurally weak. 9

<!-- 2 -->

**Financial and** **funding** **risks.** The federal test expressly includes financial and operational requirements. Zürich's practice note requires sufficient start-up capital and supports this with business plan, opening balance, lease or credit documentation. SEM's directives call for binding investor commitments where external finance is relied on. Assertions that funds are "available" or "to be raised" without documentary commitment are therefore a major refusal risk. 10

**Founder-role** **risks.** Many cases fail because the applicant's role is not properly characterised or justified. SEM says titles, contracts and register entries are not enough; the real indicators are control, absence of superiors, and entrepreneurial risk. A founder who appears dependent on a board, parent or investor may be treated as employed, and then the Article 18 framework, employer application, precedence and salary rules become relevant. Even where Article 19 is correctly invoked, the founder's necessity must still be evidenced: why this person, for this Swiss venture, at this stage? 11

**Salary** **and** **employment-conditions** **risks.** Salary/local-conditions objections arise most sharply where the founder is really being employed by a Swiss entity, but they can also surface where remuneration is unrealistic or largely equity-based. SEM requires local and occupationally customary conditions for workers and allows equity to count only in a limited way where direct subsistence funds remain sufficient. Drafting should therefore avoid implying that founders may simply take nominal pay because they are "entrepreneurs". 12

**Procedural** **risks.** SEM and cantonal authorities process only complete applications. Missing translations, unsigned or incomplete contracts, absent formation records, unsubstantiated funding, or internal contradictions across the business plan and corporate documents can sink the file before merits are fully reached. OASA also states that company formation or participation alone creates no claim in the permit process. 13

**Appeal and** **re-filing.** Procedure is two-stage: the canton screens labour-market conditions and, if positive, forwards the case to SEM for federal approval. Official federal material confirms that SEM then issues the formal decision. Federal public-law procedure is governed by VwVG, VGG and BGG; cantonal procedure is governed separately at canton level. Official mnaterials reviewed for this pack do not describe a bespoke entrepreneur reconsideration route. That matters: many weak founder refusals are better analysed as candidates for substantial evidential rebuilding and re-filing, not merely legal re-argument. 14

**Drafting** **implications.** Future content should describe founder cases as evidence-heavy,highly discretionary and quota-sensitive. It should not suggest that incorporation, capitalisation alone, or "being a founder" creates a strong approval claim. Swiss authorities look for durable economic utility, real operating readiness and a coherent legal characterisation of the role. 15

### H. Practical legal analysis

Swiss entrepreneur cases commonly fail on cumulative evidential weakness because Article 19 FNIA is framed as a compact statutory test while the real proof burden is spread across many practical questions: market demand, Swiss benefit, funding, structure, founder control, professional qualifications, admissible remuneration model, and operational readiness. The file must show not only

<!-- 3 -->

that the business could exist, but that this foreign founder's admission should be preferred within a controlled labour-market system designed for the interests of Switzerland as a whole. That is why ostensibly small defects often compound into a "weak file" outcome. A vague plan, uncommitted funding, no Swiss contracts, and overstated job-creation forecasts may each be survivable in isolation; together they usually undermine the economic-interest narrative and the credibility of the venture. 16

Appeal is not always the best answer to that kind of refusal. A legal challenge makes sense where the authority applied the wrong legal framework, misunderstood whether the founder was self-employed or employed, ignored key evidence, or committed a procedural defect. But where the authority has simply found the business case too thin, the better remedly is often a rebuilt record: committed finance, clearer Swiss-market evidence, real premises, licensing position, letters of intent or contracts, a corrected remuneration model, and a defensible explanation of why the founder must personally be in Switzerland now. Swiss federal procedure gives review rights, but review does not turn a speculative file into a proved one. 17

Re-filing also usually requires genuine change, not repetition. OASA expressly says that no claim can be derived from company formation or share participation alone. SEM's directives add that initial entrepreneur permits should normally be limited to two years and that extension may be refused if the promised targets in the business plan are not reached on timne. Those texts point in the same direction: Swiss authorities expect concrete performance against evidence, not circular reliance on the fact that a company exists. A re-filed application that merely repackages the same plan, without stronger proof or materially changed facts, remains exposed. 18

Funding claims and remuneration claims deserve particular caution in automated content. Founder narratives often present low salary, future equity upside and investor interest as strengths. Swiss guidance cuts through that rhetoric. Where employee-admission rules apply, local and sectoral conditions must be respected, and equity only helps to a limited extent if direct means of subsistence are present. Where the case is genuinely self-employed, Zürich still expects the activity to generate enough income for operating and living costs and requires enough start-up capital.A blog engine that says "you can just pay yourself little at the start because you are the founder" would therefore be materially unsafe. 19

For the same reason, automated content should not promise that a refusal can readily be overturned. Official materials reviewed here do not provide a founder-specific shortcut to reconsideration, and the procedure remains multi-layered: canton, SEM approval, and then the applicable federal or cantonal remedy route. Strong content should instead explain that some refusals are appeal-worthy because something went legally wrong, while others are signals that the evidence, structure or timing of the case was not yet good enough. 20

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The main current federal administrative material is SEM's Directives and Explanations, Chapter 4: Residence with Gainful Employment (status 1 January 2026). The directives contain the most operational guidance on entrepreneur cases. For self-employment and business start-ups, they set out: the migration-law concept of self-employment; theneed to prove a lasting positive effect on the Swiss labour market; examples of qualifying benefit; documentary expectations for the business plan and financing; and the normal practice of an initial permit limited to two years, extendable only if the promised durable positive effects are realised on time. They also state that cantonal pre-decisions on Article 19 FNIA cases are subject to SEM approval. 43

SEM's public FAQ in English is especially useful because it states, in accessible terms,that third-country nationals may be self-employed only after labour-market review under Article 19 FNIA and that they must show a lasting positive impact on the Swiss labour market. It then lists the expected supporting documents: business plan, market analysis, workforce development and recruitment plan, investments, turnover and profit forecast, organisational links, articles of association and commercial-register extract. 44

SEM's general page on the Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market is useful for the wider refusal architecture. It summarises overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, vacancy-notification, salary/local conditions and personal requirements. It expressly says applicants mnust explain and document why admission is in the overall economic interest, and notes that only limited numbers of highly qualified third-country workers are admitted. 45

<!-- 9 -->

SEM's Procedure and Application documents pages matter for procedural refusals. They explain the canton-to-SEM approval sequence; confirm that the canton makes a preliminary decision and sends approved cases to SEM; and state that SEM and the cantonal labour-market services only process applications submitted in full. The application-documents page also shows the general documentary approach that informs founder files: proof of qualifications, translations where necessary, reasons for the application, and sector-specific additions. 46

On salary and conditions, SEM's directives are the critical administrative source. They require cantons to compare local and occupational conditions against legal rules, actual pay in the same workplace and sector, and salary statistics; they also note that statistical averages usually operate as a floor in urban contexts. The directives' discussion of equity-style participation in start-ups is particularly relevant to founder drafting. 31

For procedural and appeal framing, the Federal Office of Justice explains that federal public-law procedure is governed by the Administrative Procedure Act, the Administrative Court Act and the Federal Supremne Court Act, and that these laws regulate the making of administrative decisions and parties' procedural rights such as the right to be heard. This is the cleanest official federal source for distinguishing federal appeal structure from cantonal procedural law. 47

Official materials reviewed for this pack are comparatively thin on entrepreneur-specific refusal letters, reconsideration practice and re-filing guidance. The SEM legal-protection section exists within the directives architecture, but the reviewed public materials did not expose a dedicated entrepreneur reconsideration pathway. That absence should itseIf shape drafting: future content should not imply a standard, easy post-refusal reset route unless the actual refusal notice or canton-specific procedure says so. 48

## Cantonal practice notes and case law notes

### F. Cantonal practice notes

**Canton** **Zurich.** Zurich's public page for third-country nationals defines self-employment in terms tracking OASA and then listscumulative conditions for third-country self-employment: the activity must serve Switzerland's overall economic interest; it should be expected to generate enough income to cover operating costs and living costs; sufficient start-up capital must be shown, ordinarily by a business plan or opening balance sheet; and only specialists and otherwise qualified workers can be authorised. Zurich's 2025 one-page procedure sheet for self-employed persons adds quota dependence and distinguishes between residence-taking entrepreneurs and cross-border self-employed service providers. Zurich's broader explanatory note for third-country work permit cases also states expressly that there is no legal entitlement to a permit or to extension, that first permits are quota-bound, and that authoritiesmay request additional documents. These are best treated as cantonal practice points reflecting the national framework rather than free-standing canton-only rules. Appeal routes were not described on the reviewed public pages. The points addressed are mainly evidential and discretionary, with some procedural overlay. 49

**Canton** **Geneva.** Geneva's official entrepreneur page states that any foreign national wishing to live in Geneva and work there independently requires a residence authorisation with activity. For third-country nationals, Geneva says there is no facilitated access to theSwiss labour market, that the applicant must in particular demonstrate the economic interest of the application, that only managers, specialists and qualified workers may be admitted, and that admission remains subject to availability within the quotas. Geneva also explains the institutional route: the OCPM handles the filing, while the OCIRT

examines the work-permit aspect and gives the preliminary decision, after seeking the opinion of the tripartite commission for the economy. The reviewed page lists the federal legal bases,including the OA-DFJP approval ordinance. Appeal routes were not described on the page reviewed. The points addressed are legal, discretionary and porocedural. 50

**Canton** **Vaud.** The official Vaud permit form and supporting frontier-worker form show that the canton operationalises entrepreneur cases through document-heavy filing. The forms identify "activitéindépendante" as a distinct category and, for independent frontaliers, require lease evidence for business premises, a detailed explanation of completed or planned administrative steps, a business plan detailing the projected activity and development, and proof of financial resources. These materials do not set out refusal reasons in narrative form, but they are useful cantonal evidence of what a minimally serious entrepreneur file is expected to contain. No appeal route was describoed in the materials reviewed. The points are chiefly procedural and evidential. 51

### G.Case law notes

The case law below is included conservatively. In several instances the most accessible official or officially cited material was the SEM directives' citation to the judgment rather than the full public text of the judgment itself. Where facts are thin in the reviewed sources, that is stated expressly.

**Federal Administrative Court, 11 April 2013, C-2485/2011.** The search extract identifies the appellant as a Kazakh national who was founder and owner of a distribution company in the medical and healthcare sector and was challenging SEM's refusal to approve a cantonal labour-market pre-decision. SEM's current directives cite this judgment for the proposition that entrepreneur permits should initially be time-limited and that extension may be refused if the business-plan targets and promised durable positive effects are not realised in time. Drafting relevance: this is authority against any suggestion that initial founder approval settles the case for the longer term, and it supports a hard distinction between a start-up phase and a proved operating phase. 52

**Federal Administrative Court, 9 May 2011, C-7286/2008.** The publicly accessible search extract states that SEM refused approval because the requirements for self-employed activity under Article 19 AuG/ FNIA were not met and contains a snippet showing the authority's scepticism towards deliberately conservative minimum projections designed to avoid allegations of excessive optimism. SEM's directives cite this judgment both for self-employment classification and for economic-interest analysis. Drafting relevance: entrepreneur projections must be credible, not just numerically plausible; underpowered forecasts can be as damaging as exaggerated ones if they fail to evidence meaningful Swiss benefit. 53

**Federal Administrative Court, 11 August 2011, C-6135/2008.** The case title and SEM directives show it is one of the authorities relied on for overall economic interest and for refusal/extension analysis where promised business effects are not realised. The facts were not fully retrievable from the reviewed public extracts, so this pack uses it narrowly. Drafting relevance: use it as support for the proposition that Swiss entrepreneur admission is not meant to support merely private oor particular interests detached from concrete Swiss labour-market benefit. 54

**Federal Administrative Court, 20 December 2018, F-3389/2017, and 3 June 2019, F-6434/2017.** SEM cites these judgments for the proposition that self-employment is assessed case by case and that ownership shares, organisational independence and real power matter more than labels. The specific facts were not available in the public extracts reviewed here. Drafting relevance: do not write founder content as though shareholder status, CEO title or sole signatory power automatically settles the classification question. 24

## Drafting cautions, source list and limitations

### I. Drafting cautions

Do not say that a refusal can always be appealed successfully. Swiss entrepreneur refusals often turn on evidential weakness under a discretionary, quota-bound labour-market framework rather than on an obvious legal error.56

Do not imply that re-filing the same documents is enough. Official materials show there is no permit claim merely from founding or participating in a company, and SEM ties later success to whether the promised benefits are actually evidenced and achieved. 18

Do not understate economic interest. It is the centre of the statutory and administrative analysis, not a cosmetic criterion. 57

Do not present founders as automatically "self-employed". Swiss migration law applies its own definition, and titles or register entries alone are insufficient. 58

Do not omit funding, operational readiness and role-necessity issues. Business plans, financing, investor commitments, commercial-register materials and evidence of real Swiss operations are expected. 59

Do not frame salary/local-conditions issues as irrelevant merely because the applicant is a founder. They remain central where the case is employed in substance, and SEM expressly regulates the limited use of start-up equity in remuneration analysis. 12

Do not treat quota or timming problems as purely administrative. Quotas are a substantive part of the admission system and remain in force for 2026. 34

Do not present refusal grounds as exhaustive unless sourced. The reviewed official materials are rich on criteria, but thinner on entrepreneur-specific appeal, reconsideration and re-filing routes. State that clearly. 60

<!-- 12 -->

Do not use alarmist language. The better internal framing is legal, procedural, discretionary or evidential risk, with a disciplined distinction between appeal-worthy error and weak-file rebuilding. 61

#### Open questions and limitations

Official federal and cantonal materials reviewed for this pack are strong on admission criteria and initial procedure, but noticeably thinner on entrepreneur-specific reconsideration practice, re-filing standards and canton-by-canton appeal routes after refusal. Those matters should therefore be verified against the actual refusal notice, the issuing authority, and the applicable cantonal procedural law in the live case. Case-law accessibility was also uneven: several important judgments were most readily available through current SEM directive citations or search extracts rather than fully parsed official judgment text, so the case-law section above is intentionally conservative. 74
