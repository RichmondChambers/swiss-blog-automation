## Executive legal summary

Under Swiss federal law, job creation is **not** stated as a universal standalone legal requirement for every founder or entrepreneur work-authorisation case. For third-country nationals seeking admission to work on a **self-employed** basis, the core statutory test is in Article 19 of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act: the activity must be in the interests of the economy as a whole, the necessary financial and operational requirements must be met, the applicant must have an adequate and independent source of income, and the requirements on quotas and personal/accommodation conditions must also be satisfied. Official SEM guidance consistently reframes this as a requirement to show a **lasting** **positive impact on the Swiss labour market,i.e.** overall economic interest. 1

That means job creation is usually best understood as a **strong** **evidential route** to proving economic interest, not as an invariable threshold. The SEM directives say/a sustainable labour-market benefit may exist where the new business or the self-employed person contributes to sectoral diversification of the regional economny, preserves or creates several jobs for domestic workers, makes substantial investments and generates new orders for the Swiss economy. The same directives add that, particularly for start-ups, innovation capacity and the implementation of knowledge from academic research may also be taken into account. Official canton materials in Geneva and Lucerne repeat the same logic. 2

The statutory picture is different where a founder is not genuinely "self-employed" in migration-law terms. Swiss law distinguishes self-employed work from salaried work by reference to the applicant's own organisation, control and entrepreneurial risk. The SEM directives expressly say that founder status, a CEO title, an employment contract or even a commercial-register entry are **not** decisive by themselves. If a founder is properly classified as a salaried employee of a Swiss company, Article 18applies, together with the precedence rule in Article 21. By contrast, Article 19 on self-employment does **not** cross-refer to Article 21. On the sources reviewed, the better view is therefore that the classic domestic/EU labour-market test is a feature of the **salaried-founder** route, whereas the **self-employed-founder** route is centred on overall economic interest and business viability. That is a legal-structural conclusion from the Act and the official guidance, not an express sentence in the statute. 3

Article 23 paragraph 3 letter a FNIA also matters, but it should be handled carefully. It allows "investors and entrepreneurs who maintain existing jobs or create new jobs" to be admitted **by** **way** **of** **derogation** from the ordinary qualification/integration rule in Article 23 paragraphs 1 and 2. This makes job creation legally relevant in some entrepreneur files, but as a derogation within the personal-requirements regime, not as a fixed job-creation rule for all founder cases. It follows that content should not collapse Article 23(3)(a) into a universal requirement under Article 19. 4

Official sources also make clear that projected labour-market benefit must be **documented,** not merely asserted. SEM's public guidance requires applicants to explain and document why admission is in the overall economic interest. The SEM FAQ and directives require a business plan and expect information on planned activities, financing, market opportunity, workforce development in quantitative and qualitative terms, recruitment possibilities, planned investments, turnover and earnings, existing links with other businesses and external financing commitments. Cantons may go further in their forms and checklists: Geneva asks for a full project presentation and three-year quantified forecasts including turnover, costs, profit and precise staffing levels; Solothurn asks for market andd cost analysis, Swiss cooperation contracts and proof of acquired clients; Zurich and Zug both require a business plan or planned balance sheet and evidence of sufficient start-up capital. 5

Renewal practice is especially important. The SEM directives state that permits for self-employment or new business formation should, in an initial phase, be limited to a maximum of two years, and that renewal should depend on the timely realisation of the promised sustainable positive effects of the business establishment. The directives expressly say an extension may be refused if the business-plan goals are not achieved, and they tie that to the statutory power to revoke or refuse continuation where conditions attached to the decision are not fulfilled. The Ordinance additionallystates that company formation,business participation, renting premises or similar preparatory steps do **not** by themselves create an entitlement in the permit procedure. Promising jobs without credible evidence therefore creates obvious legal and practical risk. 6

Cantonal involvement is real and can be outcome-relevant, but it should not be overstated. Cantonal labour-market authorities conduct the first economic and labour-market review, and some cantons formally involve economic or tripartite bodies. Geneva routes third-country independdent files through OCIRT and, depending on the case, the tripartite economic commission, and recommends prior contact with the cantonal economy and innovation office to assess fit with the Geneva context. Lucerne asks for prior consultation because of the complexity of these files. Zug, for its part, expressly reminds applicants that there is no right to a permit and no right to extension. These are important practice points, not alternative legal tests replacing federal law. 7

For internal drafting purposes, the safest formulation is that job creation can be a **highly** **persuasive** **indicator** of labour-market bennefit and, in some contexts, can help satisfy the entrepreneur/investor derogation in Article 23(3)(a), but Swiss officialsources do **not** support presenting it as a guaranteed, numerical or universally mandatory threshold. Early-stage and solo-founder ventures may still be arguable where they can evidence other forms of durable labour-market benefit, such as diversification, investment, innovation or Swiss economic spillovers; but they require careful proof, careful route-classification and especially cautious wording on projections. 8

## Structural overview

### Legal basis

For third-country founders, the principal federal provisions are Article 19 FNIA on self-employment, Article 18 FNIA on salaried employment, Article 21 FNIA on precedence, Article 23 FNIA on personal requirements and entrepreneur/investor derogations, together with Articles 2, 4 and 6 VZAE on the definition and classificatioon of self-employment and the rule that company creation or participation alone creates no claim in the permit procedure. SEM's chapter 4 directives supply the main official interpretive framework. 9

<!-- 2 -->

### Job creation as rule or evidential factor

The sources reviewed do not establish a general rule that a founder must create a fixed number of jobs in order to qualify. Instead, job creation appears as one recognised way to demonstrate labour-market benefit and, in Article 23(3)(a), as a circumstance that may justify derogation from the usual qualification/integration rule for investors and entrepreneurs. This is why the issue is best treated as a **qualified rule in some contexts** and otherwise as a **persuasive evidential factor.** 10

### Labbour-market benefit

Official SEM materials require proof that self-emmployment will have a lasting positive effect on the Swiss labour market. The directives describe this in terms of regional sector diversification, preserving or creating jobs for domestic workers, substantial investment and generation of new mandates for the Swiss economy. Geneva and Lucerne adopt materially the same formulation. 11

### Economic interest

Overall economic interest is a federal admission criterion for both Articles 18 and 19 FNIA. SEM says applicants must explain and document why admission is in Switzerland's and the Swiss economy's overall interest, and the directives add that the assessment should consider the labour-market situation, sustainable economic development and integration prospects while avoiding structural preservation, narrow private interests and wage/social dumping. 12

## Evidence of hiring plans

The federal expectation is documentary and forward-looking: business plans should address activities, financing, market opportunity, personnel development, recruitment options, investments, turnover and profitability. Geneva requires quantified three-year forecasts including precise staffing; Zurich and Zug require a business plan or budget balance sheet; Solothurn asks for market/cost analysis, Swiss cooperation contracts and proof of clients. Those materials do not make hiring mandatory in every case, but they do make staffing claims a matter of evidence rather than rhetoric. 13

### Indirect economic contribution

Official guidance expressly recognises indirect benefit. The SEM directives and cantonal materials include diversification of the regional economy, substantial investment, new contracts for the Swiss economy and, for start-ups, innovation and academic research transfer. This is important for early-stage files where headcount is still low or nil. 2

### Renewal-stage performance

Renewal is tied to delivery, not only to plans. SEM says initial permits for self-employment or new business formation should generally be limited to two years and that extension depends on the timely realisation of the promised positive impacts; if business-plan goals are not met, extension may be refused. The Act separately allows adverse action where an obligation attached to the decision is not fulfilled. 14

### Cantonal variation

Cantons broadly track the federal framework, but practices differ in emphasis.Geneva shows the clearest formal link between migration review and economic-development assessment; Lucerne insists on pre-consultation; Zurich, Zug, Basel-Stadt and Solothurn publish practical evidential requirements; Bern highlights the labour-market review sequence and extension approval process. Official canton material is therefore useful, but it should be treated as **practice-sensitive** and not generalisedacross Switzerland. 15

## Primary legal texts

### Foreign Nationals and Integration Act

### Article 3 - Admission principle.

Official title: Federal Act of 16 December 2005 on Foreign Nationals and Integration. The Act states, in substance, that admission of gainfully employed foreign nationals is allowed in the interests of the economy as a whole and that prospects of lasting integration in the Swiss employment market and social environment are significant. This provision matters because it frames labour-market benefit as part of the generaI admission philosophy, not merely as a sector-specific add-on. 25

### Article 18 - Salaried employment.

The Act provides, in substance, that foreign nationals may be admitted as employees if the case is in the economy's overall interest, an employer application has been filed and the requirements of Articles 20to 25 are met. This matters for founder files because founders who are not genuinely self-employed may fall into the salaried route, which imports the precedence rule in Article 21. 4

### Article 19-Self-employment.

The Act provides, in substance, that foreign nationals may be admitted to work on a self-employed basis if the case is in the overall economic interest, the necessary financial and operational requirements are satisfied, the applicant has an adequate and independent source of income, and the requirements of Articles 20 and 23 to 25 are met. This is the central statutory provision for entrepreneur/self-employment cases and the clearest reason why job creation should not be presented as the only admissibility test. 4

### Article 20 - Limitation measures.

The Act allows the Federal Council to limit first-time short-stay and residence permits for work and to distribute quotas, taking account of cantonal needs and overall economic interests. This matters because even a strong economic-interest case remains quota-sensitive. 26

### Article 21 - Precedence.

The Act provides, in substance, that foreign nationals may be permitted to work only if no suitable domestic employees or citizens of states with free-movement agreements can be found, and it defines domestic employees broadly to include Swiss nationals and several categories of resident permit holders. This matters because it applies to salaried-employment cases under Article 18, but is not expressly incorporated into Article 19 self-employment. 27

### Article 23 - Personal requirements.

Paragraphs 1 and 2 set the general rule that short-stay and residence permits for work may only be granted to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, with prospects of lasting labour-market and social integration. Paragraph 3 then allows derogations, including for investors and entrepreneurs who preserve existing jobs or create new ones. This matters because job creation appears here as a statutory plus-factor and derogation ground, not as a universal numerical threshoId across all entrepreneur files. 4

### Article 62 paragraph 1 letter d - Failure to fulfil an obligation linked to the decision.

The Act allows revocation of permits or other rulings if the foreign national fails to fulfil an obligation linked to the decision. This matters because SEM's renewal guidance ties projected business-plan outcomes and positive economic effects to the continuation of the permit. 28

## Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment

### Article 2 VZAE-Definition of self-employment.

Official title: Ordinance of 24 October 2007 on Admission, Residence and Employment. The Ordinance defines self-employment, in substance, as activity conducted within one's own freely chosen organisation, directed at income generation, under one's own authority and at one's own entrepreneurial risk. This matters because many founder cases turn first on route-classification. 22

### Article 4 VZAE - Who decides whether the activity counts as gainful employment.

The Ordinance say/s the cantonal authority responsible for labour-market admission decides whether the activity is gainful employment, with doubtful cases to be referred to SEM. This matters because founder classification issues are not merely corporate-law matters; they are migration-law questions with cantonal and SEM oversight. 22

### Article 6 paragraph 2 VZAE - No claim from preparatory steps.

The Ordinance states, in substance, that no claim in the permit procedure arises solely from steps such as buying property, renting accommodation, signing an employment contract, or forming or participating in a business. This is a crucial anti-overstatement provision for entrepreneur content. 22

### Articles 19 and 20 VZAE and the annexes-Quotas.

The Ordinance operationalises the annual quota system for short-stay and residence permits for non-free-movement nationals. For present purposes, the key point is the existence of annual quota control, not any year-specific number. This matters because labour-market benefit never removes the need to engage with quota-based discretion. 29

## Official federal guidance and administrative materials

### SEM, "Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market".

This page states the dual system clearly: EU/EFTA workers obtain easier access under free movement, while third-country nationals are admitted only on a complementary basis. It summarises overall economic interest, quotas and precedence, and says applicants must explain and document why admission is in the overall economic interest. For this topic, it is the clearest public federal summary of why founder applications must be evidence-led and why domestic/EU labour-market protection remains relevant in the broader system. 30

### SEM FAQ,"Working".

The self-employment FAQ says non-EU/EFTA nationals may work in Switzerland on a self-employed basis only after labour-market review under Article 19 FNIA and must show that self-employment will have a lasting positive impact on the Swiss labour market. It also summarises the expected documentation: business plan, market analysis, workforce development, recruitment options, investments, turnover, profit, organisational links and corporate documents. This is especially useful for evidential drafting. 31

#### SEM Directives, chapter 4.3.1 and 4.7.2.

The directives provide the most legally precise administrative explanation. They state that admission of third-country nationals can occur only where it serves Switzerland's overall economic interest,taking account of the labour-market situation, sustainable economic development and integration capacity, while avoiding structural preservation and dumping. For self-employment and new business formation, they require proof of sustainable positive effects on the Swiss labour market and identify recognised benefit indicators: diversification, maintaining or creating jobs, substantial investment, new mandates for the Swiss economy, and, for start-ups, innovation capacity and academic research transfer.They also state that founder labels and corporate formalities are not decisive for self-employment, require a detailed business plan, recommend first-phase permits of no more than two years and warn that renewal may be refused if business-plan targets are not met. 32

### Federal SME Portal, "Self-Employment".

The SME portal is not a migration-law directive, but it is an official federal portal and it cleanly distinguishes EU/EFTA nationals, who can start self-employment with cantonal permission,from most other foreign nationals, who either need existing status allowing self-employment or must apply through the canton. ## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

### Practical legal analysis

Job creation matters in Swiss entrepreneur cases because it is one of the clearest ways to show that admitting the founder serves Switzerland's overall economic interests and benefits the Swiss labour market. But the official materials do not support a simplistic "jobs = permit" formula. The SEM directives deliberately list several alternative forms of benefit, including diversification, investment, new Swiss mandates and, for start-ups, innovation and research transfer. Content that presents job creation as the only relevant criterion would therefore be legally under-inclusive; content that presents it as an automatic approval key would be legally over-inclusive. 2

Projected jobs require evidence because Swiss official sources consistently demand documentary substantiation. SEM requires applicants to explain and document the economic interest, and both SEM and the cantons expect business plans that engage with staffing development, recruitment possibilities, financing, market opportunity and projected financials. The Ordinance's rule that company formation or participation alone creates no procedural claim is an important counterweight to over-optimistic founder narratives. Put simply, Swiss authorities do not assess hoped-for economic impact in the abstract; they assess a documented and credible business case. 42

Solo-founder and early-stage businesses therefore need careful treatment, not dismissal. The official sources do not say that a founder must already employ staff before applying, and they expressly recognise other routes to labour-market benefit, especially in start-up settings. But a low-headcount file has to compensate with unusually strong evidence on innovation, market fit, investment, Swiss commercial linkages and realistic growth. It is better to say that solo-founder cases are **possible** **but** **evidence-sensitive** than to imply either that they are excluded or that they are easy. 2

Job creation also interacts with **route-classification.** If the person is not genuinely self-employed under migration law, the case may fall under salaried employment, bringing in the employer-application requirement and the precedence rule. The SEM directives are explicit that founder status, a CEO title or a shareholding entry do not settle that question. Labour-market benefit cannot cure a bad route classification. A compliant analysis must therefore ask, first, whether the founder is truly self-employed in the migration-law sense, and only then how job creation or other economic benefits support admission under the correct route. 16

Renewal-stage relevance is wwhere unsupported promises become most dangerous. SEM says first-phase self-employment or new-business permits should be limited to two years and that renewal depends on timely delivery of the projected positive effects; if business-plan targets are not met, extension may be refused. Federal sources are comparatively thin on a single national renewal document list, so that part of practice is canton-sensitive. Still, the legal direction is clear:at renewal, **actual performance** matters more than forecast language. For internal content systems, forecasts and achieved results should therefore never be treated as interchangeable. 43

Automated blog or knowledge content should avoid fixed numbers unless an official source actually provides one. None of the official sources reviewed set a fixed federal job-creation threshold for entrepreneur admission, and the cantonal materials reviewed also work with qualitative criteria and case evidence, not with a universal headcount minimum. An automated system should therefore talk about "credible labour-market benefit" and "documented positive effects" rather than "X jobs" unless a canton has issued a specific published rule for aspecific programme. 44

## Drafting cautions

·Do **not** say that creating one Swiss or local job guarantees a permit. Overall economic interest, viability, qualifications, quotas and the correct procedural route still matter. 23

·Do **not** claim a fixed numerical job-creation threshold unless a current official source states one. None of the official sources reviewed did so for general entrepreneur/self-employment admission. 10

·Do **not** present Article 23(3)(a) as if it makes job creation a universal legal requirement for all founder cases. It is a derogation within the personal-requirements regime. 4

·Do **not** say that labour-market testing works identically in all founder cases. Distinguish genuine self-employment under Article 19 from salaried-founder cases under Article 18 and 21. 45

·Do **not** equate projected jobs with actual jobos, or business incorporation with permit eligibility. Swiss official materials require proof, and the Ordinance denies any claim from mere company formation or participation. 46

·Do **not** overstate business-plan promises. SEM's renewal guidance makes continued permission dependent on timely realisation of the promised positive effects. 14

·Do **not** ignore alternative benefit pathways such as diversification, investment, innovation and new Swiss mandates, especially in start-up or solo-founder files. 2

·Do **not** generalise canton-specific economic-development features across Switzerland. Geneva's published OCEI contact point, for example, is an official Geneva practice point, not a Swiss-wide rule. 47

## Open questions and limitations

Official sources are strong on the entry criteria, route-classification rules, business-plan expectations and renewal logic. They are thinner on two points: a single harmonised **federal renewal checklist** for proving achieved job creation or labour-market benefit, and a comprehensive list of **canton-specific** **sector** **priorities** in the migration-permit context. Those areas should therefore be treated as practice-sensitive and verified canton by canton before publication or advice is finalised. 48
