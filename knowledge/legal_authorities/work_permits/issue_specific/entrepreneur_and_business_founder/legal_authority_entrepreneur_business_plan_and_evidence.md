# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Business Plan and Evidence in Founder, Entrepreneur and Self-Employment Work Authorisation Cases

## Executive legal summary

In Swiss entrepreneur and founder cases, the crucial point is that a business plan is not an end in itself. For third-country nationals, the governing legal question is whether admission to self-employment is in Switzerland's overall economic interest, whether the necessary financial and operational conditions are met, whether the applicant has an adequate and independent source of income, and whether the further statutory conditions tied into Article 19 FNIA are satisfied. The statute itself does not prescribe a universal "entrepreneur document checklist"; rather, it sets legal criteria, and the business plan and supporting documments function as evidence used by cantonal and federal authorities to test those criteria. 1

That is why bare assertions are usually inadequate. The SEM directives state that, beyond the applicant's personal qualifications, the authorities must be able to verify the enterprise's financial and operational prerequisites and the applicant's independent livelihood, and there must be proof that the planned business activity will have sustainably positive effects on the Swiss labour market. The directives then explain what that means in practice: a new business may be beneficial where it diversifies the regional economy in its sector, preserves or creates jobs for local workers, makes substantial investments, and generates new mandates for the Swiss economy; for start-ups, innovation capacity and transfer of academic research may also count. 2

The role of the business plan is therefore evidential and criterion-linked. The SEM expressly requires, as an application attachment, information on the business planning that gives insight into the proposed activities, financing, market opportunity, development of personnel capacity and recruitment possibilities, planned investments, expected turnover and profits. Existing organisational links with other undertakings and binding financing commitments fromn external investors must be disclosed, and the deed of incorporation and/or commercial register entry should in principle be filed, subject to a narrow start-up-programme exception. This is the clearest federal administrative statement on what a business plan must prove in immigration-law terms. 2

Official cantonal materials broadly track that logic, but they do not do so identically. Geneva requires a complete project dossier or business plan covering the nature of the activity,organisation of the business, and detailed three-year financial forecasts including turnover, costs, profits and specific staffing; it also asks for proof of sufficient and autonomous income, a CV, diplomas, work certificates and a motivation letter, while reserving the right to request further proof. Zurich states that, for third-country self-employment, there must be enough start capital and that a business plan or projected balance sheet is compulsory, with lease or credit agreements being helpful. Vaud's official forms for EU/ EFTA self-employment ask for a business plan, proof of financial resources, a lease for business premises, and detailed explanations of completed or planned administrative steps and professional authorisations. Basel-Stadt's official self-employment page for third-country nationals links both to a dedicated self-employed application form and to a specific business-plan checklist; the search-preview text for that checklist refers, among other things, to AHV confirmation, a company portrait/activity overview, balance sheets, investments and finance. 3

A central drafting caution is that not every founder case is legally classified as self-employment. The SEM directives make clear that the classification is fact-sensitive. A title such as CEO, the existence oof an employment contract, or a commercial-register entry does not by itself determine whether the activity is self-employed or salaried. What matters is the real organisation of the activity: control,absence of subordination, power of direction, ownership position, and exposure to entrepreneurial and financial risk. This matters because salary and local employment conditions are a formal admission criterion for salaried-employment cases under Article 22 FNIA, but not for self-employment under Article 19 FNIA. In founder cases conducted through a Swiss company, evidence often has to show not only that the business is viable, but also why the founder's role should be treated as self-employed or, if it is really salaried, why salary and labour-market requirements are met. 4

Another important point for content drafting is evidential crediboility over time. The SEM directives say that self-employment and start-up permits should initially be limited to a maximum of two years and that renewal should depend on the timely realisation of the promised sustainable positive effects; renewal may be refused, for example, if the goals stated in the business plan are not achieved.That means projected turnover, headcount, investment and market assumptions are not decorative. They can become benchmarks against which renewal is judged. Blog content should therefore avoid treating forecasts as mere presentational material. 2

For EU/EFTA nationals, theframework is materially different. Their case is not built around the third-country "overall economic interest" test in Article 19 FNIA. Instead, official materials say they must prove genuine intended or effective self-employment when applying for residence, and examples of proof include a UID number, social-insurance registration as a self-employed person, a business plan, accounting figures or a commercial-register entry. Cantonal forms, such as Vaud's, show that even in the EU/EFTA context authorities still expect documentary proof of business reality and financial means. 5

The safest drafting position for internal content is therefore this: Swiss law sets criteria, official federal guidance gives a fairly specific picture of what those criteria must be evidenced by, and cantonal materials show recurring document categories but not a single exhaustive national checklist. Any future blog content should distinguish between formal legal requirements, officially described evidential expectations, and prudent practical supporting evidence. 6

## Structural overview

**Purpose** **of business** **evidence.** Business evidence is used to prove statutory admission criteria, especially for third-country self-employment under Article 19 FNIA. The evidence must allow the authorities to assess financial and operational readiness, the applicant's independent livelihood, and whether the project brings sustainably positive labour-market effects to Switzerland. 7

**Business** **plan.** The business plan is not expressly listed in the FNIA as a statutory form requirement, but the SEM directives make it a core application attachment because it must explain planned activities, financing, market opportunities, staffing and recruitment, investments, turnover and profits. Zurich and Geneva also treat the business plan or equivalent projected financials as central.8

**Financial evidence.** Relevant official materials point to proof of sufficient and autonomous income, start capital, projected balance sheets or forecasts, binding financing commitments, bank statements and other proof of available assets. Geneva requests proof of sufficient and autonomous income; Vaud requests proof of finnancial resources; Zurich says business plan or projected balance sheet is compulsory and lease or credit contracts may be helpful. 9

<!-- 2 -->

**Operational evidence.** Official materials indicate the relevance of premises, regulatory readiness and documentary evidence that the activity can actually start. Vaud asks for a lease for business premises and details of completed or planned administrative steps and necessary permits; the Zurich self-employed service-provider list asks for proof of self-employment, insurance, equipment purchases and a signed Swiss contract describing the work. 10

**Market and** **customer** **evidence.** Federal guidance requires the business plan to address market opportunity, and Zurich's official explanatory PDF gives "market chances/comparison" and "orders" as examples of supporting material. Official sources are thinner on exactly which customer-facing documents are mandatory, so customer contracts, letters of intent, pipeline schedules and customer lists should be treated as strong practice evidence rather than universally mandatory documents unless a canton says otherwise. 11

**Founder evidence.** Founder qualifications matter because Article 19 incorporates Article 23 FNIA, and the SEM states that founders and business leaders of labour-market-significant businesses may satisfy the qualification test through the role they perform, but evidence is still needed. Geneva asks for CV, diplomas and work certificates, and Zurich identifies training, employment certificates and CV as relevant proof in third-country work cases. 12

**Employment** **and** **salary** **evidence.** Job-creation plans matter because preserving or creating jobs for Swiss/local workers is expressly part of the "sustainably positive impact" analysis. Salary and local employment conditions are a formal criterion for salaried-employment cases under Article 22 FNIA, not the self-employment criterion in Article 19 FNIA. In founder cases involving a Swiss company, evidence should therefore address both headcount/job impact and the legal classification of the founder's own role. Geneva's checklist asks for precise staffing forecasts; SEM guidance on Article 22 requires the canton to verify local, occupational and sectoral salary conditions for employees. 13

**Canton-sensitive** **documentation.** There is no single national checklist. Geneva, Zurich, Vaud and Basel-Stadt all publish different official materials, and Zurich expressly says authorities may require additional documents beyond the basic statutory criteria. Geneva likewise reserves the right to request additional proof. 14

**Common** **weak** **points.** Weak cases commonly arise where the file contains only a polished narrative without evidence of funding, market traction, regulatory readiness, founder qualifications or the founder's actual necessity and legal role. The SEM's renewal guidance shows that unsupported projections may later undermine renewal if business-plan targets are not met. 2

**Drafting** **implications.** Automated content should organise evidence by legal function and evidence category, not by universal checklist language. It should say when an item is a statutory rule, official guidance, canton-specific practice, or reasoned evidential strategy. 15

## Primary legal texts

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration of 16 December 2005, SR 142.20, Article 11.** Foreign nationals who wish to work in Switzerland need a permit regardless of the length of stay, and "gainful employment" includes both salaried and self-employed activity. In salaried cases the employer files the application. This matters because founder cases start with classification: is the person applying as self-employed, or as an employee of a Swiss company? 24

<!-- 6 -->

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 18.** Salaried employment is admissible only where it is in the interests of the economy as a whole, an employer application has been submitted, and the requirements of Articles 20-25 are met. This matters because some founder cases are in substance employee cases, especially where a Swiss company employs the founder under Swiss-law employment terms. 25

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 19.** Self-employment is admissible only if **it** is in the interests of the economy as a whole, the necessary financial and operational requirements are fulfilled, the applicant has an adequate and independent source of income, and the requirements of Articles 20 and 23-25 are met. This is the anchor provision for third-country founder and entrepreneur work-authorisation cases and explains why business, financial, founder and accommodation evidence matter. 16

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 20.** TheFederal Council may limit first-time L and B work permits by quota, and SEM allocates within the federal quota framework. This matters because even a strong evidence bundle does not remove quota constraints in third-country cases. 26

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 21.** Foreign nationals may only be permitted to work if no suitable domestic or free-movement-area workers can be foundd for the job. This matters directly in salaried founder cases, but not as a separate criterion in self-employment under Article 19 after the current statutory wording; practitioners should therefore avoid mechanically importing employee-style resident labour market evidence into every self-employment case.27

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 22.** Foreign nationals may only be admitted to work if salary and employment conditions customary for the location, profession and sector are satisfied. This matters where the founder is admitted as a salaried employee,rather than as a genuinely self-employed person. It does not appear in Article 19's self-employment condition-set. 28

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 23.** Short-stay and residence permits for work may only be granted to managers, specialists and other qualified workers. This matters because Article 19 imports Article 23; founder qualifications, sector experience and the necessity of the founder's personal role are therefore legally relevant. 29

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 24.** Suitable accommodation must be available. In business-evidence terms this is peripheral, but it remnains a formal admissibility requirement imported into Article 19 via Articles 23-25. 30

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 40.** The cantons grant the relevant permits, but the Confederation remains responsible for quotas and the approval procedure; where a foreign national has no pre-existing right to work, the cantonal authority must issue a preliminary labour-market decision for employment, change of job, or change to self-employment. This matters because entrepreneur cases are structurally canton-led but federally supervised. 31

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 99.** The Federal Council determines which cantonal permit grants and cantonal preliminary labour-market decisions must be submitted to SEM for approval. This matters because self-employment decisions for third-country nationals are not purely cantonal. 32

<!-- 7 -->

**Ordinance on Admission, Stay and the Pursuit of Gainful Employment of 24 October 2007, SR** **142.201, Article** 2. Self-employment is defined as activity carried out within one's own freely chosen organisation, directed at generating income, under one's own power of direction, with one's own entrepreneurial risk, and externally visible through a business operation; liberal professions are expressly included. This matters because founder evidence must prove real autonomy and risk, not just a corporate title. 33

**Ordinance of the FDJP concerning approval in foreign-nationals matters, SR 142.201.1, Article 1.** The ordinance text itself was not directly accessible through the official crawler in a comfortably citable form during this research, but the SEM directives expressly state that cantonal preliminary decisions admitting third-country nationals to self-employment under Article 19 FNIA are approval-subject under Article 1(a)(1)ZV-EJPD. This matters because federal approval is part of the decision structure. 34

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

At federal level, the SEM AIG Directives are the most important source on entrepreneur evidence.They explain that the immigration-law concept of self-employment is autonomous and not identical to social-security or labour-law classifications; that the authority must assess, case by case, whether genuinely self-employed activity exists; and that real indicators include work in one's own organisation, absence of hierarchical subordination, real decision-making power, and exposure to financial/entrepreneurial risk. The directives also explain the substantive economic-interest test, list typical indicators of positive labour-market impact, require a business plan covering the main commercial and financial assumptions, and tie renewal to delivery against the business-plan promises. 35

The SECO SME Portal is useful, but it should be treated as official guidance rather than binding primary law. In its French and Italian pages for third-country entrepreneurs, it states that the business must show a positive and durable effect on the Swiss labour market and explains that this may exist where the enterprise diversifies the regional economy, creates jobs for locals, makes significant investments and generates new mandates. It further says that entrepreneurs should already have a well-developed business idea before relocating, that a convincing business plan is a good basis for a positive assessment, that organisational links with other businesses should already exist, and that the application should include incorporation papers and/or commercial-register proof. Its EU/EFTA pages say that applicants must prove the intended self-employed activity and give examples such as UID number, social-insurance registration, business plan, accounting figures and commercial-register entry. 36

For cantonal materials, the safest use is to treat them as canton-specific illustrations of evidential expectations rather than as national law. Geneva's third-country self-employed page is strong and directly relevant. It states that the applicant must show an economic interest for the Swiss labour market and that this can exist where the company diversifies the regional economy in its sector, obtains or creates jobs for local labour, makes substantial investments and generates new mandates for the Swiss economny. Geneva asks for a complete project dossier/business plan describing the nature of the activity, business organisation, and detailed three-year forecasts including turnover, charges, profits and specific staffing, along with proof of sufficient and autonomous income, CV, diplomas and work certificates, and a motivation letter; it also says the administration reserves the right to require additional proof. Procedurally, the Geneva page recommends prior contact with the canton's economic and innovation office to assess the project's fit with Geneva's economic context. 37

Zurich publishes two different types of relevant material. First, its public page for third-country nationals says that, in self-employment cases, the activity must serve the overall economic interest, generate enough income to cover operating costs and living costs, and have enough start capital, which is why a business plan or projected balance sheet is compulsory; lease and credit agreements are described as helpful. Secondly, Zurich's 2024 explanatory PDF for third-country work permits states more generally that business-supporting documents for the economic-interest analysis may include a specific case explanation, a business plan containing budget, market opportunities/comparison and orders, and the balance sheet and profit-and-loss account; it also says applications are assessed individually, additional documents may be required, and documents are to be filed in German or English, wvith certified translations of diplomas where needed. These points are partly general to third-country work permits rather than self-employment alone, but they are still useful practice indicators. 38

<!-- 8 -->

Vaud's official forms are particularly useful for EU/EFTA self-employment rather than third-country entrepreneurs. Its T1 form for EU/EFTA residence with activity over three months requires, for self-employed activity, a business plan, proof of financial resources, a lease for business premises, and detailed explanations of completed or planned administrative steps needed to launch the activity with the necessary authorisations, including labour-law, trade-police, professional-practice, compensation-fund and health/sanitary authorisations. Vaud's G2 form for European frontier workers contains similar self-employment annexes. These points should not be generalised to third-country entrepreneur cases as hard federal requirements, but they are a reliable official illustration of what one canton considers useful proof of real operational readiness. 39

BaseI-Stadt's official page for self-employed third-country nationals confirms the high-level admission criteria of overall economic interest, financial and operational prerequisites, and personal qualifications, and it links to a dedicated self-employed work-permit form and a specific business-plan checklist. Because the linked document was not directly accessible through the crawler, the safest statement is limited: Basel-Stadt officially expects a distinct business-plan checklist for third-country self-employment, and the available search preview indicates that its contents include an AHV compensation-office confirmation of self-employment, a company portrait/activity overview, and finance-related items such as balance sheets and investments. Those details should be used as canton-specific practice indicators, not as a universal national list. 40

Bern provided mainly procedural materials in thesources reviewed. Its public pages confirm that third-country self-employed persons must obtain a permit from the first day and must file through the cantonal economic office; its better-developed form evidence in the retrieved materials concerned salaried employment, not entrepreneur-specific self-employment. It is therefore safer not to generalise Bern beyond the procedural point that entrepreneur cases are canton-filed and multi-authority reviewed. 41

## Practical legal analysis

A Swiss entrepreneur business plan has to connect to immigration-law criteria because the authorities are not assessing whether the proposal is an interesting venture in the abstract. They are assessing whether the legal conditions for admission are evidenced. The SEM's own formulation of required business-plan content maps directly to Article 19 FNIA: activities and investments go to operational readiness, financing and independent income go to solvency and self-maintenance, market opportunity and turnover/profit forecasts go to viability, staffing and recruitment go to labour-market effects,and organisational links plus investor commitments go to seriousness and substance. This is why future blog drafting should always explain what a document proves, not merely what the document is called.

<!-- 7 -->

<!-- 9 -->

A generic pitch deck may therefore be insufficient. A pitch deck is often investor-facing annd high level; Swiss immigration guidance expects evidence that permits assessment of legal criteria. Federal guidance specifically expects information on financing, market opportunity, staffing/recruitment, investments, turnover and profits, and Geneva wants detailed three-year figures including specific staffing. A polished deck without operational assumptions, financial substantiation, founder-proof and formal corporate documents may not allow theauthority to test the legal case. This is an inference from the official evidential requirements, but it is a strong one. 42

The evidence should address both business viability and founder-role necessity.That follows from the combined effect of Articles 19 and 23 FNIA and the SEMI's classification guidance. Authorities are interested not only in whether a real business could exist, but in why this particular person should be admitted to conduct it and whether they are in truth acting as a self-employed founder rather than simply holding an executive title in a company that could function without them. Evidence should therefore connect the founder's qualifications, industry experience, network, ownership, control rights and financial risk to the actual business model. 43

Financial forecasts require supporting assumptions because the SEM1 explicitly allows renewal to be tested against whether the promised positive impacts and business-plan goals are actually delivered. A forecast that is not tied to assumptions on customers, contracts, pricing, premises, staff, capital expenditure and compliance readiness is not merely weak persuasion; it can become a later credibility problemn. Zurich's public materials and Geneva's checklist reinforce this by expecting projected financials, start capital and, in Geneva, detailed three-year forecasts. 8

Official sources are thinner on exactly how authorities want market demand to be evidenced than on financing and structure. The SEM directives require proof of market opportunity and positive labour-market impact, and Zurich refers to market comparison and orders. From that, it is reasonable to infer that customer contracts, framework agreements, letters of intent, pipeline schedules, customer lists and evidence of active sales or pilot arrangements are useful corroborative material. But because the official texts do not set a universal list, automated content should describe these as practical supporting evidence, not mandatory documents in every case. 11

Cantonal document lists should not be treated as exhaustive. Three official sources point the sameway: FNIA Article 13 allows the competent authority to request further documents as needed; Zurich says that depending on the facts additional documents may be required; and Geneva expressly reserves the right to demand more proof. That means a canton's published list is better understood as a starting point or common minimum, not a closed evidential code. 21

For automated legal content, the most defensibole architecture is to use evidence categories rather than definitive universal checklists. A category-driven approach can say, for example, that official sources indicate the relevance of: business-model evidence; financial and funding proof; market-demand proof; operational readiness documents; founder qualification and role evidence; job-creation/employment planning; and corporate-structure documents. Within each category, the content can distinguish betweenn what the SEM expressly identifies, what particular cantons publicly ask for, and what is prudent but inferential corroboration. That is substantially safer than telling readers there is one fixed Swiss entrepreneur-permit file universally required across all cantons and all nationality frameworks. 44

There is also an important nationality and route limitation. The evidence-heavy Article 19 FNIA analysis principally concerns third-country nationals. EU/EFTA self-employed applicants operate under the free-movement framework and must prove genuine self-employment, but not the same "overall economic interest" threshold. Because public-facing content often collapses these routes into "Swiss entrepreneur permit" language, internal drafting should make the legal framework explicit before describing evidence. 45

<!-- 10 -->

**Open** **questions and** **limitations.** The most detailed official federal guidance is concentrated on third-country cases. Official sources are comparatively thin on nationally standardised lists for customer-demand proof, letters of intent, founder payroll arrangements, and corporate-governance evidence in founder-owned companies. Some cantonal linked documents, especially Basel-Stadt's business-plan checklist, were only partially accessible through crawler snippets, so their use should remain explicitly canton-specific and cautious. 46

## Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

·Do not present a single universal Swiss entrepreneur-permit document checklist unless a specific official source says so for a defined canton or route. 21

·Do not imply that a polished business plan is sufficient on its own; it must be supported by documents proving financing, market opportunity, operational readiness, founder status and qualifications. 42

·Do not confuse investor pitch material with immigration evidence. The official focus is on statutory proof, not fundraising presentation quality. 2

·Do not overstate job creation as a formal minimum requirement. It is an important indicator of positive labour-market impact, but the official texts present it as one of several ways to show sustainable benefit. 47

·Do not ignore founder qualifications and role necessity. Article 19 imports Article 23 FNIA, and the SEM expressly ties qualification analysis to founder and business-leader functions. 19

·Do not describe local salary compliance as a universal self-employment criterion. It is a formal rule for salaried-employment cases; in founder matters it becomes relevant only where the route is really salaried employment or where the founder's structure makes salary evidence material. 48

·Do not describe cantonal checklists as federal legal requirements without qualification.Geneva, Zurich, Vaud andBasel-Stadt publish different materials for different routes. 49

·Do not say any one document guarantees approval. Zurich expressly states there is no legal entitlement to a permit and that cases are examined individually. 50

·Do not ignore later credibility. The SEM says renewal may be refused if business-plan targets are not achieved. 2

·Do not merge EU/EFTA and third-country routes into one evidential standard. The legal tests differ. 45
