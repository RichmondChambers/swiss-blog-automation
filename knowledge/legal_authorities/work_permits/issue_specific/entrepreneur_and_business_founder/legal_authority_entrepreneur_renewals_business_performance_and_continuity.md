# Swiss legal authority pack on entrepreneur permit renewals, business performance and continuity

Suggested internal filename:

legalauthorityentrepreneurrenewalsbusinessperformanceandcontinuitypack.pdf

## A Executive legal summary

This pack concerns Swiss founder and entrepreneur work-authorisation cases for third-country nationals undder the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and related ordinance/guidance, not EU/EFTA free-movement cases. In this route, initial approval is only the start of the immigration analysis. A Swiss residence permit is purpose-linked, time-limited, and extendable only within a broader framework of continuing admissibility, absence of revocation grounds, integration, and proportionality. For residence permits in particular, the statute says the permit is granted for a specific purpose and may be extended if no revocation grounds exist; SEM guidance adds that, absent a separate statutory or treaty right, there is no general entitlement to renewal, and the cantonal authorities decide in light of the person's integration and the circumstances of the case. 1

In founder cases, the first structural question remains important at renewal: was the person admitted as genuinely self-employed, or as an employee of a Swiss company that they founded or control? The SEM directives expressly require that company-creation files be examined carefully to determine whether they involve salaried or self-employed activity. Migration law defines self-employment by substance: a person works in their own freely chosen organisation, for income generation, under their own authority, bearing entrepreneurial risk, and appearing externally as a business. That classification matters because a self-employed case is juddged mainly through overall economic interest, business viability, operational substance, and autonomous income, whereas an employed-founder case also attracts the salary and working-conditions rules for employment admissions. 2

For third-country self-employment, the federal benchmark is continuing overall economic interest and business substance. The SEM directives state that third-country nationals may be admitted for self-employment if personal qualifications, financial and operational conditions, a sufficient and autonomous income source, and a durable positive effect on the Swiss labour market are demonstrated. The same directives then go further for the renewal phase: in the initial business build-out stage, extension depends on the expected durable positive effect actually materialising as planned, and renewal may be refused if the targets set in the original business plan are not met. This is the closest official federal statement on underperformance risk in entrepreneur renewals. It means projections made at the start are not legally irrelevant later. 3

That said, official materials do **not** say that every shortfall in revenue, profit, investment or hiring automatically requires refusal. The federal materials frame this as a discretionary and evidence-sensitive assessment, not as a mechanical pass/fail test. SEM guidance on residence-permit renewal also stresses proportionality, and the integration guidance says individual circumstances must be examined case by case. The legally safer view is therefore that underperformance creates renewal risk because it may show that the predicted economic benefit did not concretise, but any stronger statement needs case-specific evidence or jurisprudence. 4

Evidence matters because the official materials describe the business in practical terms. Federal and cantonal guidance for entrepreneur/self-employment applications points to business plans, market analysis, forecasts for turnover and profit, workforce development, investments, balance sheet and profit-and-loss material, proof of premises, contracts or orders, and other documents showing that the business is real, outward-facing, and capable of covering both operating and living costs. Although the federal materials do not publish a dedicated national renewal checklist for entrepreneur cases,the Geneva renewal page for coonditioned permits requires the renewal form, ID, and any useful proof requested at the initial stage, while Zurich's official explanations show the kinds of commercial evidence the canton treats as relevant to economic-interest and operational-substance review. 5

Salary, tax and social-security compliance can also become renewal-sensitive, but the legal reason depends on the authorised structure. If the founder is treated as an employee, Swiss law requires local, occupational and sectoral salary and working conditions to be respected. If the founder is genuinely self-employed, the core federal issue is not a formal salary floor as such, but whether the business still generates sufficient autonomous income and remains a genuine self-emnployed activity rather than drifting into inactivity, disguised employment, or a different stay purpose. On top of that, SEM guidance on integration/public order expressly treats payment of taxes, absence of debt-enforcement problems, and compliance with public-law obligations as relevant to permit issuance, extension and revocation. Official AHV/AVS guidance also makes clear that social-security status is determined on the real economic facts, not just the contract label, and that employers must account for salary contributions while self-employed persons must register and pay their own contributions. 6

Business changes therefore need legal analysis, not just commercial explanation. Swiss ordinance law says that if an L or B permit was granted under an admission rule for a specific stay purpose, a change of purpose requires a new permit. At the same time, the federal act allows B-permit holders generally to change jobs without further permission, but the SEM's own FAQ adds an important caveat: where the B permit was granted for a specific role and expressly linked to a labour-market condition, the change should be submitted to the competent canton. In founder cases, this means that a real business pivot, a move from self-employment to salaried work, a cessation of payroll, or a major change in ownership/ control can all be legally material if they alter the authorised activity or the factual basis on which the permit was granted. 7

Long-term residence planning must also be separated from card-validity thinking.Family residence for the spouse and children of a B-permit holder is itself conditional, and family-reunification positions may fail where revocation grounds exist. Settlemnent planning is separate again: the statute allows a C permit after ten years' lawful short-stay/residence presence with the last five years on a B permit, and in some cases after five years if integration and language conditions are met. Ordinary naturalisationis stricter still: ten years' residence and a C permit are required, and the SEM states that time on an L permit does **not** count for ordinary naturalisation even though L/B time can count towards settlement under Article 34. A founder's renewal problems, long absences, de-registration, or six-month absences abroad can therefore affect not only the immediate B/L case but also the family file, C-permit eligibility, and citizenship timelines.8

## B Structural overview

### Renewal framework

Swiss residence permits are granted for a specific purpose, are time-limited, and may be extended only if no revocation grounds apply; SEM guidance adds that, absent a separate right, there is no general entitlement to renewal and the canton applies its discretion with attention to integration and proportionality. For B permits, the ordinance sets the general initial validity at one year and requires extension applications no later than 14 days before expiry, though cantonal pages often ask for filing at least 14-15 days before expiry. 9

### Continuing business substance

For self-emmployment, the central federal test is continuing overall economic interest, adequate financial and operating conditions, sufficient autonomous income, and a real business that operates under the founder's own authority and risk. The SEM explains that Switzerland looks for durable labour-market benefit such as diversification, local jobs, substantial investment, and new mandates, while the ordinance definition of self-employment requires external market presence and genuine entrepreneurial risk. 10

### Financial performance

Federal guidance does not impose one fixed numeric threshold for revenue, profit,investment,or headcount in all entrepreneur renewals. What it does say is that extension depends on the expected lasting positive effect actually being realised as planned, and may be refused if business-plan targets are not met. Zurich and Geneva official materials show that turnover, profit,staffing,market opportunities, contracts/orders, balance sheet material, and start capital are treated as core evidence categories. 11

### Salary and employment structure

If the founder is authorised as an employee, Article 22 FNIA requires local, occupational and sectoral salary and working conditions to be met. If the founder is authorised as self-employed, the legal focus shifts to sufficient autonomous income and preservation of genuine self-employed status. Because SEM guidance requires the authority to distinguish between salaried and self-employed company-creation models, and AHV/AVS status is decided on the underlying economic facts, reductions in salary, stopping payroll, or changing from employee to self-employed structures can be immigration-relevant. 12

### Compliance evidence

Swiss integration/public-order guidance expressly treats participation in economic life, compliance with authority decisions, payment of taxes, absence of debt-enforcement issues, and cooperation with public authorities as relevant to permit decisions. On the social-security side, self-employed persons must pay their own AHV/AVS/EO contributions, while employers must deduct and remit salary contributions for employees. In founder cases, this makes tax filings, AHV affiliation, payroll reporting, and debt/ compliance records potentially important renewal evidence, even though the federal migration materials do not publish a single national entrepreneur-renewal checklist. 13

### Business changes and pivots

A change of stay purpose requires a new permit under the ordinance. Separately, while B-permit holders can generally change job without further permission, the SEMA says that where the permit was granted for a specific post and expressly linked to labour-market conditions, a change application should be made to the canton. In entrepreneur matters, business pivots, activity changes, ownership/ control shifts, or movement between self-employed and employed-founder modeIs therefore need legal review against the exact permit basis and any written conditions. 14

### Underperformance and closure

Underperformance is risky because federal guidance ties renewal to the concretisation of the predicted positive economic effect, but the official materials do not support a blanket rule that any missed forecast requires refusal. Business closure, prolonged inactivity, or loss of funding usually threatens renewal because the permit is pourpose-linked and the business may no longer provide autonomous income; if that deterioration leads to social-assistance dependence or de-registration/absence, clear statutory consequences arise. 15

#### Family and long-term residence implications

Family permits linked to a B-permit holder are conditional, including housing, no social assistance, languagge and related requirements, and family-reunification positions can fail where revocation grounds exist. For longer-term planning, C-permit and naturalisation timelines are separate and stricter: C generally requires long and continuous lawful residence plus integration; ordinary naturalisation additionally requires a C permit and excludes time spent on an L permit from the residence count. 16

#### Drafting implications

Automated content should present entrepreneur renewals as a continuing, evidence-based compliance exercise, not as an automatic sequel to initial approval. It should distinguish self-employment from employed-founder models, avoid absolute claims about underperformance, and always separate permit validity from settlement or citizenship planning. 17

## D Primary legal texts

### Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration

The official law title is **theFederal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (FNIA / LEI/ AIG), RS** **142.20.** The SEM's legal-bases page identifies it as the main federal statute for residence, work and integration questions. 34

**Article** **19-** **self-employment.** Close paraphrase: third-country nationals may be admitted to self-employment where the activity serves the overall economic interest and the necessary financialand operational conditions are met; in practice, SEM guidance also reads this together with the personal-qualification rule in Article 23. It matters because entrepreneur/founder cases based on genuine self-employment are anchored here. 19

**Article** **22-salary** **and** **working** **conditions.** Close paraphrase: a foreign national may only be admitted to gainful employment if local, occupational and sectoral salary and working conditions are respected. It matters in founder files where the authorised structure is salaried work in a Swiss company rather than genuine self-employment. 28

**Article** **33-** **residence** **permit.** Close paraphrase: the residence permit is granted for stays of more than one year, for a specified purpose, may carry conditions, and may be extended if no revocation grounds exist; integration may influence its duration and conditions. It matters because entrepreneur renewals sit inside a purpose-linked and conditional residence-permit framework rather than a one-off entry decision. 35

<!-- 8 -->

**Article** **34-** **settlement** **permit.** Close paraphrase: a C permit is indefinite and normally becomes available after ten years of lawful short-stay/residence presence with the last five years continuously on a B permit, provided there are no revocation grounds and the person is integrated; in some cases it may be granted after five years if integration and language criteria are met. It matters for long-term founder planning. 36

**Article** **38-gainful employment.** Close paraphrase: L holders can exercise the authorised activity across Switzerland but need approval for job changes; B holders authorised for gainful activity can work across Switzerland and generally change jobs without further permission; a move from B-permit employment to self-employment is possible if the Article 19 conditions are met. It matters for founder pivots, incorporated structures and changes of role. 37

**Article 44- spouse and children of B-permit holders.** Close paraphrase**:** the spouse and unmarried children under 18 of a residence-permit holder may be granted and extended residence if they live together, have adequate housing, are not dependent on social assistance, meet language-related conditions, and do not trigger the supplementary-benefits restriction. It matters because family continuity is not insulated from the principal founder's permit position. 38

**Article 50-continuation after family breakdown.** Close paraphrase: after dissolution of the marriage or family unit, the spouse/children's residence position under Articles 42 and 43 may continue where the family relationship lasted at least three years and integration criteria are met, or where important personal reasons require continued stay. It matters because family continuity can, in limited cases, survive changes in the principal file. 39

**Article 51-extinction of family-reunification rights.** Close paraphrase**:** family-reunification positions under Articles 43, 48 and 50 lapse where they are abused or where revocation grounds under Articles 62 or 63(2) exist. It matters because deterioration in the principal entrepreneur case may spill into related family files. 40

**Article** **58a-integration** **criteria.** Close paraphrase: the authority assesses integration by reference to public order, constitutional values, language, and participation in economic life or education. It matters because permit extension, settlement and related family determinations may all be shaped by these criteria. 41

**Article** **61** **-** **expiry** **of permits.** Close paraphrase: permits expire on deregistration, on expiry date, or after specified absences abroad; without deregistration, L normally expires after three months abroad and B/C after six months abroad, with a limited possibility to preserve C on request. It matters for founders whose business model involves long periods outside Switzerland. 42

**Article 62-revocation grounds for non-C permits.** Close paraphrase: L/B and other non-C decisions may be revoked for false statements, serious criminality, serious/repeated breaches of public order, breach of a condition attached to the permit, social-assistance dependence, abuse relating to citizenship, or breach of an integration agreement. It mmatters because many "business continuity" problems become immigration problems through this provision. 43

### Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment

The official ordinance title is the **Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment (OASA / OASA /** **VZAE),** RS **142.201.** The SEM legal-bases page identifies it as the implementing ordinance. 34

<!-- 9 -->

**Article 2-self-employed** **gainful activity.** Close paraphrase: self-employment means work carried out in one's own freely chosen organisation, aimed at generating income, under one's own authority and entrepreneurial risk, appearing externally as a business. It matters because ownership or title alone is not enough; the immigration analysis looks at the actual business arrangement. 44

**Article 4-decision** **on** **gainful activity.** Close paraphrase: the cantonal labour-market authority decides whether an activity counts as gainful employment, and doubtful cases are submitted to SEM. It matters where a founder's role sits near the line between self-employed and employee status. 45

**Article** **54** **-** **change** **of stay** **purpose.** Close paraphrase: if an L or B permit was issued under an admission rule for a specific stay purpose, any change of purpose requires a new permit. It matters for business pivots, closure, switching into another immigration basis, or materially changing the authorised activity. 46

**Article** **58-validity** **of residence** **permits.** Close paraphrase: the initial residence permit is generally valid for one year and may be extended by two years, subject to exceptions. It matters for renewal timing and for reading entrepreneur decisions alongside more specific practice documents. 47

**Article** **59** **-** **extension** **application.** Close paraphrase: a B-permit extension application must be filed at the latest 14 days before expiry and may be filed at the earliest three months before expiry; if a request is filed, the person may remain in Switzerland during the procedure unless the authority decides otherwise. It matters because late filing, lapse management and interim work/stay rights are routine renewal issues. 47

## E Official gJuidance and administrative materials

The most important federal administrative source located was the **SEM** **Directives** I. **Domaine** **des** **étrangers,** especially **Chapter 4 on stay with gainful employment** and the general residence chapter. Chapter 4 supplies the operative entrepreneur analysis: it explains the economic-interest test for self-employment, the need for sufficient autonomous income, the kinds of labour-market benefit Switzerland looks for, the need to distinguish self-employment from salaried company-creation cases, and the key renewal sentence that links extension to the actual realisation of the predicted durable positive effect. It also states that company-creation cases are subject to SEM apoproval and that, in the application stage, the authorities expect business-plan and operational documents. 48

The general SEM directives on residence add the broader renewal framework. They explain that foreigners are bound by commitments and declarations made in the permit process, that residence permits are tied to a stay purpose, that conditions may be attached, and that refusal of extension is generally possible where revocation grounds exist, subject to proportionality. Those materials are central to any attempt to analyse founder underperformance, closure, or compliance issues conservatively. 49

The **SEM** **FAQ-Working** is valuable because it turns the self-employment rules into plain but official administrative guidance. It states that third-country nationals may work in Switzerland on a self-employed basis, but only after labour-market review under Article 19 FNIA, and it lists the expected contents of the business plan: planned activity, market analysis, workforce development, recruitment options, and turnover/porofit projections, as well as company constitutional documents and commercial-register material. It also says that B-permit holders can generally change job without further authorisation, while warning that a B permit expressly linked to a labour-market condition for a specific job should be submitted to the canton before the change. 50

<!-- 10 -->

**The SEM page on the basis for admission to the Swiss employment market** confirms the continuing relevance of overall economic interest for Articles 18 and 19 FNIA and explains that applicants must document why admission is in Switzerland's overall economic interest. It also reminds practitioners that third-country admissions are quota-controlled at entry stage, although quota issues recede in importance once the case has moved into renewal territory. 51

For compliance issues, the **SEM** **FAQ** **on** **stay** **and** **integration** **criteria** is particularly useful. It expressly says that the integration criteria are relevant to permit grant, extension and revocation. It defines participation in economic life as the ability to support oneself and one's family, and explains that respect for public order includes compliance with authority decisions, taxes, debt-enforcement position, maintenance obligations and cooperation with authorities. It also states that social-assistance dependence can have immigration consequences, but not automatically, because the canton must decide case by case and apply proportionality. 52

For long-term planning, the **SEM** **ordinary-naturalisation** **pages** are the clearest official source located. They say that ordinary naturalisation requires ten years' residennce and a C permit, and that time on a B or C permit counts, whereas time on an L permit does not count. Those pages therefore matter when drafting route content that might otherwise over-read the significance of a valid entrepreneur B/L card. 53

## G Practical legal analysis

Entrepreneur renewals are evidence-sensitivebecause Swiss federal law and guidance do not treat approval as a permanent endorsement of a founder's commercial idea. The residence permit remains tied to a defined stay purpose, and in the self-employment context the official federal test is framed around continuing laboour-market/economic benefit, sufficient autonomous income,and genuine entrepreneurial substance. The country-wide legal materials are principle-based rather than checklist-based, which means that the assessment naturally becomes evidential: the question is whether the documents now show that the business which justified admission actually exists, operates, and still matches the authorised basis. That is why official canton materials converge on business plans, accounts, market materialI, orders/contracts, premises and staffing evidence. 58

Projections made in the original application may matter later because the SEM says they may matter later. The directives do not merely require an initial business plan; they state that the extension depends on the expected durable positive effect materialising "in the predicted terms", and may be refused where the goals in the business plan have not been reached. That is unusually direct language for Swiss administrative guidance, and it means that future content should not dismiss the original forecasts as mere paper formalities. At the same time, the official materials stop short of saying that every missed milestone automatically forces refusal. The safer legal formulation is that original forecasts provide a later benchmark and that unexplained divergence increases renewal risk, but decision-makers still operate within discretion and proportionality. 59

<!-- 14 -->

Failure to create the projected jobs or revenue should therefore be treated carefully. It is too strong to say that an entrepreneur permit fails whenever the founder misses the business-plan revenue line or hires fewer staff than projected. The official sources support a more careful proposition: projections are relevant because they were part of the admission case, and underperformance may suggest that the expected economic interest did not concretise; however, Swiss renewal analysis remains contextual. Market shocks, slower-than-expected ramp-up, partial implementation, evidence of real operations, preserved capital, or continued path to autonomous income can all be legally relevant, but the present source set does not yield a hard federal formula. That is exactly why automated content must avoid binary promises. 60

Business pivots require careful analysis because Swiss law distinguishes between a change **within** an authorised activity and a change of authorised stay purpose. The ordinance says that a change of stay purpose requires a new permit. Meanwhile, the Act lets B holders generally changge jobs, yet SEM's own FAQ preserves the need for a canton application where the B permit is expressly tied to a specific post and labour-market condition. In founder cases, that means a pivot can be harmless if it remains within the same authorised business substance and permit basis, but risky if it transforms the immigration theory of the case: for example, moving from authentic self-employment to de facto salaried work, abandoning the original activity in favour of something materially different, or changing ownership/ control so that the person no longer works under their own organisation and risk. The legal documentation should therefore analyse not only what changed commercially, but also whether the change alters the permit's legal basis. 61

Salary, tax and social-security issues matter for two reasons. First, if the founder is being treated as an employee, salary and working conditions are directly regulated by Article 22 FNIA. Secondly,both migration law and social-security law insist on substantive classification. AHV/AVS guidance says the compensation office decides status on economic facts, and SEM guidance treats tax payment, debt position and public-law compliance as part of the integration/public-order analysis for permits. So even where there is no express "salary floor" in a self-employed case, reductions in salary, non-payment of founder remuneration, missing employer/social-security filings, absent AHV affiliation, or persistent tax/ debt issues can all become relevant because they either undermine the employee-case salary rules, call the self-employed classification into question, or show weak compliance and weak participation in economic life. Automated content should therefore describe tax and social-security compliance as immigration-adjacent evidence, not as purely corporate housekeeping. 62

Finally, automated route content should never equate initial approval with long-term residence security. Even a currently valid B or L permit says little on its own about settlement or naturalisation. C-permit eligibility depends on continuous lawful residence and integration, naturalisation requires a C permit and excludes L time, family files remain conditional, and absence abroad can itself terminate permit continuity. For founders, the practical consequence is simple: renewal strategy,business-compliance strategy, family strategy and settlement/citizenship strategy should be treated as one connected file from the outset. 63

## H Drafting cautions

·Do **not** imply that an entrepreneur or founder permit renews automatically after initial approval. Swiss residence permits are purpose-specific and discretionary extension issues remain live. 64

·Do **not** ignore whether the business has actually operated in substance. The official test looks to genuine self-employment, autonomous income and durable economic benefit, not merely the existence of a company shell. 65

·Do **not** treat projected job creation, turnover, or investment as irrelevant at renewval.SEM expressly links extension to whether the expected positive effect materialises as predicted and whether business-plan goals were met. 20

·Do **not** claim that any business underperformance automatically causes refusal unless a source says so. The official materials support risk, discretion and proportionality-not a rigid automatic-refusal rule. 60

·Do **not** collapse self-employed and employed-founder cases into one rule set. Salary and local working-condition rules apply directly in employee cases, while self-employed cases turn on autonomous income and entrepreneurial substance. 66

·Do **not** overlook tax, debt-enforcement and social-security compliance. SEM treats taxes and public/private-law obligations as part of public-order/integration analysis, and AHV/AVS filings depend on the real economic structure. 67

· Do **not** ignore business pivots, role changes, or ownership/control changes. A change of stay purpose can require a new permit, and permit conditions may survive beneath an apparently general B card. 68

·Do **not** confuse permit-card validity with long-term residence security. C-permit and naturalisation planning are separate analyses, and L time does not count for ordinary naturalisation. 69

·Do **not** omit family consequences. A weak principal file can affect family-reunification continuity, although family law contains limited independent continuation rules in some breakdown cases. 32

·Do **not** forget physical-presence continuity. De-registration and long absences abroad can terminate permit continuity and damage settlement/citizenship planning. 70
