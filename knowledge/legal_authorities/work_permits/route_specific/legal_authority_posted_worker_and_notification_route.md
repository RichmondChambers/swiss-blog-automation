# Swiss legal authority pack for posted workers and notification routes

## Overview

## Executive legal summary

For Switzerland, the core immigration distinction is between a **short-term** **service** **routebased** **on** **notification** and a **permit** **route.** Under the EU-Switzerland Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, persons providing services, including companies, may provide services in Switzerland for up to **90** **days** **of** **actual work** **in** **a** **calendar** **year.** Swiss administrative guidance applies that framework through the online notification procedure for three main groups: EU/EFTA nationals taking up short-term work with a Swiss employer, posted workers sent by an employer established in an EU/EFTA state, and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers established in an EU/EFTA state. For posted workers who are **third-country** **nationals,** Switzerland requires prior lawful integration in the regular labour market of an EU/EFTA state, generally evidenced by at least **12** **months** of residence status there before the posting. 1

The **notification threshold and the 90-day ceiling are not the same issue.** In ordinary sectors, notification is triggered only when work in Switzerland exceeds **eight days** in **a** **calendar** **year.In** a closed list of higher-risk sectors, notification is required **from** **day** **one.** Those sectors are construction and related finishing trades, gardening and landscaping, hotel and catering, cleaning in businesses and households, monitoring and security, itinerant trade, and the sex industry. Over 90 **working** **days** in a calendar year, the notification route stops and a **permit** **application** is required instead. Outside special service agreements such as public procurement, air transport and land transport, that over-90-day permit is handled under Swiss national law, is subject to labour-market reviewand quotas, and there is **no legal entitlement** to grant. 2

The most important operational point is that Switzerland works with **actual working** **days,** but the electronic system deducts **all days** **that** **the** **notification** **reports.** SEM therefore instructs users to enter the **effective** **working** **days** only. If weekends or public holidays are included in the reported period, those days will still be deducted from the available balance even if no work is done on them. Geneva's official cantonal guidance states the same point expressly with an example. 3

Day counting also works at **two** **levels** **at once.** The eight-day threshold and the 90-day ceiling apply both to the **posting company and to the individual posted worker.** At company level, what matters is the number of days on which the company has staff working in Switzerland, not the number of workers present on a given day. At individual level, each worker's own Swiss workdays also accumulate. That means a company can exhaust its annual boalance even with different workers, and a worker can exhaust their own balance across different Swiss assignments or different posting employers. 4

Notification is not the only compliance layer. Posted workers must receive the Swiss **mandatory** **minimum** **wage** **and** **working** **conditions** thatapply under federal law, Federal Council ordinances, generally binding collective aggreements, and certain standard employment contracts. SECO's guidance emphasises that wage compliance must be checked against Swiss rules, not merely the home-state

contract, and directs employers to the official information platform for applicable wage floors and sectoral rules. In construction, Swiss law also imposes **joint** **and** **several** **liability** on the main contractor for certain wage and working-condition breaches by subcontractors unless the main contractor can prove the required diligence. 5

Finally, notification does not override Swiss regulation of **regulated** **professions.** For regulated professions, EU/EFTA service providers for up to 90 working days generally must make a **separate** **SERI** **declaration,** and professional qualification checks may delay the start of work. SERI's materials also show that some declaration-triggering professions are **canton-specific,** which means a profession can be regulated for 90-day service work in one canton and not in another. 6

## Structural overview

For internal content purposes, the route can be expressed as a simple decision structure. First, identify whether the case involves an **EU/EFTA posting or EU/EFTA self-employed service provision;** if not, the ordinary permit regime is usually engaged instead. Second, check whether any posted **third-country** **national** satisfies the **12-month EU/EFTA labour-market integration** requirement. Third, determine whether the activity falls into a **day-one** **notification** **sector** or into an ordinary sector where the first **eight** **days** remain notification-free. Fourth, count **actual Swiss** **working** **days** at both the **company** and the **individual** leveI. Fifth, if any relevant balance exceeds **90** **working** **days,** move to the **permit** **route.** Sixth, in parallel, check **Swiss wage and working-condition compliance,** and separately check whether the occupation is **a regulated profession requiring SERI declaration or qualification** **verification.7**

## Authorities

## Primary legal texts

The core primary texts for this topic are the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** between Switzerland and the EU and its Member States, especially **Article** 5, which grants a right to provide services for up to **90** **days** of **actual work** in a calendar year. Official Swiss administrative materials consistently treat that treaty rule as the foundation of the notification route. 18

At Swiss domestic level, the key immigration texts named by cantonal authorities are the **Foreign** **Nationals and Integration Act (AIG/FNIA,** SR 142.20), the **Ordinanceon Admission, Stay and** **Employment (VZAE/OASA,** SR 142.201), **the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons (VFP, SR** 142.203), the **Posted Workers Act (EntsG,** SR 823.20), the **Posted Workers Ordinance (EntsV,SR** 823.201), and the **AFMP** itself (SR 0.142.112.681). Basel-Stadt's official practice page conveniently lists these texts together as the governing legal basis for postings and notification cases. 19

<!-- 3 -->

For regulated professions, SERI identifies the relevant specialist primary texts as the **Federal Act** of **14December 2012 on the Declaration Requirement and the Verification of Service Provider** **Qualifications in Regulated Professions** and the matching **Ordinance of 26 June 2013.** SERI also explains that those Swiss rules implement the simplified declaration model linked to **Directive** **2005/36/** EC via the AFMP framework. 20

For construction subcontracting, the primary legal basis on liability and diligence appears in the **EntsG** **and** **EntsV.** SECO's official law-text materials show the insertion of **Article** **5** **EntsG** on main-contractor liability and **Article** **8b** **EntsV** on the documentary proof that can be used to show compliance with minimum wage and working conditions in subcontracting chains. 21

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important operational source is the SEM **Notification** **procedure** page and its **User's** **Guide,** updated in 2026. The guide is unusually detailedand covers who can use the route, prior-notice rules, the eight-day threshold, day-one sectors, emergency exceptions, day counting, changes to notifications, worksite-specific filings, and the interfaces with SERI and VAT/customs issues. For internal drafting, it is the most useful current source on how the route actually functions. 22

SEM's **FAQ on free movement** and its **cross-border service provision factsheet** are the clearest official sources for the high-level rules: when a permit is needed, how the 90-day ceiling is counted, the 12-month requirement for posted third-country nationals, and the distinction between ordinary services and services covered by special sectoral agreements. 23

SEM's **annexes to the directives on the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons** remain the strongest source on tricky counting problems. They explain, through worked examples, that only effective working days should be notified, that reporting a whole period including Saturdays, Sundays and public holidays will consume those days, and that certain combinations of permit use and notification use in the same calendar year can make a notification inadmissible. 24

SECO's materials are the main federal sources on **salary** **and** **working-condition** **compliance.** The **international wage** **comparison** page states that the EntsG obliges foreign employers to guarantee the Swiss mandatory working and wage conditions and points users to the official information platform for identifying sectoral and regional wage floors. SECO's pages on the **tripartite** **commission,** **solidarity liability,** and **self-employed service provision** then explain the enforcement architecture, diligence obligations, and documentary expectations at controls. 25

For sanctions, the most useful source is the SECO **sanctions catalogue under the Posted Workers Act.** It gives concrete administrative guidance on sanctions for non-notification, false notification, wage underpayment, non-payment of sanctions, and failures connected to supposed self-employment. Although it is guidance rather than a statute, it is highly valuable for assessing compliance risk.17

For regulated professions, SERI's **legal basis, instructions, FAQ,** and **list of professions subject to** **declaration** are essential. They show that the declaration regime is separate from immigration notification, that the first declaration costs **CHF** **90,** that the one-month review period starts only once the file is complete, and that some regulated activities are canton-specific. 26

## Cantonal practice notes

## Cantonal practice notes

**Geneva** states the counting rule particularly clearly: only **actual** **working** **days** should be declared, and its example says that if a posted worker stays for two months but does not work on Thursdays and weekends, only Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday and Friday should be notified. Geneva also states that missions over **90** **days** **per year** **or** **3** **consecutive** **months** are subject to authorisation. 27

**Zurich** provides particularly useful practice guidance on timing and site controls. It states that the notification must be filed at least **eight days** before work starts, that the filing day and weekends count in calculating that notice period, and that work can only begin on the **ninth** **day.** Zurich also states that posted non-EU/EFTA nationals must generally have had **12** **months** of prior admission to the regular EU/EFTA labour market, that postings over **90** **working** **days** need a permit, and that self-employed service providers must be able to show a **notification or permit copy, A1 social-security form,and** **contract or written client confirmation;** otherwise they risk a fine up to **CHF 5,000** and an order to stop work. 28

**Ticino** adds two practical points that are especially useful for internal risk analysis. First, its official page says that for posted workers the notification must also be accompanied by the employer's declaration on the **minimum Swiss wage and working conditions,** and that **incomplete notifications will be** **refused.** Second, Ticino repeats the federal rule that service provision over **90** **working** **days** requires a permit application, and that third-country nationals posted by EU/EFTA firms must already have been admitted to the regular EU/EFTA labour market for at least 12 **months.** 29

**Basel-Stadt** organises the route in a simple operational structure: **0-8** **days, 9-90** **days,** and **over** 90**days.** It explicitly says that EU/EFTA posted workers and self-employed persons do not need a work permit for up to **90** **effective** **working** **days,** but that they do need notification. It also points users back to the SEM manual for **emergencies** and **follow-up** **work,** which underlines that cantonal practice expects users to comply with the federal SEM correction rules rather than improvising. 30

**Bern** states that over-90-day cases require a formal application for each worker, submitted **at** **least** **four** **weeks** before the planned assignment, with supporting papers including CV, diplomas, passport copy, and contract or work agreement, and that both the work permit and residence authorisation must be in place **before** **work** **starts.** That is useful evidence that the over-90-day route is procedurally heavier than the notification route and should not be left to the last minute. 31

**Aargau** is notable because its service page describes "90 workdays" as **"calendar** **days".** That wording sits uneasily beside SEM's current guidance, which tells users to notify **actual working** **days** and warns that weekends/public holidays only count when reported. For an internal content system, the safer drafting course is to follow the **SEM/Geneva** **actual-working-day** **method** and to avoid repeating Aargau's shorthand without explanation. 32

### Practical analysis

#### Practical legal analysis

The safest way to understand Swiss posted-worker cases is to treat them as a **layered** **compliance** **regime** rather than a narrow immigration filing exercise. The first layer is the AFMP-based service right, limited to **90** **actual** **working** **days.** The second layer is the immigration **notification** **procedure,** which is the normal way to operationalise that short-term right for eligible persons. The third layer is Swiss **wage-and-condition enforcemennt** under the EntsG/EntsV. The fourth layer is **sectoral and** **professional regulation,** including SERI declarations, cantonal trade-police rules, and local market controIs. A case that passes only the first layer but not the others is not lawfully deployable. 33

<!-- 5 -->

**On** **day** **counting,** SEM's materials draw a clear distinction between legal entitlement and system mechanics. Legally, the treaty rule is framed in terms of **actual work.** Operationally, the notification system deducts **whatever** **days are** **entered.** That is why SEM repeatedly tells users to input **effective** **working** **days,** especially where work is interrupted, and why the directives say a notification covering the whole period from May to September would be rejected if it wrongly treats Saturdays and Sundays as workdays. Geneva's practice page confirms the same approach in plain language. 34

**On** **multiple** **workers** **and** **multiple** **assignments,** the rule is easy to state but easy to misapply. At the **company** level, several workers on the same day normally consume only **one** **company** **day.** At the **individual** level, each worker consumes that day from their own balance. The consequence is asymmetric risk: a company may still have company days left while a particular worker has run out, or a worker may still have personal days left while the company has exhausted its own annual balance. The SEM examples expressly make both points. 35

**On** **prior** **notice,** Zurich and SEM both confirm that the standard rule is **eight calendar** **days,** not eight working days, and that the filing date counts. Emergency use within that period is tightly confined to genuinely unexpected damage, force majeure-type disruption, essential repairs, public-utility disruption, urgent life or environmental protection, and certain weather-dependent cases. Commercial urgency, late client instruuction, or ordinary staffing problems are not enough. That mmatters because "urgent" jobs are one of the most common real-world reasons employers try to compress the notice period. 36

Where a case runs **over** **90** **days,** the legal posture changes materially. Outside special service agreements, the case is no longer within the AFMP's brief-service liberalisation. It becomes a national-law permit case governed by the AIG/FNIA-VZAE framework, with checks on the **Swiss** **economic** **interest,salary and working conditions, qualifications,** and **quota availability.** Bern and Aargau both require advance filing with significant lead time, and SEM states plainly that there is no **legal** **entitlement** to such a permit in ordinary cases. 14

A further complexity arises where a service provider has already used a **permit** in the same calendar year. SEM's directives say that cantonal authorities must check whether the person already holds a Swiss authorisation and, if the notificatioon route is not legally available in that scenario, must **refuse** the notification and prohibit the activity from starting. The directives also explain how the available balance can be reduced when a notification route and a **120-day/four-month** **permit** interact in the same year. This is a high-value internal caution because it is easy to assume that notification and permit balances can simply be mixed and matched. 37

**On salary and working-condition compliance,** the legal risk is not limitedd to minimum wage. Zurich summarises the Swiss rule as covering binding standards from Swiss legislation, Federal Council ordinances, generally binding collective agreements, and standard employment contracts. SECO's wage-comparison page further emphasises that the posting employer must guarantee Swiss mandatory conditions and use Swiss comparison tools and the official posting platform to determine the applicable wage floor. In sectors without a generally binding collective agreement, Zurich notes that the relevant benchmark is the **local and customary wage.** 15

<!-- 6 -->

In construction and related subcontracting chains, the compliance burden is higher still. SECO explains that, since 2013, the main contractor can be pursued civilly for a subcontractor's failure to comply with minimum net wages and working conditions, and may avoid liability only by proving that it applied the required diligence at each onward subcontracting step. SECO's official law-text materials and guidance further show that diligence is expected to be evidenced by documentary checks and contractual/ organisational measures. For internal advice, that means large construction postings should not be analysed only at worker level; they should be assessed at **contract-chain** **level** as well. 38

Regulated professions add a separate **access-to-profession** gate. SERI's mmaterials make clear that an EU/EFTA service provider in a regulated profession for up to 90 working days must use the **SERI** **declaration** **route,** that the initial declaration fee is **CHF** 90, and that activity may only begin once the competent authority confirms there is no obstacle. SERI's profession list is especially important because it shows that some declaration-triggering activities are **canton-specific.** Examples include taxi or limousine driving only in **Geneva,** certain trustee and funeral activities only in **Ticino,** and catering only in **Fribourg** **and** **Vaud.** A content system should therefore never say simply that a profession $"is"$ $or"is$ not" regulated for the 90-day service route without checking the canton. 39

The main **miscalculation** **and** **refusal** **risks** are therefore predictable. They include: counting calendar span rather than actual workdays; reporting non-working weekends and burning days unnecessarily; forgetting that both the company and the worker have annual balances; assuming the first eight days are "free" in day-one sectors; treating commercial urgency as an emergency; failing to file a separate notice for each worksite; neglecting follow-up or replacement-worker correction rules; assuming notification alone is enough for a regulated profession; posting a third-country worker without the 12-month EU/EFTA labour-market history; and assuming an over-90-day permit is an automatic continuation rather than a fresh, quota-sensitive authorisation process. 40

### Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

For internal drafting, the safest wording is **"actual working** **days** in **Switzerland",** not simply "days in Switzerland" and not "calendar days", unless the text is expressly describing the **eight-day** **prior notice** **period,** which is counted in **calendar** **days.** Any explanation of counting should also add that all **days** **entered** in **the** **system are** **deducted,** including weekends and public holidays, even where no work is done on those days. 41

Avoid saying that the notification regime is measured **per worker** **only.** Official Swiss guidance is explicit that the eight-day threshold and the 90-day ceiling apply to both the **posting** **company** and the **posted** **worker/self-employed** **provider.** A sentence that omits the company-level balance is materially incomplete. 42

Avoid saying that the first eight days are always notification-free. That is wrong in the **day-one** **sectors,** and also wrong for **EU/EFTA nationals taking up short-term work with a Swiss employer,** who must be notified from the first day. 43

Avoid saying that a case over 90 days can simply be $"converted"$  to a permit. The over-90-day route is a **separate** **permit process** under national law, often discretionary and quota-bound, with no ordinary legal entitlement outside special service agreements. 14

<!-- 7 -->

Avoid presenting the immigration notification as if it clears the whole case. For **regulated** **professions,** there may be a parallel **SERI declaration** and qualification-verification requirement, and some service professions are regulated only in specific cantons. 44

Avoid general statements that any third-country national can be posted by an EU/EFTA employer.The Swiss rule is narrower: posted third-country nationals must already have been admitted to the **regular** **labour** **market** of an EU/EFTA state, generally for at least 12 **months.** Employers seated outside EU/ EFTA are dealt with outside the ordinary notification route and SECO summarises those cases as permit-required from day one in the listed sectors and after eight days in others. 45

Where cantonal wording simplifies federal rules, prefer the **federal SEM** **formulation** in public-facing content and treat cantonal pages as practice illustrations rather than the lead statement of law. Aargau's phrasing equating workdays with calendar days is the clearest example of why this caution matters. 46

### Open questions and limitations

This pack is grounded principally in **current official guidance and identified primary texts,** but it is not a substitute for checking the **current** **consolidated** **Fedlex** **text** of the cited statutes and ordinances immediately before publication or client use. In this session, the live Fedlex article pages were not fully retrievable through the browser tool, so the primary-law section relies on official Swiss pages that identify the texts and on official SECO law-text PDFs for certain statutory amendments. That does not affect the high-confidence operational rules above, but it is a reason to keep the pack framed as an **internal authority** **pack,** not a final opinion on any disputed edge case.
