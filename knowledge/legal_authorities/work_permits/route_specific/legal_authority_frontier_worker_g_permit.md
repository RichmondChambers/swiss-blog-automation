# Swiss frontier worker G permit authority pack

Suggested filename: legalauthorityfrontierworkergpermitpack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

A Swiss G **permnit** is a **frontier-worker** **authorisation,** not a Swiss residence permit. The core organising idea is that the worker's **main** **residence** **remains** **abroad** and the person works in Switzerland from that foreign base. For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** current SEM guidance defines frontier workers as persons who live in an EU/EFTA state, work in Switzerland, and return to their **main** **place** **of** **residence** **abroad** "as a rule every day, or at least once a week"; no border zones apply any longer, and they have broad professional and geographical mobility in Switzerland. For **third-country** **nationals,** the route is materially narrower**:** they must have a **durable residence right in a neighbouring state,** have lived for **at least six months in the neighbouring border zone,** work in the **Swiss border zone,** and return to their foreign residence **at** **least** **weekly.1**

The legal risk point is not simply whether a person occasionally sleeps in Switzerland during the week. Federal and cantonal materials expressly contemplate **weekday** **stays** in **Switzerland** for frontier workers, provided the person still returns weekly to the foreign main residence and complies with local registration or notification requirements. SEM's 2026 EU/EFTA directives say that frontier workers who stay in Switzerland during the week must report to the local commune at their place of stay as **weekly** **residents;** Ticino says a frontier worker who does not return daily must notify the commune where they sleep in Switzerland; Basel-Stadt says frontier workers who work in Basel but return weekly abroad can register as **Wochenaufenthalter** if the conditions are met. 2

What turns a G-permit case into a likely **Swiss-residence** case is therefore not merely weekly lodging, but the **loss** **of** a **credible** **foreign** **main** **residence** or a factual pattern showing that the centre of life or ordinary stay is actually in Swvitzerland. The public sources reviewed do not provide a single federal "nights per week" bright line. Instead, the practical test is factual and document-heavy: the foreign address must remain real, current and demonstrable; weekly return obligations must be observed;any Swiss secondary address or weekly stay should be openly declared where local practice requires it; and any move, address change or employer change must be notified in time. 3

A further compliance trap is **remote** **work.** SEM's 2022 circular draws a sharp distinction between immigration status, tax and social-security treatment. For EU/EFTA nationals physically present in Switzerland and working from Swiss home office for **a** **foreign** **employer** **with** **no** **Swiss** **labour-market** **connection,** the person is generally **not treated as a worker in Switzerland under the AFMP/FZA,but** rather as a person without gainful activity for immigration purposes. For third-country nationals, home-office work with an effect on the Swiss labour market remains work requiring authorisation. This matters because a person may believe that a foreign residence plus Swiss work pattern still "looks like" frontier work, while Swiss immigration law may classify the Swiss-side reality differently. 4

For drafting, the safest route is to separate **EU/EFTA** and **third-country** scenarios from the start, to use the phrases **"main residence abroad"** and **"daily or at least weekly return"** precisely, and to avoid saying that a G permit allows the person tolive **in** **Switzerland.** It allows work in Switzerland from a

foreign residential base; if theperson is actually moving to Switzerland, the correct analytical route is usually a Swiss residence permit route instead. 5

## Structural overview and table of legal propositions

### Structural overview

The Swiss frontier-worker regimeis structurally split into two tracks.

**For EU/EFTA nationals,** the governing framework is the **Agreement on the Free Movement of** **Persons** and SEM's 2026 directives under that agreement. The key present-day propositions are that the person resides in an EU/EFTA state, works in Switzerland, returns to the foreign main residence daily or at least weekly, does not need a classical Swiss residence permit as such, and can work anywhere in Switzerland because federal practice no longer uses border zones for EU/EFTA frontier workers. Employment of more than three months requires a G permit; shorter work generally falls under the online notification procedure. 6

**For third-country** **nationals,** the governing framework is primarily the **FNIA/AIG** plus SEM's AIG directives and the surviving **border-zone** **treaties** **with** **neighbouring** **states.** The route is more restrictive because the person must already be lawfully and durably established in a neighbouring country, must have lived in that foreign border zone for at least six months, and may work only in the Swiss border zone; laboour-market controls apply, and first-time permits are tightly controlled, especially for agency/temporary-lending arrangements. 7

Across both tracks, the operational logic is the same: G **status** **presupposes** **work** **in** **Switzerland** **without** **a** **transfer** **of** **residence** **to** **Switzerland.** Swiss administrative materials repeatedly anchor the concept in the person's **foreign main residence, the weekly return requirement,** and the need to report relevant changes to employer, address and local stay arrangements. 8

## Primary legal texts

The most important **primary** **instruments** are these.

The **Agreement on** **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons** between Switzerland and the EU and its Member States, especially **Annex** I, **Article** 7, defines the employed frontier worker as a person resident in one contracting party who works in the other and returns home in **principle** **daily** **or** at **least** **weekly.** The same article says frontier workers do not need an ordinary residence permit, although the employment state may issue a specific document. The treaty architecture therefore starts from **foreign** **residence** **plus Swiss** **work,** not Swiss residence plus commuting abroad. 18

**For** **third-country** **nationals, Article** **25** **FNIA/AIG** sets the substantive admission rule: the worker must have **a permanent right of residence** in a neighbouring state, must have lived for **at least six months** **in the neighbouring border zone,** and must work in the **Swiss border zone. Article 35 FNIA/AIG** then adds the frontier-worker permit consequences: the G permitis issued for work in a border zone, the holder must return to the place of residence abroad **at** **least once** **a** **week,** the permit is time-limited but extendable, and after five years of uninterrupted activity the holder has a right to extension unless revocation grounds exist. 19

The surviving **border-crossing / frontier agreements with Germany, France, Austria and Italy** still matter for third-country G cases because they determine the relevant border zones and the return obligation to the neighbouring border zone. SEM's AIG directives expressly identify those treaties and explain that, for third-country nationals living in a neighbouring state and working in Switzerland, **border zones continue to apply.** 20

The current Swiss administrative framework also relies on the **SEM** **directives** under the AFMP/FZA and FNIA/AIG. For EU/EFTA frontier workers, the 2026 VFP directives are especially important because they operationalise the modern rule of no **border zones,** clarify the permit/documentation logic, require change notifications, and recognise local week-stay registration. For third-country frontier workers, the 2026 AIG chapter 4 explains that labour-market rules must be applied strictly, firstpermits through temporary-lending structures are generally unavailable, and work outside the border zone is only exceptionally permissible. 21

### Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

At federal level, the mmost accessible current guidance is SEM's public permit guidance. The **EU/EFTA** G-**permit page** says EU/EFTA frontier workers are nationals of EU/EFTA states who reside in an EU/EFTA state and work in Switzerland, and that they must return to their **main** **place** **of residence** **abroad** every dayy as a rule, or at least once a week. It also states that no border zones exist any more for them and that the permit is generally valid for five years for employment lasting more than one year or for permanent employment. The accompanying SEM factsheet repeats the same structure and adds that the employer is entered on the frontier-worker permit. 22

**The** **non-EU/EFTA** **G-permit page** says frontier workers are foreigners with residence in a foreign border zone who work in the neighbouring Swiss border zone; they must return **weekly** **at** **least** **once** to the foreign **main** **residence;** and a third-country national will receive a G permit only if they have a durable presence right in a neighbouring state, have lived in the border zone for at least six months and meet labour-market rules. It also states that the first G permit is gJenerally issued for one year and is valid only for the border zone of the issuing canton, and that both job changes and moves into self-employment require authorisation. 23

Cantonal practice adds useful operational detail. **Geneva** requires a frontier-work permit application before work begins, states that EU/EFTA frontier workers must be domiciled in an EU/EFTA state, employed by a Swiss company and return home abroad at **least one** **day** **a** **week,** and says that if the person previously held a Swiss residence permit, they must **declare** **departure** **abroad** **first** before a frontier-work application can be lodged. Geneva's FAQ also requires employer changes and foreign-address changes to be reported, generally within **14** **days,** and confirms that a G permit cannot be obtained merely to facilitate job searching. 24

**Ticino** states explicitly that frontier workers are persons who work in Switzerland **without** **transferring** **their residence,** that those who do not return home daily must notify the commune where they sleep in Switzerland, and that in every case they must return **at** **least once** **per** **week** to their principal residence abroad. Ticino also requires renewals or modifications to be filed in time, including changes to the foreign address, and warns of fines for late filing. 25

**Basel-Stadt** describes frontier workers as persons who continue to live abroad while working in Basel, says they generally return daily or at least weekly to the foreign residence, and treats changes to the foreign residence and any Swiss week-stay address as reportable. Basel's general newcomer guidance also says a frontier worker who works in Basel but returns weekly abroad can register as a **weekly** **resident** if conditions are met. 26

**Zürich** is especially useful for third-country cases. Its frontier-worker page says EU/EFTA frontier workers need residence in an EU/EFTA state, a Swiss employer and weekly return to their foreign main residence, with the permit route applicable where work exceeds 90 days per year. For third-country nationals, Zürich spells out that they must have lived for at least six months in a recognised **German** **border** **zone,** work in the Swiss border zone, and also return weekly to their foreign main residence. The page then lists the relevant German districts and the Swiss zone for the canton. 27

**Vaud's** current G1 form is revealing for evidence expectations. It asks for the applicant's **foreign** **address,** asks whether the person has already held a Swiss permit or used the notification system, and specifically asks whether the person has a **secondary** **residence** **address** in **Switzerland** and, if so, where it is. The accompanying document checklist requires identity documents and the relevant employment, apprenticeship, stage or self-employment evidence. That form does not itself change the legal test, but it is strong evidence of what canton-level administration expects to see declared in a frontier-worker file. 28

### Practical legJal analysis

#### Legal basis for frontier-worker status

For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the legal basis is the AFMP/FZA as applied through SEM's 2026 VFP directives and public federal guidance. The decisive features are a cross-border situation, work in Switzerland, residence in another EU/EFTA state, andd return to the foreign main residence daily or at least weekly. Contemporary SEM practice also treats EU/EFTA frontier workers as having nationwide Swiss work mobility and no border-zone restriction. 29

**For third-country** **nationals,** the governing basis is national Swiss foreign-nationals law. Article 25FNIA/AIG and Article 35 FNIA/AIG, read with the neighbouring-state frontier treaties and SEM's AIG directives, collectively create a narrow cross-border commuter route rather than a residence route. That route exists only where the person is already durably based in a neighbouring country and remains truly based there. 30

<!-- 5 -->

### Residence-abroad and return-pattern requirements

**The residence-abroad** **requirement** is central. Federal public guidance repeatedly speaks of the worker's **main** **residence** **abroad,** not merely an address abroad. For EU/EFTA workers, SEM uses the phrase "main place of residence abroad" and requires return daily as a rule or at least weekly. For third-country workers, the law and SEM guidance go further by insisting on durable residence rights in the neighbouring state, at least six months' residence in the neighbouring border zone, and work within the Swiss border zone. 31

**The** **weekly** **return** **requirement** is therefore a minimum compliance floor, not the whole test. A person who returns weekly but whose actual residential centre has quietly shifted to Switzerland is still exposed. Conversely, a person who stays in Switzerland during the work week does not automatically lose frontier-worker status if the foreign main residence remains genuine and the weekly return pattern is maintained and documented. SEM's own directives and cantonal pages expressly allow for this weekly-stay reality, including local week-resident registration. 2

### Evidence of actual frontier-worker reality

The authorities reviewed strongly suggest that the most persuasive evidence is evidence that matches the **real-life** **commuter pattern.** This is an inference from the current forms and guidance, not an express federal checklist. Useful indicators will ordinarily include: a current and documentary **foreign** **principal address;** where relevant, proof of a **durable** **right** of **residence** in the neighbouring state;a work contract or activity proof showing the Swiss-side role; candid disclosure of any **Swiss** **secondary** **address** or weekly accommodation; local communal registration where required for weekly stays; and prompt updating of employer and address details. 32

In internal drafting, it is safest to treat the following as **red-flag** **evidence** **gaps**: an undeclared Swiss sleeping address; a stale foreign address that is not actually used; failure to notify address changes; inability to show a normal cross-border return pattern; and a factual setup in which the person appears to have transferredd ordinary life, family or continuous stay to Switzerland. The public sources do not supply a single universal federal proof list, but they repeatedly focus on the foreign main residence and on change reporting. 33

#### When frontier-worker status starts to look like Swiss residence

Swiss public materials draw an important conceptual boundary: a **residence** **permit** is for a foreign national who stays in Switzerland for more than three months or resides there for a longer-term purpose, while a G permit is for a person who **works** **in** **Switzerland** **while** **living** abroad.SEM's general residence page says that anyone who works during their stay in Switzerland or stays **longer** **than** **three** **months** needs a permit issued by the cantonal migration office; ch.ch similarly says foreign nationals staying **more** **than** **three** **months** **in** **Switzerland** require a residence permit; and SEM's B-permit page describes B holders as persons staying in Switzerland on a longer-term basis for a particular purpose. 34

Against that background, a G case starts to resemble a Swiss-residence case when the factual pattern no longer fits "works in Switzerland, lives abroad". The safest operational markers are these: the foreign principal residence is no longer credible; the person no longer returns at least weekly;the Swiss address has become the true ordinary living base; or the person is effectively working from Switzerland in a way that immigration law classifies as Swiss-based presence rather than frontier work. This is not a claim that every weekly sleeper in Switzerland is a resident; SEM expressly allows weekly stays. It is

instead a **risk-based** **factual analysis** anchored in the difference between a Swiss residence permit and a frontier-worker permit. 35

#### Common compliance risks

One recurring risk is **conflation** **of** **regimes.** SEM's own directives warn that the tax-law and immigration-law concepts of "frontier worker" are not the same, and Zürich's tax page says that holding a frontier-worker permit does not automatically make someone a frontier worker for tax purposes. Internal content should therefore avoid importing tax tests into immigration analysis. 16

Another recurrent risk is **non-reporting**: failure to notify a change of employer, foreign address, company seat, worksite, weekly Swiss accommodation or end of activity. SEM's 2026 VFP directives say that employer, company-seat, business-address and foreign-address changes must be reported before the new activity begins and that breaches can be sanctioned. Geneva and Ticino both reflect the same compliance logic in practical terms, including 14-day update windows and warnings about fines. 14

A further risk is trying to use the G route where the person has actually **moved** **out** of **Swiss** **residence** only on paper, or has not even formally done so. Geneva explicitly requires a former Swiss resident to **declare** **departure** **abroad** before applying for a frontier permit. That is an especially useful internal screening point where a current or recent Swiss B/L/C holder is trying to "switch"to G. 36

Third-country cases carry additional compliance traps: use of the route outside the permitted border zone, attempted first admission through a temp agency or personnel lender, or assumption that the route is a general Swiss labour-market access route. SEM's AIG directives reject that reading and require strict labour-market scrutiny. 15

Remote and hybrid work create another risk. SEM's 2022 circular makes clear that, for immigration purposes, work in Swiss home office fora foreign employer **without** **Swiss** **labour-market connection** may not be "Swiss work" under the AFMP/FZA at all; for third-country nationals, home-office work with a Swiss labour-market effect remains authorisation-relevant. In both settings, a person may have a cross-border employment story but still not fit the legalmodel of a G-permit frontier worker. 4

### Drafting cautions

Do not describe the G permit as permission to **reside** **in** **Switzerland.** The public Swiss materials consistently describe it as a frontier-worker authorisation for a person who **lives** **abroad** **and** **works** **in** **Switzerland.** If the facts indicate a move to Switzerland, switch the analysis to the appropriate Swiss residence-permit route instead of stretching the G category. 37

Do not collapse **EU/EFTA** **and** **third-country** rules into one generic sentence. The EU/EFTA route is liberalised and no longer uses border zones; the third-country route still depends on neighbouring-state residence rights, six months in the neighbouring border zone, Swiss-side border-zone work and labour-market approval. Content that blurs these tracks will be legally unreliable. 38

Do not treat **weekly** **return** as the sole legal question. It is essential, but it sits inside the larger requirement that the worker's **main** **residence** **remains** **abroad.** Internal drafting should therefore use language such as "maintains a genuine principal residence abroad and returns there in principle daily or at least weekly" rather than simply "returns once a week". 39

<!-- 7 -->

Do not omit the **municipal** **week-stay** **/ secondary-address** point. Federal and cantonal materials show that Swiss weekday accommodation is compatible with G status only when treated transparently and, where required, locally registered or notified. Any content system handling frontier-worker scenarios should therefore ask whether there is **Swiss** **weekly lodging,** whether it has been declared, and whether local registration has been completed. 40

Do not imply that all family members of a frontier worker gain a Swiss right to work. SEM's 2026 VFP directives, citing **BGE** **151** II 213, say that third-country family members of an EU/EFTA frontier worker do **not** have an AFMP/FZA-based claim to work in Switzerland because frontier workers do not reside in Switzerland in the treaty sense. 17

Do not import **tax** **or** **social-security** frontier-worker language into immigration content without a warning label. SEM's home-office circular says immigration qualification does not directly determine tax or social-security qualification, and SEM's VFP directive footnote notes that the tax-law and immigration-law definitions of "frontier worker" are not identical. 41
