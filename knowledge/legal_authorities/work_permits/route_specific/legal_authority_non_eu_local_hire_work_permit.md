# Swiss Local-Hire Work Permits for Non-EU

## Nationals

Suggested filename: legalauthoritynoneulocalhireworkpermitpack.pdf

## Executive summary

### A. Executive legal summary

This pack concerns the ordinary Swiss **local-hire** route for a **non-EU/EFTA** **national** emmployed directly by a **Swiss** **employer** into a role performed in Switzerland. It does **not** address EU/EFTA free-movement cases, UK-specific quota cases, frontier-worker routes, self-employment, or pure cross-border posting / secondment cases where the foreign employer remains the employer of record. Swiss and cantonal materials distinguish ordinary "commencement of work in Switzerland" from postings and other special categories, and the legal route for an ordinary salaried local hire sits primarily under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and the Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment. 1

For ordinary third-country local hires, the controlling logic is cumulative rather than alternative. The employer must show**: overall economic interest, an employer-filed application, quota availability,** **priority for the Swiss / EU-EFTA labour market, compliance with salary and local employment** **conditions,** and **a candidate profile that fits the "managers, specialists or other qualified workers"** **model.** For salaried work, the permit application must be submitted by the employer; a job offer on its own does not make the worker eligible to start work. 2

The federal public-facing summary still describes the route as one for a **limited** **number** **of highly** **qualified** **workers,** while the statute itself uses the more precise categories of **managers, specialists** **and** **other** **qualified** **workers.** In practice, that means this is an evidence-heavy route that concentrates on the seniority and skill-content of the role, the realism of the employer's labour-market search, and whether the remuneration package matches Swiss norms for the region, occupation and sector. 3

The process is structurally two-stage. The employer files with the relevant canton; the canton screens the case and makes the labour-market / preliminary decision; positive cases that require federal consent are sent to the State Secretariat for Migration for final approval. If a visa is required, the worker then obtains it through the Swiss representation abroad, enters Switzerland, and registers locally. The SEM states that the official decision alone does **not** yet authorise entry, and the employee may start work only after registration in Switzerland. 4

For 2026, the Federal Council left third-country quotas unchanged at **8,500** **totaI permits,** comprising **4,500 B residence permits** and **4,000 L short-stay permits.** As at the end of March 2026, SEM statistics recorded only **16%** use of the L quota and **17%** use of the B quota nationally. That does **not** remove quota risk in an individual canton, but it suggests that, at least at federal level in early 2026, ordinary refusals are likely to arise more often from weak labour-market evidence, qualification problems, salary issues, or incomplete drafting than from immediate nationwide quota exhaustion. The last sentence is an inference from the official quota and utilisation data, not an express legal rule. 5

## Route framework

### B. Structural overview

The legal entry point is the general rule that a foreign national who wishes to work in Switzerland requires a permit regardless of the period of stay, and that in salaried cases the application is made by the employer. For the ordinary local-hire route, Article 18 FNIA/AIG then requires three things at threshold level: the employment must be in the interests of the economy as a whole, an employer application must exist, and the requirements in Articles 20 to 25 must be met. Those downstream provisions then import quotas, labour-market priority, salary / conditions, qualifications and accommodation. 6

The SEM's own non-EU/EFTA guidance turns that structure into the operative route logic. It summarises the key admission criteria as: overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, the obligation to give notice of certain vacancies, salary and employment conditions customary in the region and sector, personal requirements, and accommodation. The SEM also makes clear that this framework applies across sectors, subject to additional sectoral rules in chapter 4.7 ff.of the directives.7

In practical sequence, an ordinary local-hire case normally works as follows: the employer identifies a role; carries out labour-market testing and any vacancy-notification step; prepares a conditional Swiss employment contract and evidence bundle; files with the canton; obtains a positive cantonal preliminary decision if the case passes labour-market scrutiny; awaits SEM approval where consent is required; arranges visa issuance if needed; and then the worker enters, registers and starts work. The SEM's procedure page and FAQ confirm the employer-led filing structure and the need for cantonal and then federal review in positive cases. 8

A key structural point for internal drafting is that **precedence** under Article 21 and the **vacancy-notification** **obligation** under Article 21a are related but distinct concepts. The first concerns whether suitable Swiss / EU-EFTA labour was actually available; the second is an additional formal admission requirement for occupations on the annual vacancy-notification list. The SEM's 2018 circular expressly says that, for third-country nationals, vacancy nootification is an **additional admission** **requirement,** and that if required notification was not made the application is to be refused in the permit procedure. 9

#### Authorities

### D. Primary legal texts

The core legislative instruments are the **Federal Act on Foreign NationaIs and Integration** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment.** The act contains the key route provisions for ordinary local hire, notably: Art. 11 (permit requirement and employer filing in salaried cases), Art. **18** (salaried employment), Art. 20 (limitation measures / quotas), Art. 21 (precedence), **Art. 21a** (measures for persons seeking employment / vacancy notification), Art. 22 (salary and employment conditions), Art. 23 (personal requirements), Art. 40 (cantonal permit authority and labour-market preliminary decision), and **Art.** 99 (approval procedure). The ordinance supplies the detailed machinery, including quota provisions, the salary rule in Art. 22 VZAE, and the preliminary-decision / approval-procedure framework in Arts. 83 and 85 VZAE. 17

For internal use, it is important to separate **binding** **text** from **working** **translation.** The public law mirrors used above are useful for article-level English orientation, but they themselves warn that they are **not official publications** and that only the Federal Chancellery's publication is legally binding. The Refworld-hosted English version of the act similarly notes that the English text is provided for information and has **no** **legal** force. For contested drafting points, the internal system should therefore anchor the proposition by **official-language** **article** **number** and, where necessary, quote from the official German, French or Italian Fedlex text rather than relying on an English mirror alone. 18

### E. Official guidance / administrative materials

The highest-value administrative materials are the SEM's **"Basis** **for** **admission** **to** **the** **Swiss** **employment market"** page, the SEM **procedure** page, the SEM **application documents** page, the SEM **AIG Directives, Chapter 4** as updated to **1 January 2026,** and the SEM's **2018 circular on vacancy** **notification.** Together, these sources explain the federal route logic, procedural stages, documentary expectations, the distinction between precedence and vacancy notification, the labour-market treatment of shortage occupations, and the salary-analysis methodology. 19

For salary and labour-shortage evidence, the most useful official tools are the **SECO** **National Wage** **Calculator, FSO Salarium,** and the **SECO indicator system on the Swiss labour-force situation.** The SEM directives themselves identify current statistical wage surveys and calculators as relevant evidence, while SECO and the FSO explain the methodology and limits of the calculators. In particular, SECO's NationaI Wage Calculator says expressly that its outputs are **not** **wage** **recommendations,** so they should be used as contextual evidence rather than as an automatic safe harbour. 20

## Cantonal practice

### F. Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich.** Zurich's English guidance is the most useful cantonal practice note for ordinary third-country local hires. It insists that applications for local employment are filed by the employer; identifies the same cumulative federal conditions as economic interest, qualified status, precedence and local salary / working conditions; and, in its separate English explanatory notes, lists the expected evidence for precedence as RAV and EURES postings, dated advertisements, and a candidate list recording nationality, qualifications, date of application and the reason for rejection. It also recognises an evidential easing in occupations with a pronounced shortage of skilled labour, where detailed postings and candidate lists may sometimes be dispensed with if the shortage is properly justified. Zurich further asks for an **employment contract** **signed** **by** **both** **parties,** which is stricter than the SEM's federal minimum of a contract signed at least by the employer. 21

**Geneva.** Geneva's current English page is notably stricter in presentation. It says the employer should file **about** 6 **to** **8** **weeks** **before** **entry,** requires prior acceptance by OCIRT before OCPM issues authorisation, and demands proof that the employer notified the vacancy to the cantonal employment office **regardless of the unemployment rate in the sector concerned.** It alsorequires proof of **timely,** in-depth search efforts in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA, proof that the employer cannot reasonably train an available worker, confirms that only managers / specialists / skilled workers may be admitted, and states that **the job** is **full-time.** Geneva also notes that OCIRT may grant a different authorisation than requested, impose time limits or conditions, and tie renewal to achievement of the objectives announced in the application. Those are important canton-specific drafting sensitivities. 22

**Vaud.** Vaud's standard local-hire form is useful because it shows how the canton operationalises permit categorisation and role scrutiny. The form distinguishes between B, L, **max.** 4 **months,** and **90/120days** categories; asks the employer to classify the role as director, management, qualified employee or non-qualified employee; requires the work contract as an annex; and states expressly that **employment** **may** **not begin** **until after** **the cantonal decision.** For applicants resident abroad, the file goes to the cantonal employment authority; visa-required nationals may not take up residence in Switzerland without prior population-office authorisation. 23

**Basel-Stadt.** Basel-Stadt's materials are shorter, but still useful as practice corroboration. The canton channels employers to a dedicated work-permit application form for third-country workers and expressly links them to the SEM admission criteria and the official **Salarium** salary calculator,which signals that salary benchmarking and conformity with federal criteria are treated as front-end filing issues rather than merely downstream review points. 24

**Zug.** Zug's third-country page is concise but important. It states that third-country nationals need a permit from day one and that there is **no** **legal entitlement** to a permit, to a particular permit, or to an extension. For internal drafting, that is a useful reminder not to present the ordinary local-hire route as rights-based or automatic merely because the employer has a business need. 25

## Practical analysis

### G. Practical legal analysis

The ordinary local-hire route is best understood as a **Swiss** **labour-market** **admission** **regime,** not merely an immigration status rule.That matters because the "centre of gravity" of most cases is not the worker's passport or immigration history, but the employer's ability to justify why the Swiss role should be filled boy this non-EU national, now, on these terms, after this recruitment process, at this salary level, in this canton. The SEM's structure and the cantonal guidance are consistent on that point. 26

<!-- 5 -->

**On** **legal basis and** **route logic,** internal content should present ordinary direct hires as Article 18 cases and clearly distinguish them from postings, company-internal transfers and service-provider routes. Zurich's guidance expressly contrasts "commencement of work in Switzerland" with postings, where the foreign employment relationship remains in place and the Swiss assignment is temporary and usually project-bound. That distinction matters because local-hire cases should ordinarily be evidenced by a Swiss employment contract and a genuine Swiss role, whereas assignment letters and expense-reimbursement analysis are primarily relevant to posting / transfer cases. 27

**On** **labour-market testing,** the main drafting risk is to treat the exercise as formulaic. The SEM directives say search efforts must demonstrate a genuine effort, in proper chronological sequence,to fill the role with workers enjoying priority; contact with the third-country candidate should only occur **after** those efforts have proved unsuccessful; search efforts should take place within a reasonable period beforecontract signature; and priority candidates must not be excluded by criteria that are not genuinely relevant to the work, such as unnecessary language requirements, artificial foreign-experience requirements, or tangential skill filters. The SEM also expects effort towards training or upskilling already-available labour, and Geneva states that the employer should show that available Swiss labour cannot be trained within a reasonable time. 28

On **candidate** **profile** **and suitability,** the baseline rule is strict: the ordinary route is for managers, specialists and other qualified workers. The public-facing SEM pages simplify this into "highly qualified" workers, usually with higher education plus several years' experience, but the detailed directives are more nuanced. In shortage occupations, candidates without a university degree may still qualify if they have a relevant vocational qualification or, generally, at least five years' documented experience. The internal system should therefore avoid saying "a university degree is always mandatory"; the better formulation is that a degree is common and often expected, but the statutory and administrative test is the broader question whether the worker is an appropriately **qualified** manager, specialist or other skilled worker for the specific role, with sustainable integration prospects for residence-permit cases. Unqualified roles are generally outside the route. 29

**On** **salary** **and** **locaI conditions,** the best federal reading is that the canton must verify the real, economically guaranteed package against legal norms, collective or standard agreements where relevant, same-employer / same-sector comparators, and current statistical evidence. The SEM directives say current statistical salary surveys may be relevant because collective minimum rates can sit below wages actually paid to domestic workers; they also say that, in principle, only salary elements giving rise to a **contractual entitlement** can be counted. Employee participation or start-up equity may be credited, but only if the worker still has the direct financial means necessary for living costs and the participation is proportionate to the base salary. For local-hire cases, the safest evidential approach is therefore to submit a clear fixed salary, explain any guaranteed variable components separately, and use both internal comparators and official calculators as corroborative evidence rather than as substitutes for a reasoned salary case. 30

**On** **employer** **burden** **and** **evidence,** the SEM's application-documents page is the operative minimum checklist:complete form, passport copy, precedence evidence, CV, diplomas / references, translations where needed, reason for the application, job description and company information, and a binding employment contract. For project work and start-ups, the evidential burden expands further to include project contracts, project descriptions, implementation plans, deeds oof incorporation, commercial-register evidence and a three-year business plan showing organisation, staffing and financing. The SEM says it will process applications only if they are submitted in full. In practice, this means that ordinary local-hire cases should be drafted almost like structured evidential submissions rather than simple application forms. 31

<!-- 6 -->

As a current-practice point, official 2026 quota statistics suggest that purely numerical quota exhaustion was not the dominant national bottleneck in early 2026. That makes the **quality** **of the** **evidence** **package** even more important. While quota availability always has to be addressed, a well-drafted file should treat labour-market evidence, role seniority, salary conformity, and the economic-interest narrative as the real centrepieces of the bundle. That is an inference drawn from the March 2026utilisation data together wwith the detailed federal and cantonal evidence demands. 32

### H. Drafting cautions

For internal content production, the following cautions are the most important:

**·Do** **not** **say** **that** a **job** **offer** is **enough.** The SEM FAQ says expressly that a job offer is not sufficient; the employer must show precedence, qualifications, salary / conditions and quota availability. 33

**·Do not collapse precedence and vacancy notification into a single test.** The SEM circular treats vacancy notification under Article 21a as a separate additional admission requirement for third-country nationals. 34

**·Do not frame "shortage occupation" as an automatic exemption.** Federal guidance allows evidential easing in certain shortage fields, but the vacancy-notification rules remain, and cantons can still demand targeted proof in sensitive cases. 15

**·Do not say that only university graduates qualify.** The law and directives allow suitably qualified non-academic profiles in some shortage occupations, typically with a vocational diploma or substantial experience. 35

**·Do not treat salary calculators as dispositive.** The calculators are evidence tools; SECO says its National Wage Calculator does not produce wage recommendations. 36

**·Do** **not** **rely** **on** **unsigned** **or** **vague** **contracts.** The SEM requires a binding written contract, and the directives recommend marking it as valid only subject to permit issuance. Zurich is stricter still and expects signatures from both parties. 37

**·Do not imply that a positive cantonal decision ends the process.** Positive cases requiring consent go to SEM, which may refuse, limit or attach conditions. 4

**·Do not imply the worker may start immediately upon approval.** SEM guidance says work begins only after local registration in Switzerland. Vaud's form also says employment may not begin before the cantonal decision. 38

**·Do** **not** **ignore** **canton-specific** **overlays.** Geneva's public guidance adds OCE notification regardless of sector unemployment and expects a full-time job; Zurich documents candidate-list granularity; Zug warns there is no entitlement to a permit or extension. 39

**·Do not treat English translations as binding legal text.** For contentious propositions, cite the official-language article number and, where needed, the official Fedlex wording. 18
