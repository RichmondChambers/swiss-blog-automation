# Executive legal summary

## A. Executive legal summary.

Swiss law does not treat a short stay, a foreign payroll, or the label "assignment" as decisive. The threshold question is whether the person will perform **gainful activity** in Switzerland, and the Swiss concept is deliberately broad: SEM guidance explains that any employed or self-emnployed activity, including cross-border service provision, is treated as gainful if it normally generates income or affects the Swiss labour market, even where remuneration is absent or limited. In practice, SEM examples place **contract negotiations, strategic meetings, pre-assignment visits, and passive congress** **attendance** outside gainful activity, but treat **on-the-job training integrated into production, repairs** **and maintenance, software installation, reorganisation projects, and recurring staff-management work in Switzerland** as gainful.①

Once the activity is gainful, the correct route depends on five moving parts: **nationality, employer** **seat, legal character of the work relationship, effective workdays in the calendar year,and** **whether the activity falls in a sector subject to first-day controls.** For EU/EFTA service provision and eligible postings, the default short-stay route is the **notification** **procedure** for up to **90** **effective** **working** **days** **per** **calendar** **year.** EU/EFTA nationals taking local employment with a Swiss employer can also use the notification route for work up to three months, but that is a different sub-route witha different filing deadline. Outside certain specific service-agreement sectors, work beyond the 90-day/ three-month threshold moves into **permit** territory. 2

For third-country nationals, the system is materially stricter. **Local employment** **with** **a** **Swiss employer** **requires** **a** **permit** from **day** **one.** Cross-border work performed for a foreign employer is treated differently: as a federal rule, gainful activity without taking up employment in Switzerland requires authorisation once it exceeds **eight** **days** **per calendar** **year,** and in several protected sectors authorisation is required **from** **the** **first** **day.** SEM project guidance also shows that many extraordinary, tightly documented projects are handled through either the **four-month** **/ 120-day route** or a **short-stay** **permit,** but onlywith close scrutiny of wages, qualifications, project documents, and anti-rotation concerns. 3

"Project urgency" is narrow in Swiss practice. It can shorten the ordinary eight-day prior notice period only for genuine emergency situations such as unexpected damage, force majeure, disruption of essential operations, or comparable immediate-risk scenarios; work cannot begin before the day of notification. The official criteria do **not** list ordinary commercial pressure, delayed planning, or a client go-live date. Posted-worker wage and working-condition rules continue to apply even where notification is enough, additional declaration or licensing layers can arise in regulated professions, and **foreign staff leasing into Switzerland is prohibited.** 4

## D. Primary legal texts.

The core federal texts are the **Foreign** **Nationals and** **Integration** **Act** (FNIA / LEI / AIG), listed by SEM at **CC 142.20;** the **Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment at RS 142.201;** the **Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons** between Switzerland and the EU at **RS 0.142.112.681;** and the **Freedom of Movement Introduction Ordinance at RS 142.203.** These are the texts that

<!-- 4 -->

structure the gainful-activity concept, the labour-market admission rules, short-stay project authorisations, and the EU/EFTA notification regime. 26

For article-level analysis, the most relevant provisions are: Art. 11 **FNIA** / **LEI** on gainful activity and the permit requirement; **Arts. 18-23** **and** **26** **FNIA** on admission of employees, qualifications, salary conditions, and cross-border services; **Arts.** 1-3, 14, 19, 22a, 54 **and** **56** of the admission/employment ordinance as used in SEM directives; and **Art.** 5 **AFMP** **with** **Arts. 13-15** **and** **17-22** of the free-movement implementing framework for EU/EFTA service provision. 27

A second legal layer comes from the **Posted Workers Act and Posted Workers Ordinance,** which impose Swiss minimum wage and working-condition rules on postings and are expressly used in practice to define the protected sectors and to assess cross-border compliance. The **Recruitment Act** also matters because, together with Annex I AFMP, it underpins the Swiss prohibition on foreign staff leasing into Switzerland. 25

## E. Official guidance / administrative materials.

The most operational federal guidance is the SEM **Notification** **procedure** - **User's** **Guide** (March 2026). It is the clearest single source for who may use notification, how the 8-day and 90-day thresholds work, the different filing deadlines for Swiss local hires versus postings, the emergency exception, the company-and-worker counting method, the ban on foreign staff leasing, and the extra SERI declaration layer for regulated professions. 10

The SEM **FNIA** **directives** and their annexes are the key interpretive materials for project work. The annex on the **notion** **of** **gainful activity** supplies concrete examples separating business visits from gainful work, while the annex on **project staff and** **service** **providers** explains project-bound approvals, project-change consequences, the switch from foreign posting to direct Swiss hire, wage scrutiny, and the exclusion of simple foreign staff leasing. 28

The SEM **OLCP/FMIO** **directives** are the leading source on the EU/EFTA side. They explain the limited nature of Swiss service liberalisation, the 90-day framework, the 12-month labour-market integration rule for third-country workers posted by EU/EFTA employers, the lack of entitlement to permits above 90 days outside specific service-agreement sectors, and the continuing possibility of profession-specific authorisations. 13

SECO's official posting pages remain essential because they restate the practical **first-day** **sectors,** the **8-day rule, the urgent-case exception,** the treatment of **third-country workers integrated into EU/** **EFTA** **labour** **markets,** and the continuing force of Swiss wage and working-condition protections for postings. 29

For third-country permit preparation, SEM's English-language pages on **basis** **for admission,** **procedure,** and **application** **documents** are especially useful because they consolidate the general economic interest, quotas, precedence,salary, qualification, project-document, and filing requirements in a filing-oriented format. 30

# Cantonal practice notes

**F. Cantonal practice notes.**

<!-- 5 -->

Geneva.Geneva's official employer portal is one of the clearest cantonal sources. It states that EU/EFTA-based service providers and posting employers use notification for work of 90 **days** **per year** or 3

**consecutive** **months** **or** **less,** but need a cantonal **authorisation** beyond that. For both EU/EFTA over-90-day cases and third-country service-provider cases, Geneva says the **full** file **should** **be** **lodged** **6-8** **weeks before** **the** **mission,** that work cannot start before authorisation, and that the file should include the service contract, employment contract, reason letter, CV/diplomas, detachment letter, and salary-conditions material. Geneva's labour-market office also states that it decides authorisations for **EU/EFTA service providers over 90 days** and for **third-country workers** in the canton. 31

**Zurich.** Zurich's practice is procedurally important for foreign employers. The cantoon's online work-permit page says that work-permit applications can only be submitted by a person or company **domiciledI in Switzerland,** so a foreign employer must have an **authorised Swiss representative** as applicant/contact. Zurich's explanatory notes also split cross-border services into **company-internal** **assignments** and **external assignments,** require assignment documentation tailored to that split, and say documents should be filed in **German** **or** **English.** In Zurich's explanatory notes, domestic-labour precedence is treated as **waived** for temporary cross-border service assignments under Art. 26 FNIA because the worker is in Switzerland only temporarily and returns home afterwards; that is best treated as a particularly useful cantonal practice note rather than a universal federal drafting shortcut. 32

**Basel-Stadt.** Basel-Stadtpublishes route-specific pages that are unusually useful for deployment triage. For EU/EFTA-based employers, Basel states that the first 8 **days** **per** **calendar year** are not notifiable or permit-relevant, counting **per** **company** **and** **per person,** that work from **day** **9** **to** **day** **90** uses notification, and that work **above** **90** **days** requires a permit. For third-country employers, Basel states that work is permit-free only within the opening eight-day window outside protected sectors, while protected sectors require a permit from **day** **one.** Basel also keeps separate "good to know" pages for **trade-fair/** **stand-building** and itinerant trade; that matters because federation-level SEM guidance says stand-building that actually falls into main or secondary construction triggers first-day notification or authorisation, while pure design, management, or coordination work may still benefit from the eight-day rule. 33

As a federal practice point that matters in multi-canton projects, SEM states that the **canton** **where** **the** **work** is **carried** **out** is competent for the authorisation, and for multi-canton "120-day" cases the application is made to the **first canton** of work, which then obliges the employer to inform later cantons about subsequent service periods on their territory. 34

# Practical legal analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis.

The most important legal distinction is between **observer/meeting activity and productive project** **delivery.** Swiss guidance is activity-based, not label-based. An executive who comes to Switzerland to negotiate terms, attend strategy meetings, or sign a contract remains close to the business-visit side. The same person is on the gainful-activity side if the Swiss stay includes delivering the project, installing systems, repairing machinery, training staff on the job, or regularly conducting staff reviews and setting objectives in Switzerland. That means immigration copy should not rely on phrases such as "business trip", "knowledge transfer", or "temporary support" unless the underlying facts fit the non-gainful examples used by SEM. 35

For **EU/EFTA-service** routes, threshold analysis should be built around **effective** **Swiss** **workdays.** The 90-day ceiling and the eight-day opening window do not count elapsed assignment duration; they count days actually worked in Switzerland. The counters also attach to both the **sending company** and the **individual worker.** This matters in project scheduling: simultaneous workers do not multiply the company's day count for the same day, but sequential assignments do; and if a company already knows a planned assignment will exceed eight exempt days, the first day should be notified rather than waiting until a later phase. Protected sectors override the de minimis rule and require day-one filing. 7

<!-- 6 -->

**For** **third-country** **project** **work,** the safest default is permit analysis rather than notification analysis, unless the worker is entering through the EU/EFTA or UK service-provider regimes. SEM's project guidance is relatively accommodating for tightly scoped, extraordinary tasks, but it also shows where short projects fail: the work must be tied to a **specific** **documented** **project,** the salary must meet Swiss local standards, the wvorker must have demonstrable special know-how, and the permit cannot be used for serial rotations. If the project changes, if there is a prolonged delay, or if the worker moves from foreign employment into a direct fixed-term role with a Swiss affiliate, Swiss authorities treat that as a legally different case requiring renewed admission analysis. 36

**On** **employer** **preparation** **and** **timing,** Swiss authorities expect a file that explains not only who is coming,but why this person, for this project, on this timetable, under this wage structure. SEM's application guidance and cantonal practice nootes together point to the same core dossier architecture: passport copy, CV, diplomas/reference evidence, signed employment or assignment documentation,specific reason letter, evidence of the order or project contract, project description, implementation plan, and salary/expense details. For classic third-country permits, federal guidance also expects evidence on precedence and vacancy notification where applicable. Geneva's official lead time of 6-8 **weeks** for permit cases and Zurich's requirement for a **Swiss** **filing** **representative** show why project mobilisation should be built backwards from filing reality rather than client delivery pressure. 37

### Common weak points recur across routes:

·Misclassifying **installation, repair, warranty, or productive training** as a business visit. SEM's own examples place these activities on the gainful side. 38

·Forgetting that **protected** **sectors** trigger day-one controls and that the **company** as well as the **worker** has day counters. 39

·Treating ordinary project pressure as **urgency.** The official emergency criteria are narrow and damage-oriented. 9

·Filing as a posting where the facts actually show a **new** **Swiss** **employment** **relationship** or **foreign staff leasing.** 40

·Under-documenting **project specificity,** especially where SEM expects a contract, implementation plan, and precise project description. 41

·Ignoring the **Posted** **Workers** **Act** layer or a possible **SERI declaration** for regulated professions. 25

## H.Drafting cautions.

A content system should not say **"short-term work in Switzerland is permit-free"** without immediately stating **for** **whom** **and** **on** **what route.** That statement is only safe for non-gainful business visits or for eligible notification routes; many short projects still require notification or a permit. 42

Do not ask only **"How long is the assignment?"** Ask first **"What wiII the person do in Switzerland?"** and **"Who** **remains** **the** **employer?"** Those two questions determine whether the route is visitor, local employment, service provision, or an impermissible labour-leasing model. 43

<!-- 7 -->

Use separate drafting branches for **Swiss-employer local hires, foreign-employer postings to a Swiss** **group company,** and **foreign-employer service fulfilment for a Swiss client.** Swiss guidance treats those as materially different structures, and Geneva and Zurich both ask for different documents depending on route. 44

Do not use **"urgent** **project"** as if it were a recognised legal category. The valid legal concept is an **emergency** **exception** **to** **prior** **notice,** and the published criteria concern unexpected damage,force majeure, disrupted essential systems, immediate health/environment risks, and certain weather-dependent technical cases. It is therefore a strong inference that routine client deadlines, late planning, or resource shortages will usually be insufficient. 9

Where a project may last **over** **90 days,** do not imply that an EU/EFTA service provider has an automatic permit right. Outside the specific service-agreement sectors flagged in SEM guidance, the federal directives state that there is no **entitlement** to the permit. If the work does fall in a special-agreement sector, the drafting should say so expressly. 13

Add a specific UK **caveat.** UK nationals are no longer EU/EFTA nationals for Swiss local-employment purposes, but short-term UK **service** **provision** remains facilitated under the temporary services mobility regime, with the familiar notification logic and a 12-month labour-market integration test for posted third-country nationals. 45

Finally, build in a **cantonal variability** **warning.** Geneva, Zurich, and Basel-Stadt all publish useful but not identical operational rules, and Swiss filings are handled by the competent cantonal labour-market and migration authorities. Internal content should therefore avoid promising filing channels, lead times, language acceptance, or evidence standards without a canton-of-work qualifier. 46
