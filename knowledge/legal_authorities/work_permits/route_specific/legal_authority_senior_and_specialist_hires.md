# Swiss legal authority pack for senior and specialist hires into Switzerland

Suggested filename: legalauthorityseniorandspecialisthirespack.pdf

# Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not create a free-standing immigration category for a "commercially critical hire". In practice, these cases succeed only if the application fits one of the recognised work-permit pathways and proves the statutory elements that matter most for higher-value third-country hires: overall economic interest, employer sponsorship, labour-market priority, market salary and conditions, and a candidate profile that qualifies as managerial, specialist, or otherwise sufficiently qualified.For ordinary salaried hires, the core legal route is the third-country admission framework in the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, especially Articles 18-24. For temporary cross-border service providers and intra-group assignees, the relevant framework extends to Article 26 FNIA, Article 30(1)(h) FNIA, and the implementing guidance on internal transfers in Article 46 OASA and the SEM directives. 1

The ordinary architecture is federal in law but strongly cantonal in practice. The employer files the application, the competent canton makes the labour-market and immigration screening in the first instance, and applications approved by the canton are then sent to the State Secretariat for Migration for final approval. Switzerland continues to operate quota limits for first-timne third-country work permits; for 2026 the Federal Council maintained the ordinary third-country quota at 8,500, comprising **4,500 residence permits (B)** and **4,000 short-stay permits (L).** 2

For internal content purposes, the most important practical conclusion is that "senior", "specialist", and "commercially critical" are **evidential** **themes,** not legal shortcuts. A strong application therefore has to do five things at once: explain the Swiss business need in concrete terms; show why Swiss and EU/EFTA recruitment did not solve the need; prove that the role genuinely requires managerial or specialist qualifications; offer locally customary salary and conditions on a contract the authorities can test; and fit the chosen route exactly, especially where the employer is relying on a short-stay, posting, or intra-group transfer structure. Shortage-occupation evidence can make the burden lighter, but it does not remove the other statutory conditions or cantonal discretion. 3

Weak cases usually fail for one of a small number of recurring reasons: a role that is dressed up as "specialist" but is operationally generic; recruitment evidence that shows the third-country candidate was chosen first and the Swiss/EU search ran afterwards; salary packages that depend too heavily on bonuses, equity, or allowances rather than guaranteed base pay; language requirements that are not actually role-relevant; or an attempt to use the under-four-month or transfer route for what is really a standard local hire. The SEM directives and cantonal notes are unusually helpful on these pressure points and should be treated as core operational authority. 4

One drafting point matters at the outset: the accessible English FNIA text expressly states that English is **not an official language of the Swiss Confederation** and that the translation is **for information** **purposes** only **and** **has** **no legal** force. For authoritative propositions, the operative texts remain the

official-language versions on Fedlex; English materials are useful internal aids, not dispositive authority. 5

# Authorities and guidance

## Primary legal texts

The core statutory base is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration.** For this topic, the most important provisions are Article 11 on permit requirement and employer filing, Articles 18-24 on salaried employment, self-employment, quotas, priority, job-registration measures,salary and working conditions, and personal requirements, Article 26 on temporary cross-border services, Article 30 on derogations from admission requirements, and Articles 32-33 on the short-stay and residence permit structures. The currently accessible English text is explicitly informational only and has no legal force. 24

The principal implementing ordinance is the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment.** For higher-value hires, the most useful implementing provisions are Article 22 on salary and employment conditions, Article 22a on reimbursable expenses during long-term postings, and Article 46 on internal transfers in internationally active companies. The ordinance's annexes are the quota vehicle,but for current numbers the more reliable current authority is the Federal Council's 2026 quota announcement. 25

For vacancy-notification issues, the federal legal mechanism sits in Article 21a FNIA, while the annual occupation lists and the "Check-Up" tool are published through **Arbeit.swiss** **/** **SECO.** This is important because the formal vacancy-notification trigger changes every year and should not be hard-coded from historic lists. 16

# Official guidance and administrative materials

The single most important administrative source is **SEM's directives** **on** **residence** **with** **gainful** **employment,** Chapter 4. The directives explain how the authorities understand overall economic interest, priority, wage review, personal qualifications, shortage occupations, language/integration questions, and internal transfer practice. They also collect and summarise relevant Federal Administrative Court case law on the priority rule, anti-dumping logic and labour-market proof. For practical drafting, this guidance is more revealing than the statute alone. 26

SEM's **procedure** page and **application** **documents** page are also operationally valuable. Together they confirm the canton-first / SEM-second structure, distinguish longer stays from under-four-month wvork, and provide the federal checklist of minimum filing documents: passport copy, evidence of labor-market search, CV,qualifications, reasons the vacancy must be filled, job description, company information, and the signed employment contract or transfer confirmation stating salary, allowances and expenses arrangements. 27

On labour-market evidence and salary benchmarking, the official federal tools are significant. SECO's **indicator system to assess the Swiss labour force situation** is the reference framework the SEM directives themselves invoke wwhen discussing shortage occupations. For salary benchmarking, the SEM directives specifically reference the Federal Statistical Office's **Salarium** and SECO's **National wage** **calculator,** and both tools remain available. 28

# Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal practice is not just procedural packaging; it directly shapes what evidence will realistically be accepted.

**Zurich** is the clearest published example of a document-driven canton. Its explanatory note for third-country employment requires a specific application rationale for economic interest, expects RAV and EURES advertising plus dated adverts and a detailed candidate list for ordinary local hires, and expressly allows the labour-market burden to be relaxed where the employer can properly show a pronounced shortage occupation. Zurich also states that third-country workers must be managers, specialists or otherwise qualified, and that non-academic shortage roles still need either vocational qualification or usually five years' experience. For postings, Zurich is particularly useful because it lists the expected assignee documentation in detail, including base salary at home, assignment allowance, expense coverage, insurance and signature requirements. 29

**Geneva** publishes a stricter-looking practice note for third-country salaried hires. It tells employers to file roughly **six** **to** **eight** **weeks** **before** **entry,** requires the employer to show that Swiss and EU/EFTA recruitment was explored and failed, expects notice to the Geneva employment office **regardless** **of the** **branch** **unemployment level,** and states that the role must be **full-time.** Geneva also warns that a granted authorisation may be time-limited and that renewal or extension may be made conditional on achieving the objectives announced in the employer's application. These are highly relevant operational cautions for mission-critical commercial hires, particularly restructurings, start-ups and project roles. 30

**Vaud** is useful because its official work-authorisation form exposes what the canton wants to see on the face of the file: the route type (including internal transfer, service provider and employer change),the proposed permit type, the exact activity category within the company **(director,** **manager, qualified** **employee, non-qualified** **employee** or other), weekly hours, gross salary, 13th-month salary treatment, and the proposed start date. The form expressly states that employment may begin only after the cantonal authorities have decided and that the contract of employment must be attached. 31

**Zug** makes an important discretionary point explicit: there is no **legal entitlement** to a specific work permit or to its extension. It also highlights the need for an assignment confirmation in posting cases. Even where this merely restates broad federal discretion, it is a useful internal reminder for risk-weighting. 32

# Practical legal analysis

## Senior and specialist fit within work permit law

For higher-value non-EU hires, the key legal question is not whether the person is "important to the business" in a general commercial sense, but whether the person fits the legally recognised profile of a **manager,** **specialist,** or **other** **qualified** **worker,** or falls within one of the express exceptions in Article 23(3) FNIA. At federal level, short-stay and residence permits for work purposes may ordinarily be granted oonly to those categories. The legislation then opens a narrower exception space for investors and entrepreneurs creating or maintaining jobs, recognised persons in science/culture/sport, persons with special professional knowledge or skills where there is a need, executive transfers, and persons indispensable to economically significant international business relationships. 15

The SEM directives make the category more concrete. They explain that the necessary qualification can arise at different levels: university degree, university of applied sciences diploma, specialised training with several years of experience, a profession with additional training, or extraordinary indispensable specialist knowledge in a specific field. They also add an important practical point: in some cases the required qualification may be inferred from the function itself, for example business founders or business leaders of labour-market-significant companies. That is particularly relevant for senior commercial hires whose value lies in strategic leadership and business development rather than in a narrow technical discipline. 33

The same directives also show where the boundary lies. Unqualified workers and helpers are stated not to be in Switzerland's ooverall economic interest and not to satisfy the personal requirements. In other words,a role that looks operationally generic cannot be rescued simply by attaching a high title, a high pay package, or a business-critical narrative. The duties themselves must reveal managerial authority, specialist indispensability, or a truly qualified profile. 34

Where the role is non-academic, the application usually needs a two-part explanation. First, it must show that the role still belongs to a recognised shortage or specialist category. Secondly, it should document either a vocational qualification or, more usually, several years of directly relevant experience. Both the SEM directives and Zurich's explanatory note use the same practical benchmark:for this special category the authorities usually expect at least a formal vocational qualification or roughly **five** **years of relevant professional experience.** 35

## Employer justification and necessity

The legal translation of "commercial necessity" is the requirement that the hire serves the **interests** of **the** **economy** **as** **a** **whole.** The SEM directives make clear that this is not a licence to support narrow private preferences. The authorities are directed to consider the labour-market situation, sustainable economic development, and integration prospects, while avoiding low-wage structural preservation, pure particular interests, and wage or social dumping. A convincing senior-hire narrative therefore focuses on concrete Swiss economic benefit: protecting revenue lines, delivering specialised projects, enabling investment, safeguarding loocally significant operations, or building functions that strengthen Swiss business activity on a durable basis. 36

This is why the Zurich materials are especially useful for internal drafting. They explicitly treat a **specific** **application** **justification** as mandatory for the economic-interest limb and suggest supporting business-plan, budget, market-opportunity and financial materials where relevant.For commercially critical hires, that means the employer justification should usually do more than describe the candidate; it should describe the **Swiss** **business** **problem** the role solves and why that problem matters economically in Switzerland. 37

<!-- 7 -->

The filing should also be route-specific. In Zurich, an external posting file is expected to contain not only a specific justification but also the Swiss customer contract or works contract and a project description. Geneva's public guidance similarly indicates that renewals may be conditioned on the objectives stated in the request. The practical implication is that employers should avoid vague statements such as "supporting growth" or "strategic expansion" and instead tie the role to defined deliverables, contractual mandates, operational milestones, or revenue-critical functions that can still be defended at renewal stage. 38

### Labour market and candidate-profile issues

The labour-market case sits at the heart of ordinary third-country hiring. Under Article 21 FNIA, a third-country worker may be approved only if no suitable domestic employee or citizen of a state benefiting from free movement can be found. The protected labour pool is wider than many employers assume, including not just Swiss nationals and EU/EFTA nationals but also settlement permit holders, residence permit holders authorised to work, temporarily admitted persons and protected persons who may work. 13

SEM's application-document checklist and the directives show the expected evidence. At a minimum, the authorities expect confirmation of notification to the regional employment office and EURES where required, copies of ads in relevant media and specialist portals, information on other search efforts in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area, any agency involvement, and a record of applications received with reasons why candidates could not be considered. Zurich's note is even more explicit, asking for RAV and EURES advertising, dated adverts, and a detailed candidate list including nationality, qualification, application date and rejection reason. 39

The SEM directives also sharpen two common weak points. First, search efforts must be real, not formalistic. Contact wwith a third-country candidate should only occur after meaningful Swiss and EU/ EFTA search efforts have genuinely failed. Secondly, protected candidates must not be excluded by criteria that are not professionally relevant, such as unnecessary language requirements, international exposure requirements, or niche criteria with only weak relevance to the role. This is a major drafting point for internationally recruited commercial teams. 40

Shortage occupations are the principal softening mechanism. The SEM directives state that where a profession is demonstrably affected by a pronounced structural shortage, the authorities may interpret the proof burden generously and can even dlispense with requiring concrete search efforts, provided the application plausibly explains why the case falls within a shortage occupation. The same directives list a number of fields where this can apply, including management positions in R&D, health, education, ICT, consulting, finance, insurance, machinery, electrical engineering, metallurgy, chemicals and pharmaceuticals, andd food production, as well as business specialists in management and organisational analysis, engineering andspecialised ICT roles. But the directives also preserve cantoonal discretion to request more concrete search evidence in a critical case. 22

The 2026 vacancy-notification list adds a useful caution. Because the federal list includes some managerial and scientific profiles, a role can look commercially senior and still fall within an occupation for which advance vacancy notification is mandatory. The published 2026 list, for examnple, includes **sales** **andI marketing** **managers** and several scientific occupations. Internal content should therefore never assume that "managerial" means exempt from Article 21a considerations. 41

Candidate profile evidence should be drafted in parallel with the labour-market case, not afterwards. The role description, the CV, the degree certificates, references and experience narrative must all point to the same conclusion: that the person is not merely preferred, but **legally** **admissible** as a qualified third-country worker. Where the job is regulated, there is an additional layer: recognition or professional-practice authorisation may be needed, and sectoral professional approval does not replace the immigration authorisation. 42

### Salary and role design

The salary and role-design limb is more exacting than many employers expect. Article 22 FNIA requires salary and conditions customary for the location, profession and sector, and the SEM directives explain that the cantons must test this systematically against legal norms, sectoral rules, and actual comparable conditions in the same business and industry. The directives go on to say that minimum wages in collective agreements often sit below actual local pay, so current statistical wage surveys should also be used, and that in urban settings those averages are generally treated as the floor. 43

For internal drafting, that makes the official wage-calculation tools particularly important. The Federal Statistical Office's **Salarium** and SECO's **National wage calculator** are both official tools specifically identified in the SEM dlirectives as relevant references. Because both state that they provide statistical estimates rather than recommendations, they should usually be used as **evidence** **of** **plausibility,** not as the sole basis for setting pay; where the market is fast-moving or the role is unusually niche, employers should supplement them with sector-specific materials and internal comparables in Switzerland. 44

The contract must be permit-ready. SEM's directives require the essential employment terms to be submitted in writing and say the authorities must obtain a written, binding employment contract or binding offer signed at least by the employer. Thecontract should include at least the function, work location, duration, working time, salary, social benefits and deductions, and shouuld be drafted subject to permit issuance. Vaud's official form shows the same orientation: it asks for weekly hours, gross salary excluding the 13th-month salary, salary deductions, 13th-month treatment and the requested period of authorisation, and it requires the employment contract as an attachment. 45

Guaranteed remuneration matters more than theoretical upside. The SEM directives state that only remuneration components subject to a contractual entitlement should generally be counted. They allow employee participation instruments to be taken into account where these are properly documented and the employee still has sufficient direct financial means for living expenses, but make clear that the participation should remain proportionate to the base salary. That points strongly against building permit justifications around discretionary bonuses, speculative commission, or equity-heavy compensation structures without a robust fixed salary. 19

For postings and transferred specialists, wage drafting must also keep **expenses** separate from **salary.** The statute and directives state that travel, accommodation and subsistence compensation connected with cross-border services or operational transfers are not part of salary and must not be deducted from gross pay. Zurich's posting note goes further and sets out the expected contents of the assignment paper, including home salary, Swiss allowance, expense coverage, insurance and social-security arrangements, and-in its model approach-either lawful reimbursement of all posting expenses or a lump-sum allowance of at least **CHF** **3,000** **per** **month** plus actual travel costs for at least twelve months from entry. 46

Language andd integration need careful framing. The SEM directives accept that in highly specialised multinational businesses or internationally oriented research and development contexts, very good English can be sufficient to support sustainable integration. But the same guidance warns that English mother tongue alone is not a qualification and that irrelevant language criteria must not be used to screen out protected candidates. The right approach, therefore, is to explain why English is genuinely the working language of the specialist function, rather than treating English fluency as a proxy for calibre. 21

### Common weak points

The most common structural weakness is **overclaiming** **specialist status.** If the role description reads like a general commercial, administrative, or mid-level operational job, the file is unlikely to survive merely because the employer prefers the named overseas candidate. This problem becomes acute where the role title is senior but the actual tasks are routine, or where the role is part-time without a strong explanation. Geneva's published note expressly expects a full-time job, illustrating how quickly cantons can become sceptical about reduced-percentage roles. 47

The second frequent weakness is a **bad** **chronology** on recruitment evidence. SEM's directives are explicit that labour-market search efforts must not be a mere formality and should occur within an appropriate period before a contract is signed with the named third-country candidate.If the authorities infer that the employer identified the overseas candidate first and ran the Swiss/EU search afterwards, the poriority case weakens materially. 40

The third weakness is **poor** **market evidence.** Employers often cite general industry shortage, but fail to show that the specific role, at the proposed salary, in the proposed location, could not be filled from the protected labour pool. The SEM directives do allow a shortage-based relaxation in some professions, but they also reserve the canton's ability to ask for concrete evidence in sensitive cases. A strong file therefore connects the macro material-SECO shortage indicators, current vacancy-notification lists, hard-to-fill occupation data-to the actual Swiss vacancy the employer tried to fill. 48

The fourth recurring weakness is **compensation** **design.** If the salary is below local norms, if the package depends too heavily on flexible elements, or if posting expenses are mixed into salary, the application risks being read as a wage-dumping case rather than a high-value hire. This is precisely the concern the SEM directives say the authorities must guard against. 49

The fifth weakness is **misusing** **the** **short-stay** **or transfer route.** The under-four-month /120-day route is not for trial periods, sequential deployments, or back-door long-term staffing, and Zurich's guidance for external postings expects a real project contract and project description. Likewise, an intra-group transfer should not be drafted as a quasi-local hire with a transfer label tacked on at the end; the file should show genuine intra-group logic, real temporary or transfer-based business need, and an assignee whose seniority or indispensability explains the route choice. 50
