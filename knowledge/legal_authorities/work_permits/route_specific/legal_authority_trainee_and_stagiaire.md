# Swiss trainee and stagiaire work-authorisation authority pack

Suggested filename: legalauthoritytraineeandstagiairepack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not treat "trainee", "stagiaire", "exchange", or "intern" as loose commercial labels. In immigration terms, these routes are narrow exceptions to the ordinary admission rules for third-country employment. The main federal baselines are the ordinary labour-market rules in the FNIA for third-country hires, and the exception machinery in Article 30 FNIA together with the OASA rules on study-related placements, exchange programmes, and stagiaires/Young Professionals. In practice, the most important special routes are: treaty-based **Young** **Professionals** / **stagiaires** under Article 42OASA; **international exchange** programmes under Article 41 OASA; annd **compulsory study-related** **internships** under Article 39 OASA or the broader internship guidance in the SEM directives. If a case does not genuinely fit one of those exceptions, it falls back into ordinary emnployment analysis. ①

The controlling logic is **purpose.** For exchange-based and training-based routes, the training, perfectionnement, or exchange objective must remain the principal reason for the stay. The SEM directives state expressly, for Article 41 OASA, that exchange must remain the main purpose, reciprocity is central, the stay must be temporary, and the route cannot be used to bring in a person who primarily wants to work. The same logic appears in the study-related internship rules: training must remain the main purpose of stay, and long productive placements cease to be educational programmes and are treated differently. 2

The Swiss system is also deliberately broad in what counts as "gainful activity". SEM's directives say the concept is interpreted widely; an activity can requireauthorisation even if it is unpaid or only minimally compensated,if it is the kind of work normally performed on the labour market for remuneration. That matters because employers cannot avoid immigration scrutiny merely by calling a placement "unpaid training". 3

For the **Young** **Professionals** / **stagiaires** route, SEM's public and directive materials are unusually clear. The route exists to let eligible nationals of contracting states expand their professional and language skills in Switzerland. The applicant must normally be within the agreement's age band, have completed vocational or higher education, and work in the profession or discipline in which they were trained. The public SEM material currently lists 15 practically relevant partner states on the inward-looking Young Professionals page, while the SEM directives still list35 agreements overall and explain that the EU/EFTA stagiaire agreements remain in place but are no longer used in practice because free movement rules are more favourable. 4

The **training plan is a legal control document, not an afterthought.** SEM requires a written employment contract with an integrated training programme. The contract should specify the job description, schedule and courses, duration, salary and notice terms, working hours, holidays, insurance, and travel expenses. SEM's standard Young Professionals contract goes further and requires a specific on-the-job programme aimed at improving linguistic and professional skills, together with

details and a named responsible person. The application form itself requires the employment contract with training programme as a core enclosure. 5

Common refusal or reclassification risks are consistent across the federal and cantonal material: the role is not in the applicant's trained field; there is no serious training content; the placement looks like a normal junior vacancy, probation, or outsourced labour arrangement; salary is below regional or sectoral norms; the employer proposes part-time work, self-employment, or staff leasing; the applicant starts work before approval; or, in study cases, no credible university or school document proves that the internship is an integral and compulsory part of the course. Cantonal guidance sharpens the same idea: Geneva distinguishes recognised trainingstages from stages that are really employment; Ticino defines stage by training and reorientation logic; St. Gallen says an internship is not for testing suitability and that language deficits alone do not justify an internship; Basel-Stadt takes a restrictive line for third-country internship permits. 6

## Structural overview

**Ordinary** **employment** **baseline.** Switzerland follows a dual admission system. EU/EFTA nationals benefit from free movemnent rules; third-country nationals are admitted only in a complementary system under the FNIA and OASA. For ordinary third-country local hires, the main rules are overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, vacancy-notification obligations, customary salary and employment conditions, personal qualifications, and suitable accommodation. SEM's own general guidance also says Article 30 FNIA contains the exceptions, including internships and training placements. 7

**Treaty-based** **stagiaires** / **Young** **Professionals.** This is the clearest special trainee route. It is tiedto bilateral trainee exchange agreements, processed under SEM's Young Professionals framework, and designed for people who have already completed relevant education and want a temporary Swiss training experience in their trained profession or studied discipline. Labour-market situation is not the controlling filter here, but wage and working-condition protection remains fully applicable. 8

**Programme-based exchange under Article 41 OASA.** This route is broader than treaty stagiaires and covers temporary participation in educational, further-training, or exchange programmes organised on a bilateral or multilateral basis. The Article 41 route is especially important where the sponsor organisation and reciprocity architecture do the legal work that a bilateral stagiaire treaty would otherwise do. 9

**Compulsory** **study** **placements.** The SEM directives distinguish mandatory internships during studies, especially for third-country students at foreign higher education institutions, as well as compulsory placements for students in Switzerland. These cases live in a different legal space from post-graduate stagiaire placements and are heavily document-driven: integral course requirement, duration caps, educational supervision, and usually a detailed training programme. 10

**Fallback** **to** **ordinary** **employment.** If the position is really just work with an "internship" label, Swiss authorities and cantons can treat it as ordinary employment. That means the ordinary third-country criteria revive, or the canton may simply reject the application because the role is not a genuine training stage. Geneva, Ticino and St. Gallen all provide official materials that reinforce this practical distinction.

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts

The core statutory backbone is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration** and the **OASA/** **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment.** SEM's general labour-market guidance identifies the baseline provisions for ordinary employment as Articles 18 to 24 FNIA, and identifies Article 30 FNIA as the source of relevant exceptions, including internships and training. The SEM directives then map the special route articles more precisely: Article 39 OASA for education with compulsory internship; Article 41 OASA for international exchange; Article 42 OASA for stagiaires; and Article 56 OASA for renewal logic. The directives also note that the Federal Council may conclude stagiaire agreements under Article 100(2)(e) FNIA read with Article 30(1)(g) FNIA. 20

For the **stagiaire** **/** **Young** **Professionals** route specifically, the most useful primary-text pointers are: Article 42 OASA as the route rule; Article 30 FNIA as the exception basis; and the bilateral exchange agreements listed by name and SR number on the SEM "Stage professionnel à l'étranger" page. That page currently lists the practically relevant treaty partners as South Africa, Argentina, Australia, Canada, Chile, the United States, Indonesia, Japan, Monaco, New Zealand, the Philippines, Russia, San Marino, Tunisia and Ukraine, and also identifies the relevant treaty instruments and entry-into-force dates. 21

There is also a recent **2025** **quota-related** **change** worth preserving in any internal authority pack. The 2025 OASA amendment inserted Article 42(2bis), allowing the SEM to issue short-stay permits where a stagiaire agreement does not itself fix maximum numbers, and Annex 1 set a specific annual quota of 300 for persons covered by the 11 October 2024 Switzerland-United States agreement. That matters because some older internal materials may not reflect the post-2024 U.S. treaty architecture. 22

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most operational federal guidance is SEM's **Young** **professionals** page and the accompanying **Instructions** **for young** **professionals.** Taken together, they confirm that the Swiss inward Young Professionals route is for people who have completed education and wish to broaden their professional and language skills in Switzerland for up to 18 months; that the process is simplified for agreement countries; that salary must track local and sectoral norms; and that the route is full-time, temporary and training-led. 23

**The Instructions** **for** **young** **professionals** are especially valuable because they function like an administrative checklist. They set out the nationality and age gates, the requirement of completed training and field match, the ban on part-time work and self-employment, the anti-staff-leasing rule, the 5% workforce cap, the requirement to remain outside Switzerland during the application process, the filing package, extension procedure, and change-of-job procedure. They also clarify that applications are filed by the trainee through the competent authority in the country of origin, not bysimply starting work in Switzerland and regularising later. 24

The SEM **standard employment agreement** and **official application form** are near-primary evidence of what the administration expects to see in a compliant file. The agreement requires a structured on-the-job programme to improve linguistic and professional skills, asks for substantiated details and a responsible person, and embeds the ordinary employment terms that still matter on this special route. The application form requires the contract with training programme, diplomas, CV, work certificates and passport copy. 25

For exchange and internship routes outside bilateral stagiaire treaties, the SEM directives are the controlling administrative source. They explain that Article 41 OASA covers bilaterally or multilaterally organised training, further-training or exchange programmes; that reciprocity and return are central; that only organisation-led applications are normally appropriate for international youth exchange; and that the application should include programme content and duration, proposed remmuneration, a training-responsibility confirmation, and guarantees for living and travel costs. 9

## Cantonal practice notes

**Geneva** draws one of the clearest official lines between a true training stage and disguised employment. Its student-stage page requires that the stage be an integral part of the studies, that it last less than half the total training programme, and that a permit be sought even if the stage is unpaid. Geneva's labour-market page then adds that recognised training or insertion stages are exempt from the Geneva minimum wage, whereas a stage assimilable to employment must comply with sector rules and cannot fall below the cantonal minimum wage. This is a useful authority pair for showing how Swiss practice differentiates educational placements from ordinary work. 26

**Basel-StadIt** takes a comparatively restrictive position for third-country internship permits. Its official page says that third-country nationals need a work permit and that such permits are available only for **intra-group** **or** **mandatory** **university** internships, with applications channelled through IAESTE or AIESEC. For a content system, that is a strong signal that "standalone corporate internship for a third-country national" is not a low-friction category in all cantons. 27

<!-- 5 -->

**Ticino** provides particularly helpful conceptual guidance. The official 2024 **Linee** **guida -Criteri per** **definire** **lo** **STAGE** say that stage should primarily function as an extension of the person's educational or reorientation path, and they treat items such as a three-party contract with a training institution, reintegration/new orientation, pre-apprenticeship periods, end-of-study placements, and institutionally required stages as relevant markers. The same guideline says that simple introduction to an already-acquired profession after an apprenticeship is not properly a stage. Ticino's student-stage notice also says that for third-country students, work may begin only after authorisation, wvhile compulsory curricular stages are not capped at 15 weekly hours in the way ordinary student ancillary work is. 28

**St. Gallen** and **Bern** add further practical markers. St. Gallen defines an internship as fixed-term gainful employment with an educational character, says it is not for testing the employee's suitability, requires proof that a school-related internship is recognised by the educational institution or supervisory authority, and states that lack of language knowledge does not justify an internship. Bern's standard form for third-country work starts requires, for students, a valid residence permit, a signed contract, and confirmation from the university that the internship is compulsory or that the university consents to the ancillary work; it also reiterates wage, accommodation and insurance duties. 29

#### Practical legal analysis

The federal and cantonal materials point to a usable **route-selection** **hierarchy.**

Where the applicant is a national of a current treaty country, has completed relevant education, and will work in the same profession or studied discipline, the **Young** **Professionals** **/** **stagiaire** route is normally the cleanest fit. The route is explicitly designed to broaden occupational and linguistic skills, is processed under an administrative framework that relaxes the ordinary labour-market situation filter, and comes with detailed federal forms that tell the drafter what must be shown. The trade-off is that the route is tightly policed on structure: full-time only, no staff leasing, no self-employment, finite duration, strong field match, and a real training programme. 30

If there is **nno** **bilateral stagiaire** **treaty** **hook,** but the placement is part of a reciprocal, organisation-led exchange programme, Article 41 OASA becomes the key authority. Herethe strongest files are not employer-led junior-hire files. They are programme files: sponsor organisation, reciprocity, defined exchange logic, temporary stay, identifiable educational content, cost coverage, and a clear return expectation. The SEM directives are nootably sceptical of private-person projects unless there is a well-justified exception and confidence that Article 41 principles are respected. 9

If the person is still in **education** and the internship is compulsory, the study-relatedroute is different again. For foreign-university students, the SEM directives require proof that the internshipis mandatory under the study rules and normally allow it only once per degree stage for a total of up to 12 months. Geneva's page adds the same integral-link requirement and says the placement must be less than half the overall course. These authorities are powerful because they convert a vague "internship" into a document-heavy educational pathway. 31

The **big** **distinction** **from** **ordinary** **employment** is therefore not remuneration alone, nor seniority, nor the use of the word "intern". It is whether the file shows a legally recognisable training architecture. Swiss materials consistently ask: Is the placement tied to a course, exchange programme, recognised treaty, or post-qualification perfectionnement? Is there a detailed educational programme? Is the role in the trained field? Is the duration proportionate? Is reciprocity or school supervision present where expected? If the answer is no, the label "internship" does not prevent reclassification into ordinary work logic.Geneva, Ticino and St. Gallen all reinforce that conclusion from different angles. 32

<!-- 6 -->

The route map also explains a recurring drafting error: trying to use a trainee rouute for what is actually **probationary** **recruitment.** St. Gallen says the internship does not serve to test a worker's suitability. Ticino says a post-apprenticeship introduction to the already-certified profession is not truly a stage. Geneva says a stage equivalent to employment is treated as employment for wage purposes. In substance, these sources all say the same thing: the more the trainee performs normal business output as a junior employee, and the less the file evidences a structured learning project,the weaker the trainee-route argument becomes. 33

A final analytical point is the **EU/EFTA** **distinction.** The SEM directives still list many older European stagiaire agreements, but they also say those agreements have no practical use after the end of the transitional periods because free movement rules provide better access. For an internal content system, that means route advice should not steer EU/EFTA users into the treaty-stagiaire pathway unless there is a very specific reason to discuss the historical agreements. 34

## Drafting cautions

When drafting for the **Young** **Professionals** **/** **stagiaire** route, the safest formula is to **state** **the** **route** **expressly** and then build the evidence around it. The contract and supporting letter should identify the case as a temporary perfectionnement stay under the bilateral trainee/Young Professionals framework, explain why the Swiss placement broadens professional and language skills, and anchor the work squarely in the applicant's trained profession or studied discipline. Avoid describing the role like an ordinary vacancy. 35

The training plan should be drafted as if an authority will ask whether it is legally real. SEM's forms expect specifics: job description, schedule, any courses, defined on-the-job learning content, and a named responsible person. A weak annex saying only "practical training" or "exposure to Swiss practice" is materially less persuasive than a timetable with learning modules, supervision checkpoints, language goals, and a short statement of expected competencies by the end of the stay. 36

The employment terms should remain recognisably compliant with Swviss labour-market protection. On treaty and study-related routes alike, salary and working conditions must track local and sectoral norms, and Geneva's materials show that if the "internship" is really employment, ordinary wage rules can bite. The safest drafting practice is therefore to pitch pay at defensible entry-level or sectoral rates and to ensure that working time, holidays, insurance, and travel-cost treatment are all explicitly documented. 37

Do not draft around the route's structural prohibitions. Young Professionals files should not suggest part-time work, self-employment, or staff leasing. Nor should the employer presentation imply that the trainee will rotate among third-party clients. If several trainees are employed, the file shoould quietly confirm compliance with the 5% workforce-cap logic. 38

**For compulsory** **study** **placements,** the school documents are often more important than the employer letter. The file should include current enrolment, a clear statement that the internship is an integral and compulsory part of the course, and enough information to show that the internship is shorter than half of the overall training and proportionate to the programme. If the case is pre-study, special care is needed because the SEM directives generally expect such practical requirements to be fulfilled abroad unless specific exception conditions can be shown. 18

**For Article** **41 exchange** cases, the sponsor organisation and reciprocity proof should not be left implicit. The file should identify the exchange organisation, explain the reciprocity mechanism, describe the programme architecture, confirm temporary stay and return logic, and include any guarantees for living and travel costs where relevant. Employer-only papers are usually not enough for a strong Article 41 file. 39

<!-- 7 -->

Finally, timeline language should be drafted conservatively. SEM's instructions say the applicant should remain outside Switzerland during the application process, that entry cannot take place before approval, and that registration after arrival is required before work begins. Internal content should therefore avoid wording that implies "start first, regularise later" or that treats the route as a same-country conversion process. 40
