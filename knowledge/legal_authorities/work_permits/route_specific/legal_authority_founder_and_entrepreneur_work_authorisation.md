## Executive legal summary

For third-country nationals, Swiss founder and entrepreneur cases sit principally under the self-employment route in Article 19 AIG rather than the employee route in Article 18 AIG. The third-country self-employment route is not entitlement-based: the applicant must show that the project serves Switzerland's overall economic interest, that the necessary financial and operating conditions are met, and that the quota, personal-qualification and housing requirements are satisfied. In addition, a positive cantonal pre-decision in Article 19 cases must be submitted to SEM for approval. 2

Swiss immigration law separates immigration classification from company-law form. SEM's directives state expressly that the migration-law definition of self-employment is not identical to social-security, employment-law or other definitions, and that job title, employment contract, or even a commercial-register entry with sole signatory power do not, by themselves, decide whether a founder is self-employed or employed. The decisive questions are control, subordination, ownership and entrepreneurial risk. The Federal AdministrativeCourt has likewise treated a founder who economically controlled the company as self-employed notwithstanding the existence of an employment contract. 3

The practical centre of gravity in third-country founder cases is business substance. SEM requires a business plan addressing the proposed activity, financing, market prospects, staff development and recruitment, planned investment, turnover and expected profit. Existing group links and binding financing commitments from outside investors must also be disclosed. Initial permits for new-business/ self-employment cases should in principle be limited to a mnaximum of two years, and renewal is tied to timely achievement of the promised positive economic effects; SEM's directives expressly contemplate refusal of extension where business-plan targets are not met. 4

For EU/EFTA nationals, the legal position is materially different. Under Annex I AFMP, a self-employed national of a contracting party is entitled to a residence permit of at least five years if he or she establishes, or wishes to establish, a genuine self-employed activity; SEM's public factsheets and directives frame this as a B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years on proof of actual self-employment.But this route is stilIl evidence-heavy: the activity must be real and effective, not merely marginal or formal, and the authorities may ask for fresh proof during the permit period if there is serious doubt about the genuineness or intensity of the activity or the existence of regular income. 5

The recurring risk pattern is therefore straightforward. Weak founder cases usually fail because they rely on incorporation formalities instead of immigration-law substance, present speculative or stale business plans, cannot show Swiss labour-market or regional-economic benefit, do not evidence real financing, or cannot explain why this specific founder is professionally fitted and genuinely needed for the proposed business. That risk is visible both in SEM guidance and in the case law cited below. 6

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts.

The core federal gateway is the AIG/FNIA and the VZAE/OASA, as identified on SEM's legal-foundations page. In structure, Article 11 AIG imposes the baseline authorisation requirement for gainful activity; Article 18 AIG applies to employed work; Article 19 AIG applies to self-employment; Article 20 AIG covers quota power; Article 23 AIG addresses personal qualifications; Article 24 AIG concerns adequate housing; and the ZV-EJPD identifies Article 19 self-employment pre-decisions for mandatory SEM approval. For EU/EFTA nationals, the controlling treaty layer is Annex I AFMP, especially Article 12 on residence for self-employed persons and Article 15 on equal treatment. 23

The practical consequence of the text structure matters. Article 18 AIG, as reproduced in case material, expressly links employed cases to the chain of conditions in Articles 20 to 25 AIG, including employer-led labour-market control. Article 19 AIG, by contrast, is built around economic interest, business prerequisites and compliance with Articles 20 and 23 to 25 AIG. That textual difference is why founder cases turn first on classification: once the facts point to genuine self-employment, the file moves away from the ordinary employer route and into the narrower Article 19 founder logic. This is a strong textual inference from the statutory structure and from SEM's implementing guidance. 24

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials.

For third-country cases, the most important practical instrumnent is SEM's AIG guidance, Chapter 4 on residence with employment, and in particular section 4.7.2 on self-employment and new business creation. This is the source that sets out the immigration-law definition of self-employment, the founder-control/risk factors, the economic-interest indicators, the initial two-year permit approach, and the expected contents of the business plan. SEM's public pages on third-country labour-market admission, procedure and application documents translate those principles into workflow and documentary requirements. 25

For EU/EFTA cases, the operational materials are SEM's AFMP factsheets, AFMP FAQ, and the OLCP directives. Together theystate that self-employed EU/EFTA nationals receive a B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years on proof of actual self-employment, that a change into self-employment is reportable and requires a new permit, and that the authorities may continue to test whether the activity is real, intense and income-generating enough to sustain the applicant. The OLCP directives are especially valuable because they explain what evidence counts: legal creation of the undertaking, accounting records, orders, proof of effective existence, regular income and independence from third-party direction. 26

The quota position is current and should be built into internal route notes for third-country files.The Federal Council maintained the 2026 national ceilings at 4,500 B permits and 4,000 L permits foor third-country workers, with separate UK contingents; the same federal communication notes that the relevant VZAE amendment entered into force on 1 January 2026. 27

## Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal practice does not replace federal law, but it materially affects packaging, sequencing and documentary emphasis. Zürich's guidance is especially useful because it states the third-country self-employment conditions in operational terms: the activity must be in Switzerland's overall economic interest, it must be expected to generate enough income to cover business and living costs, sufficient start-up capital must exist, and a business plan or projected balance sheet is mandatory; rental or loan documents are also helpful. Zürich's EU/EFTA guidance then shows what "actual self-employment" means in practice: proof of an active Swiss undertaking or establishment, SVA recognition, and income/ asset evidence. 28

Vaud's practice note is document-rich and therefore useful for template design. It describes the third-country independent-activity route as dependent on viability, likely durable positive effects for the Swiss economy,fit between the applicant's qualifications and the business activity, appropriate housing, and quota availability. For the dossier, Vaud asks for form 1350, a motivation letter describing the business, branch, projected activity, clientèle and competition study, CV and diplomas, passport copy, incorporation or commercial-register documents, and a three-year operating plan covering organisation, staffing and finances. 29

Geneva's materials are among the clearest for founder-specific workflow. For third-country entrepreneurs, Geneva says the application should be filed six to eight weeks before the planned start date and that independent activity may only begin once the work authorisation has been granted. Geneva also says that, as a rule, the authority will first issue a short-stay permit, and that the OCIRT will test Swiss economic interest, sufficient autonomous income, high qualifications, business/financial conditions and quota availability before the matter goes to SEM. It specifically recommends prior contact with the cantonal economy and innovation office to assess the project's fit with Geneva's economic context. 30

Geneva's EU/EFTA note is equally practical. It states that the applicant must prove actual independent activity, that the permit is not tied to a canton or specific activity and is valid throughout Switzerland for five years, and that a complete file should include proof of the project or business plan and proof of the professional address. Geneva also allows the activity to begin once the complete file has been transmitted, except where the profession is regulated and an additional licence is needed. 31

Basel-Stadt is useful because it makes two points explicit. First, some people do not need an additional self-employment authorisation at all, including certain C-permit holders and certain spouses. Secondly, where an application is needed, Basel requires overall economic interest, financial/business prerequisites and personal prerequisites, and it offers both a dedicated self-employment application and a business-plan checklist. Bern's migration service is procedurally plainer, but it confirms that third-country self-employed persons need permission from the first working day and that extensions usually still require labour-market and, depending on the case, SEM approval. Ticino's population service, by contrast, largely channels applicants back to SEM material rather than restating detailed founder criteria on its own pages. 32

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

### G. Practical legal analysis.

The most important legal move in a Swiss founder file is to classify the activity correctly before pleading the merits. If the applicant is truly acting on own account, controls the business and bears

<!-- 5 -->

entrepreneurial risk, Article 19 AIG is the route. If the applicant is in substance being engaged by a Swiss company as an employee, then Article 18 AIG applies, with the employer-led labour-market and wage conditions that go with it. Cases involving founder-CEOs are not immune from reclassification: the authorities and courts look through the corporate form to the real governance and risk position. 33

Economic-interest pleading should be specific, quantified and Swiss-facing. SEM's own indicators are diversification of the regional economy, preservation or creation of multiple jobs for local workers, substantial investment and new mandates for the Swiss economy; for start-ups, innovation and transfer of academic know-how also matter. By contrast, case material shows that modest or poorly evidenced operations, vague future hopes, or projections without reliable market support will not suffice. In C-7286/2008, the founder's profile as a musician did not convincingly establish fit for the management role she claimed in a newly founded concert business; in F-45/2016, an outdated business plan and non-binding offers failed to show a sustainable labour-market benefit; in F-2320/2023, the court was not persuaded by loosely substantiated restructuring narratives or by profits alone as proof of a positive long-term prognosis. 6

Funding and business substance need to be pleaded as present facts, not future aspirations. SEM wants operating and financial prerequisites for the new enterprise, and Zürich and Geneva both ask for practical proof of start capital, operating documents and real business infrastructure. For EU/EFTA cases, the OLCP directives make the same point from a different angle: the creation of the undertaking must be coupled with effective economic activity, accounting records or orders, regular income and a business that can support the person without recourse to social assistance. The Federal Supreme Court's case law also shows that social-security recognition is relevant but not exhaustive evidence; other forms of proof must remain open. 34

Role necessity in founder cases is usually indirect rather than formulated as a separate statutory test. In practice, it appears through personal qualifications, business fit and the plausibility that this founder's Swiss presence is integral to the project's success. Courts have treated mismatches between the applicant's real professional profile and the proposed managerial role as damaging. The better view is that every founder file should explain not only why the business should exist in Switzerland, but why this person must be the person running it from Switzerland at this stage. 35

Renewal strategy should be treated as part of the initial drafting, not as a later compliance problem. Because SEM's directives favour an initial permit of up to two years for start-up/build-out phases and allow extension to be refused when the promised effects do not materialise, the initial file shouldset measurable, defensible milestones which can later be evidenced. This is particularly important for content automation: the system should expect both an "entry file" and a future "renewal evidence bundle". 36
