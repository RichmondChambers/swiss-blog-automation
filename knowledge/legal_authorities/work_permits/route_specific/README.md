# Route-Specific Swiss Work Permit Legal Authority Packs

This folder contains route-specific legal authority packs for the Swiss work permits and employer sponsorship blog automation workflow.

Route-specific packs are used by `generate_and_publish.py` to ground legal analysis, drafting and validation for blog posts that focus on a specific Swiss work permit route, applicant category, employment model or assignment structure.

## Current Route-Specific Packs

### `legal_authority_non_eu_local_hire_work_permit.md`

This pack should cover non-EU local hire work permits, including employer sponsorship, labour-market testing, priority rules, salary and local conditions, professional qualifications, quotas, cantonal and federal procedure and refusal risks.

### `legal_authority_senior_and_specialist_hires.md`

This pack should cover senior and specialist hires, including management or specialist status, qualifications, experience, salary, economic interest, employer evidence and risks where the role is not sufficiently senior or specialised.

### `legal_authority_intra_company_transfer.md`

This pack should cover intra-company transfers, including group-company relationship, prior employment abroad, assignment purpose, Swiss host entity, employment or secondment structure, salary and local conditions and permit duration.

### `legal_authority_short_term_assignment_and_project_based_work.md`

This pack should cover short-term assignments and project-based work, including temporary deployment, project evidence, assignment duration, employer structure, permit or notification choice and risks where the project becomes ordinary local employment.

### `legal_authority_posted_worker_and_notification_route.md`

This pack should cover posted workers and notification routes, including cross-border service provision, employer abroad, Swiss client or host, permitted duration, notification requirements, posted-worker compliance and limits of the notification procedure.

### `legal_authority_frontier_worker_g_permit.md`

This pack should cover frontier worker G permits, including residence abroad, cross-border work, return frequency, EU / EFTA and third-country distinctions, employer evidence and canton-specific issues.

### `legal_authority_trainee_and_stagiaire.md`

This pack should cover trainees and stagiaires, including training purpose, eligible schemes, duration, employment conditions, bilateral arrangements where relevant and risks where the placement is ordinary employment.

### `legal_authority_graduate_switch_from_study_to_work.md`

This pack should cover graduates switching from study to work, including Swiss study history, post-study job search, labour-market access, specialist role, employer sponsorship and timing risks.

### `legal_authority_remote_work_and_foreign_employer_fact_patterns.md`

This pack should cover remote work and foreign-employer fact patterns, including Swiss residence with foreign employment, place-of-work analysis, payroll and social security issues, employer presence, work authorisation risk and drafting cautions.

### `legal_authority_founder_and_entrepreneur_work_authorisation.md`

This pack should cover founder and entrepreneur work authorisation, including founder role, company formation, employment or self-employment structure, economic interest, business plan, funding, job creation, canton discretion and renewal risk.

## Purpose of the Route-Specific Folder

Use this folder for packs focused on a specific Swiss work permit route, applicant category or employment structure.

Route-specific packs should identify the legal basis, applicant category, employer or host structure, eligibility conditions, entitlement or discretion, procedure, evidence, risks and drafting cautions for the specific route.

## Legal Framework to Cover

Route-specific work permit packs may need to address the relationship between:

the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act (LEI / AIG); the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment (OASA / VZAE); the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP / FZA), where relevant; SEM Directives and official procedure guidance; cantonal labour-market and migration authority practice; employer sponsorship; labour-market testing; salary and local conditions; quotas; notification procedure; posted-worker rules; intra-company transfer structures; frontier worker rules; trainee arrangements; graduate transition rules; entrepreneur and founder work authorisation; renewal and refusal practice.

## Recommended Structure for Route-Specific Packs

Each route-specific pack should, where possible, include:

an executive legal summary; a structural overview of the route; a table of legal propositions; primary legal texts; official guidance and administrative materials; key case law, where relevant; cantonal practice notes, where relevant; practical legal analysis; drafting cautions; source list; open questions or limitations.

## Legal Proposition Categories

Legal propositions should distinguish clearly between:

entitlement; eligibility only; discretion; facilitation; guidance; procedure; evidential practice; labour-market practice; cantonal practice; unresolved or practice-sensitive points.

Do not describe guidance as law. Do not describe route eligibility as automatic approval. Do not describe notification routes as substitutes for work permits where the facts require authorisation. Do not describe cantonal practice as uniform Swiss law.

## Route-Specific Analysis Points

Route-specific work permit authority packs should preserve the distinctions that determine the legal analysis, including:

applicant nationality; current residence status; employer or host entity; foreign employer; Swiss employer; proposed role; assignment structure; employment contract; secondment letter; service agreement; salary; local employment conditions; qualifications; experience; labour-market evidence; quota availability; duration; place of work; canton; federal approval; permit type; family implications; renewal; change of employer; refusal grounds; refiling or appeal strategy.

## Relationship with Other Folders

Use `core/` for foundational framework points that explain the overall Swiss work permit and employer sponsorship system.

Use `broad/` for recurring thematic issues that apply across several work permit routes, such as non-EU admission criteria, salary and local conditions, quotas, short-term assignments, cantonal practice, renewals and refusals.

Use `route_specific/` for packs focused on a specific defined work permit route or employment model.

Use `issue_specific/` for narrower risk issues or complex sub-categories that need deeper treatment, such as entrepreneur business evidence, weak labour-market evidence, remote work risk or renewal after changed circumstances.

## Terminology

Use both English and Swiss legal references where useful.

Preferred public-facing terminology includes:

Swiss work permit; work authorisation; employer sponsorship; non-EU local hire; senior specialist; intra-company transfer; short-term assignment; project-based work; posted worker; notification procedure; frontier worker G permit; trainee; stagiaire; graduate switch from study to work; remote work; foreign employer; founder work authorisation; entrepreneur work permit; salary and local employment conditions; labour-market test; quota; LEI / AIG for the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act; OASA / VZAE for the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment; AFMP / FZA for the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons; SEM Directives for SEM’s immigration directives.

Avoid terminology that suggests all work activity can be regularised through the same route.

## Important Drafting Cautions

Route-specific packs should not flatten distinct work authorisation routes into a single Swiss work permit test.

The analysis usually depends on nationality, route, employer structure, role, salary, labour-market evidence, quota availability, duration, canton, place of work, assignment model and procedural posture.

Authority packs in this folder should help prevent public blog posts from overstating eligibility, treating notification as equivalent to authorisation, or implying that a route designed for one employment model can safely be used for another.
