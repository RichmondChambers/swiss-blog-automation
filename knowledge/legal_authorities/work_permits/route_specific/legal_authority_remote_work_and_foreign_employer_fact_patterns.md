# Swiss legal authority pack on remote work from Switzerland for foreign employers

## Overview

## Executive legal summary

Swiss federal law does not create a standalone statutory category called "remote work", "telework", or "digital nomad work" for immigration purposes. The clearest federal source directly addressing the issue is a State Secretariat for Migration circular of 5 April 2022 on home-office activity,which expressly analyses remote work from Switzerland for a foreign employer under both the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the third-country regime. Outside that circular, the statutory framework remains the ordinary law of gainful employment, non-gainful residence, cross-border services and commuter permits. The analysis therefore has to be done by matching each fact pattern to an existing legal route rather than by invoking a dedicated remote-work route. 1

For EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM circular states that a person working from Switzerland in home office for an employer seated abroad is not a "worker in Switzerland" under the free-movement agreement, because worker status in Article 6 Annex I AFMP presupposes an employer in the host state. Where there is no link to the Swiss labour market or local econommy and no Swiss customer-facing activity, SEM's stated practice is to classify such persons as **non-gainfully** **active** under Article 24 Annex I AFMP. In that scenario, the foreign salary may be counted as part of the person's sufficient financial means, and mere physical presence in Switzerland with a laptop does not by itself create Swiss worker status. For stays over 90 days, the person must register as non-gainfully active and show sufficient means and health insurance. 2

For third-country nationals, the starting point is different and stricter. Article 11 FNIA requires a permit for anyone wishing to work in Switzerland, regardless of duration, and Article 1a OASA defines salaried employment broadly to include work for an employer seated **either** in **Switzerland** **or abroad,** regardless of whether pay is made in Switzerland or abroad and even if the work is only occasional or temporary. The 2026 SEM directives also emphasise that the notion of gainful employment is deliberately broad, and that the key question is whether the activity influences the Swiss labour market. 3

Even so, the 2022 SEM circular introduces an important qualification for third-country nationals in pure foreign-employer remote-work cases. It says that a newly arriving third-country national who wants to live in Switzerland and work in home office for an employer abroad **without** **influence** **on** **the** **Swiss** **labour market** should be handled under a **residence permit without gainful employment** such as family reunification or education/training, rather than under a work-authorisation route. Bycontrast, if the home-office activity has an effect onthe Swiss labour market, SEM says it is permit-requiring employment subject to the ordinary labour-market tests. The same circular records that, in a cantonal survey, the cantons had not granted new work-and-residence permits to newly arriving third-country nationals for exclusively home-office activity. 4

<!-- 1 -->

That creates the central drafting point for internal use: **foreign** **employer** and **remote** **work** do not answer the Swiss immigration question by themselves. The decisive issues are nationality regime, physical presence in Switzerland, whether the person is trying to reside in Switzerland,whether the activity has a Swiss labour-market nexus, and whether the case is really remote work at all, or instead a posting, cross-border service, commuter case, or self-employment case. In several important areas, especially for third-country nationals with no independent residence basis, the official materials are still thin and the correct answer depends on reasoned legal inference rather than an express rule. 5

This pack is orgJanised around the fact patterns that most often create confusion:

·Third-country nationals already in Switzerland who switch to, or continue with, a foreign employer.

## Federal authorities

### Primary legal texts

The statutory backbone is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. Article 11 FNIA requires a permit for any foreign national who wishes to work in Switzerland, irrespective of duration. The same article defines gainful employment broadly as any salaried or self-employed activity normally carried out for payment, even where no payment is actually made, and provides that in salaried cases the employer submits the application. For third-country local employment, Articles 18, 21 and 22 add the general-economic-interest requirement, labour-market priority and Swiss customary pay and employment conditions. Article 30 allows derogations only in listed exceptional situations. 15

The same Act separately regulates cross-border commuters and cross-border services. Article 25 FNIA sets the commuter route; Article 26 allows admission for temporary cross-border services if the activity is in the general interests of the economy and applies labour-market conditions mutatis mutandis. Articles 35 and 39 govern the commuter permit and job-change rules, while Article 40 confirms that permits under Articles 32-39 are granted by the cantons, with the Confederation retaining quota, derogation and approval powers. 16

The Ordinance on Admission, Residenceand Employment is especially important for remote-work analysis. Article 1a states that salaried employment includes any activity for an employer with a seat in Switzerland **or abroad,** regardless of where salary is paid and regardless of whether the work is only hourly, daily or temporary. Article 3 defines cross-border service provision as a time-limited service in Switzerland under a contractual relationship by a person or enterprise domiciled abroad. Article 4assigns the decision whether an activity counts as gainful employment to the cantonaI labour-market authority, with doubtful cases to be referred to SEM. Articles 9 and 10 regulate permit-free and registered stays without gainful employment; Articles 12 and 14 regulate short-term work, including the 8-day rule for cross-border work and postings by foreign employers.8

The 2026 SEM directives are useful because they explain how the authorities currently read those statutory concepts. They say the concept of gainful employment is to be interpreted **widely** for controlled admission policy, and that the real question is not whether the person needs the income to live in Switzerland, but whether the activity is in principle one performed for remuneration on the Swiss labour market. They add that the decisive assessment is whether the activity has an effect on the Swiss labour market, and that the cantonal labour-market authority decides the issue in the first instance. They alsoexplain the category of "gainful employment without taking up a local post", which includes activity for an employer seated abroad and is generally permit-free only up to 8 days per calendar year in the cross-border service context. 17

For EU/EFTA nationals, the treaty framework is the AFMP. The SEM circular applies Article 6 Annex I AFMP for workers and Article 24 Annex I AFMP for persons without gainful activity. Public federal factsheets then operationalise Article 24 by requiring sufficient means and comprehensive health insurance, with registration required for stays longer than 90 days. 2

### Official guidance / administrative materials

The most important administrative authority is the SEM circular of 5 April 2022, because it is the only federal source located that deals squarely with home-office activity from Switzerland for a foreign employer. It was issued precisely because Covid-era and post-Covid work patterns led cantons, employers and legal advisers to ask for guidance on the immigration classification of persons living in Switzerland while working remotely for foreign employers. The circular expressly addresses both EU/ EFTA and third-country cases. 9

<!-- 4 -->

For EU/EFTA nationals, the circular states that a foreign employer seated outside Switzerland is not an "employer of the host state" for Article 6 Annex I AFMP purposes. It therefore concludes that an EU/EFTA national who works in Switzerland in home office for an employer seated abroad is not a Swiss "worker" under the AFMP. If the person remains integrated into the foreign work organisation, has no link to the Swiss labour market or local economy, and does not perform Swiss-customer acquisition or support, SEM's stated constant practice is to treat the person as non-gainfully active under Article 24 Annex I AFMP. The circular also states that the salary earned from the foreign employer counts when assessing sufficient financial means, and that mere physical presence in Switzerland does not create worker status in Switzerland. 9

For third-country nationals, the same circular explains that the analysis is different because Article 1a OASA uses an expanded employer concept that includes employers seated abroad. It therefore says that any home-office work with influence on the Swiss labour market is permit-requiring employment under Article 11 FNIA regardless of duration. It then divides cases functionally: existing residents with a permit that already authorises employment and professional mobility can work in home office for a Swiss or foreign employer when the activity affects the Swiss labour market; persons switching from a Swiss-employment setup to a foreign employer without a Swiss establishment and without Swiss labour-market impact are treated as ANOBAG and need a **new** **non-gainful** residence basis; and new admissions for pure foreign-employer home-office activity without Swiss labour-market impact must likewise occur through a **permit** **without gainful employment,** such as family reunification or education/training. The circular also records that the cantons surveyed had not issued work-and-residence permits to newly arrivingthird-country nationals for exclusively home-office activity. 9

The SEM factsheet on residence without gainful activity and the ch.ch guidance are the main supporting federal materials for the "non-gainful residence" route. They confirm that EU/EFTA nationals can reside in Switzerland without gainful employment if they have sufficient means and adequate insurance, and that non-EUJ/EFTA nationals who wish to settle in Switzerland without taking up gainful employment must apply to the cantonal authorities before arrival and, depending on nationality, obtain a visa from the Swiss representation abroad. The same ch.ch page adds that visa-free non-EU/EFTA nationals may remain up to three months without a permit only if they are **not working** in **Switzerland.** 18

The SEM short-term notification procedure pages matter because they show where "foreign employer" cases are legally treated as postings or service provision rather than as pure remote work. They confirm that the notification route is available, in principle, for EU/EFTA nationals employed by a Swiss company for up to three months, posted workers from an EU/EFTA company regardless of nationality, and EU/ EFTA self-employed service providers. They also confirm that not every foreign-employer scenario fits that route. 12

The SEM and federal social-insurance authorities separately warn that the definitions used in immigration law, social security and tax law are not the same. The 2022 circular says the immigration classification of a home-office worker does not directly decide the social-security or tax analysis. Federal social-insurance guidance then shows that cross-border telework now has its own coordination rules, including arrangements allowving up to 50% telework in certain state combinations without changing the applicable social-security system. Those materials are important not because they decide work authorisation, but because they show why tax or social-security advice must not be used as an immigration shortcut. 19

<!-- 5 -->

For UK nationals, official federal guidance states that from 1 January 2021 newly arriving UK nationals are generally treated as third-country nationals for Swiss employment purposes, unless they fall within the citizens' rights agreement. That matters because many remote-work questions still assume AFMP treatment for UK citizens when the official position is now the third-country regime. 20

## Cantonal and route-level notes

## Cantonal practice notes

Publicly available cantonal material is much thinner than the federal circular for remote-work-specific scenarios. The clearest cantonal sources located largely confirm the route structure rather than creating new rules. In other words, cantonal public practice largely channels cases into standard categories such as local hire, posting, self-employment, commuter permits, or non-gainful residence. 21

Bern's migration service publishes a useful factsheet for EU/EFTA non-gainful residence under Article 24Annex I AFMP. It states that EU/EFTA nationals who do not work have a right to a residence permit if they have sufficient means, and that the permit may be revoked or not renewed if the financial-means condition later ceases to be met, for example if the person receives social assistance or supplementary benefits. That is a practical cantonal confirmation of how the EU/EFTA foreign-employer remote-work scenario is expected to be housed in ordinary residence practice. 22

Zurich's labour-market guidance for third-country nationals is valuable because it shows the canton's pubolic route map. It separates local employment under Article 18 FNIA, cross-border services/postings under Article 26 FNIA, and self-employment under Article 19 FNIA. It states that third-country nationals need a permit from day one, that there is no legal entitlement to a permit, and that first permits are quota-limited. For local employment it requires an employer application, proof of labour-market priority, Swiss pay and conditions, and appropriate qualifications. For postings it distinguishes internal and external assignments and lists the required posting agreements, project contracts and Swiss employment-condition protections. That public structure reinforces the point that a remote-work case must be consciously classified; it cannot be left as an undefined "foreign employer" scenario. 23

Basel-Stadt's public guidance on commuter permits confirms that commuter status is for persons who **live** **abroad** **and** **work** in **Switzerland,** not for residents in Switzerland working abroad. It states that EU/EFTA commuters may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA and work anywhere in Switzerland, usually returning daily or at least weekly, and that for assignments of up to 90 days the federal online notification procedure is available. For third-country nationals it repeats the permanent-residence-in-neighbouring-state and six-month-border-zone requirements, along with labour-market conditions. 24

Geneva's public business portal on foreign labour separates Swiss employer cases, commuter cases, self-employment cases, and postings/service provision. For non-EU/EFTA enterprises or independents wishing to post staff into Geneva or offer services there, the canton requires an authorisation, applies the Swiss-economic-interest test, qualification requirements, quotas and Swiss salary conditions, and says the activity may not begin until the permit is granted. The posted worker or independent must reside in Switzerland during the mission. That is useful for distinguishing a **real Swiss** **mission** **or** **service** **contract** from a person who merely resides in Switzerland while doing back-office work for a foreign employer. 25

Ticino has a limited-context but still relevant public statement on its Ukraine crisis page for holders of protection status S: if they work by telework for a foreign employer and the activity has no reference or link to Switzerland, no work authorisation is required. Because that statement is embedded in a protection-S information page, it should not be generalised beyond its context. Still, it shows that at least one canton publicly uses the same Swiss-link analysis in a special-status setting. 26

<!-- 6 -->

One further cantonal caution: Geneva's recent public telework pages for frontier workers mainly concern **tax** **reporting** **and** **withholding** rather than immigration classification. They are useful for compliance planning, but they do not answer the work-authorisationquestion and should not be cited as if they did. 27

## Practical legal analysis

The clearest route is the EU/EFTA person who moves to Switzerland but continues to work only for an employer seated abroad, with no Swiss customers and no Swiss labour-market link. That scenario is expressly addressed by SEM. The person is not treated as a Swiss "worker" under the AFMP, but as non-gainfullyactive under Article 24 Annex I AFMP. For stays over 90 days, the immigration analysis therefore turns on sufficient means and insurance, not on obtaining a Swiss work permit as a worker. This is one of the few remote-work conclusions the official materials state directly rather than merely imply.2

The position becomes more difficult when Swiss links appear. SEM's EU/EFTA analysis depends on the absence of a connection to the Swiss labour market and the absence of Swiss customer support or acquisition. Once the person begins dealing with the Swiss market, the case can stop looking like pure remote work and begin to look like service provision, local economic activity, or a Swiss-labour-market case. In that zone, the official materials do not give a bright-line percentage test for how much Swiss activity is too much; the analysis becomes fact-sensitive. That is an area where legal inference and careful drafting are necessary. 9

For third-country nationals already lawfully in Switzerland, the analysis is route-dependent.If the person already holds a permit with work authorisation and professional mobility, the 2022 circular indicates that home-office work for a Swiss or foreign employer with Swiss labour-market impact can be carried out without an additional permit, because the person already has the relevant employment entitlement. But if a person who is in Switzerland on an employment-based permit switches to a foreign employer without a Swiss establishment and without Swiss labour-market impact, SEM says the case should be recast as ANOBAG and the person needs a **new** **permit** **without gainful employment,by** way of purpose change. That is a powerful reminder that ANOBAG is not itself an immigration status. 9

For a newly arriving third-country national who wants to settle in Switzerland while working only for a foreign employer, the official materials do **not** provide a freestanding "remote worker" admission route. The 2022 circular says that if the activity affects the Swiss labour market, the ordinary work-permit regime applies. That means an employer application, general economic interest, labour-market priority, Swiss customary pay and conditions, personal qualifications and, where applicable, quotas. If the activity does **not** affect the Swiss labour market, the circular says admission should occur within a **permit without gainful employment** such as family reunification or education/training, and not via a work-authorisation file. The circular does not create a separate residence ground for persons whose only purpose is to live in SwNitzerland while working remotely for a foreign employer, and the public "stay without work" materials also channel non-working residence into the ordinary categories of students, retirees and persons of private means. The conclusion that there is no clearly supported standalone route for that fact pattern is therefore a **reasoned** **inference,** albeit a strong one, from the official materials reviewed. 28

<!-- 7 -->

Cross-border service and posting cases should not be folded into the remote-work analysis.Swiss law already has a detailed framework for foreign employers sending staff into Switzerland or for foreign service providers performing a temporary contract in Switzerland. The ordinance defines cross-border services, applies the 8-day threshold, imposes day-one permit rules in sensitive sectors, and the federal notification pages set out the 90-days-per-calendar-year regime for eligible EU/EFTA cases. Geneva and Zurich public practice materials reinforce that a true service mission into Switzerland is a normal posted-worker or cross-border-service case, not a digital-nomad case. 29

Cross-border commuter cases are also distinct. Where the person lives abroad and works in Switzerland, the legal route is the commuter permit or, for short eligible assignments, the notification procedure. Basel-Stadt and Bern both present the commuter regime in those terms. By contrast,the question of a Swiss-based person working from abroad for a Swiss employer is not directly answered by the Swiss immigration materials gathered here; that scenario typically shifts attention to the law of the state where the work is physically performed, while Swiss tax and social-security coordination questions may still arise. Any drafting on that inverse scenario should therefore state expressly that the point is only partially covered by the Swiss immigration authorities reviewed in this pack. 30

Short-stay visitor scenarios require particular care. Swiss law and public guidance distinguish permit-free non-gainful stays from work and from long stays requiring authorisation. Non-EU/EFTA visitors who do not need a visa may stay up to three months without a residence permit if they are not working in Switzerland, and national D visas are used for long stays over three months that are subject to authorisation, such as gainful employment or famnily reunification. What the reviewed official materials do not squarely answer is whether a third-country visitor on a tourist or visa-free stay may incidentally answer emails or continue some remote work for a foreign employer while physically in Switzerland, where the activity has no Swiss labour-market link. Because the direct federal circular discusses settlement scenarios and non-gainful residence routes, not tourism, the safest internal drafting is to present this as an **open** **interpretive** **issue** rather than as an approved practice. 31

## Drafting and bibliography

## Drafting cautions

The firstcaution is to separate the legal regimes at the outset. A statement that is broadly correct for an EU/EFTA national under the AFMP can be wrong for a third-country national under the FNJIA and OASA, because the third-country rules expressly treat work for an employer seated abroad as salaried employment. UK nationals should also be split between acquired-rights cases and post-2021 new entrants. 32

The second caution is to avoid treating "foreign employer" as a complete answer. The key factual discriminator in the reviewed Swiss materials is whether the activity has a **Swiss** **labour-market effect.** Swiss customer acquisition, Swiss client servicing, Swiss project delivery, or a temporary Swiss mission can move a case out of the "pure remote work" box and into a local-hire, service-provider, posting or self-employment route. 33

The third caution is not to confuse immigration law with tax, employment lawv or social insurance. The SEM circular says expressly that the immigration classification of home-office work does not directly determine the tax or social-security classification, and federal social-insurance guidance now has its own telework arrangements. Internal content should therefore avoid formulas such as "up to 50% telework is fine", unless it is specifically discussing social-security coordination rather than work authorisation. 34

<!-- 8 -->

The fourth caution is to avoid presenting ANOBAG as if it were an immigration permission. It is a social-insurance concept used for workers in Switzerland whose employer is not contribution-liable in Switzerland. In the SEM circular, ANOBAG appears as a description of a foreign-employer setup, but the immigration consequence is still that the person may need a different **residence** basis without gainful employment.9

The fifth caution is to avoid saying that Switzerland has a dedicated digital-nomad visa or digital-nomad residence route. No such dedicated federal route was identified in the official materials reviewed. The official pages instead route long stays into standard categories such as gainful employment, non-gainful residence, family reunification and study. That is an **inference** **from** **the** **materials** **reviewed,** not an express statutory prohibition phrased in those words. 35

The sixth caution is evidential. Because official remote-work materials are still thin, drafting should clearly identify whether a proposition is grounded in an express source, in stated administrative practice, or in reasoned inference from the existing categories. In this area, overstating certainty is a bigger risk than understating it. The biggest open points are incidental remote work during short tourist stays, hybrid cases with some Swiss market contact, and scenarios where a third-country national has no independent residence ground other than the wish to live in Switzerland while working for a foreign employer. 36
