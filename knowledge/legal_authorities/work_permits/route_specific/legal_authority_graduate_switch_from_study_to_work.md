## Executive legal summary

**For third-country** **nationals** who complete studies in Switzerland, the core federal route is **not** a broad "post-study open work" permission. The key provision is **Article** **21(3) AIG/FNIA,** which allows a foreign national with a **Swiss higher-education qualification** to be admitted, **by derogation from the** **ordinary Swiss/EU/EFTA precedence test,** if the intended employment is of **high scientific or** **economic** **interest.** The same provision now also states that such persons are admitted for **six** **months** **after completing education or training in Switzerland** in order to find corresponding employment. Separately, **Article** **27(3) AIG** provides that continued stay after comnpletion or discontinuation of studies is governed by the **general admission** **requirements,** which means the person must move off pure student status and onto the relevant post-study or work route rather than simply remain on a study permit. 1

That graduate route is **narrower than many common-law "graduate visa" models.** Federal guidance makes clear that the graduate exception removes only the **precedence** requirement; graduates remain subject to the **other labour-admission conditions in Article 20 ff. AIG,** and the SEM's 2026 directives emphasise that the comnpleted training must match the job profile and that **generalist** **or study-remote** roles-such as routine administrative work without a qualified link to the degree-remain excluded. The SEM also explains that evidence of sectoral need may be drawn from labour-shortage indicators and job-registration data. 2

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the route is fundamentally different because it is governed chiefly by the **Agreement** on **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** and the implementing residence-permit regime for EU/ EFTA citizens. EU/EFTA nationals may work in Swvitzerland with a valid employment contract, must register with the commune within **14** **days** of arrival and **before** **starting** **work,** and short employment of less than **three** **months** is generally handled through the **notification** **procedure** rather than a permit. EU/EFTA jobseekers may receive an **L** **EU/EFTA** **permit** **without gainful employment,** but that practice does not create an entitlement to social assistance. 3

Employer involvement is central on the **third-country** side. SEM and cantonal materials consistently state that the **employer files the relevant work-authorisation application,** including for student side work and for ordinary third-country work admissions. In the standard third-country route the employer must evidence labour-market searches, salary compliance and qualifications; in the **Swiss-graduate** **exception,** the employer should instead focus on proving the **degree-linked** nature of the role and its **high scientific or economic interest.** 4

A major drafting caution is that the much-discussed reform that would have exempted Swiss graduates **from** **annual quotas** has **not** become the operative rule for this route. The SEM's 2026 directives still state that Article 21(3) cases remain subject to the other admission conditions in Article 20 ff. AIG,and the separate parliamentary project on quota relief was returned to the Federal Council rather than

enacted in the form originally proposed. Federal Council quota decisions for 2026 also continue the third-country and UK quota system. 5

## Structural overview

This topic divides into **two** **distinct nationality** **tracks,** with a smaller **in-study work** layer that often creates timing confusion. The route map below is the cleanest way to structure content for an internal immigration system. 6

**·Third-country student to post-study search to work-authorised status.** Student admission is governed by Article 27 AIG. During studies, side work is tightly controlled. After completion, Article 21(3) AIG gives a six-month period to find corresponding work, but actual employmnent still depends on the graduate-role test and the remaining admission conditions. 7

**·EU/EFTA student to worker or jobseeker status.** This track is governed by free movement. The student/graduate normally moves into worker or jobseeker residence through commune registration and the EU/EFTA L or B permit framework, not through the third-country labour-market admission test. 8

**·UK** **nationals.** Since 1 January 2021, newly arriving UK nationals are treated in principle as **third-country** **nationals** for work purposes, and current federal quota decisions maintain a separate UK quota for 2026. 9

**·In-study work is a separate legal box from post-study work.** Third-country students at Swiss universities or universities of applied sciences may normally only take supplementary work after six months, with a 15-hour weekly term-time cap; this does **not** mean they automatically acquire a post-study work right later. 10

A content system should therefore present the route in this procedural order: **student** **admission** **basis** **→any authorised side work during studies → completion of studies →post-study search stay if** **available→employer-led work-authorisation step → commune registration / issue of the** **relevant** **new** **permit.** That sequencing is consistent with the federal legislation, SEM guidance and the cantonal practice notes reviewed below. 11

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

### Primary legal texts

**·Federal Act on Foreign NationaIs and Integration (AIG/FNIA), SR 142.20:**

**Article** **21(3)** is the core graduate-transition rule; **Article** 27 governs student admission and expressly sends post-study stay back to the general admission framework; **Article** **30** is the general derogations provision used as the legal basis for several education/training exceptions. 23

<!-- 3 -->

**·Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Gainful Employment (VZAE/OASA), SR 142.201:** **Article** **38** regulates study/training with supplementary work; the SEM directives also map the surrounding structure at **Articles** **39-41** for compulsory internships, work during university continuing education, and international exchange. 24

**·Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP), SR 0.142.112.681,** together with the **Ordinance on Free Movement of Persons (VFP), SR 142.203:** these are the main legal bases for the EU/EFTA route. 25

## Official guidance and administrative materials

·The **SEM** **"Working** **in** **Switzerland"** overview is the clearest federal summary of the nationality split: EU/EFTA nationals work through contract-plus-registration; third-country nationals require a permit, an employer application, and satisfaction of strict admission conditions. 26

·The **SEM "Foundations of labour-market admission"** page sets out the dual admission system, the overall economic interest requirement, annual quotas, priority/precedence, job-registration obligations, salary and working-condition compliance, and expected employer evidence such as RAV and EURES recruitment. 27

·The **SEM** **FAQ** **-** **Working** page states, in English, that third-country graduates of a Swiss higher-education institution have six **months** from the end of their course to look for a job matching their qualifications and explains the federal rule on student side work. 28

·The **SEM AIG directives, Chapter 4 "Stay with gainful employment",** updated **1 January 2026,** are the most important administrative authority for internal drafting. They sharpen the meaning of "high scientific or economic interest", exclude unrelated general jobs, confirm that Article 21(3) cases remain subject to the other admission requirements, and record that the ordinary two-stage canton-SEM consent procedure does not apply to these graduate admissions. 29

·For EU/EFTA nationals, the SEM permit pages confirm the operative residence instruments: L **EU/** **EFTA** for short-term employment or jobseeking and **B** **EU/EFTA** for longer contracts or non-working residence with sufficient resources and insurance. 30

## Cantonal practice notes

**Geneva** has the clearest publicly accessible graduate-transition workflow.For **third-country** **graduates,** Geneva says students who completed studies at a recognised or accredited Swiss higher education institution may request a **short-stay** **L** **permit** to look for work; the request must be filed **no** **later** **than** **15** **days** **before** **the** **end** **of studies,the** permit begins from the date studies are completed-where the **final** **record** **of** **marks** is treated as determinative-and it is valid for **six** **months** and stated to be **non-renewable.** Geneva also says graduates from institutions outside the recognised/accredited list,and **post-doctoral** **researchers,** are not eligible for that specific permit, and it requires proof of funds and accommodation for the six-month period. Geneva further states that students who already had authorised accessory work during studies may maintain it during the job-search period. For **EU/EFTA** **graduates,** Geneva offers a separate post-study **L** **permit** valid for **364** **days,** also described as non-renewable. 31

**Zurich** presents the route in two stages. During studies, third-country students at Swiss universities or universities of applied sciences may obtain side-work authorisation only under restricted conditions, including the six-month waiting period, schoolconfirmation and the 15-hour weekly term-time cap; the **employer** files the application. After ggraduation, Zurich states that third-country graduates with a Swiss higher-education qualification may be admitted to work only in defined cases of **high** **scientific or** **high** **economic** innterest, that the **precedence** **test** **is** **not** **examined,** and that otherwise the remaining third-country rules still apply. Zurich also warns expressly that **not** **every** **course** **completed** in **Switzerland** **automatically gives access to employment.** 32

<!-- 4 -->

**Bern** highlights timing on the employer side. For a Swiss company hiring a third-country national,Bern instructs employers to file the application **at least eight weeks before the planned start date,** to attach a justification letter, CV, diplomas, contract, passport copy and evidence of recruitment efforts, and says both the labour-market authorisation and the migration authority's agreement must be in place **before** the person starts work. That page is general rather than graduate-specific, but it is operationally relevant where a graduatecannot use, or cannot prove, the Article 21(3) route. 33

**Aargau** likewise emphasises that the employer must file early and that work may begin only after a **positive** **decision** and commune registration; it also notes that SEM approval is required in **most** non-EU/EFTA cases. **Basel-Stadt** provides a dedicated first-entry third-country work-permit filing route and links directly to the federal labour-admission criteria and the underlying legislation. **Ticino,** for student side work, says third-country students may file only after six months from the issue of their permit and may start work only **after authorisation;** it also warns of possible fines for late filing. These materials are useful as practice signals that cantons expect careful advance preparation and do not treat student-to-work transitions as informal status changes. 34

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

The strongest internal reading is that the Swiss third-country graduate route is a **two-step** **bridge** rather than a single status switch. First, the graduate relies on **Article 21(3) AIG** to remain for a short period to find corresponding employment. Second, once an employer is ready to hire, the legal focus shifts to the **specific** **proposed** **role**: its scientific or economic importance, its fit with the graduate's completed studies, and the rest of the labour-admission criteria that survive the waiver of precedence. This is why the route is best described as **"post-study search plus employer-sponsored graduate** **admission",** not as an unrestricted right to stay and work. 35

The employer's evidential burden also changes depending on the route used. In the **ordinary** third-country route, the employer must show labour-market searches in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area, compliance with salary norms, qualifications and quota availability. In the **Swiss-graduate** **exception,** the ordinary precedence test drops away, but the employer should replace that evidence with a more tailored dossier: a role description showing a **close** **match** **to** **the** **degree,** an explanation of the high **scientific or** **economic** **interest,** and where useful evidence drawn from labour-shortage indicators, shortage-occupation lists or the company's innovation and growth plans. 36

The nationality split must be stated cleanly. **EU/EFTA** **nationals** usually move through free movement residence categories and commune registration; **third-country** **nationals** move through the AIG/VZAE labour-admission system; **UK** **nationals** are now treated in principle as third-country nationals for work purposes; and **Croatian** **nationals,** as of the current federal position in 2026, again benefit from full free movement because the safeguard thresholds were not reached. Internal content should therefore avoid a single undifferentiated "foreign student after graduation" rule. 37

### Common weak points or timing errors

·Treating the six-month graduate period as a **right** **to** **work,** rather than a period to **look** **for** **corresponding** **employment** that still requires the right admission basis before the new role starts. 14

·Assuming that **any** **job** qualifies once the person has a Swiss degree. The SEM directives and Zurich guidance both say the role must have a serious study-link and high scienntific or economic interest; routine unrelated work is excluded. 16

·Filing too late. Geneva's public practice requires the third-country job-search request 15 **days** **before** **the** **end** **of studies,** and Bern asks employers to file work applications at **least eight** **weeks** before the intended start date. 38

·Using the **diploma** **issue** **date** as the trigger without checking cantonal practice. Geneva expressly treats the **final** **record** **of** **marks** as the operative completion date. 21

·Forgetting that the **employer must** **apply** for the relevant work authorisation on the third-country side, including for student side work. 39

·Drafting as if the precedence waiver also waived **quotas** or **all other** **conditions.** Federal guidance says it does not. 40

·Generalising a **cantonal accommodation** into national law-for example, Geneva's statement that prior accessory work may continue during the post-study search period. That should be labelled **Geneva** **practice,** not national doctrine. 41

·Treating every Swiss tertiary qualification or every private school diploma as falling within the graduate-search route. Geneva limits the route to recognised/accredited higher-education institutions and excludes certain cases such as post-docs from that permit pathway. 21

## Drafting cautions

·Avoid saying a third-country graduate **"has** **a** **right** **to** **remain** **and** **work".** Safer formulation:the person **may be admitted for six months to seek corresponding employment,** and **may be** **admitted** to **work** if the proposed activity is of high scientific or economic interest. 13

·Avoid saying the Swiss-graduate exception creates a **quota** **exemption.** The more cautious current statement is that it creates a **precedence** **exemption,** while the remaining admission conditions continue to apply; the separate quota-relief legislative project has not displaced the current route. 5

·Where referring to consent/approval sequencing, distinguish between the **general third-country rule** and this **route-specific graduate exception.** Most general third-country materials speak in terms of canton-plus-SEM approval, but the SEM's 2026 directives say Article 21(3) Swiss-graduate admissions do not follow the ordinary two-stage consent procedure. 42

·Keep **federal law, SEM administrative guidance,** and **cantonal practice** separate in the drafting. Geneva's filing deadline, Geneva's treatment of the final marks record, and Geneva's continuation of side work are good examples of rules that should not be universalised. 41

·For EU/EFTA content, avoid implying an unrestricted welfare-backed job-search right. The SEM states that the **L** **EU/EFTA jobseeker** **permit** does **not** create entitlement to social assistance. 20

·For nationality notes, keep current categories current: UK as third-country for work; **Croatia** currently back within full free movement in 2026. 43
