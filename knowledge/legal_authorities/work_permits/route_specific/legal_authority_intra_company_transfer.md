# Executive legal summary

## A. Executive legal summary

Switzerland does not operate a single, stand-alone federal "ICT permit" in the EU Directive sense. In practice, Swiss ICT-style cases are dealt with through the ordinary labour-admission framework under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, together with specific simplifications for intra-group exchanges, short defined missions, EU/EFTA notification cases, and Switzerland's GATS-related intrafirm commitments. That is the operative Swiss route architecture for intra-company transfers. 1

For third-country nationals, the baseline admissibility tests remain demanding: the case must serve Switzerland's overall economic interest, quota availability normally matters for L/B permits, Swiss/EU/ EFTA labour-market precedence usually applies unless a recognised exception is engaged,salary and working conditions must be locally customary, and admission is ordinarily limited to managers, specialists and other qualified workers. Suitable accommodation is also required. 2

For route design, thekey split is between four practical pathways. First, EU/EFTA direct hires or posted workers from EU/EFTA-based employers can use the AFMP notification procedure for up to 90 effective working days per calendar year. Secondly, third-country nationals on tightly defined missions of up to four months in a twelve-month period may use the Art. 19 para. 4 let. a OASA short-stay route. Thirdly, longer third-country assignments use the ordinary L/B permit framework under Arts. 18-24 FNIA. Fourthly, where the facts genuinely fit an international-company transfer, Art. 46 OASA and, in some cases, the GATS intrafirm commitments relax parts of the domestic-priority analysis. 3

Corporate-link evidence is central even though the official materials do not prescribe one closed evidential formula. The federal materials define GATS intrafirm transfer by reference to work in a Swiss subsidiary, branch or affiliated company, and Zurich's internal-transfer checklist requires the file to identify the home company and host company. The strong practical implication is that a persuasive Swiss ICT file should prove both the legal relationship between the entities and the operational assignment relationship between them. Public commercial-register extracts, statutes and filings available through Swiss register systems are therefore strong core documents, especially when the group link is not obvious on the face of the assignment letter. This is an evidence inference drawn from the official sources, not an express statutory checklist. 4

Role necessity must be route-specific and concrete. Swiss materials consistently push applicants away from broad "knowledge transfer" language and toward particularised explanations of the function, project, task scope, duration, execution site, and the reason the assignee's know-how is indispensable. For project and service-provider files, SEM and cantonal guidance require project descriptions,order or works contracts, implementation plans, and letters explaining why a third-state provider is necessary.

For internal transfers, the same logic should be translated into intra-group business necessity: why this person, why this Swiss host, why now, and why this role cannot be met through ordinary local staffing. 5

Salary alignment is not a cosmetic requirement. Swiss authorities examine salary, social contributions and working conditions against local, professional and sectoral norms; they may require a binding employment contract or a formal transfer confirmation; and assignment expenses for travel, accommodation and meals do not count toward salary. SEM expressly points applicants to Swiss benchmarking tools such as Salarium and the national salary calculator, and SECO publishes an international wage-comparison instruction for posted-worker analysis. 6

## Route structure and legal propositions

### B. Structural overview

The first routing question is nationality and the legal position of the sending employer. If the worker is an EU/EFTA national directly employed in Switzerland, or a posted worker sent by an EU/EFTA-based company, the AFMP route may apply. Up to 90 effective working days per calendar year can usually be handled through notification rather than a permit; for posted workers from EU/EFTA-based companies, this route applies regardless of citizenship, but third-country nationals must have been permanently admitted toan EU/EFTA labour market and are generally expected to have held a normal or permanent residence permit there for at least twelve months. Beyond the 90-day threshold, a permit is required and there is no legal entitlement to it. 7

The second routing question is duration. For non-EU/EFTA cases, Switzerland recognises a special short-stay route for gainful activity of up to four months within twelve months. These permits are not quota-counted,but the labour-market review remains important, and the authorities insist that the real purpose and foreseeable duration be shown clearly in the written contract or secondment confirmation. The 120-day variant is meant for pre-defined missions requiring several short stays; it must not be used for trial periods, extensions by stealth, or rotation staffing. 8

The third routing question is whether the application is truly an internal transfer or whether it is operationnally a client-site service case. Zurich explicitly separates "firm-internal secondment" from seconded service providers working for a third company. Geneva likewise requires service contracts, remuneration forms and a necessity letter where a third-state enterprise posts staff or offers services in the canton. If a Swiss third-party client is materially invoIved, the file should be built as a service-provider/project case and not described loosely as a pure intra-group transfer.9

The fourth routing question is whether an exception softens the usual labour-market priority test. SEM's general guidance confirms that, for operational transfers within international companies involving persons employed by the same company for at least six months, the vacancy-notification obligation may not apply. Separately, Art. 46 OASA provides a simplified recruitment mechanism for senior managers, highly qualified workers and indispensable specialists in international companies and research institutes, with the Swiss directives stating that the priority principle does not apply where this category is recruited for a fixed or indefinite period. GATS-related intrafirm cases go further for certain executives and highly qualified specialists of foreign service suppliers with a Swiss commercial presence, provided the person has served the foreign enterprise for at least the preceding twelve months. 10

#### Authorities and official materials

### D. Primary legal texts

The principal federal texts are the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, especially the provisions on economic interest, quotas, precedence, salary and employment conditions, personal requirements and accommodation; and the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, especially the provisions on short stays of up to four months, locally customary wage/working conditions for posted workers, simplified recruitment within international undertakings, and change of purpose. SEM's English admissions summary identifies Arts. 18-24, 21a and 30 FNIA as the operative statutory cluster, and its directives and annexes repeatedly cross-reference OASA arts. 19 para. 4 let. a, 22a, 46, 54 and 56in ICT-relevant contexts. 19

The international-law layer is also material. For EU/EFTA cases, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons governs notification-based service provision up to90 working days and the treatment of posted workers. For WTO/GATS-based cases, SEM's directives recognise specific Swiss market-access commitments for intrafirm transfers of executives and highly qualified specialists of foreign service suppliers with a commercial presence in Switzerland. 20

For posted-worker compliance, the Posted Workers Act and related ordinance remain important because they extend Swiss minimum labour conditions to inbound posted workers; SECO's official posting platform explains that the foreign employer must respect mandatory Swiss wage and labour standards laid down in federal law, ordinances, generally binding collective agreements and standard employment contracts. 21

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The single most important administrative source is the SEM directives on residence with gainful activity, current in the 1 January 2026 version. The directives contain thecontrolling administrative explanations on quotas, Art. 19 para. 4 short stays, wage and working-condition review, language issues, Art.46OASA intra-company exchanges, and GATS intrafirm categories. Although the English SEM site is useful, it expressly notes that the full documentation remains in German, French and Italian.22

SEM's English "Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market", "Procedure", and "Application documents" pages are especially useful for internal drafting because they gather the core requirements in one place: evidence of economic interest, precedence evidence, translations of certificates, company and job information, project documents, and either a signed Swiss employment contract or an employee-transfer confirmnation stating salary, expatriate allowances and expense arrangements.They also confirm that cantonal authorities conduct the first screening and that approved cantonal applications go to SEM for final approval. 23

For salary and compliance structure, SEM's model "Confirmation of assignment" is a high-value authority because it shows exactly what Swiss authorities expect to see in a secondment file: home and host employers, assignment duration, task during assignment, salary paid abroad,assignment allowances, tax allowances if paid by the employer, Swiss social insurance deductions, and an express undertaking that travel, accommodation and meal costs will be borne separately and not deducted from salary. 24

<!-- 4 -->

The SEM annex on project staff and service providers is equally important for difficult ICT-adjacent cases, especially in IT. It warns that seconded staff must not compete with local/EU workers through generic IT tasks, requires a precise and well-documented project, treats project changes as employment changes requiring authorisation, limits long-duration project residence authorisations to particular cases, confirms that seconded workers remain under the foreign employment contract, and explains that direct Swiss hiring by the subsidiary triggers a new admission analysis. It also gives a sector-specific salary marker for IT entrants and states that ordinary salary-calculator outputs are treated as minimum salaries. 25

SECO's "International wage comparison" page is the main official add-on for compensation methodology. It states that SECO has issued guidance on how to compare domestic and posted-worker pay and links to both the instruction and a calculation example; paired with the official posting platform, it is the best current administrative authority for testing whether a foreiggn compensation package withstannds Swiss minimum-condition review. 26

## Cantonal practice and local evidential expectations

## F. Cantonal practice notes

Zurich is the clearest canton for documentary expectations. Its published checklist for **firm-internal** **secondments** says there is no third company involved and requires a specific application justification dealing with the business, the task and the assignee. For secondments over four months,the assignment contract must identify the employee, the home company and host company, the function, task scope, place of work, start and duration, home-country base salary, assignment allowance, confirmation of at least twelve months' coverage of travel/accommodation/meals from entry, health and accident insurance arrangements, social-chargearrangements, Swiss-law fallback for unregulated contract points, and signatures. Zurich also requires CVs, diplomas and passport copies, and states that filings must be in German or English. 27

Zurich separately publishes a different checklist for posted employees working at a **third-party** **client.** That route requires a project description and the Swiss order or works contract in addition to the transfer documents. This is valuable for route selection: oncethe Swiss assignment is operationally a client-site service engagement, Zurich expects the file to look like a service-provider/project case, not merely an internal transfer dossier. 28

Geneva's practice is similarly explicit and particularly useful for necessity drafting. For third-country enterprises posting staff or offering services in Geneva, the complete file should be filed six toeight weeks before the mission; work cannot begin before authorisation unless the person already holds the specific entry or stay assurance noted on the page. Geneva requires, among other things, a signed service contract, a signed employment contract, a motivation letter stating duration, place of performance and the reasons showing the necessity of using a third-state serviceprovider, the worker's CV and diplomas, a signed secondment letter detailing remuneration and travel/accommodation/food arrangements, a remuneration form, and a foreign-enterprise details form. Geneva also warns that L/B contingents exist and that grant is not guaranteed. 29

<!-- 5 -->

Vaud's standard work-and-residence permit form is useful evidence that the canton treats "transfert interne" as a recognised filing category within the ordinary labour-market process. The form allows the applicant to tick "transfert interne", requires selection of permit type, identifies whether the proposed role is director, manager, qualified or unqualified employee, requests working time and gross salary information, and requires a work contract as an annex. This strongly supports drafting Swiss ICT cases as a route within the normal work-permit architecture, not asa free-floating special status. 30

Basel-Stadt's published pages are less granular but still useful for practice points. The canton confirms that third-country postings over eight days require a permit, links applicants to the SEM admissions criteria and to the official salary calculator, and provides an assignment-confirmation example as part of its posting materials. That reinforces the practical importance of salary proof and formal secondment attestations across cantons, not just in Zurich and Geneva. 31

## Practical analysis and drafting cautions

## G. Practical legal analysis

**Corporate-link** **evidence.** The legal threshold is not just that the employee belongs to a multinational group in a business sense; the file must show the relevant legal and operational connections for the route being invoked. For GATS intrafirm cases, the Swiss directives define the transfer by reference to temporary work in a Swiss subsidiary, branch or affiliated company, and Zurich's internal-transfer checklist requires the home and host companies to be expressly identified.A robust authority-pack template should therefore require: the signed secondment contract or confirmation; Swiss commercial-register extracts and, where useful, statutes or public filings; a group chart showing control chain;and foreign corporate evidence if the Swiss filing alone does not make group affiliation obvious. The strong evidential value of register extracts follows from the official availability of Swiss commercial-register records and supporting company documents. This final point is an inference grounded in the official materials rather than a verbatim SEM rule. 32

**Assignment rationale** **and** **role** **necessity.** Swiss authorities appear most receptive where the submission explains the necessity in operational terms. SEM's application-documents page requires reasons the vacancy must be filled, a job description and company information; for project work it calls for the order evidence, project description and implementation plan. Geneva goes further by expressly requiring a letter explaining why a third-state provider is necessary. The SEM project/service-provider annex adds that the assignment should be confined to specialists whose project-specific know-how cannot be replaced by local staff and should not involve generic IT tasks. The practical lesson is that "knowledge transfer" must be translated into concrete, verifiable necessity: product ownership,non-transferable platform expertise, transition-critical leadership, regulatory sign-off authority,or implementation knowledgge tied to a defined Swiss rollout. 33

**Duration** **and** **route** **integrity.** Duration drives credibility. If the mission is under four months or a 120-day pattern, the duration and purpose should appear consistently across the contract, secondment letter, project plan and covering submission. SEM expressly warns that the 120-day route is only for pre-defined missions requiring multiple short stays and must not be used to evade quota rules, employment-market review, tax/social charges or the four-month cap. For longer deployments, internal-transfer filings should avoid pretending to be temporary if the real intention is de facto local placement. 8

**Compensation** **and** **compliance** **structure.** The Swiss review of remuneration is package-sensitive but rule-based. Local salary, social contributions and work conditions must be customary; a signed

conditional Swiss contract or foreign transfer confirmation is compulsory evidence; and assignment expenses for travel, accommodation and meals are separate from salary. SEM specifically references Salarium and other benchmark tools, while SECO provides a dedicated methodology for international wage comparison. In practice, the best record is a clean compensation table splitting home salary, Swiss assignment allowance, tax equalisation if any, reimbursed expenses, and Swiss deductions, together with a note on insuranceand social-security arrangements. 6

**Typical evidential weak** **points.** The recurring weak spots are consistent across federal and cantonal materials: vague justifications; no precise home/host identification; no project description where the case is really a services matter; role descriptions that sound generic rather than specialist; contracts missing expense, insurance or social-charge terms; salary tables that improperly count travel or accommodation reimbursements as salary; missing translation of qualification documents; and inconsistent route characterisation, especially where the facts look more like local hire or labour leasing than secondment. 34

### H. Drafting cautions

A sound drafting template for Swiss ICT cases should observe the following cautions:

·Do not describe the route simply as an "ICT permit". Identify the actual Swiss legal path instead: AFMP notification, Art. 19 para. 4 let. a OASA short stay, ordinary FNIA L/B permit, Art. 46 OASA simplified intra-company recruitment, or a GATS intrafirm case. 35

·Do not assume that all intra-group cases are exempt from labour-market priority. The general precedence rule remains the default for third-country nationals, and exceptions need to be tied to the correct legal basis. 13

·Do not rely on vacancy-notice exemptions as if they displaced the whole labour-market test. SEM's six-month intra-company operational-transfer relief is narrower and specifically framed around the vacancy-notification obligation. 12

·Do not submit a generic "knowledge transfer" letter. Spell out the assignment task,deliverables, location, timing, Swiss business impact, and the reason the individual's know-how is indispensable. 33

·Do not blur an internal transfer with a third-party services case. Zurich's separate checklists show that client-site work triggers additional project and contract evidence. 36

·Do not count travel, housing ormeal reimbursements as salary. Swiss forms and guidance treat these as separate assignment expenses. 15

·Do not omit the compliance architecture. The secondment instrument should cover insurance, social-security handling, expense coverage, working time, holidays and signatures; otherwise the file looks under-specified. 37

·Do not use the 120-day route for trial periods, rolling staffing or de facto permanent occupancy of the same post by a succession of foreigners. SEM expressly warns against those uses. 8

·Do not ignore language-document issues. SEM expects copies in the original language and certified translations unless the material is already in a Swiss official language or English. 38

·Do not paper a local hire as a secondment if the Swiss company is in fact making a new direct engagement. SEMI's project/service-provider annex says that a new direct fixed-term hire by the Swiss subsidiary is a change of purpose and requires a new admission procedure. 39

·Do not structure the arrangement like labour leasing from abroad. SEM's AFMP guidance states that foreign staff leasing into Switzerland is prohibited. 18

·Do not overstate national-language requirements for senior international specialists. SEM states that in highly specialised international environments, very good English may suffice and managers/specialists may be exempt from proof of a Swiss national language. 40

#### Open questions and limitations

This pack is strongest on current official texts, directives, forms and cantonal practice notes. I did not identify, in this research pass, a directly on-point published Swiss high-court decision focused specifically on Art. 46 OASA or GATS-style ICT admissions, so the pack relies primarily on current SEM/ SECO/cantonal administrative authority rather than judicial precedent. The Federal Supreme Court and Federal Administrative Court databases remain the right next-stop repositories if a litigation-focused addendum is later needed. 47
