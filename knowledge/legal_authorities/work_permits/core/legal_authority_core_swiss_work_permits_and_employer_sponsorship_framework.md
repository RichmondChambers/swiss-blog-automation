# Swiss Work Permits and Employer Sponsorship Authority Pack

**Suggested filename:** `legal_authority_core_swiss_work_permits_and_employer_sponsorship_framework_pack.pdf`

## Executive legal summary

Swiss work authorisation is built on a dual system. For third-country nationals, admission to employment is principally governed by the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, which makes access to the labour market discretionary and subject to economic-interest, employer, quota, labour-market, salary, and qualification criteria. For EU/EFTA nationals, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing framework displace or soften those restrictions, so the system is much more rights-based and often runs through residence registration or notification rather than a substantive sponsorship assessment. The act itself expressly states that, for EU/EFTA nationals and certain posted workers, it applies only insofar as no different treaty rule exists or where Swiss law is more favourable. [1]

For third-country local hires, there is no single generic “sponsorship” concept, but employer sponsorship is functionally central. The employer must file the application, show that the admission serves the Swiss economy, establish that no suitable worker with priority status could be found, prove compliance with local salary and working conditions, and support the worker’s qualification profile. Initial short-stay and residence permits for employment may also be quota-limited, and admission is generally restricted to managers, specialists, and other qualified workers, subject to defined exceptions. [2]

For EU/EFTA employment, the framework is materially different. EU/EFTA nationals working for up to three months in a calendar year generally fall into the online notification system rather than a permit system. For work of more than three months, they register locally and receive an L or B permit depending on duration; the residence permit functions as the work permit. EU/EFTA cross-border commuters use the G permit. EU/EFTA service provision and EU/EFTA-based postings are also liberalised up to 90 effective working days per calendar year, again chiefly via notification; beyond that threshold a permit is required and there is no legal entitlement to it. [3]

For posted work and assignments, route differentiation is critical. A Swiss local hire, a posting by a foreign employer, a service provision by a self-employed EU/EFTA national, a third-country cross-border service project, and an intra-group transfer are not the same route and they do not share the same conditions. Swiss official guidance is explicit that posted workers remain employed by the foreign employer; the employment relationship stays abroad, the assignment is temporary, and Swiss wage and working-condition rules still matter. In the EU/EFTA notification regime, posted third-country workers may only use that route if they have already been admitted permanently to the regular labour market of an EU/EFTA state, generally understood as at least 12 months on a standard or permanent residence permit there. [4]

The procedural structure is shared but layered. Cantons issue permits and conduct the labour-market screening. In third-country cases, the employer files with the competent cantonal employment or migration authority, the canton makes the preliminary labour-market decision, and approved cases go to SEM for federal review where required. Visa issuance, entry, local registration, and card issuance then follow through the migration authorities and, where relevant, Swiss representations abroad. Cantonal forms, channels, fees, and timings differ in practice. [5]

At a high level, the most important compliance themes for employers are these. First, route selection must be right from the outset. Secondly, recruitment evidence, wage evidence, and worker qualification evidence must be substantive, not formulaic. Thirdly, notification-only routes do not remove compliance risk: posted-worker wage rules, documentary obligations, and sanctions remain active. Fourthly, job-change, renewal, unemployment, and permit-continuity rules differ sharply by permit type and nationality cluster, so later drafting should never generalise from one route to another. [6]

## Structural overview and table of legal propositions

The Swiss framework is best understood as a matrix rather than a single route. The main axes are: **nationality cluster** (EU/EFTA versus third-country); **relationship to the Swiss labour market** (local hire, cross-border commuter, self-employment, family-linked work rights); **location of the employer** (Swiss employer versus foreign employer); **nature of the work** (ordinary employment, posting, service provision, intra-group transfer, project work); and **duration** (notification range, short stay, longer residence, renewal). Federal law and SEM guidance repeatedly organise the subject in exactly this way. [7]

A useful internal taxonomy is therefore: **EU/EFTA local employment and registration routes**; **EU/EFTA notification routes for short work and services**; **third-country employer-sponsored local hire routes**; **third-country cross-border service routes**; **third-country short-duration authorisations including the practice often called the 120-day route**; **intra-company transfer exceptions**; **posted-worker compliance routes under the Posted Workers Act**; and **permit-mobility / continuity rules after admission**. That taxonomy is more faithful to the primary materials than any generic “Swiss work visa” label. [8]

## Primary legal texts

The central federal statute is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, SR 142.20**, authentic in German, French, and Italian. For this pack, its load-bearing provisions are: the scope rule in art. 2; the admission principles in arts. 18-26; mobility and cantonal-change rules in arts. 37-40; expiry and revocation rules in arts. 61-62; and the EU/EFTA unemployment continuity rule in art. 61a. Those provisions establish the ordinary third-country admission model and the interaction point with AFMP rights. [24]

The free movement treaty layer is the **Agreement of 21 June 1999 between Switzerland and the European Community and its Member States on the free movement of persons, SR 0.142.112.681**, together with the EFTA framework. SEM’s legal-bases page lists the treaty as the primary legal basis for EU/EFTA free movement, alongside the implementing free-movement ordinance, SR 142.203. SEM’s public AFMP pages explain that the treaty liberalises residence and work for EU nationals and extends in parallel to EFTA nationals. [25]

The main implementing ordinance for third-country employment is **SR 142.201, the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment**. In this subject area, it supplies definitions of dependent and independent work, special short-term regimes, cantonal labour-market pre-decisions, federal approvals, and special categories such as intra-company transfers. Although this pack was able to inspect only selected official excerpts of the consolidated ordinance text directly, its current application is strongly cross-checked through the SEM directives current to 1 January 2026 and official SEM legal-bases pages. [26]

The free-movement implementing ordinance, **SR 142.203**, remains essential for EU/EFTA residence categories, mobility, and sanctions within the AFMP system. SEM’s legal-bases page identifies it explicitly, and current SEM VFP directives are maintained as of 1 January 2026. [27]

The posted-work compliance layer sits in the **Federal Act on Posted Workers, SR 823.20**, and its ordinance, **SR 823.201**. SECO’s official labour-market pages identify these instruments as the core legal bases for the Swiss minimum wage and working-condition protections applicable to foreign employers posting workers into Switzerland, alongside the enforcement system of tripartite and parity bodies. [28]

A final ancillary layer is the domestic vacancy-registration framework linked to FNIA art. 21a. SEM explains that the execution rules for that mechanism sit primarily in the Employment Services Ordinance provisions cited in the directives, while the immigration relevance is handled through SEM guidance and evidence requirements in work-permit applications. [29]

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

The most important federal administrative materials for internal drafting are the current **SEM directives** and the route-specific SEM pages. Chapter 4 of the SEM AIG directives, current as of 1 January 2026, is the main administrative text for employment-related admission under the FNIA. It covers priority, wages and conditions, personal requirements, exceptions, short-duration permits, sectoral practice, intra-company transfers, cantonal/federal approvals, and order-period guidance. SEM also maintains a separate set of VFP directives, likewise current to 1 January 2026, for AFMP implementation. [30]

The public SEM route pages are useful for high-confidence propositions that recur in content drafting. On the non-EU/EFTA side, SEM’s “Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market” page states the substantive criteria, while the “Procedure” and “Application documents” pages show the expected documentary build of an application, the cantonal filing role, the need for SEM final approval in approved cases, and the practical evidence used to prove priority, qualifications, and salary compliance. [31]

On the EU/EFTA side, the highest-value public materials are the SEM AFMP overview, the FAQ on free movement, the L/B/G permit pages, and the notification page. Together, they establish the short-work notification system, longer permit categories, commuter treatment, the 90-day service rule, the 8-day prior-notification rule, the always-notify sectors, and the distinction between rights-based residence and non-entitled service authorisations beyond 90 days. [32]

SECO’s posted-worker guidance is critical for employer compliance content. SECO states that the Posted Workers Act applies Swiss minimum labour standards to posted workers, that tripartite commissions and parity commissions perform enforcement, and that SECO publicly lists service providers subject to final service bans. SECO’s international wage-comparison page also points to a binding instruction for control bodies and cantonal sanction authorities on how to compare domestic and posted-worker pay, plus the entsendung.admin.ch platform for checking applicable minimum wages in sector and region. [33]

SECO’s self-employed service-provider pages add one practical compliance layer sometimes missed in generic immigration writing: documentary proof of genuine self-employment. EU/EFTA self-employed service providers may operate under the notification system up to 90 days, but on site they may be required to produce the notification confirmation or permit, an A1 form, and the client contract or confirmation in an official language; failure can lead to sanctions. [34]

Cantonal practice matters most for filing mechanics, timelines, dossier assembly, and local communications. Zurich’s official pages separate the labour-market pre-decision by the Office for Economy from the residence permit issued later by the migration authority, make clear that third-country nationals need permission from day one, and state that the employer is the filing party for local hires. [35]

Geneva’s employer page is particularly useful for practice notes. It states that a Swiss employer engaging a third-country national must in principle file with the OCPM, that prior acceptance by the labour authority is required, that the employer should file approximately 6 to 8 weeks before entry, that favourable cases may then go to SEM for approval, and that the employee may work only once lawfully authorised. It also publishes route-specific checklists and recognises distinct options for 120-day / 4-month authorisations, L permits, and B permits. [36]

Vaud’s official pages show another cantonally specific process. For a third-country long-stay employment case, Vaud calls for a base form and supporting documents on motivation, CVs, diplomas, recruitment evidence, and compliant contract terms, and says the dossier is submitted in three copies to the local foreigner’s office of the commune where the company is based; from there it moves to the competent cantonal labour-market and migration authorities, and, if approved, to the federal authority. [37]

Basel-Stadt is a useful reminder not to over-nationalise formality. Its official pages mainly publish the contact points, consultation hours, and renewal channels for work authorisations, including a separate renewal path for third-country nationals and posted workers. That is less doctrinally important than Zurich, Geneva, or Vaud, but it reinforces the practical point that cantonal UX is part of the real procedure. [38]

## Practical legal analysis

The Swiss system should be drafted as a bundle of route-specific authorisations, not a single work-permit regime. For third-country nationals, the core local-hire route runs through FNIA arts. 18-25: economic interest, employer request, quota exposure for first grants, labour priority, local salary/work conditions, qualifications, and suitable housing. SEM’s application-document checklist reflects those same statutory tests by requiring recruitment evidence, RAV/EURES material, reasons for rejecting other applicants, CVs and diplomas, and a binding contract or assignment confirmation. This is where “employer sponsorship” is at its strongest in Swiss law. [39]

A major drafting mistake is to treat salary, local conditions, and labour-market testing as abstract legal ideas rather than evidence-based requirements. SEM’s current directives say cantons must systematically check whether foreign workers are being offered the same local and occupational terms as domestic workers, and that authorities should look first to legal norms and comparative employment conditions in the same undertaking and sector, while also using contemporary statistical salary surveys where necessary. That is stricter than simply saying “the wage must meet the minimum wage” and is especially important where collective agreement minima sit below actual market norms. [40]

In addition, job-vacancy registration under art. 21a FNIA is not the same thing as the labour-market priority test, though the two are related. SEM explains that occupations, sectors, or regions above the unemployment threshold are subject to vacancy-registration with public employment services, and that employers seeking third-country work permits in those categories should submit proof of official registration. For later drafting, the safer internal position is that vacancy registration is an extra evidential and compliance layer, not a replacement for the broader obligation to show unsuccessful recruitment on the domestic and EU/EFTA labour markets where required. [12]

Local hire, posting, assignment, and transfer must remain separate routes in future content. A local hire means a Swiss employer employs the worker in Switzerland. A posting means the worker remains employed by the foreign employer and is temporarily deployed into Switzerland. Zurich and SECO both state that posted employees remain under the foreign employment contract and that the assignment is temporary and generally project-bound. A cross-border service by a third-country provider is governed through the specific service-admission rule in art. 26 FNIA, not through the AFMP notification model. By contrast, intra-company transfer is a special exception route under SR 142.201 art. 46, and SEM’s directives treat it as a simplified recruitment channel for managers, highly qualified professionals, and indispensable specialists, waiving ordinary labour priority where the route genuinely applies. [41]

For EU/EFTA nationals hired by Swiss employers, the legal architecture is much lighter. The permit is essentially residence-based: under three months, notification; over three months, local registration and issue of an L or B permit based on contract length. EU/EFTA cross-border commuters use the G permit and enjoy broad geographical and professional mobility. Swiss official pages repeatedly present this framework as a residence-and-registration model, not a discretionary labour-market-admission model. This is why later content should not describe EU/EFTA routes as if they were third-country sponsorship cases. [42]

The service-provision / notification branch has its own internal logic. Under AFMP-based rules, EU/EFTA service providers, including posted workers and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers, may work in Switzerland up to 90 effective working days per calendar year by notification. Posted workers and self-employed providers are subject to the 8-day prior-notification rule, except in defined emergency cases, and in certain sectors notification is required from the first day regardless of duration. Those sectors include construction and secondary contract work, gardening and landscaping, hotel/restaurant/catering, cleaning, monitoring/security, itinerant trade, and the sex industry. [43]

The third-country-specific short-duration route deserves special care in drafting. SEM’s public procedure page distinguishes non-EU/EFTA stays of less than four months from longer stays, and the current directives explain that so-called 120-day authorisations are generally for pre-defined assignments requiring several time-limited stays within a 12-month period. SEM expressly warns that they must not be used to circumvent quotas, labour-market control, registration duties, or tax/social obligations. This means later content should treat “120-day permits” as a tightly controlled practice route, not as a generic faster version of a standard work permit. [44]

On procedure, the basic model is canton first, federation second where required. The canton handles the labour-market assessment and pre-decision. Positive cases are submitted to SEM where federal approval is mandatory or has been reserved. SEM’s third-country procedure page is explicit that the employer files with the cantonal authority, the cantonal authority screens under the FNIA, approved cases go to SEM, and the official SEM decision is then followed by visa-authorisation steps where relevant. Geneva and Vaud show that this abstract framework translates into different local filing channels, lead times, copy requirements, and fee structures. [45]

On changes of employment and permit continuity, the federal act draws route-sensitive lines. For third-country short-stay permit holders, job changes require important reasons and continued satisfaction of salary and qualification conditions. For residence-permit holders admitted to gainful employment, the statute is materially more liberal and allows job changes without further permission. For changes of canton, B-permit holders have a claim if they are not unemployed and no revocation grounds apply. EU/EFTA status is different again, because unemployment can trigger the specific continuity limits in art. 61a, and SEM notes that a first B EU/EFTA renewal can be reduced to one year after prolonged involuntary unemployment. [46]

At a high level, the most common refusal patterns can be inferred from the statutory structure and official checklists, even though Swiss authorities do not publish a single universal refusal taxonomy. The recurrent weak points are likely to be: ordinary or replaceable roles presented as specialist positions; thin or formulaic recruitment evidence; salary offers unsupported by local comparators; attempts to use short-duration or posting routes for ordinary local employment; incomplete dossiers; and commencement of work before authorisation. That synthesis is an inference from the legislation, SEM’s application-document requirements, Geneva’s employer guidance, and the directives on 120-day permits and salary control rather than a codified refusal list. [47]

From a downstream strategy standpoint, later route-specific drafting should always begin with a six-point screening question set: nationality cluster; employer location; worker residence location; legal relationship to the employer during the Swiss activity; total days/months of work in Switzerland in the relevant period; and whether the role is genuinely managerial/specialist/qualified or instead a service, training, or posted-work case. Those questions map directly onto how the federal and cantonal authorities structure the law and procedure. [48]

## Drafting cautions

Do not use “Swiss work permit” as though it were a single legal object. Swiss official materials consistently separate at least: residence-linked employment permits, notification-only work, commuter permits, posted-worker routes, cross-border service routes, self-employment, and route-specific exceptions. Later content generation should preserve that granularity. [49]

Do not blur work authorisation and residence status. For EU/EFTA nationals, the residence permit is generally the work authorisation, while sub-three-month work is often notification only. For third-country nationals, the labour-market approval is a substantive admission decision that may then lead to entry, residence documentation, or a short-term authorisation depending on the route. Some short-duration authorisations do not map neatly onto the standard residence-card logic. [50]

Do not carry third-country concepts into EU/EFTA content. Labour-market priority, quotas, and the qualified-worker filter are third-country concepts unless a special country-specific transitional measure applies. EU/EFTA routes are rights-based and AFMP-driven, even though they still involve registration, notification, and some compliance rules. [51]

Do not reduce salary compliance to “minimum wage”. SEM’s current guidance expects cantons to assess local, occupational, and sectoral comparability, potentially using statistical tools as well as collective agreements. Posted-worker salary comparison has its own SECO compliance guidance. [52]

Do not treat all foreign-employer cases as “posted workers”. A foreign employer can be involved in at least three legally different ways: AFMP-based posted work or self-employed service provision from the EU/EFTA area; third-country cross-border service provision under art. 26 FNIA; and intra-company transfer under SR 142.201 art. 46. The right route depends on nationality, location of establishment, service duration, and the continuing employment relationship. [53]

Do not assume cantonal procedure is interchangeable. Geneva’s timing guidance, Vaud’s commune-based forwarding practice, Zurich’s split between labour-market pre-decision and migration issuance, and Basel’s separate renewal channels show why procedural drafting must either stay federal and abstract or clearly label cantonal practice as such. [54]

Do not publish static statements on annually or periodically changing items without a fresh check. That includes annual quota figures, vacancy-registration lists and thresholds, cantonal forms and fees, and country-specific transitional measures. The official pages themselves point users to annually updated lists and current cantonal authorities. [55]

Finally, where wording precision matters, later drafting should use the authentic German, French, or Italian federal texts. Many SEM legal-bases pages expressly note that the legal-base documents are not available in English, and the official English pages are helpful as explanatory guidance rather than as the only text to quote for contentious legal propositions. [56]

## Open questions and limitations

This pack is designed as a core authority architecture, not a route-by-route litigation note. It deliberately does not include a Swiss case-law layer from the Federal Administrative Court or Federal Supreme Court, so if later drafting needs propositions on judicially tested issues such as “high economic interest”, the margin of discretion under art. 23 FNIA, or the limits of AFMP-derived retention rights after job loss, a supplementary jurisprudence pack should be added.

Some current consolidated Fedlex HTML pages were not fully inspectable in-browser in this environment. For SR 142.201 and SR 142.203 in particular, the pack therefore relies heavily on current SEM directives, official SEM legal-bases pages, and selected official law extracts already accessible through the federal document mirrors. That is sufficient for a conservative architecture pack, but later publication-quality route notes should still verify any article-level quotation against the authentic current consolidated text on Fedlex before final use.

Cantonal pages are included only as practice notes, not as authorities on federal law. Their value lies in showing filing channels, timing, checklists, and practical differentiation. They should not be cited later as if they altered federal statutory tests.
