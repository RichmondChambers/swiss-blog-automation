# Swiss citizenship by naturalisation authority pack

## Executive legal summary

Swiss citizenship acquired by official decision sits within a federal structure in which citizenship of a commune and a canton forms part of Swiss citizenship itself. The Federal Constitution gives the Confederation power to regulate acquisition and loss of citizenship, to set minimum requirements for cantonal naturalisation of foreign nationals, and to grant federal naturalisation permits. Ordinary naturalisation therefore remains a shared federal, cantonal and often communal process, whereas facilitated naturalisation and reinstatement are decided by the State Secretariat for Migration (SEM) at federal level after cantonal participation in the investigation or consultation process. 1

The main first-time route for foreign nationals without a qualifying Swiss family connection is ordinary naturalisation. At federal level it requires a valid settlement permit (Permit C) when the application is filed and ten years’ residence in Switzerland, of which three years must fall within the five years before filing. Residence between ages eight and eighteen counts double, but actual physical residence must still total at least six years. Residence counting is not uniform across immigration categories: B and C permits count in full; F status counts at half; L and N time does not count; time with an FDFA legitimation card or a Ci permit counts. SEM guidance also states that, in practice, the applicant must keep a valid C permit not only at filing but through the procedure and up to the cantonal grant. 2

The C permit is therefore necessary for ordinary naturalisation, but it is not sufficient. Ordinary naturalisation also requires successful integration, familiarity with Swiss living conditions, and no threat to Switzerland’s internal or external security. The Citizenship Act states the principal integration criteria; the Citizenship Ordinance concretises them. Together they require respect for public security and order, respect for constitutional values, ability to communicate in everyday life in a national language, participation in economic life or education, and support for the integration of family members. Familiarity with Swiss living conditions includes basic knowledge of geography, history, politics and society, participation in social and cultural life, and contact with Swiss nationals. 3

Language is regulated more precisely than many public summaries suggest. The federal minimum is B1 oral and A2 written in a national language. Proof may be provided through mother tongue, five years of compulsory schooling in a national language, completion of secondary II or tertiary education in a national language, or a recognised language certificate. SEM’s current FAQ also confirms that only recognised certificates on SEM’s list are acceptable and that attendance certificates or online placement tests are not enough. The Ordinance allows departures from some integration criteria where disability, serious illness or other significant personal circumstances make compliance impossible or materially more difficult. 4

Good conduct in naturalisation is broader than the absence of a serious criminal conviction. The Ordinance treats repeated or serious breaches of law, wilful non-payment of important public or private obligations, and certain criminal-record entries as indicators of failed integration. Pending criminal proceedings suspend the federal process. Financial compliance is especially important in SEM practice: the handbook treats tax arrears, debt enforcement entries, loss certificates, unpaid maintenance, and similar failures as material obstacles. Social assistance within the three years before filing, or during the procedure, blocks the criterion of participation in economic life or education unless it has been fully repaid, subject to recognised personal-circumstances exceptions. These points are partly black-letter law and partly official administrative guidance; drafting should keep those layers separate. 5

Cantonal and communal law materially affects timing, evidence and assessment. Federal law requires cantonal law to impose a cantonal residence requirement of between two and five years; cantons then decide the authorities and procedure, and communal participation may remain significant. The federal statute requires reasons for refusal and protects privacy if a commune uses a popular vote. Official cantonal sources show substantial local variation: Zurich requires two years in the same commune before filing and describes a roughly two-year process; Bern requires two years in the canton and commune and uses a written naturalisation test with published exemptions; Geneva requires two years in the canton including the final twelve months and insists on effective residence in the canton throughout the process, concluding with an oath ceremony. 6

Facilitated naturalisation is not merely accelerated ordinary naturalisation. It is a separate federal route with separate applicant classes and procedure. For a foreign spouse living in Switzerland, the statute requires three years in marital union and five years’ residence in Switzerland, including the year immediately before the application. For a spouse living abroad, it requires six years in marital union and close ties with Switzerland. SEM guidance stresses that the marital union must be actual and stable both at filing and at decision, and it is this stability requirement that often drives later nullity cases. Third-generation foreign nationals have their own facilitated route with specific ancestry, birth, schooling, C permit and age-limit requirements. Reinstatement is further distinct again because it presupposes prior Swiss citizenship and is available only after forfeiture, release or other loss scenarios defined in the Act. 7

Post-grant vulnerability is a real planning issue. A naturalisation can be declared null if it was procured through false statements or concealment of material facts. Federal law and SEM guidance set a relative two-year period from SEM’s discovery of the relevant facts and an absolute eight-year period from acquisition, with a fresh two-year period after each communicated investigative step. After a final nullity decision, a new application is generally barred for two years, subject to a children’s carve-out. Separate from nullity, Swiss law also contains a deprivation power for dual nationals whose conduct seriously prejudices Swiss interests or reputation, but that is a different and much more exceptional mechanism.

## Structural overview of the naturalisation framework

### Legal nature of Swiss citizenship by naturalisation

Swiss citizenship is structurally composite. A Swiss citizen is also a citizen of a commune and of the canton to which that commune belongs. The Confederation regulates acquisition and loss and sets minimum requirements for naturalisation by the cantons, but ordinary naturalisation culminates in a cantonal decision that carries the communal and cantonal citizenship with it. The Confederation’s role is therefore indispensable, but it is not the sole decision-maker in ordinary naturalisation. 9

### Ordinary naturalisation

Ordinary naturalisation is the standard route for foreign nationals who do not qualify for a facilitated route. The applicant files with the authority designated by cantonal law. Canton and, where applicable, commune assess the file first; only if they can support the application is it sent to SEM for the federal authorisation. If SEM grants the federal naturalisation permit, the cantonal authority must still make the final ordinary naturalisation decision. The citizenship is acquired only when that cantonal decision becomes legally final. 10

### Facilitated naturalisation

Facilitated naturalisation is a federal procedure decided by SEM. The principal categories relevant here are spouses of Swiss citizens, third-generation foreign nationals, stateless children, certain children of naturalised parents, and persons who were wrongly treated as Swiss. For the spouse route, the statute distinguishes between applicants living in Switzerland and applicants living abroad; for the latter, “close ties with Switzerland” is central. SEM, not the commune or canton, decides the application, although cantonal investigations or hearings may still be required. 11

### Reinstatement

Reinstatement is legally distinct from first-time naturalisation because it is reserved for former Swiss citizens who previously held Swiss nationality and later lost it. The Act requires successful integration for applicants in Switzerland, close ties to Switzerland for applicants abroad, respect for public order and constitutional values, and no threat to internal or external security. Within ten years of loss, the person may apply whether resident abroad or in Switzerland; after that ten-year period, reinstatement generally requires three years’ residence in Switzerland. 12

### Residence and C permit requirements

For ordinary naturalisation, the federal rule is a valid C permit at filing plus ten years’ qualifying residence, including three years in the five years before filing. Time between ages eight and eighteen counts double, subject to a six-year actual-residence floor. Residence counting differs by permit category: B and C generally count in full, F at half, L and N not at all, and FDFA legitimation-card/Ci time counts. Cantonal law must also impose a cantonal residence requirement of between two and five years. By contrast, SEM’s official public material on the facilitated spouse and third-generation routes does not state a federal C permit requirement for the spouse route, while the third-generation route expressly requires the child applicant to hold a C permit. 13

### Integration, language, and civic knowledge

Integration under the Citizenship Act is not a single test but a cluster of statutory and ordinance criteria. It includes compliance with public order, respect for constitutional values, language ability, economic participation or education, and support for family integration. Separate from those criteria, the Act also requires familiarity with Swiss living conditions. The Ordinance explains that this includes civic and factual knowledge about Swiss geography, history, politics and society, participation in social and cultural life, and contacts with Swiss nationals. Cantons may test this knowledge and may add further integration criteria. 14

### Public-order and financial-compliance expectations

The Ordinance and SEM guidance show that naturalisation authorities look at criminal records, pending proceedings, taxes, debt enforcement, unpaid maintenance and social assistance. Certain VOSTRA entries are outright disqualifying while visible; pending criminal proceedings suspend the federal procedure. Social assistance in the three years before the application or during the procedure generally fails the economic-participation criterion unless fully repaid, subject to statutory and ordinance allowances for disability, illness or other significant personal circumstances. Tax arrears and debt-enforcement history are heavily emphasised in SEM’s handbook, but those points should be characterised as official practice guidance rather than freestanding statutory tests. 15

### Cantonal and communal procedure

Federal law leaves procedure in the canton and commune to cantonal law, subject to federal minimum requirements such as reasons for refusal and privacy protection. In practice, ordinary naturalisation often involves interviews, civic-knowledge checks, local reports and repeated record checks. The Ordinance requires renewed VOSTRA and financial checks before the final cantonal decision, particularly where more than six months pass after the federal authorisation. Moving during the procedure may or may not derail the case, depending on whether the competent local authority has already completed the investigation required for its support or decision. 16

### Refusal and post-grant vulnerability

A refusal of ordinary naturalisation must be reasoned. Where the canton refuses, the file never reaches SEM; where SEM refuses the federal permit, ordinary naturalisation cannot be completed; where new negative facts arise after the federal permit, the canton must refuse the final grant. After acquisition, nullity remains possible if the citizenship was procured by false statements or concealment of material facts. That risk is especially acute in facilitated-spouse cases where the marital union was not in truth stable or where a relevant change in circumstances was not disclosed. 17

### Downstream planning significance

The immigration-law step to a C permit and the nationality-law step to naturalisation should be planned together but not conflated. A stable address history, local rootedness, clean tax and debt records, social-assistance history, language evidence and family-maintenance compliance all affect naturalisation readiness. Because cantonal and communal processing can be lengthy and can include local tests, interviews or oath requirements, naturalisation planning should usually begin well before the intended filing date. 18

## Ancillary migration-law context

The C permit is an immigration status governed by migration law, not a kind of citizenship. SEM describes the settlement permit as an unlimited status, generally granted after five or ten years’ residence depending on category, and separate from the citizenship regime. That separation is important: a person may be settled for immigration purposes long before being ready for naturalisation, and the counting and evidence rules should not be assumed to match without source support. 49

## Official guidance and administrative materials

### SEM handbook and route-specific materials

SEM’s citizenship handbook is the single most useful official interpretive resource found in this research. The handbook page lists the current chapters relevant to ordinary naturalisation, spouse facilitation in Switzerland and abroad, other facilitated routes, reinstatement, and nullity/loss. The ordinary-naturalisation chapter, last updated on 21 February 2024 in the search result, gives the strongest available official synthesis of residence counting, C-permit continuity, criminal-record handling, language evidence, civic-knowledge testing, debt and tax practice, social-assistance effects, and cantonal procedure. The spouse chapter explains the distinct logic of facilitated naturalisation and the need for a genuine, stable marital union. The nullity chapter, updated on 24 August 2023 in the search result, is the clearest official material on fraud, concealment, time limits and reapplication consequences. 50

SEM’s public route pages provide reliable current summaries for internal drafting. The ordinary-naturalisation page gives the federal residence-counting rules, while the general overview page states the three official-decision routes: ordinary naturalisation, simplified naturalisation and reinstatement. The spouse page explains when the spouse route is unavailable, the abroad-based “close ties” concept, and the evidential/document route through SEM or the Swiss representation. The third-generation page states the headline statutory requirements in plain terms. The reinstatement page gives a concise current statement of eligibility in Switzerland and abroad, including language requirements. The dual-nationality page confirms Switzerland’s unrestricted acceptance of dual nationality under Swiss law. 51

### Language evidence, civic knowledge, public order, and financial compliance

SEM’s language-requirements page confirms that applicants for ordinary and early naturalisation must prove language skills and that the framework is aligned with immigration and integration policy. The FAQ then provides the operational details: recognised certificates must appear on SEM’s current list; course-attendance confirmations and online placement tests do not suffice; proof can also rest on schooling or education in a national language; and authorities can accommodate applicants who genuinely cannot provide ordinary language proof because of disability, illness or serious literacy-related difficulties. 52

On civic knowledge and local-level assessment, the SEM handbook and FAQ are careful rather than absolutist. The Ordinance authorises cantonal knowledge tests, but only if candidates can prepare adequately and pass with the language level required for naturalisation. The handbook also explains that the cantonal investigation report should cover familiarity with Swiss living conditions and can rely on a knowledge test about Switzerland. This matters for drafting because “civic knowledge” is not a pure federal written-exam requirement; it is a broader federal concept operationalised differently at cantonal and communal level. 53

On public order and finance, the sharpest official material is still the SEM handbook. It explains how the criminal-record rules in the Ordinance are applied, including the effect of VOSTRA visibility and the suspension of a case during pending criminal proceedings. It also treats taxes, debt enforcement, loss certificates, unpaid maintenance and other financial failures as part of the applicant’s “financial reputation”. Because the handbook itself distinguishes between statutory/ordinance rules and administrative practice, later drafting should do the same. The safest formulation is that these matters are expressly relevant under the public-order and economic-participation criteria, and that SEM practice details how they are assessed. 54

## Cantonal and communal practice notes

### Canton of Zurich

The Canton of Zurich’s official ordinary-naturalisation page states that the applicant must have lived for at least two years in the same commune before filing, or, if under twenty-five, at least two years in the canton. It also sets out an explicitly staged procedure through cantonal pre-check, commune assessment, cantonal citizenship grant, SEM federal permit, and final cantonal completion of Swiss citizenship. Zurich estimates the ordinary process at about two years. The same page warns that a move can have decisive procedural consequences: if the commune has not completed its investigation, the process may stop and a fresh application may be needed; once that investigation is complete, a move within or outside the canton may be manageable. These are local procedural rules and explanations, not general federal law. 55

Zurich’s page also shows how federal integration criteria are localised in practice. It requires clean financial records, no recent social assistance unless repaid or otherwise excused, participation in social and cultural life, contacts with Swiss citizens, German at A2 written/B1 oral, and basic knowledge of Switzerland, Zurich and the local communal system. These points are partly federal minimum criteria and partly Zurich-specific articulation of how they are checked. 55

### Canton of Bern

Bern’s official naturalisation portal states a local residence requirement of two uninterrupted years in the canton and two uninterrupted years in the commune at filing. It also confirms federal residence rules, including double counting between ages eight and eighteen and half-counting of F status. Bern’s integration page repeats the federal structure but adds expressly that the commune may impose additional conditions. Its language page requires A2 written/B1 oral in the language of the administrative district, and its test page explains that Bern uses a written naturalisation test covering geography, history, democracy, the health system, work, and citizens’ rights and duties, with specified exemptions for school-educated applicants and younger minors. These are excellent examples of local implementation rather than deviations from federal law. 56

### Canton of Geneva

Geneva’s official naturalisation page states two years’ residence in the canton, including the final twelve months before filing, and insists on effective residence in Geneva throughout the entire procedure. It warns that departures abroad of more than six months, or in some cases a year, or a move to another canton before the examination of naturalisation conditions, can lead to the case being closed. Geneva also requires a valid C permit throughout the process and lists a structured set of cumulative integration conditions, including French at B1 oral/A2 written, knowledge of Switzerland and Geneva, economic participation, family self-support, and adaptation to Genevan life. 57

Geneva’s page is especially useful for separating law from practice. In a starred note, the service says it may issue a non-entry decision at registration if the file shows social assistance in the previous three years, debts exceeding CHF 1,500 in total, or tax-payment arrears of any amount. That threshold and screening approach should be treated as local administrative practice, not a federal statutory rule. Geneva also closes the ordinary procedure with an oath ceremony and warns that unjustified non-attendance may render the cantonal naturalisation order ineffective and lead to rejection. 57

### City of Zurich

The City of Zurich’s public page is brief but useful. It sends foreign-national applicants to the cantonal self-check, distinguishes ordinary from facilitated naturalisation, and reminds applicants that Switzerland permits dual nationality under Swiss law though their state of origin may not. For Swiss applicants seeking municipal citizenship, it also confirms a two-year city-residence rule. For foreign-national naturalisation work, the value of the page is mainly explanatory and signposting rather than legally operative. 58

### City of St Gallen

St Gallen’s municipal pages are a strong example of genuinely communal practice. The city states that applicants must satisfy federal, cantonal and communal residence periods and hold a C permit. It then elaborates local expectations: orderly finances, social relations in work or neighbourhood life, public-interest and local-participation awareness, knowledge of state structure and history plus local, regional and Swiss geography evidenced by a test, and good German. One page states that if the requirements are not fulfilled, applicants are advised not to file; another explains that communal and Ortsgemeinde citizenship are decided by local naturalisation councils composed of city and local-bourgeoisie representatives. These are local explanatory and procedural points, not federal generalisations. 59

## Practical legal analysis

Naturalisation and immigration status are legally related but conceptually different. A C permit is an immigration status of permanent settlement under migration law. It allows indefinite residence, but it does not make its holder Swiss, does not itself confer political rights, and is not a substitute for the multi-level citizenship decision required by public law. Naturalisation, by contrast, results in communal, cantonal and Swiss citizenship and therefore brings constitutional and political consequences. Any drafting that collapses “settled” into “ready for citizenship” is legally unsound. 60

That is why the C permit is necessary but not sufficient for ordinary naturalisation. The federal formal threshold in art. 9 BüG is only the entry gate. The substantive questions are integration, familiarity with Swiss life, and security. SEM’s handbook then shows how those headings become evidence-heavy: record extracts, tax compliance, debt enforcement, support obligations, social-assistance history, work or education, language proof, and civic and social embeddedness. In practice, a person may be perfectly secure in Swiss residence law and yet be a weak naturalisation candidate because the file is poorly prepared or because integration evidence is thin. 61

Residence continuity matters for more than the numerical ten-year minimum. The Citizenship Act counts only specific categories of lawful residence, and both the Act and local guidance show how physical presence and centre of life remain relevant. SEM’s spouse-facilitation handbook explains that extended absences and a relocation of the centre of life can defeat residence-based routes; Geneva’s guidance says prolonged absence or a move can lead to closure; Zurich links the procedural survival of an ordinary-naturalisation case to how far the local investigation has progressed. Long-term planning therefore has to look beyond the arithmetic of years and consider address stability, school/work ties, and whether the file will still be jurisdictionally viable if the person moves. 62

Integration, language and local participation matter because Swiss naturalisation is designed to test membership in a lived political community, not only lawful residence. That is why the legal framework separates successful integration from familiarity with Swiss living conditions, and why cantons and communes often use interviews, knowledge tests and evidence of social participation. Bern’s written naturalisation test, Zurich’s emphasis on local institutions, Geneva’s adaptation-to-Genevan-life criterion, and St Gallen’s focus on local social relations all demonstrate different official ways of operationalising the same federal core. The correct drafting approach is therefore to present federal integration as the baseline and local testing as the method by which some cantons and communes examine it. 63

Tax, debt, criminal record, social assistance and family-support issues create risk because they operate at several doctrinal levels at once. Criminal matters are partly hard-coded in the Ordinance; pending proceedings suspend the process. Debt, taxes and maintenance fall under wilful non-performance of important public or private obligations and therefore under public-order/integration analysis. Social assistance is expressly regulated in art. 7(3) BüV. The SEM handbook shows that, operationally, these can be decisive even where the applicant speaks the language well and has many years in Switzerland. Internal drafting should therefore resist the common temptation to treat these as merely evidential detail. They are often case-dispositive. 5

Facilitated naturalisation is not just a shortened ordinary route with fewer years. It serves a different legislative logic. The spouse route assumes a relationship to a Swiss citizen and therefore shortens formal residence requirements, but it also substitutes a highly specific substantive criterion: a real, stable marital union, or, abroad, close ties to Switzerland. The decision-maker is SEM, not the commune or canton. The spouse handbook makes plain that the stability of the marital union is not ceremonial; it goes to the heart of the right to the facilitated route and later nullity analysis. That is why this pack treats facilitated naturalisation as a separate doctrinal branch, not as an appendix to ordinary naturalisation. 64

Naturalisation planning should begin before the formal application stage because several risks cannot be cured easily once the file is filed. Language proof may need time to obtain. Social assistance may need to be repaid. Tax and debt records may need to be cleaned up. Moves may disrupt local residence requirements or even terminate an advanced case in some cantons. Children aged twelve and above must be analysed on their own age-appropriate merits, and those aged sixteen and above need written consent. In spouse facilitation, the stability and disclosure obligations around the marriage need to be understood before filing, not after an interview request arrives. The practical lesson is that “eligibility date” and “filing readiness” are not the same thing. 65

## Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

- Do not describe holding a C permit as equivalent to being eligible for Swiss citizenship. It is a necessary federal threshold for ordinary naturalisation, but not a complete readiness test. 66
- Do not ignore cantonal and communal residence requirements. Federal law mandates a local residence layer, and official cantonal sources show materially different implementations. 67
- Do not flatten ordinary and facilitated naturalisation into a single route. They have different statutory bases, competent authorities, qualifying classes and risks. 68
- Do not treat residence counting for C permits and naturalisation as identical without source support. They arise under different regimes and can diverge. 69
- Do not present local criteria or practices as if they were federal law. Geneva’s debt threshold, Bern’s written test and Zurich’s move rules are official local practice points, not nationwide blackletter rules. 70
- Do not describe naturalisation as automatic once formal criteria are met. Ordinary naturalisation still requires local support, SEM’s federal permit and a final cantonal decision. 35
- Do not omit criminal-record, debt, tax, social-assistance and maintenance-obligation issues. They are central to public-order and economic-participation analysis and often dispositive in practice.
- Do not present language evidence generically. Check the recognised-proof categories and SEM’s current recognised-certificate list, and remember that some cantons or communes may ask for more. 72
- Do not understate the spousal-union requirement in facilitated naturalisation by marriage. A genuine and stable marriage at filing and decision is central, and misstatements can later support nullity. 73
- Do not overlook post-grant nullity risk where false information or concealment is involved. This is a live legal consequence, not a theoretical one. 74
