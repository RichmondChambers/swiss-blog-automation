# Core Swiss Citizenship Legal Authority Packs

This folder contains core legal authority packs for the Swiss citizenship and naturalisation blog automation workflow.

Core packs are used by `generate_and_publish.py` to ground legal analysis, drafting and validation for blog posts that require an overview of the Swiss citizenship and naturalisation framework before moving into route-specific, issue-specific or broader thematic detail.

## Current Core Pack

legal_authority_core_swiss_citizenship_by_naturalisation_framework_pack.md

This pack should provide the foundational legal architecture for Swiss citizenship by naturalisation, including the distinction between ordinary and facilitated naturalisation, federal, cantonal and communal competence, residence requirements, C permit requirements, integration, language, public-order considerations, procedural sequencing and recurring drafting cautions.

## Purpose of the Core Folder

Use this folder for authority packs that explain the overall Swiss citizenship and naturalisation framework.

Core packs should be broad enough to support general blog posts across multiple citizenship and naturalisation routes, including:

ordinary naturalisation;
facilitated naturalisation;
naturalisation by marriage;
naturalisation for children of Swiss citizens;
naturalisation for third-generation foreign nationals;
re-naturalisation;
residence counting and continuity;
C permit requirements;

refusal, postponement, appeal and reapplication.

## Legal Framework to Cover

A core Swiss citizenship pack should explain the relationship between:

the Swiss Citizenship Act (SCA / BüG / LN);
the Swiss Citizenship Ordinance (SCO / BüV / OLN);
SEM guidance and official federal naturalisation materials;
Federal Supreme Court authority and other relevant case law;
cantonal and communal naturalisation authority guidance;
ordinary naturalisation;
facilitated naturalisation;
federal, cantonal and communal competence;
residence requirements and residence counting;
C permit requirements, where relevant;
integration and language requirements;
public-order, compliance and good-character considerations;
procedure, discretion and evidential assessment.

## Recommended Structure for Core Packs

Each core pack should, where possible, include:

a table of legal propositions;
primary legal texts;
official guidance and administrative materials;
key case law, where relevant;
cantonal and communal practice notes, where relevant;
practical legal analysis;
drafting cautions;
source list;
open questions or limitations.

## Legal Proposition Categories

Legal propositions should distinguish clearly between:

entitlement;
eligibility only;
discretion;
facilitation;
guidance;
procedure;
evidential practice;
cantonal practice;
communal practice;
unresolved or practice-sensitive points.

Do not describe guidance as law. Do not describe eligibility as an automatic right to Swiss citizenship. Do not describe discretionary or evaluative naturalisation decisions as entitlements. Do not describe cantonal or communal practice as uniform Swiss law.

## Relationship with Other Folders

Use the core pack for foundational framework points.

Use `broad/` for recurring thematic issues that apply across several citizenship routes, such as ordinary eligibility, facilitated naturalisation, C permit requirements, residence counting, integration, language evidence, procedure, refusals, postponements or appeals.

Use `route_specific/` for packs focused on a specific citizenship or naturalisation route, such as ordinary naturalisation, facilitated naturalisation by marriage, facilitated naturalisation for children of Swiss citizens, facilitated naturalisation for third-generation foreign nationals or re-naturalisation.

Use `issue_specific/` for narrower risk issues or complex sub-categories, such as interrupted residence, insufficient language evidence, integration concerns, public-order problems, social assistance, debt enforcement, cantonal objections, communal objections or procedural fairness issues.

## Important Drafting Cautions

Swiss citizenship and naturalisation should not be treated as a single uniform route.

The analysis usually depends on the naturalisation route, nationality, residence history, length and continuity of residence, C permit status, marriage or family relationship, residence counting rules, absences, integration record, language evidence, participation in economic life or education, tax compliance, debt enforcement, social assistance history, criminality, public-order concerns, cantonal procedure and communal practice.

Authority packs in this folder should help prevent public blog posts from overstating rights, treating eligibility as an entitlement to citizenship or implying that naturalisation follows automatically after a fixed number of years.
