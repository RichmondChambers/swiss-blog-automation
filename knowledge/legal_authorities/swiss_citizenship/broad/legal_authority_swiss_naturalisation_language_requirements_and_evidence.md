# Swiss Naturalisation Language Requirements and Evidence Authority Pack Suggested filename: legal_authority_swiss_naturalisation_language_requirements_and_evidence_pack.pdf

## A. Executive legal summary

Under Swiss federal citizenship law, language ability is one element of the broader integration test for
naturalisation, not a free-standing requirement divorced from the overall assessment. For ordinary
naturalisation, the applicant must show successful integration, and one of the statutory indicators of
successful integration is the ability to communicate in everyday life in a national language, orally and in
writing. The Citizenship Ordinance then fixes the federal minimum at B1 oral and A2 written. The same
federal language levels apply to facilitated naturalisation when the applicant is resident in
Switzerland. 1

Federal law recognises four principal routes to proving those language skills in Switzerland: the
applicant speaks and writes a national language as a mother tongue; the applicant completed at least
five years of compulsory schooling in a national language; the applicant completed secondary II or
tertiary education in a national language; or the applicant holds a language certificate based on a
quality-assured test. The SEM’s current guidance and manuals treat these routes as equivalent forms of
proof. 2

For facilitated naturalisation from abroad, the position is materially different. The statutory integration
criteria apply only “by analogy”, and the Ordinance’s “close ties to Switzerland” test asks, as to language,
only whether the person can communicate orally in everyday life in a national language. Current SEM
guidance for facilitated naturalisation abroad states that the in-Switzerland article 6 language-proof
regime is not applied literally, that basic oral knowledge suffices, and that the issue is assessed in a
personal interview at the Swiss representation rather than by requiring a formal certificate. 3

The SEM publishes the official list of recognised language certificates for nationality and immigration
procedures, and that list is updated regularly. Recognition is tied to the date on which the application is
filed: a certificate is recognised if it was on the list when the application was lodged, with Annex I
preserving earlier recognised certificates for older filing dates. The list is highly important in practice,
but SEM guidance says it is a tool for legal certainty and uniformity rather than an absolutely exclusive
legal universe; in principle, a competent authority may accept a non-listed certificate if it can guarantee
that the certificate meets the required quality standards. 4

There is no fixed federal rule in the current materials making language certificates automatically invalid
after a set number of months or years. The official guidance instead handles staleness by allowing the
authority to require a renewed assessment where a certificate is old and there are doubts about the
applicant’s current level. Course attendance confirmations and online placement tests do not suffice as
proof. 5

For ordinary naturalisation, cantons and communes remain important because federal law lays down
minimum standards only; cantons may set additional integration criteria, and current official local
guidance shows that ordinary naturalisation is often administered by reference to the language of the
canton, district or commune of residence. Zurich channels applicants into German proof or the cantonal
KDE test; Bern requires the language of the relevant administrative district; Vaud’s current forms focus
on French evidence; and Ticino’s official materials focus on Italian evidence. Those are local
administrative practices layered onto the federal floor, not replacements for it. 6

Special circumstances matter. Federal law requires appropriate account to be taken of disability, illness
or other major personal circumstances that make it impossible or materially harder to meet the
language and economic-participation criteria. SEM guidance gives concrete examples, including
significant learning, reading or writing difficulties, and local materials in Vaud and Ticino show that
medical evidence or other specialist evidence may be required in practice to obtain derogation or
adapted handling. 7

## B. Structural overview of language requirements and evidence

The most important structural distinction is between ordinary naturalisation, facilitated
naturalisation while resident in Switzerland, and facilitated naturalisation from abroad. In the first
two categories, the federal benchmark is the same: everyday communication in a national language at
B1 oral / A2 written, proved through one of the accepted routes in article 6 of the Citizenship
Ordinance. In the third category, the federal materials shift away from article 6’s formal proof regime
and toward an interview-based assessment of whether the applicant can communicate orally in
everyday life in a national language and otherwise has close ties to Switzerland. 8

A second distinction is between mandatory legal criteria and evidential practice. The legal criteria
are fixed in the Citizenship Act and Ordinance. The evidential routes are then operationalised by SEM
guidance, the SEM certificate list, SEM manuals, SEM checklists, and local practice. That is why future
drafting should separate: the legal standard itself; the officially recognised ways of proving it; and local
administrative handling, including interviews, communal reports, and local tests. 9

A third distinction is between federal minimum and ordinary-naturalisation local overlay. Federal
law speaks of “a national language”. But official cantonal materials for ordinary naturalisation
commonly require proof in the language used locally: German in Zurich, the language of the
administrative district in Bern, French in Vaud practice, and Italian in Ticino practice. That does not
change the federal wording, but it is highly material to preparation and document choice in ordinary
cases. It should not, however, be assumed to carry over unchanged into facilitated naturalisation
handled by the SEM. 10

A fourth distinction concerns the form of proof. The law accepts educational pathways and mother-
tongue pathways as alternatives to certificates. SEM guidance also makes clear that not every
educational document qualifies: continuing-education qualifications such as CAS or DAS do not count as
a secondary II or tertiary completion for this purpose, whereas a qualifying secondary II or tertiary
diploma in a national language does, even if obtained outside Switzerland. For applicants without such
equivalents, the recognised-certificate route is the practical default. 11

## D. Primary legal texts

The core federal legal basis is the Swiss Citizenship Act (Bürgerrechtsgesetz / Loi sur la nationalité /
Legge sulla cittadinanza). For ordinary naturalisation, article 11 requires successful integration,
familiarity with Swiss living conditions, and no threat to internal or external security. Article 12 then
defines successful integration, including the ability to communicate in everyday life in a national
language, orally and in writing, and article 12(2) requires appropriate account to be taken of disability,
illness or other major personal circumstances affecting the language and economic-life criteria. Article
12(3) allows cantons to provide further integration criteria in ordinary naturalisation. 12

For facilitated naturalisation, article 20 of the Act is the key bridge provision. It states that the
integration criteria in article 12(1) and (2) must also be met in facilitated naturalisation, and that these
conditions apply by analogy to applicants who do not reside in Switzerland. That provision is the reason
later drafting must not treat all facilitated cases as identical: the same integration provisions are carried
across, but they are then concretised differently in the Ordinance and official guidance depending on
whether the case is in Switzerland or abroad. 22

The Citizenship Ordinance (Bürgerrechtsverordnung / Ordonnance sur la nationalité / Ordinanza sulla
cittadinanza) is where the language thresholds and proof rules are fixed. Article 6 requires proof of oral
knowledge of a national language at least at B1 CEFR and written competence at least at A2 CEFR. The
same article provides the four recognised evidential bases: mother tongue; at least five years of
compulsory schooling in a national language; secondary II or tertiary education in a national language;
or a language attestation based on a language test meeting generally recognised quality standards.
Article 6(3) expressly assigns the SEM a support role in assessing language attestations and in helping
cantons devise cantonal language tests. 23

Article 9 of the Ordinance governs special circumstances. It requires the competent authority to take
the applicant’s situation into account appropriately when assessing the language, economic-life and
Swiss-living-conditions criteria, and gives examples such as physical, mental or psychological disability,
serious or long-term illness, major learning, reading or writing difficulties, caring responsibilities, and
certain first-training situations. This provision is central to any analysis of health, disability or literacy
issues affecting language evidence. 24

For residence abroad, article 11 of the Ordinance defines “close ties with Switzerland”. Its language limb
is narrower than article 6: the person must be able to communicate orally in everyday life in a national
language. That distinction, read with article 20(3) of the Act and the current SEM manuals, is the basis
for saying that a formal B1/A2 certificate model should not be transplanted uncritically into facilitated-
naturalisation cases handled from abroad. 25

The Ordinance also matters procedurally. Article 17 requires ordinary-naturalisation enquiry reports to
address the applicant’s current language skills, while article 18 provides that facilitated-naturalisation
enquiry reports for applicants in Switzerland are drawn up like ordinary enquiry reports. Article 19, by

contrast, frames facilitated-naturalisation enquiry reports for applicants abroad around public order,
constitutional values, economic participation, family integration support, and close ties to Switzerland.
That procedural architecture reinforces the substantive differences between in-Switzerland and abroad
language handling. 26

Finally, article 2 of the Ordinance links language and civic-knowledge testing. If a canton imposes a
knowledge test on Swiss geography, history, politics and society, it must ensure that the applicant can
prepare adequately and that the test is passable by a person who has the oral and written language
skills required for naturalisation. The legal point is important: local knowledge testing must not operate
as a hidden, unlegislated language threshold above the applicable naturalisation standard. 27

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The SEM’s public language requirements page states that the required language level is a minimum
requirement, that the SEM publishes the list of recognised language certificates, and that further
details should be obtained from the competent cantonal authority. That page is useful because it
confirms both the federal minimum nature of the standard and the continued importance of local
implementation. 28

The SEM’s public citizenship FAQ provides current, practical guidance for both ordinary and facilitated
naturalisation. For ordinary naturalisation, it states that the applicant needs B1 oral and A2 written in a
national language and notes that cantons may require higher language levels. For facilitated
naturalisation in Switzerland, it repeats the same B1/A2 requirement and lists the same categories of
accepted proof. For facilitated naturalisation abroad, it instead emphasises oral communication in a
national language as one element of “close ties”. The same FAQ also states expressly that course
attendance confirmations and online assessments are insufficient, and that disability, illness or major
literacy barriers may justify adapted handling supported by specialist evidence. 29

The SEM’s current list of recognised language certificates, dated 1 April 2026, is the operative federal
reference for recognised certificate-based proof. It states that it covers both nationality and
immigration procedures, that a certificate is recognised if it was on the list when the application was
filed, and that Annex I preserves former recognised certificates for the relevant historical filing periods.
The list is updated every three months. 30

As at 1 April 2026, the list recognises certificate families across the national languages. For German, the
list includes the SEM’s own fide instruments, Goethe certificates, the Swiss Digital Language Test SDS,
telc certificates, TestDaF / Digital TestDaF, and ÖSD certificates. For French, it includes fide instruments,
DELF and DELF Junior, TCF variants subject to the list’s specific notes, TEF / TEFAQ variants, and Diplômes
de Français Professionnel Affaires. For Italian, it includes fide instruments and CELI certificates. For
Romansh, it includes the official examens da rumantsch route, with oral levels up to B1 and written
levels up to A2. Because the list changes quarterly and contains route-specific notes, later drafting
should refer to the list date and not rely on a static catalogue. 31

The list’s footnotes and the current fide materials are especially important for the fide language
passport. Current official fide pages describe the language passport as an official document evidencing
language skills in the Swiss national languages and state that it is obtained via the fide-Test, fide-Test
edu, fide dossier or examens da rumantsch. The SEM list adds that the fide dossier is a simplified route
for people who already have at least B1-level skills but lack a recognised proof, and that successful
completion leads to a B1 language passport. Current fide guidance says the dossier route is for
persons aged 16 or over who already have B1+ skills and certain supporting evidence. 32

The SEM’s separate FAQ on recognised certificates contains several practical rules that are highly
relevant to refusal-risk analysis and document checking. It states that the list is designed to provide
legal certainty and uniform practice; that listed certificates meet the accepted quality criteria; that
authorities may in principle deviate only if they themselves can guarantee the necessary quality
standard; that an apparently similar certificate title is not enough unless it matches the listed certificate;
that certificate levels must correspond to the CEFR rather than vague labels such as “good” or “very
good”; that where reading and writing levels differ, the lower or adjusted written level rules apply; and
that even if a candidate failed the exam overall, a sufficient oral or written component from a
recognised exam can still be used, including in combination with result sheets from other recognised
certificates. 33

The SEM’s manuals add useful precision to the statutory equivalents. They explain that five years of
compulsory schooling or a qualifying secondary II or tertiary qualification in a national language need
not have been completed in Switzerland; that the applicant should produce an attestation showing
which years counted as compulsory schooling; that post-tertiary continuing-education awards such as
CAS or DAS do not count as qualifying educational completion for language-proof purposes; and that a
diploma as translator or interpreter in a Swiss national language is recognised even if issued by a
foreign university or university of applied sciences. 34

## F. Cantonal and communal practice notes

Official cantonal materials confirm that ordinary naturalisation is locally sensitive in language-
evidence terms, even though the federal minimum is framed as competence in “a national language”.
The practical question is usually not only whether the applicant can produce a federally acceptable
proof, but whether the proof and interview performance fit the linguistic expectations of the canton,
district or commune in which the ordinary-naturalisation case is being processed. 35

In Zurich, the official KDE page states that naturalisation in the canton requires German, with B1 for
listening/speaking and A2 for reading/writing. The page also lists the standard federal exemption
pathways: German as mother tongue, current schooling in German, five years of compulsory schooling
in German, a secondary II qualification in German, a tertiary qualification in German, or an already held
recognised German certificate. If none of those apply, the applicant takes the KDE (cantonal German
test for naturalisation). The City of Zurich’s page states that the applicant may file the naturalisation
application even without a language certificate, after which the city can arrange the KDE; it also lists the
certificate issuers it accepts in local practice and notes exceptions where proof cannot be produced for
health or psychological reasons. 36

In Bern, the canton’s official naturalisation page makes the local overlay especially explicit: an applicant
for naturalisation in Bern must have knowledge of the language of the administrative district in
which they live. It confirms the federal minimum of A2 written / B1 oral, but then states that no extra
language test is needed if the applicant’s mother tongue is the district language, if the applicant
completed at least five years of compulsory school in the district language, or if the applicant obtained
a vocational, university or gymnasium qualification in the district language. Otherwise, a recognised
certificate is required and is submitted as part of the application dossier. 37

In Vaud, current official process material states that the ordinary-naturalisation applicant must be
integrated into the Vaud community, including through knowledge of French and civic rights, and that
the communal authority prepares an enquiry report and conducts a hearing in ordinary-naturalisation
cases. Current official Vaud forms used on the canton’s website ask the applicant to indicate whether
French is the person’s mother tongue, whether at least five years of compulsory schooling were

completed in French, whether a secondary II or tertiary qualification was obtained in French, or whether
a qualifying French diploma route is held. If none of those apply, the form states that a fide language
passport must be provided. The same form recognises local derogation practice under article 9 of the
Ordinance through either a detailed medical certificate or an attestation from the literacy association
Lire et Écrire. Lausanne’s official page adds that the municipal naturalisation office organises
interviews and knowledge tests. These materials are best treated as current evidential practice notes,
not as autonomous law. 38

In Ticino, the official cantonal page states the language requirement as written Italian A2 and spoken
Italian B1. It says that knowledge of Italian is treated as shown where the applicant has CELI or FIDE
certification, speaks and writes Italian as a mother tongue, completed at least five years of Italian-
medium compulsory schooling, or completed a secondary II or tertiary qualification in Italian, in
principle in Ticino. The same page also describes a formal exemption mechanism where major personal
circumstances, such as serious illness or disability, prevent learning Italian: the applicant may apply to
the naturalisation service with a reasoned request supported by medical evidence, and the service
decides whether to grant total or partial exemption. 39

These local materials also show the practical relationship between formal proof and interview
performance. Zurich and Bern prominently organise formal testing or documentary proof at the front
end. Vaud couples documentary proof with a communal hearing. In all of those settings, language is
both a documentary matter and a lived feature of the applicant’s interaction with the authorities. That is
entirely consistent with SEM guidance allowing a fresh assessment where an old certificate no longer
appears reliable. 40

## G. Practical legal analysis

The first practical point is to separate route by route. For ordinary naturalisation, later content should
treat B1 oral / A2 written as the federal minimum, check immediately whether the canton requires the
locally used language or a higher level, and then choose the cleanest evidence route: mother tongue,
schooling, education, or recognised certificate. Where relying on schooling or education rather than a
certificate, the file should include documents that clearly identify the language of instruction and, for
compulsory schooling, the relevant years. That is especially important because SEM guidance expects a
specific attestation for the schooling route, and because some educational awards, such as CAS or DAS,
do not count. 41

The second point is that facilitated naturalisation while resident in Switzerland is not a “lighter”
language case. Current federal materials for spouses in Switzerland apply the same B1/A2 framework
as ordinary naturalisation, and the SEM’s current article 21 checklist requires either school/education
evidence or a qualifying certificate or language passport where the applicant’s mother tongue is not a
Swiss national language. This route should therefore be prepared like a federal certificate-equivalence
case, even though the residence and family-link conditions are different. 42

The third point is that facilitated naturalisation from abroad must be drafted differently. Future
content should not say, without qualification, that facilitated naturalisation always requires a B1 oral/A2
written certificate. The materials for residence abroad instead focus on the applicant’s ability to
communicate orally in everyday life in a national language and on the Swiss representation’s interview.
The preparation task is therefore interview-centred: oral communication, familiarity with Swiss life and
institutions, and proof of close ties. A formal certificate may still be sensible as supportive evidence in
some files, but current official guidance does not make it a mandatory federal requirement for this
category. 43

The fourth point concerns timing and certificate selection. The SEM list is updated every three
months, and recognition is locked to the filing date. A certificate that is recognised today may be
removed later, but that does not necessarily matter if it was recognised when the application was filed.
Conversely, a certificate that merely looks similar to a recognised certificate may fail if the title or issuer
does not match the list. That makes documentary checking critical. Internal drafting should therefore
tell later writers to record the list date used, preserve a copy of the relevant list version where possible,
and confirm the exact certificate title and issuing body. 44

The fifth point concerns staleness and interview mismatch. Current official guidance allows the
authority to require fresh assessment where a certificate is old and the present language level is
doubtful. That means a technically valid certificate may still be risky if the applicant’s present spoken or
written performance is materially weaker than the certificate suggests. In practical-preparation content,
this should be framed as a refusal or delay risk rather than a formal invalidity rule. For ordinary
naturalisation, that risk arises especially at communal interview; for facilitated naturalisation abroad,
the personal interview is the core assessment itself. 45

The sixth point concerns partial scores and non-standard proof situations. The SEM FAQ says that if
the applicant failed an exam overall but obtained a sufficient oral or written part, the sufficient part may
still be used, and result sheets from different recognised certificates may be combined. It also explains
how to treat divergent reading and writing scores when one written sub-skill is stronger than the other.
Those rules matter for marginal files and mean that document review should extend beyond the pass/
fail headline on the certificate. 46

The seventh point concerns children and schooling pathways. SEM guidance provides alternative
evidential routes for included foreign children aged 12 to 15 who cannot produce a standard certificate
and have not yet reached the five-year compulsory-schooling threshold in a national language: school
reports may be used, and for those in Swiss international schools the school may provide a reasoned
evaluation of the child’s language competence together with school records. Separately, the current
SEM checklist for third-generation facilitated naturalisation does not publicly foreground a language
certificate, which appears consistent with that route’s built-in Swiss compulsory-schooling requirement.
That should nonetheless be expressed carefully: the integration criteria still apply, but the
administrative proof route may differ because the statutory schooling conditions already do part of the
evidential work. 47

The eighth point concerns special circumstances. Where literacy, disability, illness or other major
personal circumstances are genuinely in issue, the file should be built early around causation and
evidence. Official guidance expects specialist support such as medical reports, literacy evidence, course
attestations, or other documentation showing why the ordinary proof route cannot reasonably be met.
In local practice, Vaud and Ticino currently illustrate that this is not merely theoretical. The argument
should be framed under article 12(2) of the Act and article 9 of the Ordinance, not as a general
discretion to overlook language requirements. 48

## H. Drafting cautions and source list

Future drafting should not say that every naturalisation route requires the same language certificate.
The clearest legal distinction is between cases in Switzerland, where article 6 of the Ordinance governs
B1/A2 proof, and facilitated-naturalisation cases from abroad, where the official materials focus on oral
communication in a national language and interview-based assessment of close ties. 49

Future drafting should also not say that any language certificate is acceptable if it shows B1/A2. The
safe rule is that the certificate should appear on the SEM’s recognised list for the date of filing, unless
the competent authority has itself accepted a non-listed certificate because it can guarantee the
required quality standard. Similar-sounding certificates, generic course letters, and online placement
tests should not be described as sufficient. 50

It would also be inaccurate to say that certificates “expire” after a fixed federal period. The safer
formulation is that current federal materials do not prescribe a single universal expiry rule, but an old
certificate can still lead to a request for fresh proof if the authority doubts the applicant’s current level.
That is the formulation best supported by the current official materials. 20

Future content should avoid collapsing language issues into generic integration prose. The federal law
makes language one integration criterion, but the evidential architecture around article 6 of the
Ordinance is detailed and formal. Later drafting should therefore deal expressly with minimum level,
accepted equivalents, recognised certificates, filing-date sensitivity, old certificates, local overlays, and
special-circumstance handling. 51

Future content should also avoid overstating local practice. Cantonal and communal materials are
highly relevant in ordinary naturalisation, where federal law fixes minimum standards and cantons may
add criteria. But those local overlays should not automatically be transplanted into facilitated
naturalisation, which is federally decided and follows its own SEM guidance. 52

Finally, future content should not say that only German, French and Italian count. The federal legislation
speaks of a national language, and the SEM recognised-certificate list includes a Romansh route. At
the same time, ordinary-naturalisation practice in many cantons or communes is locally language-
specific. The correct internal formulation is therefore that federal law uses the category of “national
language”, while ordinary-naturalisation preparation must be checked against the relevant canton’s or
commune’s local language practice. 53
