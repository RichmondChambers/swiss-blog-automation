# Swiss Ordinary Naturalisation Eligibility Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

Ordinary naturalisation in Switzerland is a federal–cantonal–communal process for acquiring Swiss
citizenship by o cial decision, not a mere immigration-status upgrade. Constitutionally, the
Confederation sets minimum rules and issues the federal naturalisation licence, while the cantons
remain primarily responsible for ordinary naturalisation and the applicant ultimately acquires
communal, cantonal and Swiss citizenship when the cantonal naturalisation decision becomes nal. The
statutory core sits in the Federal Act on Swiss Citizenship of 20 June 2014, especially Articles 9 to 19,
supplemented by the Citizenship Ordinance and extensive SEM guidance. SEM’s handbook expressly
describes ordinary naturalisation as operating on a federalist model requiring cooperation between
Confederation, cantons and communes. 1

For the ordinary route, the federal formal requirements are strict but limited in number: the applicant
must hold a settlement permit (permis C / C permit) when the application is led and must prove ten
years’ residence in Switzerland, of which three years must fall within the ve years immediately before
 ling. Years spent in Switzerland between the ages of 8 and 18 count double, but the actual stay must
still total at least six years. Residence counting is not identical to immigration history in the abstract:
stays with B or C permits count in full; time with provisional admission (F permit) counts only by half;
and time with L, N or S documents is not counted. A short absence abroad with intent to return does
not interrupt residence, but residence is deemed abandoned on deregistration or where the person
actually lives abroad for more than six months; the ordinance treats a stay abroad of up to one year for
an employer posting or for education/training as a short absence with intent to return. 2

The C permit is therefore necessary for ordinary naturalisation, but it is not su cient. Federal material
requirements go materially further: the applicant must be successfully integrated, familiar with Swiss
living conditions, and must not endanger Switzerland’s internal or external security. Integration is
shown in particular by respect for public security and order, respect for constitutional values, ability to
communicate in a national language in everyday life, participation in economic life or education, and
support for the integration of family members. The ordinance concretises those criteria. It xes the
federal minimum language threshold at B1 oral and A2 written in a national language, and it sets the
accepted ways to prove language ability, including mother tongue, ve years of compulsory schooling
in a national language, secondary II or tertiary education in a national language, or a recognised
language certi cate. SEM publishes the list of recognised language certi cates and con rms that
cantons may impose higher language requirements than the federal minimum. 3

Public-order and good-conduct issues are broader than criminal convictions alone. Under the
ordinance, a person is not successfully integrated if they seriously or repeatedly breach legal rules or
o cial orders, wilfully fail to ful l important public or private obligations, or publicly endorse serious
crimes of the sort listed in the ordinance. The ordinance also ties ordinary naturalisation to the VOSTRA
authorities’ criminal-record extract and requires SEM to suspend the procedure where criminal
proceedings are pending. SEM’s handbook then gives important administrative content to “important
public or private obligations”: recent unpaid taxes, wage garnishment, certi cates of loss, and non-
payment of family-maintenance obligations are treated as serious problems; social assistance received

in the three years before ling or during the procedure blocks ordinary naturalisation unless fully
repaid, subject to accommodation for disability, serious illness, and other weighty personal
circumstances. O cial guidance also treats economic participation and education as alternative ways to
satisfy the statutory criterion, while insisting on the applicant’s ability, in principle, to support
themselves and their family. 4

Cantonal and communal residence rules remain essential. Federal law requires cantonal legislation to
prescribe a local minimum residence period in the range of two to ve years, but the way each canton
structures that requirement varies. O cial cantonal guidance shows real di erences: Zurich requires
two years in the same municipality before ling; Bern requires two uninterrupted years in both canton
and commune; Vaud requires two years in canton including the year immediately before ling and
notes that some communes also require one year’s residence in the commune; Geneva requires two
years in canton including the last twelve months and e ective residence in the canton throughout the
procedure; Ticino requires ve years in the canton and three years in the commune, including the two
years immediately before ling. Cantons and communes also di er in the way they assess civic
knowledge, language and local integration, including standardised tests, interviews and commune-run
stages. 5

The procedure is sequential. The application is led with the authority designated by cantonal law. The
canton investigates and, where cantonal law so provides, the commune also decides or gives its
assurance. Only if the canton and, where applicable, the commune support naturalisation is the le
transmitted to SEM. SEM then issues the federal naturalisation licence if the formal and material federal
conditions are met. The canton must make its nal decision within one year of that licence, and Swiss
citizenship is acquired only when the cantonal decision enters into legal force. Rejection must be
reasoned. Importantly, SEM’s handbook states that the federal naturalisation licence does not itself
create an entitlement to naturalisation. Ordinary naturalisation should therefore not be described as
automatic once the headline conditions are met. A prudent planning approach starts well before ling
and treats residence counting, C-permit timing, local residence, language proof, tax and debt clean-up,
social-assistance history, and family-support compliance as pre-application workstreams rather than
matters to sort out after ling. 6

## Structural overview of ordinary Swiss naturalisation

Legal basis and legal nature. Ordinary naturalisation is governed principally by Article 38 of the
Federal Constitution, the Federal Act on Swiss Citizenship, and the Citizenship Ordinance. It is an
acquisition of citizenship by o cial decision, not a visa or permit conversion. Because Swiss citizenship
is tripartite, the applicant acquires communal and cantonal citizenship together with Swiss citizenship
when the cantonal decision becomes nal. The Confederation supplies minimum law and the federal
licence; the cantons and communes handle the ordinary route on the ground. 7

Formal federal eligibility. At ling, the applicant must hold a C permit and show ten years’ residence in
Switzerland, including three years in the ve years immediately before ling. A special ordinary-route
rule applies to registered partners of Swiss citizens under Article 10 BüG, allowing ve years in
Switzerland, one of them immediately before ling, plus three years of registered partnership. Years
spent in Switzerland between ages 8 and 18 count double, subject to a six-year actual-stay oor. 8

Residence counting. Countable residence includes stays with B or C permits, provisionally admitted F
status at half value, and certain DFAE legitimacy-card / Ci situations. Non-countable residence includes
at least L, N and S status in the o cial SEM guidance located. A short absence abroad does not
interrupt residence, but deregistration or living abroad for more than six months is deemed an

abandonment of residence; the ordinance preserves continuity for up to one year abroad on an
employer posting or for education/training. 9

Integration, language and civic familiarity. Federal law requires successful integration and familiarity
with Swiss living conditions. The ordinance de nes familiarity with Swiss living conditions by reference
to basic knowledge of Swiss geography, history, politics and society, participation in social and cultural
life, and contacts with Swiss persons. The federal minimum language threshold is B1 oral and A2
written in a national language, with speci ed evidential routes. Cantons may impose higher language
requirements and may require knowledge tests, though the ordinance requires they make preparation
possible and set a test passable with the language level needed for naturalisation. 10

Public order, nances and family obligations. Respect for public security and order includes serious
or repeated legal breaches, wilful non-ful lment of important public or private obligations, and relevant
criminal-record entries. SEM suspends ordinary naturalisation where criminal proceedings are pending.
Economic participation is met where the person can cover living costs and maintenance obligations
through income, assets or third-party bene ts to which there is a legal entitlement, or is in education or
training. Social assistance in the three years before ling or during the procedure blocks eligibility
unless fully repaid, subject to accommodation for major personal circumstances. SEM guidance treats
taxes, debt enforcement, certi cates of loss and unpaid maintenance as major risk areas, but those
points should be identi ed as o cial administrative practice built on the broader public-order criterion
rather than stand-alone federal statutory wording. 11

Cantonal and communal residence rules. Federal law requires cantonal legislation to set a local
minimum residence period between two and ve years. In practice, cantons translate that into their
own canton and commune residence requirements, and those local rules can be outcome-
determinative. Moving during the procedure can be fatal if the originating authorities have not yet
completed their investigations, although Swiss federal law preserves original competence where the
canton and commune have already fully examined the federal material conditions. 12

Procedure and decision-making. The designated local or cantonal authority receives the application,
investigates the case and, where necessary, conducts local interviews or tests. The le reaches SEM only
if canton and, where applicable, commune support naturalisation. SEM then decides on the federal
licence. The canton must then take the nal naturalisation decision within one year. Rejection must be
reasoned. Swiss citizenship is acquired only on the nal cantonal decision becoming legally e ective.

Post-decision vulnerability. False statements or concealment of material facts can lead to nullity of the
naturalisation. SEM may declare a naturalisation void within two years of learning the relevant facts
and, in any event, within eight years of acquisition, with the statutory quali cations set out in Article 36
BüG. This is a signi cant drafting caution for content on evidence, disclosure and timing. 14

O     cial guidance and administrative materials
SEM’s handbook on citizenship, chapter 3 on ordinary naturalisation, is the most important o cial
interpretative source located for this pack. It explains the federalist structure, the sequence of the
cantonal/communal and federal stages, the countable residence statuses, the public-order and
criminal-record framework, the practical content of tax and debt compliance, the language and civic-
familiarity criteria, the treatment of social assistance, and the proposition that the federal naturalisation
licence does not itself create an entitlement to a nal naturalisation grant. For internal drafting, it
should be treated as the principal o cial administrative guide sitting beneath the statute and
ordinance. 35

SEM’s public page on ordinary naturalisation is a concise o cial summary of the ordinary route. It
con rms the ten-year residence requirement, the C-permit requirement, the double-counting of years
between ages 8 and 18, the countable and non-countable residence categories, the existence of
additional cantonal and communal minimum residence rules, the role of the canton in preparing the
investigation report, and the need for federal, cantonal and often communal approval. It is useful as a
checked public-facing con rmation of the core statutory framework. 36

SEM’s FAQ on Swiss citizenship is especially useful for operational drafting points. It con rms that the
Confederation lays down only minimum rules for ordinary naturalisation, that the cantons may add
further naturalisation conditions, that the formal conditions must already be met at the time of ling,
that C status is the required immigration status for the ordinary route, that years with N do not count
and years with F count only by half, and that the federal language minimum is B1 oral and A2 written. It
also con rms the special ordinary-route rule for registered partnerships with Swiss citizens. 37

SEM’s language-requirements page and the associated list of recognised language certi cates are the
key o cial sources on language evidence. They explain that a national language is required for ordinary
naturalisation, that the published list of accepted certi cates is regularly updated, and that the federal
levels are minimum requirements. The recognised-certi cates list should be checked afresh when
generating later practical content, because accepted certi cates and their validity can change
administratively even where the underlying legal threshold remains constant. 38

The o cial SEM handbook further explains the practical content of economic participation. It states that
participation in economic life and acquisition of education are alternative criteria of equal value, that
the person should in principle be able to support themselves and their family in the foreseeable future,
and that nancial independence can be shown through employment income, assets, or third-party
bene ts to which there is a legal entitlement. It also notes that persons with assets or pension income
are not automatically excluded. That guidance is important when later drafting about retirees, students,
carers and working-poor applicants. 39

The handbook is also the main o cial source located for debt, tax and family-maintenance issues. It
states that tax arrears can justify refusal of the federal licence, that wage garnishment prevents
naturalisation until lifted, that recent certi cates of loss are generally an obstacle, and that non-
payment of family maintenance and support obligations counts against nancial good standing. These
are not stand-alone statutory conditions in the same way that the C permit or the ten-year residence
rule are; they are SEM’s operational reading of the statutory public-order and economic-participation
criteria and should always be presented as such. 40

## Cantonal and communal practice notes

Zurich. Zurich’s o cial cantonal guidance says the applicant must have a valid C permit, ten years’
residence in Switzerland, and two years in the same municipality before ling. It also sets out canton-
speci c operational expectations: no relevant criminal-record entries or pending criminal proceedings,
no unpaid entries in the debt-enforcement register during the last ve years, no social assistance in the
last three years or during the procedure unless repaid or excused in speci ed situations, German at A2
written/B1 oral, and knowledge of Switzerland, the canton and Zurich municipal institutions. Zurich also
runs a cantonal German test and a standardised basic-knowledge test. These points are a mix of local
rule, local procedure and local evidential practice; they should never be universalised across
Switzerland. 41

Bern. Bern’s o cial portal states that the applicant must have held a C permit and lived ten years in
Switzerland, with three of those years in the ve years before ling; the canton additionally requires two
uninterrupted years in the canton and two uninterrupted years in the commune at the ling date. Bern
assesses integration by looking at public order, constitutional values, everyday communication in
German or French, economic participation or education, family integration, familiarity with Swiss living
conditions, social and cultural participation and contacts with Swiss persons. Bern also requires a
written naturalisation test and links language proof to the language of the administrative district. These
are local implementation rules and practices under the federal framework. 42

Vaud. Vaud’s o cial guidance requires ten years in Switzerland, three years in Switzerland during the
 ve years before ling, two years in Vaud including the year immediately before ling, and e ective
residence in Switzerland throughout the procedure and in Vaud until the end of the communal stage.
Some Vaud communes additionally require one year’s residence in the commune. Vaud requires French
at B1 oral/A2 written, generally via the de language passport unless an o cial exemption applies; it
also has a knowledge test on Switzerland, the canton and the region, formally organised by the
commune. Vaud further states that, in the ve years before ling and during the procedure, the
applicant must have no tax debts, no debt-enforcement proceedings above CHF 1,500, no wage
garnishment and no certi cates of loss, and no insertion-income social assistance in the three years
before ling or during the procedure unless fully repaid. These are highly material local rules for Vaud-
speci c drafting. 43

Geneva. Geneva’s o cial naturalisation page is unusually detailed. It requires ten years’ residence in
Switzerland, two years in the canton including the twelve months immediately before ling, and
e ective residence in the canton throughout the entire procedure. It expressly states that the C permit
must remain valid throughout the procedure. It also lists cumulative and imperative integration
conditions including public order, no threat to Swiss security, French at B1 oral/A2 written, adequate
knowledge of Swiss and Genevan geography/history/politics/social features, economic participation or
education, self-support for the applicant and dependants, no social assistance in the previous three
years or during the procedure unless fully repaid, links showing adaptation to Genevan life, respect for
the federal and Genevan constitutions, and support for family integration. Geneva further warns that
the service may issue a non-entry decision where, at le-registration stage, it nds recent social
assistance, total debts above CHF 1,500, or unpaid taxes of any amount. It also requires an oath
ceremony at the end of the cantonal process and says unjusti ed non-attendance can make the State
Council’s decree lapse. These are canton-speci c rules and practices, not general Swiss law. 44

Ticino. Ticino’s o cial page for ordinary naturalisation of foreign nationals with a C permit requires ten
years in Switzerland with B, C or F permits, with F counted by half and years between 8 and 18 doubled
subject to a six-year actual stay; ve years in Ticino; and three years in the commune of application
including the two years immediately before ling. Ticino’s integration criteria go beyond the federal
minimum in locally expressed terms: Italian at A2 written/B1 oral; knowledge of history, geography and
civics; social and cultural participation; economic participation or education; ties with Swiss citizens;
familiarity with Swiss living conditions; and support for family integration. Its ethical-legal conditions
include respect for federal and cantonal constitutional values, no criminal convictions on the Swiss
criminal-record extract, no open criminal proceedings, no enforcement proceedings, full payment of
municipal/cantonal/federal taxes including provisional and nal amounts, and no social assistance
during the ten years before ling, at ling, or during the procedure unless repaid before application.
Ticino also records possible derogations for certain personal circumstances. These are markedly stricter
local rules than the federal baseline and therefore need canton-speci c treatment in future content.

No stand-alone communal guidance was relied on as primary authority for this pack. Where commune-
speci c practice matters, the o cial material located tends to appear on cantonal portals, especially in
Zurich and Vaud, which explain commune-run tests, commune residence rules, or commune-stage
procedure. 46

## Practical legal analysis

Ordinary naturalisation should be drafted as a citizenship-acquisition process with its own legal
architecture, not as the nal step of migration status. Immigration status remains highly relevant
because the federal law requires a C permit and only counts speci c residence statuses, but the legal
question is whether the applicant quali es for communal, cantonal and Swiss citizenship under
citizenship law, not whether they are simply well placed in permit law. That is why the statute speaks in
terms of integration, civic familiarity and state security, and why the nal legal e ect is acquisition of
the communal and cantonal citizenships together with Swiss citizenship. 47

The C permit is therefore best understood as a gateway requirement. It determines whether the
ordinary route can even be led under federal law, but it does not answer whether the applicant is
naturalisation-ready. A person may hold a C permit and still fail because their countable residence is
insu cient, because their recent residence continuity is broken, because they lack the required local
residence in a canton or commune, because they do not meet language or civic-knowledge
expectations, or because debt, tax, criminal-record or social-assistance issues undermine the public-
order and integration assessment. The practical drafting implication is simple: content that treats
“holding a C permit” as shorthand for ordinary-naturalisation eligibility is legally inaccurate. 48

Residence planning is often decisive. Swiss citizenship law has its own counting rules: F status counts
only by half; non-countable statuses such as L and N do not contribute; adolescence can be double-
counted; absences abroad can break continuity; and the law requires three years in the ve years
immediately before ling. On top of that, cantons superimpose their own local-residence clocks. The
result is that a person may be “long-term resident” in an everyday sense and still be unready for ling in
the relevant canton or commune. This is particularly important for applicants who have moved between
cantons, spent time on non-countable permits, or need to rely on adolescent double-counting. 49

Integration in ordinary naturalisation is not reducible to a general impression of “good integration”. The
law and ordinance separate out several distinct strands: language, civic familiarity, social and cultural
participation, contact with Swiss persons, constitutional values, public order, family integration, and
economic participation or education. In practice, cantons often operationalise those strands by
requiring knowledge tests, local-language tests, questionnaires, commune interviews or documentary
self-declarations. The fact that some cantons use tests does not mean the test is the whole assessment:
Vaud expressly says the knowledge test is only one element of the overall naturalisation process, while
Zurich and Bern structure the route through both language and civics testing. Future drafting should
therefore describe tests as evidential tools inside a wider integration assessment, not as a universal or
exhaustive federal requirement. 50

Financial compliance is one of the most signi cant risk areas and should be treated proportionately but
seriously. The black-letter federal basis comes from public order and economic participation; the
concrete operational content comes largely from SEM guidance and cantonal rules. SEM guidance links
tax arrears, debt enforcement, wage garnishment, certi cates of loss and unpaid maintenance to
failure of the public-order or nancial-integration assessment. Cantonal authorities often turn that
guidance into express local lters, including debt thresholds or longer no-social-assistance periods.
Geneva and Vaud both use a CHF 1,500 debt threshold in their o cial guidance, while Ticino uses a

notably stricter ten-year social-assistance lookback. These examples illustrate why later content should
distinguish between federal black-letter law, SEM administrative practice, and stricter local rules. 51

The procedural structure itself a ects eligibility analysis. The application is not sent to SEM until canton
and, where relevant, commune support it. That means local refusal risk is not merely cosmetic; it
determines whether the le reaches the federal stage at all. Conversely, even after SEM issues the
federal licence, the nal naturalisation decision still lies at cantonal level and the federal licence does
not itself create an entitlement to be naturalised. In practical terms, “eligibility” for ordinary
naturalisation should be drafted as a layered concept: federal ling eligibility, local acceptance eligibility,
and nal grant readiness. That layered approach also captures post- ling risk, such as moving house
too early, losing local-residence continuity, accruing tax or debt problems, receiving social assistance
during the procedure, or becoming the subject of criminal proceedings. 52

Finally, planning should begin before ling. The lookback periods themselves show why: social
assistance has a three-year federal lookback, recent tax arrears and debt-enforcement issues commonly
trigger scrutiny, local residence rules may require one to ve years in a canton or commune, and
language/civics evidence often takes time to obtain. A good internal drafting model is therefore to
present ordinary naturalisation readiness as a pre-application audit of residence counting, settlement-
permit timing, local residence, language proof, civic-knowledge evidence, public-order history, and
  nancial compliance, with clear warnings that false or incomplete disclosure can produce nullity even
after grant. 53

## Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

- Do not describe a C permit as equivalent to eligibility for ordinary Swiss naturalisation. Federal
        law makes it necessary at ling, but the material conditions remain separate and substantial.

- Do not omit cantonal and communal residence requirements. The federal statute leaves room
        for local residence periods, and o cial cantonal guidance shows major variation. 55
- Do not treat residence counting for permits and naturalisation as interchangeable. Swiss
        citizenship law has its own counting rules, including half-credit for F status and non-counting of
        L/N/S in the o cial guidance located. 56
- Do not present language evidence generically. Check the federal proof routes and the current
        SEM list of recognised certi cates, then verify whether the canton channels applicants into a
        local language test or uses stricter evidential practice. 57
- Do not reduce integration to language alone. Civic familiarity, social/cultural participation,
        contacts with Swiss persons, constitutional values, family integration and economic
        participation/education are also part of the legal analysis. 58
- Do not present tax, debt, criminal-record, social-assistance and maintenance issues as optional
        side matters. They are central risk areas in both SEM guidance and cantonal practice. 59
- Do not say ordinary naturalisation is automatic once formal criteria are met. O cial SEM
        guidance states that the federal naturalisation licence itself does not create an entitlement to
        naturalisation. 60
- Do not overstate cantonal examples as federal law. Geneva’s debt threshold, Vaud’s commune
        rule, and Ticino’s ten-year no-social-assistance rule are local rules or practices, not generally
        applicable Swiss-wide propositions. 61

- Do not forget child-speci c rules where family applications are involved. Children may be
       included, but from age 12 the material conditions are assessed independently and age-
       appropriately, and from age 16 the child must declare their own will in writing. 14
- Do not ignore post-grant vulnerability. False statements or concealment of material facts can
       lead to nullity after grant. 14
