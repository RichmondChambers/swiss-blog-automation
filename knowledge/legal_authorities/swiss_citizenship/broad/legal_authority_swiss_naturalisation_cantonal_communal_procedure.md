# Swiss naturalisation authority pack on cantonal and communal procedure Suggested filename: legal_authority_swiss_naturalisation_cantonal_communal_procedure_pack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

## A. Executive legal summary. This pack focuses on ordinary naturalisation, because that is the route

in which cantonal and communal administration plays the largest procedural role. At constitutional
level, the Confederation sets minimum rules for cantonal naturalisation and grants the federal
naturalisation authorisation; the cantons implement federal law and retain broad organisational
autonomy; communes may also have law-making and decision-making powers where cantonal law
gives them that role. The result is a layered system: federal minimum standards, cantonal procedure,
and commune-level enquiry or approval where the canton so provides. 1

At federal level, the core ordinary-naturalisation conditions are: a valid settlement permit C at filing;
generally 10 years’ residence in Switzerland, with time spent between ages 8 and 18 counting double
but with at least six years’ actual residence; successful integration; familiarity with Swiss living
conditions; and no threat to Switzerland’s internal or external security. Federal law also allows cantons
to adopt additional integration criteria and requires cantonal legislation to impose a cantonal/
communal residence period of between two and five years. 2

The federal authorisation is a prerequisite, not the final grant of citizenship. In ordinary
naturalisation, the application is filed with the authority designated by cantonal law; the canton and,
where cantonal law provides, the commune examine the file first; only then is the dossier sent to the
State Secretariat for Migration (SEM). If the federal formal and material requirements are met, SEM
issues the federal authorisation and returns the file to the canton for the final cantonal decision. Swiss,
cantonal and communal citizenship are acquired only when the cantonal naturalisation decision enters
into force. 3

Federal law does not impose a single nationwide model for interviews, local civic tests, communal
reports, fee levels, or total processing time. The Citizenship Ordinance allows a canton to require a
knowledge test and regulates the contents of the cantonal enquiry report, but the detailed form of
interviews, communal hearings, local reports, and document checklists is left to cantonal procedure and
local administration. That is why official canton and commune sources show wide variation in practice.

For drafting and advisory purposes, the safest formulation is therefore: federal law sets the floor;
cantonal law structures the process; communal actors often perform the local fact-finding and, in
some cantons, confer communal citizenship or issue a decisive pre-approval. Timing, documentary
burden, interview format, civic-knowledge checks, fee load, and the consequences of moving are all
points on which local practice materially affects real-world outcomes. 5

## Structural overview and table of legal propositions

## B. Structural overview of cantonal and communal procedure. In the ordinary route, the broad

sequence is: filing with the authority designated by cantonal law; local or cantonal pre-check for
completeness and eligibility; enquiries into integration and familiarity with life in Switzerland, often
including an interview and sometimes a civic-knowledge test; communal or cantonal pre-approval
where required by cantonal law; transmission to SEM for the federal authorisation; then a final cantonal
act conferring citizenship, sometimes followed by a ceremony or oath under cantonal law. The SEM
handbook expressly describes ordinary naturalisation as a federalist process in which federal, cantonal
and communal authorities each carry responsibility for their own part. 6

The most important structural divide is between minimum federal eligibility and local procedural
implementation. Federal law controls the minimum residence period in Switzerland, the permit
requirement, the core integration criteria, the federal authorisation, the one-year validity period of that
authorisation for the cantonal decision, federal fees, and some procedural safeguards. Cantonal law
controls the filing route, local residence periods within the federal two-to-five-year band, whether and
how the commune participates, what local authority decides communal citizenship, what additional
integration criteria are used, what interviews or tests are organised, and the cantonal and communal
fee regime. 7

## Primary legal texts and official federal guidance

## D. Primary legal texts. The main federal texts are the Swiss Constitution (especially arts. 37–38 on

citizenship and art. 46 on cantonal implementation of federal law), the Swiss Citizenship Act (especially
arts. 9–19 on ordinary naturalisation, integration, filing, decision-making, communal decision-making,
and local residence periods), and the Swiss Citizenship Ordinance (especially arts. 2, 6–9, 12–13, 17,
21, 23 and 25–28 on tests, language, personal circumstances, changes of domicile, enquiry reports,
cooperation, SEM timelines and federal fees). 19

Representative cantonal primary texts identified on official cantonal pages include Zürich’s Kantonales
Bürgerrechtsgesetz and Kantonale Bürgerrechtsverordnung; Aargau’s Gesetz über das Kantons-

und das Gemeindebürgerrecht and implementing ordinance; Fribourg’s Loi sur le droit de cité
fribourgeois and implementing regulation; Geneva’s Loi sur le droit de cité genevois; and Vaud’s Loi
sur le droit de cité vaudois and implementing regulation. These instruments are the correct starting
point whenever drafting moves from federal minima to cantonal or communal procedure. 20

## E. Official federal guidance. SEM’s public guidance states, in terms, that the federal authorisation is

granted by SEM, but that communes and cantons are responsible for the procedure, so the
application must be filed at the applicant’s place of residence or with the designated canton. SEM’s
ordinary-naturalisation page adds that cantonal legislation prescribes an extra canton/commune
residence period, that the canton creates an enquiry report, and that the federal authorisation is
followed by a cantonal decision within one year. 21

SEM’s FAQ is also useful for drafting because it confirms several points that are often misunderstood in
public-facing content: for ordinary naturalisation, the form is obtained from the local competent
authority; the application is filed with the municipality or canton according to cantonal rules; SEM sets
only the federal charge and does not publish local fees; and the federal residence and permit rules
must already be satisfied at filing. 22

The SEM Handbook on Citizenship, Chapter 3, is the most useful federal guidance document for
procedural analysis. It expressly describes ordinary naturalisation as grounded in federalism; says
communes may have legislative and decision powers depending on their autonomy; states that the
competent authority registers the application, checks identity, carries out enquiries and an interview;
and confirms that the case is sent to SEM only if canton and commune support the naturalisation. It
also states that a definitive ordinary naturalisation cannot be granted without the federal authorisation
and that the canton must revisit the file before the final cantonal act. 23

## Cantonal and communal practice notes

## F. Cantonal and communal practice notes. The notes below use representative official sources from

German-, French- and Italian-speaking cantons and communes. They are not exhaustive of all 26
cantons, but they show the kind of procedural divergence that future content should expect and reflect.

Zürich. The Canton of Zürich requires filing with the cantonal Gemeindeamt, even though the
application is then examined by the applicant’s residential commune and later again by the canton. Its
official page gives an indicative total duration of about two years, states that applicants must usually
have lived at least two years in the same municipality before filing, and publishes canton-level fees
while leaving commune fees to each municipality. Zürich also makes clear that a move is tolerated only
after the commune has completed its enquiries; otherwise the process stops, subject to a specific
under-25 exception. The City of Zürich separately states that applicants must have lived two years in
the city, lists documents such as the Swiss civil-status extract, employment or education confirmations
and school confirmations for children, and says the city process itself takes about two years. 25

Bern. The Canton of Bern routes applicants first to their municipality, which supplies forms, explains
required documents, conducts a personal interview, and forwards the file after communal citizenship
assurance and payment of cantonal/communal fees. Bern publishes a clear local residence rule of two
years’ uninterrupted residence in the canton and two years’ uninterrupted residence in the
municipality at filing. It also requires language at A2 written / B1 oral in the language of the
administrative district and operates a formal naturalisation test, from which certain categories are
exempt. Bern publishes its own cantonal fee scale, while leaving commune fees to each municipality.

The City of Bern adds a stricter local handling point: its published brochure warns applicants not to
move away from the city before the municipal council has decided, otherwise the city ends the
procedure. 26

Basel-Stadt. Basel-Stadt now allows digital filing and says ordinary naturalisation usually lasts one
and a half to two years. Its published workflow shows the Migrationsamt conducting the statutory
enquiries, interviews and report, followed by a further interview with the responsible Bürgergemeinde;
citizenship becomes effective through the cantonal act after the federal authorisation. Basel-Stadt’s
official page is unusually transparent on fees: it publishes separate federal, cantonal and commune-
level figures for Basel, Riehen and Bettingen, and notes a legislated fee reduction under which
cantonal fees are to be abolished from 1 July 2026, subject to the filing date. Basel-Stadt also
publishes a substantial list of required evidence, including civil-status extract, tax certificate, residence
confirmations, debt-enforcement extracts and identity documents. 27

Geneva. Geneva is one of the clearest examples of a strongly cantonalised ordinary-naturalisation
system. The OCPM’s service of naturalisations manages the process. The canton requires 10 years in
Switzerland, 2 years in Geneva, including the 12 months immediately before filing, and effective
residence in Geneva throughout the whole procedure; moving to another canton before
examination can lead to the case being filed off, and long absences abroad may also do so. The filing
process is staged: the applicant first requests a dossier by sending an identity document; if formal
conditions are met, the service sends the appropriate forms. Geneva requires a test certificate on
Swiss and Genevan history, geography and institutions for those who are subject to the test, plus proof
of French at B1 oral / A2 written, tax, debt-enforcement and social-assistance attestations, civil-status
extract, permit C copy, photographs, and other documents depending on the case. The canton also
publishes age-based cantonal fees and states that, for adults, naturalisation takes effect at the oath
ceremony, which is part of the cantonal procedure. Geneva’s conditions page is also a strong example
of local administrative strictness: it says non-entry may be issued if there was social assistance in the
previous three years, debts above CHF 1,500, or tax arrears at dossier registration. 28

Vaud and Lausanne. A recent Vaud publication for communes states that ordinary naturalisation
requires communal, cantonal and federal analysis in sequence, leading respectively to a positive
communal opinion on communal bourgeoisie, a positive cantonal opinion on cantonal citizenship, and
the federal authorisation; it also states that Swiss nationality is acquired at the oath ceremony. Official
canton material for communes further shows that, in the ordinary procedure, the commune prepares a
report of enquiry and conducts the applicant’s hearing. Vaud also publishes current preparation tools,
including A‑Vaud‑Test and a didacticiel for the official knowledge test. At communal level, Lausanne’s
Bureau des naturalisations states that it is responsible for preparing the reports and organising the
naturalisation tests, and Lausanne’s municipal tariff sets a commune-level fee of CHF 250 for an
ordinary naturalisation application. One point should be checked at source each time: the materials
gathered for this pack clearly establish the Vaud sequence, report/hearing structure and test
preparation tools, but they do not provide, in one current consolidated page, a simple canton-wide
summary of today’s residence-years rule, so future drafting should verify the current LDCV/RLDCV text
and the competent commune before stating a fixed Vaud residence period. 29

Fribourg. Fribourg’s procedure is notable for its pre-procedure civil-status step. The official page says
that ordinary naturalisation cannot be formally filed until the applicant is entered in the Swiss civil-
status register, and that this preliminary step can itself take 24 to 36 months depending on the case.
After that, the dossier is filed with the cantonal service, a CHF 200 amount is collected on filing, and an
interview for the enquiry report is expected after an average of 12 months. The commune is the first
formal deciding authority, and the applicant may be called before the commune’s naturalisation
commission; the SEM is the second deciding authority on the file without a hearing; and the Grand

Council is the third and final deciding authority, with a further hearing being possible before its
commission. Fribourg’s published formal conditions include three years’ domicile in the canton, with
two of those years in the last five, while the communal residence requirement varies by commune
between one and three years. The official FAQ also reports that the total ordinary procedure typically
lasts 24 to 36 months excluding civil-status registration, that children may apply individually from 14,
that children are interviewed from 12, and that a move becomes much less problematic once the
communal citizenship has already been granted. 30

Ticino and Bellinzona. Ticino’s official guidance requires 5 years in Ticino and 3 years in the
commune concerned, including the 2 years before the application, alongside the federal Swiss-
residence requirement. The applicant begins by making an appointment with the communal chancery,
which checks whether the basic conditions appear to be met and hands over the forms and document
list. Ticino’s published procedure is then commune → Confederation → canton. The canton also
publishes its own fees (CHF 800 for an individual procedure, plus CHF 100 per foreign minor child
included, and CHF 200 for procedures withdrawn before decision), while saying commune fees are set
locally and that both commune and canton collect charges regardless of outcome. Ticino is also a good
example of a potentially stricter local social-assistance rule: its official page says the applicant must
not have received social assistance during the 10 years before the application, at the time of filing, or
during the procedure, unless repaid in full. Bellinzona’s communal page in turn states that local forms
are available from the city’s multifunction counters and that the communal fee for an individual
ordinary naturalisation is CHF 1,200. 31

Aargau. The Canton of Aargau is useful because its official public guidance and municipal handbook
both set out the multi-level decisional structure in unusually plain language. The public page says
that Bund, Kanton und Gemeinden each examine the application independently and that if any one of
the three rejects it, the process ends. Aargau’s municipality-level requirements include 5 years in the
canton and a 3-year uninterrupted residence in the commune, plus sufficient civic and language
knowledge and familiarity with conditions in Switzerland, the canton and the commune. The canton
notes that overall duration depends heavily on municipal organisation, but that about 10 additional
months should be expected for cantonal and federal examination on top of communal handling time.
The official handbook for communes shows a process with municipal first advice, civil-status
preregistration, a civics test, collection of required annexes, a municipal naturalisation interview,
communal decision on communal citizenship, cantonal review and application to SEM, followed by steps
involving the cantonal commission and Grand Council. 32

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

## G. Practical legal analysis. For future drafting, the most defensible analytical split is this. Federal law

governs the minimum threshold for eligibility and the indispensable federal authorisation: permit C,
Swiss residence counting rules, core integration criteria, the possibility of personal-circumstances
adjustments, what must appear in the enquiry report, the duty to cooperate, federal fees, and the
validity of the federal permit. Cantonal law and local administration govern where the file is lodged,
how long the applicant must have lived in the canton or commune, who interviews the applicant, who
runs any civic-knowledge test, which authority drafts or supplements the report, which body grants
communal citizenship, how local fees are charged, and what happens operationally if the applicant
moves. 33

The practical effect is that the same federal eligibility profile can still produce very different
procedural experiences across cantons and communes. A Geneva file may begin at cantonal level
with a request for the dossier, a formal knowledge-test certificate and multiple fiscal/social attestations;

a Bern file begins with the municipality and includes a municipal interview and usually a Bern
knowledge test; a Zürich file is filed with the canton but then examined by the commune; a Ticino file
starts at the communal chancery; and a Fribourg file may be delayed for a long period by prior civil-
status registration before the naturalisation dossier can even be accepted. 34

For application strategy, the highest-value operational points are usually these: first, verify the local
residence model before filing, because residence-years vary substantially; secondly, check whether the
canton or commune expects a civil-status preregistration or fresh civil-status extract; thirdly, front-
load any language certificate and, where relevant, the knowledge-test certificate because some
cantons require them before the dossier is accepted; fourthly, check debt, tax and social-assistance
history against local practice, not only federal minimum rules; and fifthly, avoid moves before the
critical local stage identified by the competent authority. Those are the points most likely to change
timing or even admissibility in practice. 35

## H. Drafting cautions. Future content should not say that an ordinary-naturalisation application is filed

with SEM. That is wrong for the ordinary route. The correct formulation is that the application is filed
with the authority designated by cantonal law, commonly a commune or a cantonal service; SEM
becomes involved later when the file is forwarded for the federal authorisation. 36

Future content should also not present interviews, civic tests, communal hearings, or local reports as if
they were identical nationwide federal requirements. Federal law allows a cantonal authority to require
a knowledge test and prescribes what the cantonal report must contain, but the concrete mechanisms
remain local. Bern, Geneva, Vaud/Lausanne, Basel-Stadt, Aargau and other cantons show that
interviews and tests are common, but they are structured differently and cannot safely be stated as a
standard Swiss rule without qualification. 37

Likewise, future content should not state one standard local residence period, one standard fee, or one
standard processing time for “Switzerland”. Official sources show clear divergence: Bern uses 2 years
canton + 2 years commune; Basel-Stadt uses 2 years in the relevant commune; Aargau uses 5 years
canton + 3 years commune; Geneva uses 2 years in canton including the last 12 months; Ticino uses
5 years canton + 3 years commune; and Fribourg’s commune requirement varies between 1 and 3
years. Fees and timing also vary sharply, especially once commune fees are added. 38

Moving rules need especially careful drafting. Federal law says the original authority stays competent
once the required enquiries for cantonal assurance are complete, but official cantonal and communal
guidance is often stricter before that stage. Zürich says the process stops if the applicant moves before
the commune has completed its enquiries; Fribourg says a move before communal citizenship can
obstruct or significantly delay the case; Geneva warns that a move to another canton before
examination may lead to the case being closed; and the City of Bern warns applicants not to move away
before the municipal decision. Any general statement about moving should therefore be framed as
stage-dependent and canton-sensitive. 39

A final caution concerns local ceremonies and oaths. Oath or ceremony requirements are not
contributions of federal law as such; they arise in cantonal or local procedure. Geneva states that, for
adults, the oath ceremony is part of the cantonal process and that failure to attend without valid reason
may cause the cantonal naturalisation order to lapse. Vaud and Fribourg also use ceremony/oath stages
in their official materials. Those steps should therefore be drafted as canton-specific end-stage
procedure, not as a universal federal feature. 40

## Open questions and limitations. This pack is intentionally conservative. It uses high-confidence official

sources and representative cantonal and communal examples, but it does not attempt an exhaustive
26-canton residence-and-fee matrix. In particular, the Vaud materials gathered here clearly establish the
current procedural sequence, communal report/hearing role and test-preparation tools, but they do not
give, in one current official page, a simple canton-wide residence-years summary fit for black-letter
drafting; that point should be checked directly against the current LDCV/RLDCV text and the competent
commune before publication. 41
