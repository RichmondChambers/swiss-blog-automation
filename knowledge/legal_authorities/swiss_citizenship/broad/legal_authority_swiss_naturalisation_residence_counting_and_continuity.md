# Swiss Naturalisation Residence Counting and Continuity Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

For ordinary Swiss naturalisation, residence is governed first by the Federal Constitution’s three-level
citizenship structure and then by the Swiss Citizenship Act and Swiss Citizenship Ordinance, with
cantonal and communal law adding local residence minima and procedure. At federal level, an applicant
for ordinary naturalisation must hold a settlement permit, prove ten years’ residence in Switzerland,
and show that three of those years fall within the five years before the application. Years lived in
Switzerland between the ages of 8 and 18 count double, but the applicant must still have at least six
years of actual residence. The federal framework is minimum law only: cantons and, where local law
allows, communes add their own residence clocks, procedures and evidential expectations.
The statutory residence concept for naturalisation is not the same as a simple permits-based
immigration chronology. The SEM’s citizenship manual makes clear that ordinary naturalisation requires
lawful and actual residence in Switzerland: an unlawful stay does not count, but neither does merely
holding a permit without actually living in Switzerland. The same manual explains that what matters is a
real centre of life in Switzerland, reflected in family, work, school and material ties. That point is
especially important when assessing absences, commuter-style patterns, or cases where the applicant
keeps an immigration status but no longer lives in Switzerland in substance.
Not all Swiss residence categories count equally. Time with a B or C permit counts in full. Time with an
FDFA legitimation card or a comparable title such as a Ci permit also counts. Time spent in Switzerland
under temporary admission with an F permit counts only by half. By contrast, time with an N permit
during asylum proceedings does not count, and SEM guidance also states that L permits, G permits, S
protection status, tourist-visa stays and stays under a false identity are not counted towards the
naturalisation residence period. These distinctions are central for drafting on residence counting,
because they mean that “years in Switzerland” is not a legally sufficient formulation unless the
underlying status is checked.
Continuity is the most sensitive area. The black-letter wording of Article 9 of the Citizenship Act
expressly requires ten years in Switzerland, three of them in the five years before filing. It does not itself
say that the three years must be continuous. However, the SEM’s current citizenship manual interprets
the rule to mean that, while the overall ten-year period may be interrupted, the applicant must have
lived in Switzerland uninterruptedly for three years within the five years before filing. That continuity
point should therefore be treated as an official administrative interpretation and practice position,
rather than as explicit statutory wording.
On absences, the federal law is stricter than many client summaries suggest. Short departures with an
intention to return do not interrupt residence. Residence is treated as abandoned if the person
deregisters from Switzerland or actually remains abroad for more than six months. The Citizenship
Ordinance adds an important carve-out: an absence of up to one year for the employer’s assignment or
for education or training is treated as a short departure with intent to return. The SEM manual adds
that during the naturalisation process an applicant may spend more than six months abroad for work

or study without interruption if the Swiss centre of life is preserved and return is shown, but the same
manual also says that an absence of more than twelve months cannot be counted towards the federal
residence period. That makes long absences a mixed legal/administrative question rather than a purely
mechanical one.
The settlement permit requirement must be understood separately from residence counting. For
ordinary naturalisation, the applicant must have a valid C permit when filing and must keep it
throughout the naturalisation process, including until the federal naturalisation authorisation is issued
and until the cantonal naturalisation decision is taken. But a C permit is only a threshold condition.
Immigration law counts residence differently for settlement-permit purposes: the SEM’s AIG manual
explains that ordinary C-permit acquisition generally requires ten years with short-stay or residence
permits plus five years of uninterrupted possession of a residence permit in Switzerland, with some
five-year treaty-based routes and separate early-grant mechanisms. The same AIG guidance also allows
a C permit, on application, to be maintained during a longer absence abroad for up to four years. As a
matter of legal analysis, that does not mean naturalisation residence continues to accrue: citizenship
law still turns on actual residence and its own interruption rules.
Local residence rules can be materially stricter than the federal minimum. Bern requires two years’
uninterrupted residence in the canton and in the commune. Vaud requires two full years in the canton,
including the year before filing, and allows communes to impose one additional communal year; it also
defines local residence by registration as principal residence, while leaving counting, non-interruption
and end of residence otherwise to federal law. Ticino publishes a notably stricter local rule of five years
in Ticino and three years in the commune, including the two years before filing. Zurich publishes a two-
year same-commune rule and, for applicants under 25, an exception based on two years in the canton.
These are local rules, not federal ones, but they can determine whether an application is possible at all
on a given date.
Residence is proved administratively, not just asserted. Federal guidance requires the cantonal
authority to prepare an inquiry report covering the residence period in Switzerland alongside permit
status and other naturalisation criteria. Vaud’s implementing regulation requires the official form to
record the applicant’s place of residence and CV, including previous residences, and allows the canton to
consult other cantons’ social services if the applicant lived elsewhere in the preceding three years.
Zurich’s checklist requires formal documentary uploads and date-valid documents. The practical
consequence is that residence continuity issues frequently surface through population-register data,
civil-status extracts, local interviews, and cross-cantonal checks rather than through a single “years
count” calculation.
Ordinary naturalisation should not be drafted as automatic once formal residence clocks run out.
Official federal and cantonal materials describe a multi-level assessment and a decision-making process
by commune, canton and Confederation. SEM’s jurisprudence annex, reproducing Federal Supreme
Court authority, states that there is no general entitlement to naturalisation, even though the applicant
is entitled to a lawful, reasoned and non-discriminatory process. For residence drafting, the safe
position is therefore that meeting residence thresholds is necessary, but not synonymous with success.

## Framework overview and legal propositions

## Structural overview of residence counting and continuity

Federal legal basis
The federal residence framework for ordinary naturalisation is built primarily on Article 9 of the Swiss
Citizenship Act, Article 33 of the same Act, and Article 16 of the Swiss Citizenship Ordinance, all
operating within the Constitution’s commune–canton–Confederation citizenship structure. Article 18 of
the Act then leaves room for cantonal and communal minimum residence rules between two and five
years.
How residence is counted
At federal level, the total period required is ten years. B and C permit time counts in full; FDFA
legitimation-card and comparable Ci time counts in full; F-permit time counts by half. N, L, G and S
status do not count according to SEM guidance, and the SEM manual further states that tourist-visa
stays and residence under a false identity are excluded. Time between ages 8 and 18 counts double,
subject to a real-residence floor of six years.
Continuity and absences
The legislation expressly says that short departures with an intention to return do not interrupt
residence, and that residence is deemed abandoned on deregistration or after more than six months’
actual stay abroad. The Ordinance adds that employer-assignment or study/training absences of up to
one year are treated as short departures with intent to return. The SEM manual then interprets Article 9
to allow interruption in the overall ten-year period but to require an uninterrupted three-year period
within the last five years before filing. That uninterrupted-three-years proposition is therefore best
treated as official guidance, not explicit statutory wording.
Local residence and jurisdiction
Cantonal legislation may require between two and five years’ residence. Article 18 also provides that the
canton and commune where the application was filed remain competent after a move to another
commune or canton if they have already fully examined the federal substantive conditions. Local law
can nonetheless make early moves procedurally disruptive. Zurich states that a move before the
municipality has finished its assessment stops the process, while a later move does not.
Evidence and proof
Residence is tested through an inquiry report and supporting documents rather than a single statutory
certificate. SEM guidance says the cantonal report must record residence duration and permit type, and
may draw on inter-authority assistance. Vaud’s regulation requires official forms to capture residence
information and the applicant’s CV, including places of residence, and Zurich requires uploaded civil-
status and other supporting documents with strict currency dates.
Relationship to settlement-permit law
Naturalisation residence and settlement-permit residence are related but legally distinct. The
naturalisation route needs a valid C permit at filing and throughout the procedure, but citizenship law
counts some statuses that cannot themselves be used to file, and immigration law has its own rules on

how C is earned and how it may be maintained despite a period abroad. That is why preserving or
obtaining C does not automatically settle the naturalisation residence analysis.

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The SEM page on ordinary naturalisation is the clearest short-form federal guidance source. It
summarises the ten-year rule, the C-permit requirement, the double-counting between ages 8 and 18,
and the permit-category counting rules; it is particularly useful for quick confirmation that B and C
count fully, F counts half, and N and L do not count.
The SEM FAQ on Swiss citizenship adds several practical clarifications of direct drafting value: the formal
conditions must be met at the time of filing; a C permit is required; N does not count, F counts by half,
L does not count, and FDFA legitimation/Ci time counts. Its value is operational rather than legislative,
and it should be cited as official guidance rather than black-letter law.
The SEM citizenship manual, chapter 3, is the principal interpretive source for residence counting and
continuity. It gives the current administrative position on actual lawful residence, centre of life,
exclusions such as tourist visas and false-identity stays, the difference between the interruptible ten-
year total and the uninterrupted three-year period within the last five years, the treatment of absences
under Article 16 of the Citizenship Ordinance, and the requirement that the C permit continue
throughout the procedure. For contentious drafting, this manual is the most important federal
administrative source after the legislation itself.
The official ch.ch naturalisation page is useful as a short explanatory source because it plainly states
that each commune may have its own rules within cantonal and federal law, and that cantonal/
communal residence minima can range from two to five years. It is not a substitute for cantonal law,
but it is a reliable official overview source.
The SEM AIG manual is not a citizenship source, but it is essential for avoiding category mistakes in
drafting. It explains how settlement permits are earned and how they may be preserved during an
approved longer absence abroad. That makes it the right official source for contrasting immigration
residence with citizenship residence.

The SEM jurisprudence annex reproducing Federal Supreme Court decisions is the strongest official
source for the proposition that there is no general entitlement to ordinary naturalisation, while still
requiring a lawful, reasoned and non-discriminatory procedure. This is particularly important for
drafting aimed at legal reliability rather than public-facing simplification.

## Cantonal and communal practice notes

Canton of Bern
Bern’s official naturalisation site states that, beyond the federal rules, the applicant must have lived
uninterruptedly in the canton for two years and in the commune for two years at the time of filing. The
same page also restates the federal permit-counting rules and notes that a minor filing independently
can do so only once the residence requirements are met — which Bern explains means, in practice, no
earlier than after the completed ninth year of age. These are local residence rules and procedural
explanations, not federal black-letter law.
Canton of Zurich
Zurich’s official page says that the applicant must have lived in the same commune for at least two
years before filing. It then adds an under-25 exception under which two years in the canton are
sufficient. Zurich also gives a highly practical move rule: if the municipal examination has been
completed, the application may continue after a move within Zurich or elsewhere in Switzerland; if the
move occurs earlier, the application is not continued and a fresh local residence period may be needed.
Those are local procedural rules, not federal ones.
Canton of Vaud
Vaud’s cantonal citizenship law provides that, at filing, the foreign applicant must satisfy the federal
formal conditions, reside in the Vaud commune whose bourgeoisie is sought, and have completed two
full years in the canton including the year before the application. Vaud further allows a commune, by
regulation, to impose one year of local residence. The law expressly defines “residing” for these
purposes as being registered in the communal population register with principal residence, but leaves
the broader calculation of residence duration, continuity and end of residence to federal law. This is a
particularly useful official example of interaction between federal counting rules and cantonal residence
law.
Commune of Lausanne
Lausanne’s official guidance says that the commune no longer receives new filings directly — the
canton is the entry point — but the Lausanne bureau then prepares the communal report, organises
interviews and organises knowledge testing. Lausanne’s municipal directive adds procedural detail: the
bureau fixes the interview date, drafts the report in the candidate’s presence based on documents
produced, organises a written knowledge test on federal, cantonal and communal subjects, uses 48
questions selected from a pool of 160, sets a 70 per cent pass mark, and allows up to three retakes.
These are commune-specific procedural rules and testing practices rather than federal residence law.
Canton of Ticino
Ticino’s official ordinary-naturalisation page states a materially stricter local residence regime than the
federal minimum: at least five years in Ticino and at least three years in the commune of application,

including the two years before filing. The same page routes the applicant first to the communal
chancery and emphasises that all requirements must remain satisfied throughout the whole procedure.
This is a strong example of the way local residence clocks can dominate timing analysis even where
federal residence has already been accrued.
City of Zurich
The City of Zurich’s own page is useful as evidence-oriented communal guidance. It repeats the federal
permit-type distinctions, states the two-year City of Zurich residence rule, identifies local financial and
social-assistance expectations, and explains that moves within the city are always possible while moves
to another commune are possible only after the city’s examination has been completed. Zurich’s 2023
checklist also requires supporting documents to be uploaded online and sets currency limits for those
documents. These are communal procedural and evidential points, not federal residence rules.

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

## Practical legal analysis

Residence for naturalisation is a citizenship-law construct, not just an immigration-status record. The
SEM’s own guidance treats the applicant’s actual living situation in Switzerland as central, and expressly
warns that neither unlawful actual presence nor a merely formal permit without real residence satisfies
the federal residence requirement. That makes “time in Switzerland” too crude a drafting formula
unless it is tied to lawful status, actual presence and the applicant’s centre of life.
The C permit is necessary for ordinary naturalisation, but plainly not sufficient. First, the person must
still satisfy the separate naturalisation residence rules in Article 9 of the Citizenship Act. Secondly, some
periods can count toward the naturalisation residence total even though they were spent under
statuses that do not allow an ordinary-naturalisation filing by themselves, such as temporary admission
counted by half. Thirdly, the applicant must keep the C permit throughout the ordinary-naturalisation
process itself. The most accurate drafting position is therefore that the C permit is a threshold
immigration status superimposed onto a separate citizenship-residence test.
The law and guidance together also show why absence analysis must be handled with care. The statute
itself focuses on short absences, deregistration and actual stays abroad longer than six months. The
Ordinance extends protection to certain work- and study-related absences of up to one year. The SEM
manual then adds a further administrative explanation about maintaining the Swiss centre of life during
the process. A careful drafter should therefore distinguish expressly between: the statutory text; the
ordinance carve-out; and SEM’s practice-oriented explanation of how those rules are applied. The same
care is needed for the “three years in the last five” issue, because the uninterrupted-continuity
proposition comes from current SEM guidance rather than the literal wording of Article 9.
The difference between naturalisation residence and settlement-permit residence is a long-term
planning issue, not a technical footnote. Immigration law can grant a C permit after five years in some
treaty or practice-based cases, or preserve an existing C permit during an approved longer absence
abroad. Citizenship law, however, continues to insist on its own residence clock and interruption logic.
As an inference from the official sources, a person may therefore remain immigration-lawfully “settled”
for some purposes while no longer satisfying the actual-residence logic needed for ordinary
naturalisation. That is why residence planning for naturalisation should begin well before filing,
especially in cases involving studies abroad, secondments, or expected moves between communes or
cantons.

Cantonal and communal law can materially change the filing date and the consequences of moving.
Vaud ties local residence to principal-residence registration and allows an additional communal year.
Bern requires uninterrupted local residence in both canton and commune. Ticino uses much longer
local periods. Zurich gives stage-sensitive move rules that can either preserve the application or end it.
For internal drafting, this means local residence is not a peripheral footnote: it is often the factor that
decides whether an applicant is genuinely application-ready.
Finally, ordinary naturalisation should not be described as automatic once residence thresholds are
met. Official federal material describes a multi-stage assessment, and official jurisprudence confirms
that there is no general entitlement to naturalisation even though applicants are entitled to a lawful
process. Residence is therefore best presented internally as a necessary eligibility and timing
framework, not as a self-executing right.

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe a valid C permit as equivalent to naturalisation readiness. It is a threshold
requirement, but not the residence test itself.
Do not say that all time physically spent in Switzerland counts equally. Permit category matters,
and some statuses count only in part or not at all.
Do not present the “three years in the last five years must be continuous” proposition as explicit
statutory text. It is the SEM’s official interpretation and practice position.
Do not assume that keeping or restoring a C permit means naturalisation residence has
remained intact. Immigration-law preservation of C during an absence abroad does not itself
answer the citizenship-law residence question.
Do not transpose settlement-permit counting rules into naturalisation drafting. The AIG and
citizenship regimes use materially different residence logic.
Do not ignore cantonal and communal residence periods. Federal residence may be complete
while local filing requirements are not.
Do not treat a move during the procedure as legally neutral. The effect depends on both Article
18 and the procedural stage reached under local law.
Do not reduce residence to paperwork alone; the official guidance treats actual living ties and
centre of life as part of the analysis.
Do not characterise ordinary naturalisation as automatic once the residence clock expires. The
residence criteria open the door to a decision-making process; they do not eliminate local
assessment or discretionary judgment within legal bounds.
Do not present commune-specific interview or test arrangements as federal law. They are local
procedural or evidential rules.

## Open questions and limitations

This pack is strong on federal law, SEM guidance, and selected cantonal and communal official
publications. It is not exhaustive at commune level: many communes do not publish detailed public
rules on residence evidence, move-stage consequences or internal practice. For commune-specific
drafting beyond the examples cited here, the relevant communal regulation or official checklist should
be checked directly before publication or advice.
