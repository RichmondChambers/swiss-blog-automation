# Swiss Facilitated Naturalisation by Marriage to a Swiss Citizen

## Executive legal summary

## A. Executive legal summary

This pack concerns the federal route usually described in German, French and Italian as erleichterte /
facilitée / agevolata naturalisation. Official English federal pages often call the same route “simplified
naturalisation”. It is legally distinct from ordinary naturalisation, is decided at federal level by the State
Secretariat for Migration (SEM), and must not be described as citizenship being acquired automatically
by marriage. Marriage to a Swiss citizen opens a possible route to apply; it does not itself confer Swiss
citizenship.
For a spouse living in Switzerland, the core statutory route requires cumulative satisfaction of two
formal conditions: at least three years in a marital union with the Swiss spouse, and a total of five years’
residence in Switzerland, including the year immediately before filing. Unlike ordinary naturalisation,
this facilitated spouse route does not require a settlement permit C as a formal prerequisite; qualifying
residence can include certain other residence titles recognised by federal citizenship law.
For a spouse living abroad, the route is materially different. The applicant must have lived in a marital
union with the Swiss spouse for at least six years and must be “closely connected with Switzerland”.
Federal guidance treats the “close ties” criteria cumulatively: sufficient stays in Switzerland, everyday
spoken ability in a Swiss national language, basic knowledge of Swiss geography/history/politics/
society, and active contact with Swiss citizens, supported by Swiss-resident reference persons. The in-
country and abroad spouse cases therefore must not be collapsed into a single residence rule.
A genuine and stable marital union is central, and it is more than the formal existence of a marriage
certificate. Federal ordinance and SEM guidance require a real life-community, an intact mutual
intention to live in a stable marriage, and—save where important reasons justify separate residences—
cohabitation at the same address. The marital union must exist both when the application is filed and
when the naturalisation decision is made. If separation or divorce plans exist, or the relationship has
already broken down, the facilitated route is not properly available.
Facilitated naturalisation by marriage also requires substantive suitability. By statute and ordinance, the
applicant must satisfy integration criteria, respect public security and public order, respect
constitutional values, participate in economic life or education, support the integration of family
members, and not endanger Switzerland’s internal or external security. In-country spouses must usually
prove written language at A2 and spoken language at B1 in a Swiss national language; abroad
applicants are assessed on everyday oral communication in a Swiss national language as part of the
“close ties” inquiry. Tax arrears, debt-enforcement problems, loss certificates, criminal matters, pending
criminal proceedings, and unresolved social-assistance issues are all potentially serious obstacles.
The process is evidence-heavy and not merely formal. For Swiss-resident spouses, SEM requires a
signed application with a federal document bundle and obtains cantonal investigation reports. For

spouses abroad, the application goes through the Swiss representation, which conducts a mandatory
personal interview and compiles an evidential report for SEM. Throughout the process, the applicant
has a duty to cooperate and to notify material changes. Where declarations about the marital union are
false, or material facts are concealed, naturalisation may later be annulled under Article 36 BüG. Federal

## case law further accepts a rebuttable presumption of deception where separation or divorce follows

very shortly after grant, although that presumption can be displaced by a credible, concrete post-grant
event or explanation.

## Structural overview and legal propositions

## B. Structural overview of facilitated naturalisation by marriage

The federal spouse route operates in two principal configurations. The first is the spouse living in
Switzerland route under Article 21 paragraph 1 BüG. The second is the spouse living abroad route
under Article 21 paragraph 2 BüG. The former turns primarily on Swiss residence plus three years’
marital union; the latter turns on six years’ marital union plus close ties to Switzerland. The common
material requirements in Article 20 BüG apply across facilitated naturalisation, but for persons not
resident in Switzerland they apply mutatis mutandis.
There is also an important derivative spouse scenario. Federal guidance confirms that a foreign spouse
may still use the facilitated spouse route where the Swiss spouse acquired Swiss citizenship after the
marriage by reinstatement or by facilitated naturalisation based on descent from a Swiss parent. By
contrast, the route is not available where both spouses were foreign when they married and the now-
Swiss spouse later acquired Swiss citizenship through ordinary naturalisation or through facilitated
naturalisation as a third-generation foreign national.
Registered partnership must be handled with care. Official SEM FAQ material states that facilitated
naturalisation is open only to married couples, not to couples who remain in a registered partnership;
those cases belong to ordinary naturalisation. Federal handbook guidance adds, however, that where a
registered partnership has been converted into a marriage, certain partnership periods may in some
circumstances be credited towards the marital-union period in accordance with the partnership
legislation and the transitional timing described by SEM.
Minor children may usually be included in a facilitated naturalisation application if they live with the
applicant, but their inclusion is not mandatory. SEM’s guidance states that children aged 12 and over
must have the Article 12 integration criteria assessed in an age-appropriate and independent way, and
the other parent’s consent is relevant where required.

## F. Cantonal or communal practice notes, if relevant

No cantonal or communal source identified for this topic changes the federal substantive legal test
for facilitated naturalisation by marriage. The canton and, in practice, sometimes the commune matter
mainly as evidence providers or investigators: they issue residence certificates, tax and social-assistance
confirmations, debt-enforcement extracts, and the cantonal report requested by SEM; the canton may
also choose whether to exercise its right to be heard before grant. Ordinary naturalisation remains the
route in which canton- and commune-specific substantive rules are most prominent.

## Practical legal analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis

The safest way to structure this topic is to begin with the route distinction. For spouses in Switzerland,
Article 21 paragraph 1 BüG is a residence-based federal spouse route. For spouses abroad, Article 21
paragraph 2 BüG is a close-ties route. Those routes share the Article 20 material conditions, but the
formal architecture is different. Federal FAQ and handbook material also make clear that where the
Swiss spouse became Swiss only after the marriage, the applicant needs an additional check: facilitated
naturalisation remains open where the spouse became Swiss through reinstatement or through
facilitated naturalisation based on Swiss parentage, but not where both were foreign at marriage and
the spouse later became Swiss through ordinary naturalisation or as a third-generation facilitated case.

For the Swiss-resident route, residence analysis is technical and should not be flattened into “five years
in Switzerland”. The federal materials say the applicant must show five years’ residence in Switzerland,
of which one year must be immediately before filing, and must hold a qualifying residence title not just
before filing but throughout the procedure until decision. Countable residence includes stays with a B
or C permit, temporary admission F (counted at half), and certain FDFA-issued or comparable titles such
as a Ci-type status; L, G, N and S permits do not count, nor do tourist-visa stays. Short absences do not
necessarily interrupt residence, but an actual stay abroad of more than six months normally breaks it,
subject to the ordinance rule treating up to one year abroad for an employer’s assignment or for study/
training as a temporary departure with intent to return.
The marital-union requirement deserves separate treatment because it is not a mere duration test. The
ordinance definition, reproduced in SEM guidance, requires both the formal existence of the marriage
and an actual life-community in which the shared will to maintain a stable marital union remains intact.
SEM’s handbook says that, as a rule, the spouses should live at the same address and there must be no
separation or divorce plans, although separate homes may be tolerated where there are important
professional or health reasons and the future-oriented marital intention still objectively exists. The same
standard applies in the abroad spouse chapter. This is why federal materials insist on a declaration
concerning the marital union, refreshed if older than six months at decision stage, and why they treat
pending divorce proceedings, judicial separation, domestic-violence situations, and similar facts as
indicators requiring closer scrutiny.
Integration must also be handled carefully. The statute makes the ordinary-naturalisation integration
criteria applicable to facilitated naturalisation, and SEM expressly states that the required integration
level is aligned with the ordinary route. That means the spouse route is not a mere shortcut around
integration. For Swiss-resident spouses, SEM’s official materials say successful integration includes
respect for public security and public order, respect for constitutional values, the required language
ability, participation in economic life or education, and support for family integration; SEM FAQ adds
that a personal interview is used to assess knowledge of Switzerland, social and cultural participation,
and contacts with Swiss people. For applicants abroad, the Article 20 conditions still apply mutatis
mutandis, and the “close ties” assessment adds its own Switzerland-specific elements.
Language is a common drafting trap. For spouses living in Switzerland, SEM handbook and FAQ
material require minimum written A2 and spoken B1 in a Swiss national language. The accepted ways of
proving this are also tightly specified: mother tongue in a Swiss national language; at least five years of
compulsory schooling in such a language; a secondary-II or tertiary qualification completed in such a
language; or a recognised language certificate/language passport on the SEM-recognised list. SEM FAQ
expressly says that simple course-attendance certificates and online placement tests are not enough. By
contrast, for spouses abroad, the official threshold in the “close ties” analysis is everyday oral ability in a
Swiss national language, assessed in interview by the Swiss representation.
Public-order and good-standing issues are legally significant but should be presented with calibrated
language. The SEM English page summarises successful integration as including timely tax payment, no
debt-enforcement proceedings, no loss certificates and no criminal record. The more detailed handbook
states that SEM may refuse where tax arrears, debt-enforcement proceedings or loss certificates
appear in the relevant recent period, and it treats important unpaid public or private obligations—such
as taxes, health-insurance premiums, fines, rent arrears or family maintenance obligations—as adverse
to the required financial standing. Social assistance is also a major issue: the ordinance rule reproduced
in the handbook says a person who received social assistance in the three years immediately before
filing or during the procedure does not meet the economic-life/education criterion unless the
assistance has been fully repaid, subject to accommodation for significant personal circumstances
under Article 12 paragraph 2 BüG and Article 9 BüV.

Criminal matters require separate treatment again. The handbook reproduces ordinance rules under
which certain VOSTRA entries are automatic obstacles while they remain visible to SEM, and it states
that pending criminal proceedings in Switzerland or abroad prevent grant and lead SEM to suspend the
facilitated naturalisation procedure until the criminal matter concludes. The same federal material
makes clear that if a sanction still appears in the relevant VOSTRA authority extract, naturalisation is
excluded until it disappears from that extract; in other cases the SEM undertakes an individual
assessment. For applicants abroad, the Swiss representation is instructed to check compliance with the
law in the country of residence and to ensure that criminal-record extracts from the country of
residence—and from other states in which the applicant has lived in the previous six years—are filed.
On procedure, the Swiss-resident route is federal but investigation-heavy. The application is filed with
SEM; the form is obtained from SEM or locally and returned with the federal evidence bundle. Before
filing, the applicant must already have signed the declaration concerning the marital union, the
declaration concerning respect for public security and public order, the authorisation for the authorities
to obtain information, and the document checklist. SEM then examines completeness and requests
cantonal investigation reports for the relevant five-year period. The home canton may comment if it
exercises its right to be heard, but SEM is not bound by the cantonal proposal; if a canton or commune
disagrees with a positive SEM decision, federal guidance says it may challenge that decision before the
Federal Administrative Court.
The evidence expected for Swiss-resident spouses is unusually concrete in the federal checklist. SEM
requires, among other things, a recent Swiss family-status extract, residence certificates from the
communes for the previous five years, the applicant’s valid residence title, a recent debt-enforcement
extract covering the last five years for both the applicant and the spouse, a tax-authority confirmation
that cantonal/communal taxes and direct federal tax for the previous five years have been paid,
language evidence if the mother tongue is not a Swiss national language, current employment/self-
employment/school or benefits evidence, and a social-assistance confirmation showing no benefits in
the prior three years or full repayment. The checklist also requires the public-order declaration, marital-
union declaration and authority-to-obtain-information form.
The abroad route operates differently. The application is made through the competent Swiss
representation, not directly through a Swiss canton or commune. The representation conducts an
obligatory personal interview with the applicant and, according to SEM’s handbook, also invites the
Swiss spouse and any children included in the application. The interview is generally conducted in a
Swiss national language; the representation prepares the evidential report for SEM and records how the
applicant handled the language and Swiss-knowledge elements. Federal guidance says the applicant
should be told in advance the points on which questioning will occur, particularly Swiss knowledge. If
formal conditions are missing and the applicant maintains the application, the file is sent to SEM for a
formal non-entry decision.
The evidential burden for “close ties” is also explicit. Stays in Switzerland can be proved by flight tickets,
rail tickets, credit/debit-card extracts or accommodation invoices. Swiss knowledge is tested by simple
questions in a Swiss national language, with age-sensitive handling and, where the mother tongue is a
Swiss national language, possible written questioning. Contacts with Swiss citizens can be shown by
witness statements, correspondence, photographs, participation certificates or other dated evidence of
Swiss-connected activities. The applicant must also identify Swiss-resident reference persons, and SEM
contacts those persons directly; the handbook says at least three persons’ contact details must be
available. In addition, the abroad checklist requires criminal-record extracts from every state in which
the applicant and included children aged 12+ have lived during the previous six years, employment or
studies evidence, and evidence concerning the Swiss spouse’s AHV/pension/social-assistance status.

Official FDFA representation pages add that the representation may also require documents such as
debt-register extracts, tax certificates, social-assistance confirmations and youth-offence confirmations
from the residence state.
Relationship change issues are where content systems most often overstate or understate the law. First,
if the Swiss spouse dies before the application is filed, official SEM FAQ material says a facilitated
spouse application cannot be made; the possible remaining route is ordinary naturalisation where its
own requirements are met. Secondly, if the Swiss spouse dies during an already pending facilitated
naturalisation procedure, the SEM handbook states that the grant may still be possible, but only if the
applicant had already satisfied the naturalisation conditions at the time of death and there is no serious
reason to doubt that a real, stable marital union existed before the spouse died. If the applicant
remarries a non-Swiss person before decision, the facilitated spouse route is no longer available.
Thirdly, separation or divorce during the procedure will usually be fatal because the marital union must
exist both at filing and at decision, and the signed declaration expressly confirms that no separation or
divorce plans exist. Federal guidance also imposes a continuing duty to cooperate and to report
relevant changes without delay. Even changes that do not necessarily destroy eligibility—such as
moving to another canton, moving abroad from a Swiss-resident application, or moving into
Switzerland from an abroad application—may trigger additional inquiries and delay. That is important
for internal drafting: a spouse case is dynamic, and the legal position must be tied to the facts at filing
and at decision, not just at the date of marriage.
Post-grant risk should be stated plainly but carefully. Under Article 36 BüG, SEM may annul a
naturalisation obtained through false statements or concealment of material facts. Federal case law
under the current Act says that the mere absence of substantive conditions is not enough; there must
have been deceptive procurement, though not necessarily criminal-fraud “arglist”. The same body of
law states that annulment may be pronounced within two years after SEM learns of the relevant facts
and, in any event, not later than eight years after the person acquired Swiss citizenship. Article 36 also
extends annulment, subject to statutory exceptions, to children whose Swiss citizenship depends on the
annulled naturalisation.
The marriage-breakdown case law is especially important for internal risk language. The Federal
Supreme Court has long held that a very short period between facilitated naturalisation and separation/
divorce can justify a factual presumption that no stable marital union existed at the decisive time. A
2024 judgment summarised the doctrine by saying that “short” generally means several months to
about one year, but not more than two years. However, the presumption is rebuttable, and the
administration still bears the overall burden of proving deceptive procurement. The same 2023
judgment under the current Act stressed that the applicant can defeat the presumption by presenting a
plausible, concrete explanation—such as an extraordinary event occurring only after naturalisation or a
credible reason why the seriousness of the relationship problems was not yet apparent at the decisive
time. The practical consequence is that divorce soon after grant is not an automatic annulment trigger,
but it is a serious evidential danger area.

## Drafting cautions

## H. Drafting cautions

When drafting future public-facing or internal content on this route, the safest formulations are these:
Do not say or imply that marriage to a Swiss citizen automatically gives Swiss citizenship. Say
instead that marriage may open a route to apply for facilitated naturalisation, subject to
statutory conditions and SEM’s decision.
Keep ordinary and facilitated naturalisation separate. Do not import ordinary-naturalisation
prerequisites such as ten years’ residence or a C permit into the facilitated spouse route without
specific source support.
Split spouse content into at least two branches: living in Switzerland and living abroad. The
former uses the three-years-marital-union/five-years-residence formula; the latter uses six years’
marital union plus close ties to Switzerland.
Use the term marital union rather than treating “marriage” as sufficient. Explain that a genuine,
stable, future-oriented relationship must exist at filing and at decision, and that separate
residences need a documented important reason.
State the language rules precisely. Swiss-resident spouses: usually B1 spoken / A2 written with
specified proof routes. Spouses abroad: everyday spoken ability in a Swiss national language,
assessed in interview, plus Swiss knowledge and reference-backed close ties.
Include public-order and economic-standing issues in every serious explanation of eligibility.
Taxes, debt enforcement, loss certificates, social assistance, criminal record entries and pending
proceedings are not peripheral points.
Always add a relationship-change warning. Before-filing death of the Swiss spouse blocks a fresh
spouse application; breakdown before decision usually defeats the route; false declarations or
concealment can later trigger annulment under Article 36 BüG, and a short interval between
grant and breakdown may create a rebuttable presumption of deception.
Be careful with partnership cases. If the couple are only in a registered partnership, facilitated
naturalisation is not the route; where a partnership has been converted into a marriage, timing
and crediting rules must be checked against SEM’s handbook.
