# Swiss Naturalisation Drafting Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

Swiss naturalisation content is unusually vulnerable to overstatement because the legal framework is
layered, evaluative, and only partly standardised at federal level. Ordinary naturalisation is not a single
federal checklist followed by automatic approval. Federal law sets minimum formal and material
requirements, the State Secretariat for Migration applies the federal layer, and cantons and communes
may add residence, procedural, integration, interview, testing, and fee requirements within
constitutional and statutory limits. The SEM’s own guidance describes ordinary naturalisation as a
federalist process requiring cooperation by federal, cantonal and communal authorities, and
emphasises that the application is sent to SEM only after canton and commune support naturalisation.
The same handbook also confirms that the SEM grants federal authorisation only if federal conditions
are met, after which the canton must still decide the naturalisation and re-check conditions before the
final grant. Formal eligibility therefore does not equal approval, and federal compliance does not
remove cantonal or communal scrutiny.
The safest drafting posture is therefore conservative and source-led. It is usually correct to say that a
person may be able to apply once formal conditions are met; it is often unsafe to say that a person
qualifies, is entitled, or will be approved unless the proposition is tied to a specific legal route and
carefully qualified. Federal law requires, among other things, a C permit for ordinary naturalisation, ten
years’ residence counted under naturalisation rules, successful integration, familiarity with Swiss living
conditions, and no threat to Switzerland’s internal or external security. But the same official materials
make clear that cantons may add integration criteria, impose local residence periods within the federal
range, and run their own procedures, including interviews and knowledge checks.
Automated blog content must also distinguish sharply between ordinary naturalisation, facilitated
naturalisation, and reinstatement. These are different routes with different legal bases, different
authorities, and different evidential questions. Marriage to a Swiss citizen does not itself confer Swiss
nationality; it may create access to facilitated naturalisation if the statutory timing and marital-union
conditions are met, together with the general material conditions. Third-generation foreign nationals
form a separate facilitated route with lineage and schooling evidence requirements. Reinstatement is
different again and presupposes prior possession and later loss of Swiss citizenship. Any system that
collapses these routes into a generic claim about “Swiss citizenship after X years” will reliably create
legal error.
Residence counting is another recurring drafting trap. Naturalisation residence is not identical to
immigration history read at a glance. Official SEM material states that B and C residence counts in full, F
counts only in half, and N and L do not count for the federal residence period. Time between ages eight
and eighteen counts double, but at least six years of actual residence are still required. The SEM
handbook goes further and stresses that the residence condition requires both lawful status and
effective residence in Switzerland, with stable and durable ties showing that the centre of interests is in
Switzerland. It also explains that residence can be interrupted if the person deregisters or actually lives
abroad for more than six months within a twelve-month period, subject to narrow exceptions such as
certain employer-directed or study absences of up to one year. This means that preserving immigration
status during an absence does not automatically preserve naturalisation readiness.

Integration must likewise be drafted as a multi-factor assessment, not a language-only test. Federal law
and ordinance criteria include public order, respect for constitutional values, ability to communicate in a
national language, economic participation or education, and support for the integration of close family
members. Ordinance material and the SEM handbook also connect public-order assessment to criminal
matters, tax arrears, debt enforcement, maintenance obligations, and social assistance history, while
also requiring authorities to account appropriately for disability, illness, and other major personal
circumstances. This makes absolute blog statements especially dangerous. Debt, social assistance,
criminal matters, unpaid taxes, or unpaid maintenance can create risk, but the legal effect depends on
the nature, timing, seriousness, repayment, and any personal-circumstances adjustment recognised by
law.
Post-grant risk should be drafted with restraint. Swiss citizenship is not generally precarious once
granted, but it can be declared null if obtained by false statements or concealment of material facts,
subject to statutory time limits. Official SEM guidance states that nullity is possible for up to eight years
after naturalisation, with a two-year waiting period before a fresh application. Federal Supreme Court
authority shows that mere later non-fulfilment is not enough; there must be deceptive conduct, and the
doctrine has been applied particularly where facilitated naturalisation was obtained on a false picture of
a stable marital union or where relevant criminality was concealed. These risks are real enough to justify
cautionary drafting, but not so routine as to justify alarmist language.
This pack is therefore designed as an internal drafting-control source, not a client memo and not a
marketing explainer. Its function is to supply reusable legal propositions, legally necessary
qualifications, prohibited formulations, and source-checking rules for future automated blog content on
Swiss naturalisation. Any future drafting system should treat “formal eligibility”, “application readiness”,
and “likelihood of approval” as different concepts, should separate federal law from cantonal or
communal practice, and should prefer qualified propositions over broad promises.

## Structural overview of drafting-risk areas

Ordinary naturalisation
The main drafting risk is presenting ordinary naturalisation as a federal entitlement that follows
automatically from time spent in Switzerland. Official SEM material states that a person with ten years’
residence and a C permit can submit an application, not that approval follows automatically, and it also
confirms that canton and commune remain central actors in the process. The SEM handbook expressly
ties ordinary naturalisation to federalism and separate roles for federal, cantonal and communal
authorities.
Facilitated naturalisation
The main risk is folding all facilitated routes into “citizenship through marriage” or “fast-track
citizenship”. Facilitated naturalisation includes several legally distinct routes, including spouses of Swiss
citizens, certain children of Swiss citizens, third-generation foreign nationals, stateless minors, and
some other limited categories. Each route has its own formal conditions and still requires satisfaction of
general material criteria.
Residence counting and continuity
The main risk is assuming that permit history alone answers the residence question. SEM guidance
states that B and C count in full, F counts only in half, and N and L do not count, while the handbook

adds that lawful and effective residence with the centre of interests in Switzerland is required. The
handbook also explains when residence is considered interrupted and when narrow exceptions may
preserve continuity.
C permit requirement
The main risk is saying either too little or too much. It is correct that a C permit is required for ordinary
naturalisation at the time of application; it is incorrect to imply that the C permit is enough. The
handbook further notes that the C permit must remain valid during the procedure and until the federal
authorisation and cantonal decision stages are completed.
Integration and language
The main risk is reducing integration to language or reducing language to “holding a certificate”.
Federal law lists several integration criteria, and the ordinance fixes minimum B1 oral and A2 written
standards while also specifying recognised alternative ways to prove language competence. SEM
guidance stresses that the level is a minimum and that recognised certificates are published and
updated by SEM.
Public order, debt, tax, and social assistance
The main risk is categorical drafting. Ordinance and SEM handbook material link successful integration
to compliance with public-law and private-law obligations, and the handbook treats tax arrears, debt
enforcement history, social assistance dependency, and maintenance obligations as important parts of
the analysis. But the same sources also require consideration of major personal circumstances and
permit certain adjustments.
Cantonal and communal procedure
The main risk is describing one canton’s process as if it were Swiss law. Official cantonal sources show
real procedural variation: Zurich publishes a roughly two-year process with communal and cantonal
stages and a cantonal knowledge test; Bern uses a written naturalisation test and a personal communal
interview; Vaud uses an official knowledge test organised by the commune and publishes canton-
specific fee ranges; Geneva requires effective residence in the canton throughout the procedure; Basel-
Stadt and Ticino describe their own local sequence and fee regimes. These differences are genuine local
practice or local law, not federal universals.
Children and family members
The main risk is assuming that a family naturalises as a block. The SEM handbook states that, under
federal law, there is no general obligation to include minor children in an ordinary naturalisation
application, and it also notes that minor children can only be included if the parent fulfils the
naturalisation conditions or can rely on recognised personal circumstances. The handbook chapter on
facilitated routes adds that minor children are generally included where statutory conditions are met
and they live with the applicant, but those children may still need separate age-sensitive examination,
especially from age twelve and again from age sixteen as regards own will.
Absence abroad and interrupted residence
The main risk is confusing immigration continuity with naturalisation continuity. The SEM handbook
distinguishes actual residence, stable ties, and the centre of interests in Switzerland from mere

possession of a permit, and explains that deregistration or substantial actual residence abroad can
interrupt the residence relevant to naturalisation. Content should therefore avoid promising that
preserved status alone protects a future citizenship application.
Refusal, appeal, and reapplication
The main risk is either denying meaningful review or overstating appeal prospects. Federal Supreme
Court case law confirms that refusal decisions must be reasoned and are subject to judicial review on
law and facts at canton level, while still leaving room for local evaluative judgment. A refusal is not
immune from review, but nor is an appeal a routine way to overturn any negative integration
assessment.
Dual nationality and consequences of citizenship
The main risk is stopping the analysis at Swiss law. Switzerland recognises dual nationality without
requiring renunciation of prior citizenship, but SEM and EDA guidance both state that the law of the
other country may still cause loss of the previous nationality or other consequences, and Swiss
authorities cannot give binding advice on that foreign-law issue.
Nullity, withdrawal, and post-grant risk
The main risk is alarmism on the one hand and omission on the other. Official SEM guidance states that
naturalisation obtained by false statements or concealment of material facts may be declared null
within eight years, while revocation is reserved for serious cases affecting Switzerland’s interests or
reputation and applies only to dual nationals. Supreme Court authority limits nullity to cases involving
deceptive conduct rather than mere later disagreement about whether substantive conditions were
met.

## Topic-specific cautions and article templates

Ordinary naturalisation
Drafting should avoid entitlement language. The correct federal starting point is that a person with ten
qualifying years and a C permit may submit an application for ordinary naturalisation, but approval still
depends on material conditions and the cantonal/communal layer. Every ordinary-naturalisation article
should therefore distinguish three ideas: access to apply, satisfaction of federal minimum conditions,
and final approval in the multi-level process.
The federal, cantonal and communal structure should be visible in the introductory paragraph. Safe
phrasing is that federal law sets minimum conditions, while cantons and communes control important
practical aspects such as local residence, interviews, tests, timing and fees. Unsafe phrasing is any
sentence suggesting that SEM alone “grants Swiss citizenship” in ordinary naturalisation. SEM grants
the federal naturalisation authorisation; Swiss citizenship is acquired when the cantonal naturalisation
decision becomes legally binding.
Residence counting
Residence should be described as a legal counting exercise rather than a rough calendar total. A safe
core sentence is that qualifying residence depends on the status held during the relevant periods and
on continuity rules. Add that time between ages eight and eighteen counts double but at least six years
of actual residence are still required.
Avoid equating naturalisation residence with immigration residence. The SEM handbook expressly links
the naturalisation residence condition to lawful and effective residence, stable and durable presence,
and the centre of interests in Switzerland. Content should not assume that a long permit history, by
itself, proves naturalisation readiness.
C permit requirement
The safest formulation is that a valid C permit is ordinarily required to apply for ordinary naturalisation.
That is a federal threshold condition. It should immediately be followed by a second sentence making
clear that a C permit does not by itself establish suitability for citizenship.
Where the article touches timing, add that the SEM handbook expects the settlement permit to remain
in place during the procedure and through the federal authorisation and cantonal decision stages. This
is useful for planning articles about filing too early or moving during a pending case.

Integration
Integration should be presented as multi-factorial. Federal law says that successful integration is shown
in particular by compliance with public order, respect for constitutional values, practical language ability,
participation in economic life or education, and support for the integration of close family members.
Blog drafting should also reflect the link between integration and “familiarity with Swiss living
conditions”. This helps avoid the common mistake of presenting integration as either a document
exercise or purely linguistic. If local civic knowledge tests or interviews are mentioned, label them as
ways some cantons or communes operationalise broader federal ideas, not as a single nationwide
federal test.
Language evidence
Do not say “pass a language test” as though one method were universal. Article-safe wording is that
applicants must prove minimum oral and written competence in a national language, and Swiss law
recognises several proof routes, including mother tongue, five years of compulsory schooling in a
national language, secondary or tertiary education completed in a national language, or a valid
recognised language certificate.
Do not say “any German, French or Italian certificate is accepted”. The SEM publishes a dynamic list of
recognised certificates and updates it regularly; the 1 April 2026 edition expressly states that the
certificates on the list satisfy the minimum quality requirements for immigration and citizenship
proceedings, and recognition depends on the list position when the application was filed.
Exemptions or adjustments require caution. Authorities must take proper account of disability, serious
illness and other major personal circumstances, but content should not promise a waiver. Safer wording
is that the authority may adjust the assessment where the statutory personal-circumstances rules
apply.
Cantonal and communal procedure
All procedure content should carry a local-variation warning. Safe wording is that the exact filing
authority, sequence of checks, interviews, tests, fees and timing depend on the canton and sometimes
the commune. SEM itself directs applicants to commune or canton for the form and local process.
When giving concrete examples, identify them as local. Zurich publishes an online filing route and an
approximate two-year duration; Bern describes a communal interview and a written naturalisation test;
Vaud says the commune organises the official knowledge test; Geneva requires effective residence in
the canton throughout the procedure; Basel-Stadt describes a communal conversation and current fee
changes; Ticino publishes its own communal, federal and cantonal sequence and its own fee structure.
These examples are useful, but they must stay canton-specific.
Facilitated naturalisation by marriage
The drafting hazard is the phrase “Swiss citizenship through marriage”. Swiss citizenship does not arise
from marriage itself. The safe proposition is that marriage to a Swiss citizen can create access to
facilitated naturalisation if the statutory conditions are met. Those conditions differ depending on
whether the applicant lives in Switzerland or abroad.

The marriage route must also be described as requiring a real marital union. Article 10 BüV defines
marital union as requiring both the formal existence of the marriage and an actual life together in
which the shared intention to maintain a stable marital union remains intact; the requirement must
exist at filing and at naturalisation. Content should therefore avoid promising approval where the
relationship has already broken down or is unstable.
Facilitated naturalisation for third-generation foreign nationals
This route should never be described as ordinary naturalisation with a shorter clock. It is a separate
facilitated route with its own statutory lineage, birth, settlement-permit, schooling and age conditions.
The applicant must generally apply before age twenty-five.
Drafting should highlight the evidential burden. Official SEM material for this route includes forms and
evidence related to grandparents’ residence status, a parent’s residence and schooling, school
authorities, tax authorities, social assistance authorities and, where relevant, juvenile criminal matters.
This is evidence-heavy content and should not be written as a casual “if you are third generation, you
qualify” proposition.
Children and families
Avoid assuming that children or spouses move together legally. In ordinary naturalisation, the SEM
handbook says there is no federal-law obligation to include minor children, and it also says minor
children can be included only if the parent fulfils the naturalisation conditions or can invoke recognised
personal circumstances.
For facilitated routes, children may sometimes be included or may have their own facilitated route, but
the rules are route-specific. Official SEM pages on children of Swiss citizens also show that a child
omitted from a parent’s earlier naturalisation may later rely on a separate facilitated route if statutory
age and residence conditions are met. Family articles should therefore separate the position of each
family member.
Absences and interrupted residence
Drafting should separate five concepts: permit preservation, lawful residence, actual residence, centre
of life, and readiness to apply. The SEM handbook is explicit that someone with a lawful permit but no
effective residence in Switzerland does not meet the federal residence condition for naturalisation.
Where absences are discussed, use cautious phrasing such as “may interrupt qualifying residence” and
name the official exception where appropriate. Under Article 16 BüV, an absence abroad of up to one
year on employer instructions or for study purposes can still count as a short-term departure with the
intention to return, but longer absences normally interrupt residence.
Refusal and appeal
Refusal risk should be described probabilistically, not deterministically. It is safe to say that applications
can be refused if federal or local conditions are not satisfied, or if new negative facts emerge before the
final cantonal decision. It is unsafe to say that a given issue “will definitely lead to refusal” unless the
proposition is tied to a specific, hard federal rule.
Appeals should not be oversold. Federal Supreme Court case law confirms that negative naturalisation
decisions must be reasoned and that cantonal courts must conduct free review of law and facts, while

still respecting the legitimate evaluative room of lower authorities. The same case law also shows that
unfair procedure, undisclosed testing methods, discriminatory reasoning, or lack of reasons can
invalidate a decision. But success remains fact-sensitive.
Dual nationality
The safe core sentence is that Switzerland permits dual nationality without requiring renunciation. That
statement always needs a second sentence warning that the law of the applicant’s other country may
impose loss of the previous nationality or other consequences.
Avoid global claims about “no consequences”. EDA guidance also notes that multiple nationality can
affect the practical scope of Swiss consular assistance abroad where the person also holds the
nationality of the state of residence. That point is usually not central to a naturalisation article, but it
reinforces the need not to use absolute language.
Post-grant risk
The safest way to mention post-grant risk is to say that Swiss citizenship is ordinarily stable once
granted, but can later be declared null where it was obtained through false statements or concealment
of material facts. Add the official eight-year maximum and two-year waiting period for a fresh
application after a nullity decision becomes final.
Do not imply a general power to reopen citizenship merely because an authority later changes its
assessment. Supreme Court authority confirms that nullity requires deceptive conduct; simple later
discovery that substantive requirements were in fact lacking is not enough by itself. That distinction is
central to drafting about post-grant vulnerability.

## Automation controls

Risk classification system for automated drafting
A green proposition may be used broadly if cited and if the article stays within the relevant route.
Examples include: “Ordinary naturalisation usually requires a C permit”; “Switzerland generally permits
dual nationality”; “Marriage to a Swiss citizen does not itself confer Swiss citizenship”; and “Federal law
sets only minimum conditions for ordinary naturalisation, with cantonal and communal layers still
relevant.” Those propositions are repeatedly confirmed in official SEM sources and the governing
legislation.
An amber proposition may be used only with qualification. Examples include: “Debt can affect
naturalisation”; “Absences abroad can interrupt qualifying residence”; “Local interviews or civic tests
may apply”; and “A criminal record can affect approval.” These are directionally accurate, but their legal
effect turns on seriousness, timing, local law, exceptions, or procedural detail.
A red proposition should not be used without jurisdiction-specific, canton-specific or fact-specific
verification. Examples include: “The procedure takes about two years”; “You need two years in the
canton”; “Your language certificate will be accepted”; and “Debt for the last five years is disqualifying.”
Those can be true locally, but official sources show they vary by canton, by route, by date and by the
evidence on file.
A prohibited proposition should not be used because it is legally misleading. Examples include: “You
automatically qualify after ten years”; “A C permit gives you the right to citizenship”; “Marriage gives you
Swiss citizenship”; “All cantons apply the same procedure”; and “Once granted, Swiss citizenship can
never be challenged.”

Internal drafting rules for the blog writer
Always distinguish ordinary naturalisation from facilitated naturalisation and from reinstatement.
Always state that ordinary naturalisation involves federal, cantonal and communal layers. Never state or

## Practical legal analysis

Swiss naturalisation is unusually sensitive to overgeneralisation because it combines federal minimum
law, cantonal and communal autonomy, evaluative integration criteria, and strong evidential
dependence. The same topic can move from bright-line rule to fact-sensitive judgment in a single
paragraph: a C permit is a clear federal threshold, but whether a person is “successfully integrated”,
“familiar with Swiss living conditions”, or still has continuous residence is often a mixed question of law,
fact and local practice. That is why automated content should avoid broad promises and instead work
from a bank of conservative propositions paired with route-specific and locality-specific qualifications.
Formal eligibility must also be separated from evaluative assessment. A person may satisfy the filing
criteria and still face difficulty on integration, financial conduct, residence continuity, or the local
procedural assessment. Conversely, some apparently adverse facts do not always lead to refusal
because federal ordinance rules require the authority to account for disability, serious illness and other
major personal circumstances. This is a poor fit for simplistic “yes/no” blog copy and a strong fit for a
controlled authority pack.
Federal law alone is insufficient for many practical blog topics. If the article asks where to file, how long
the process takes, whether there will be an interview, what knowledge will be tested, what fee will apply,
or how long a person must live in the commune, the answer often lies in cantonal or communal
materials rather than in the federal act. Official pages from Zurich, Bern, Geneva, Vaud, Basel-Stadt and
Ticino illustrate this clearly.
“Eligibility”, “application readiness”, and “likelihood of approval” should therefore be treated as separate
concepts in automated drafting. Eligibility answers whether the route is legally open in principle.
Application readiness asks whether the filing conditions, evidence and current facts line up. Likelihood
of approval is a much more evaluative judgment and should rarely be expressed in public-facing blog
content except in very qualified terms.
This authority pack should also be refreshed periodically. The SEM handbook is updated, recognised
language-certificate lists are refreshed, and cantonal procedures and fees change over time. In this
research set alone, the SEM language-certificate list has a dated April 2026 edition and Basel-Stadt
publishes a 2026 fee reform. A static output is therefore not enough for certificate-specific or fee-
specific automation unless it is routinely checked and refreshed.

## Drafting cautions and open questions

Do not describe Swiss naturalisation as a points-based or automatic process. Do not say that ten years’
residence alone is enough. Do not say that a C permit alone is enough. Do not ignore the federal/
cantonal/communal structure. Do not generalise from one canton or commune. Do not merge ordinary
and facilitated naturalisation. Do not imply that marriage automatically leads to Swiss citizenship. Do
not omit integration, public order, financial conduct and language issues. Do not present all absences
abroad as having the same effect. Do not confuse permit continuity with citizenship-residence
continuity. Do not overstate dual-nationality protection without checking the other country’s law. Do not

and the context remains carefully qualified.

stable form, so certain statutory article wordings were cross-checked through the publicly available SEM
handbook and statute mirrors that reproduce the consolidated text. That is sufficient for a conservative
drafting-control pack, but for litigation-grade citation or publication of extended statutory extracts,
direct Fedlex verification should be repeated at the time of use.
