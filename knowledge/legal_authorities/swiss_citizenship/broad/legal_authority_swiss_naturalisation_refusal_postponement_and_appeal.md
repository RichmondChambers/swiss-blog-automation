# Swiss Naturalisation Refusal and Appeal Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

## A. Executive legal summary. Swiss naturalisation law distinguishes sharply between ordinary

naturalisation, which is federally framed but primarily run through cantonal and often communal
procedures, and facilitated naturalisation, which is principally a federal procedure decided by the
State Secretariat for Migration (SEM). For ordinary naturalisation, the federal minimum structure is: a
valid C permit at filing, at least ten years’ residence in Switzerland including three of the last five
years, successful integration, familiarity with Swiss living conditions, and no threat to Switzerland’s
internal or external security. Cantons may add further integration criteria and regulate the cantonal and
communal procedure. By contrast, facilitated naturalisation for spouses and other specified categories
is decided by the SEM; for spouses living in Switzerland a valid residence permit throughout the
procedure is required, but not a C permit, and the domestic facilitated route applies the same
integration criteria as ordinary naturalisation. 1

In adverse cases, it is important to separate three different phenomena. First, there is formal
ineligibility: for example, no C permit in an ordinary case, insufficient residence, non-qualifying
residence status, or an incomplete file that is not procedurally ready. Secondly, there are evaluative or
suitability findings, such as insufficient integration, inadequate language, poor local or civic
knowledge, failure to respect constitutional values, or lack of familiarity with Swiss life. Thirdly, there are
dynamic procedural impediments that may justify delay, suspension, or write-off rather than an
immediate merits refusal, notably pending criminal proceedings, changes of residence during the
process, expiry or loss of the relevant residence status, or the need for supplementary investigation.
Federal and cantonal materials treat these categories differently, and later drafting should do the same.

The strongest recurring substantive risk factors in both ordinary and facilitated cases are public-order
concerns, criminal-record issues, pending criminal proceedings, financial non-compliance, social-
assistance dependence, weak language evidence, poor proof of everyday integration, and weak or
contradictory evidence about residence, marriage, family life, or civic familiarity. Some of these operate
close to mandatory bars in federal law or ordinance; others remain contextual and can vary by canton
or commune. Not every debt, tax issue, or local integration concern automatically produces refusal,
especially in ordinary naturalisation, where the SEM handbook repeatedly leaves broad space to
cantonal assessment, and where personal circumstances must be taken into account for certain criteria.

Procedural fairness is legally significant. Negative ordinary-naturalisation decisions must be reasoned;
communal assemblies may reject only on a reasoned motion; privacy rules constrain what is disclosed
to local voters; and the SEM must hear the applicant before refusing a federal naturalisation permit or
facilitated naturalisation. The leading Federal Supreme Court authorities also make clear that
unreasoned, secret-ballot refusals are incompatible with constitutional guarantees, and that
naturalisation refusals cannot rest on discriminatory assumptions such as headscarf-wearing as such.
Remedies differ by route: ordinary cantonal refusals go first through cantonal remedies and only in
limited form onward to the Federal Supreme Court; SEM refusals of the federal permit in ordinary cases

go to the Federal Administrative Court and stop there; SEM refusals of facilitated naturalisation go to
the Federal Administrative Court and, unlike ordinary naturalisation, Federal Supreme Court case law
indicates that a further public-law appeal is generally open. 4

On reapplication, the materials reviewed do not disclose a single Switzerland-wide waiting period for an
ordinary or facilitated refusal as such. Instead, timing is defect-specific and often locally governed: Bern
allows consensual suspension for up to two years where conditions are not yet fully met; Vaud allows
suspension for up to one year; Zurich states that if a move occurs before the municipality has finished
its inquiry, the procedure is not continued and a fresh application in the new municipality requires two
years’ residence there. A distinct, express reapplication bar exists after an annulment for deceit: SEM
guidance states that a fresh naturalisation request may be made only after a two-year waiting period
once annulment becomes final. 5

## Structural overview and legal propositions

## B. Structural overview of refusal, postponement, and appeal. In ordinary naturalisation, the usual

sequence is communal or cantonal filing, cantonal and often communal fact-finding, transmission to
the SEM only if canton and—where cantonal law provides—the commune can support naturalisation,
SEM scrutiny for the federal permit, and then a final cantonal grant or refusal. The SEM’s federal permit
is a necessary condition for validity, but it does not itself create an entitlement to naturalisation. Even
after the SEM has issued the federal permit, the canton must re-check whether the requirements still
hold; if new disqualifying facts emerge, the canton may still refuse. If the applicant loses the C-permit
condition, including through departure abroad and revocation, the cantonal authority may write the file
off rather than continue. 6

Facilitated naturalisation has a different architecture. For spouses and the other categories in Articles
22, 23, 24 and 51 BüG, the SEM conducts the procedure in substance and decides the case. Cantonal or
communal bodies may be heard and can challenge a positive SEM decision, but they do not control the
decision in the same way as in ordinary naturalisation. For applicants abroad whose route depends on
“close connection” rather than domestic integration, the relevant inquiry is adapted accordingly and
includes evidence of visits to Switzerland, oral competence in a national language, and knowledge of
Swiss conditions, gathered through Swiss representations abroad. 7

Primary texts and official guidance

## D. Primary legal texts. The main legal framework consists of the Federal Constitution (especially

Articles 37 and 38 on citizenship and competence), the Swiss Citizenship Act (Bürgerrechtsgesetz,
BüG, SR 141.0), and the Ordinance on Swiss Citizenship (Bürgerrechtsverordnung, BüV, SR 141.01).
The Zurich cantonal handbook accurately summarises the constitutional division of powers: the
Confederation regulates facilitated naturalisation and other modes of acquisition exhaustively, while in
ordinary naturalisation it sets minimum rules and the cantons regulate the rest, including the cantonal
and communal procedure. SEM’s own legal-bases page identifies the BüG and BüV as the core federal
enactments and places them alongside migration- and integration-law instruments such as the AIG
where permit status and integration interact with citizenship proceedings. 20

For ordinary naturalisation, the priority provisions are: Article 9 BüG on formal requirements; Articles
11 and 12 BüG on material requirements and integration; Articles 13 and 14 BüG on the ordinary
procedure and the federal permit; Article 16 BüG on reasoned refusals; Article 17 BüG on privacy; and
Articles 46 and 47 BüG on remedies. In the ordinance, the most important provisions are Articles 2 to
7 BüV on familiarity, security, public order, constitutional values, language, and economic participation;
Article 9 BüV on personal circumstances; Articles 13 to 17 BüV on procedural implementation; Article
21 BüV on the duty to provide complete and updated information; Article 23 BüV on SEM’s indicative
processing times; and Article 27 BüV on fee advances. For facilitated naturalisation abroad, Article 11
BüV on “close connection” is especially significant. 21

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials. SEM’s Handbuch Bürgerrecht is particularly

important because it is expressly intended as a reference work for SEM staff, cantonal and communal
naturalisation authorities, and Swiss representations abroad in interpreting the Act and Ordinance. The
current handbook chapters especially relevant here are Chapter 3 on ordinary naturalisation, Chapter
4 on facilitated naturalisation for spouses living in Switzerland, and Chapter 6 on other facilitated
categories. SEM also publishes practical public-facing guidance on ordinary naturalisation, language
requirements, facilitated routes, and category-specific eligibility, such as spouses and third-generation
foreigners. Those public pages should not replace the handbook, but they are useful as concise official
summaries of current administrative practice. 22

The SEM materials are especially useful on three points. First, they show which matters are truly federal
minimum standards and which are left to cantons or communes. Secondly, they explain that the
ordinary-naturalisation investigation report must cover residence, permit status, public order,
constitutional values, language, economic participation, family-integration support, familiarity with
Swiss life, and any personal circumstances requiring accommodation. Thirdly, they show the continuing

duty of applicants to provide truthful, complete, and updated information and to report later facts
that may obstruct naturalisation. 23

## Case law and cantonal practice

## F. Case law notes. The basic procedural authority remains BGE 129 I 232, where the Federal Supreme

Court held that naturalisation refusals are subject to a duty to give reasons under the constitutional
right to be heard and the prohibition of discrimination; this is why secret-ballot refusals are structurally
incompatible with constitutionally compliant review. Closely related is BGE 129 I 217, which recognised
that in the naturalisation field an applicant may, despite the absence of a general substantive
entitlement to ordinary naturalisation, still invoke constitutional complaints about arbitrariness or
discrimination. 24

On substance, BGE 132 I 167 confirms that refusal for lack of integration can be lawful where the facts
genuinely support a finding that the applicant has consciously lived apart from the local population and
has not integrated into the social environment. The counterweight is BGE 134 I 49, which treats refusal
based simply on the wearing of a headscarf as impermissibly discriminatory, and BGE 134 I 56, which
makes the same point while still allowing refusal where there are independently sufficient grounds such
as inadequate language and civic knowledge. Together, those decisions support a careful distinction
between legally relevant integration facts and prohibited stereotyping. 25

The Federal Supreme Court has also insisted on proportional handling of disability, caregiving, and
local-integration questions. In BGE 135 I 49, an economic self-sufficiency rule for naturalisation was
scrutinised because it affected persons with disabilities in a specific way. In BGE 138 I 242, the Court
rejected a rigid view that a working woman caring for her disabled son lacked local integration simply
because she was not visibly involved in clubs or associations. Conversely, BGE 138 I 305 accepted that a
refusal for poor local integration could survive review where the applicant had no significant personal
ties with the community and had made no meaningful integration efforts. These authorities are central
when later drafting around exceptions, disabilities, or local-integration evidence. 26

A separate line of authority matters for remedies in facilitated cases. Federal Supreme Court case law
has repeatedly stated that the Article 83(b) BGG exclusion applies to ordinary naturalisation
decisions, and that facilitated naturalisation decisions are therefore not excluded in the same way. That
makes the remedial landscape materially different depending on whether the case is ordinary or
facilitated. 27

## G. Cantonal and communal practice notes. Official cantonal and communal materials confirm that

local practice is not uniform. In Zurich, the cantonal handbook states that the canton regulates the
substantive requirements for ordinary naturalisation conclusively and that Zurich municipalities may
not add their own extra substantive criteria; their autonomy lies mainly in the internal communal
procedure, the deciding communal organ, and local fees. Zurich’s official page also states that if an
applicant moves before the municipality has completed its inquiry, the procedure is no longer
continued and a fresh filing in the new municipality is possible only after two years’ residence there.
Zurich’s communal practice also uses specific language and civic-knowledge instruments, including the
KDE language test and a basic-knowledge test with preparation materials. 28

In Bern, the official cantonal site describes a three-level ordinary-naturalisation process involving
municipality, canton, and Confederation. The Bern ordinance on the naturalisation procedure expressly
allows the application, by agreement with the persons concerned, to be suspended for up to two
years where the conditions are not yet fully met. Bern’s official language page ties the language

requirement to the language of the applicant’s administrative district and reiterates the federal
minimum of A2 written / B1 spoken. At communal level, the City of Bern explains that applicants
undergo a local process and makes available an interview/test information sheet. 29

In Vaud, official material shows a distinctly inquiry-based communal stage. The commune conducts an
inquiry report and hearing in ordinary naturalisation; the municipality may suspend for up to one year
or refuse, and the cantonal authority may also suspend for up to one year or refuse. The recourse
identified in Vaud’s official procedural material is to the CDAP within 30 days. At communal level, the
City of Lausanne states that its naturalisation office prepares information reports, organises hearings,
and runs knowledge tests, while the city also supports applicants through preparatory offers for the
communal hearing. 30

In Ticino, the official portal says that ordinary naturalisation is examined in sequence by the commune,
the Confederation, and the canton, and stresses that the applicant must remain eligible throughout
the whole procedure. Ticino’s regulation also provides that where the municipal legislature decides on
communal citizenship, the decision must be notified to the applicant, reasoned, and accompanied by
information on remedies; refusal by the legislature must rest on a negative municipal message or
commission report. That is a useful illustration of how communal political organs remain legally
constrained. 31

In Geneva, the OCPM Service des naturalisations is officially described as the body that conducts all
forms of citizenship procedure under federal and cantonal law, and it also handles facilitated
naturalisation files on behalf of the Confederation for the canton. Geneva therefore illustrates a
more centralised cantonal-administrative model than the more municipality-intensive patterns seen in
parts of German-speaking Switzerland and Vaud. 32

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

## H. Practical legal analysis. The first analytical checkpoint in an adverse Swiss naturalisation case

should be whether the problem is threshold-based or assessment-based. Threshold problems include
no C permit in ordinary cases, no valid residence permit in facilitated spouse cases, insufficient
residence, non-qualifying residence history, non-payment of a required federal fee advance, or loss of
the relevant residence status before decision. Those issues can produce rejection, non-entry,
discontinuation, or write-off without a deeper merits assessment. Assessment-based problems arise
only after the file survives the threshold screen. 33

For ordinary naturalisation, the most difficult drafting area is financial and social integration. Federal
materials make clear that non-payment of important public or private obligations may justify refusal,
expressly mentioning taxes, health-insurance arrears, fines, rent, and family maintenance. But the same
materials also say that, in ordinary naturalisation, examination of financial leumund is largely left to the
cantons and that debt-enforcement and bankruptcy questions require assessment of the whole
situation. Future drafting therefore should avoid turning every debt issue into an automatic bar; the
better formulation is that such matters are serious negative indicators which may amount to failure to
respect public order, especially where the pattern is repeated, substantial, or wilful. 15

For social assistance, the federal rule is stricter. Recent or current social-assistance dependence means
the criterion of participation in economic life or education is not met unless the assistance has been
fully repaid, and cantons may impose stricter rules. Even so, the personal-circumstances clause remains
important. The authorities must appropriately consider disability, long-term illness, severe literacy
difficulties, poverty, caregiving duties, and education-related dependency where those factors are not

self-induced. That makes later drafting on social assistance particularly sensitive: it is generally a major
obstacle, but not an area where one should suppress the explicit statutory accommodation mechanism.

For criminal and security issues, the law separates ordinary public-order defects from higher-order
security threats. Visible VOSTRA entries in the categories specified by Article 4 BüV make the applicant
“not successfully integrated” for federal purposes while the entry remains visible; in less severe entry
situations the SEM assesses the case with reference to sanction type, service of sentence, and expiry of
probation. Pending criminal proceedings are handled procedurally: the SEM suspends the file until the
criminal case is finally concluded. Where the issue is internal or external security, the SEM’s materials go
further still: no criminal conviction is required if, after investigation, the authority has no remaining
concrete and genuine doubts that the person constitutes a security threat. 34

For language and civic familiarity, the federal minimum in domestic naturalisation remains A2
written / B1 spoken, but cantons may go higher and local practice on testing varies. The ordinance
allows cantonal authorities to require a knowledge test, but only on conditions designed to preserve
fairness: suitable preparation materials or courses must be available, and the test must be passable
with the language level already required for naturalisation. This makes poor performance at interview
or testing legally relevant, but it also limits over-demanding local practices. Where the applicant is
abroad and relies on a facilitated route based on close connection, the inquiry changes: the applicant is
assessed through a consular-style interview focused on visits to Switzerland, oral use of a national
language, and knowledge of Swiss conditions, rather than necessarily the same domestic written-proof
model. 35

A recurrent source of adverse outcomes is evidence management. Applicants in ordinary and
facilitated procedures must provide truthful and complete information and promptly disclose later
changes that may obstruct naturalisation, including changes in residence, marital status, pending
criminal investigations, and dependency on social assistance. In ordinary cases, the investigation report
is expected to cover a wide menu of facts relevant to the criteria. In facilitated spouse cases, the SEM re-
collects declarations about genuine marital community and respect for public order at the start and, if
necessary, shortly before decision. Misleading or incomplete information therefore matters both
prospectively, as a refusal risk, and retrospectively, because an already granted naturalisation may later
be annulled for deceit. 36

On delay and postponement, the safest legal position is that not all delay is unlawful and not all
delayed cases are weak on the merits. Some delays are structurally inherent in three-level ordinary
procedures and in fact-finding for facilitated procedures. Some are formally recognised postponements:
Bern’s two-year consensual suspension and Vaud’s one-year suspension mechanism. Some stem from
changed facts, such as a move during the process or the need for a supplementary report because the
existing report has become stale. The SEM handbook also records indicative federal processing times—
generally eight months for an ordinary federal permit after receipt of complete documents, and twelve
months for facilitated naturalisation after the investigation report—yet those are administrative targets,
not absolute statutory guarantees of decision by a fixed date. 37

## I. Drafting cautions. Future drafting for an internal content system should distinguish carefully

between refusal, suspension, discontinuation/write-off, and non-entry. A pending criminal case, an
agreed postponement, a move interrupting communal residence, or failure to pay a fee advance are not
the same legal event as a reasoned merits refusal. 38

It is also essential not to collapse ordinary and facilitated naturalisation into a single refusal logic.
Ordinary naturalisation depends on federal minimum rules plus cantonal and often communal

implementation, requires a C permit, and has narrow federal judicial review routes. Facilitated
naturalisation is decided by the SEM, does not necessarily require a C permit, and has a more
federalised appeal structure. Within facilitated naturalisation, domestic-spouse cases and “close
connection” cases abroad are materially different. 39

Local practice should never be presented as uniform. Zurich denies municipalities extra substantive
criteria but leaves them procedural autonomy; Bern and Vaud institutionalise suspensions; Lausanne
organises hearings and knowledge tests; Ticino requires a reasoned communal legislative decision;
Geneva centralises much of the procedure within the OCPM. Any content that speaks of “the commune”
or “local interview” as though Switzerland had a single model would be legally misleading. 40

Finally, appeal prospects must not be overstated. Ordinary naturalisation does not offer a full federal
merits appeal to the Federal Supreme Court. What is usually open is cantonal review and then, at
federal level, only limited constitutional review, or in the case of SEM refusal of the federal permit,
review by the Federal Administrative Court only. Facilitated-naturalisation refusals allow more federal
review, but still require careful identification of the correct procedural path and time limit. 41
