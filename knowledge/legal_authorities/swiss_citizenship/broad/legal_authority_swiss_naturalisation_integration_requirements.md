# Swiss naturalisation integration authority pack Executive legal position

## A. Executive legal summary

Under Swiss federal law, ordinary naturalisation requires three distinct material conditions:
successful integration, familiarity with Swiss living conditions, and no threat to Switzerland’s
internal or external security. The integration limb is then broken down by statute into five core
manifestations: respect for public security and order, respect for constitutional values, ability to
communicate in a national language in everyday life, participation in economic life or education, and
encouragement and support of the integration of certain family members. The statute also requires
appropriate account to be taken of disability, illness and other major personal circumstances affecting
the language and economic-life criteria, and it expressly allows cantons to provide additional
integration criteria. 1

Facilitated naturalisation is not identical in form to ordinary naturalisation. The federal statute
expressly requires, for facilitated naturalisation, compliance with the article 12 integration criteria
and an absence of risk to internal or external security. It does not separately restate the ordinary-
naturalisation requirement of familiarity with Swiss living conditions in article 11(b). SEM’s current
handbook nevertheless explains that the revised law brought the level of integration expected in
facilitated naturalisation closer to that required for ordinary naturalisation, and applies the same article
12 integration criteria to facilitated cases. That distinction matters for later drafting. 2

Integration is therefore multi-factorial and evaluative. It should not be reduced to language alone. At
the same time, the framework is not wholly open-textured: the Citizenship Ordinance and SEM
handbook specify concrete disqualifying or strongly adverse matters, including certain criminal-record
entries, pending criminal proceedings, willful non-fulfilment of important public or private obligations,
and recent social-assistance dependence unless the assistance has been fully repaid, subject to the
statutory/personal-circumstances carve-out. 3

For ordinary naturalisation, cantons and communes remain central. Federal law leaves cantonal and
communal procedure to cantonal law, requires a positive cantonal—and where cantonal law so provides
communal—pre-assessment before the file goes to SEM, and permits cantonal residence periods of two
to five years. Official federal and cantonal materials show that local integration is frequently
operationalised through commune interviews, local reports, civic-knowledge testing and evidence of
social participation. 4

## Federal framework

## B. Structural overview of integration requirements

For ordinary naturalisation, the hierarchy is:

    1. Federal statute: article 11 sets the three material conditions; article 12 defines the integration
       criteria; articles 13 to 15 allocate federal, cantonal and communal roles; article 18 permits

       cantonal residence periods of two to five years; article 12(3) allows cantonal additional
       integration criteria. 5

    2. Federal ordinance: articles 2 to 9 specify what counts as familiarity with Swiss living conditions,
       threats to security, non-respect of public security and order, constitutional values, language
       proof, participation in economic life or education, family-integration support, and
       accommodation of personal circumstances. 6

    3. SEM administrative guidance: the current SEM handbook translates those provisions into
       operational decision-making for federal, cantonal and communal authorities, including
       evidential examples and cautionary rules on tax arrears, debt, criminal record entries, social
       assistance and family-integration support. 7

For facilitated naturalisation, the federal statute cross-refers to article 12 integration criteria and adds
the security condition, while SEM’s handbook applies substantially the same integration analysis.
Facilitated naturalisation is handled primarily by SEM, though enquiries may still be carried out with
cantonal involvement. 2

## Primary authorities and official guidance

## D. Primary legal texts

The federal statute’s present structure is clear. Article 11 states the three ordinary-naturalisation
material conditions. Article 12 gives the integration criteria and makes the list non-exhaustive by using
“in particular”. Article 13 requires the file to be sent to SEM only once the canton—and, where cantonal
law so requires, the commune—can support the grant. Article 15 leaves cantonal and communal
procedure to cantonal law. Article 18 allows cantonal residence periods of two to five years. Article 20
then sets the material conditions for facilitated naturalisation by cross-referring to article 12 integration
and adding the security condition. 17

The ordinance is the critical source for operational analysis. Article 2 defines familiarity with Swiss
living conditions for ordinary naturalisation by reference to elementary geographic, historical, political
and social knowledge, participation in Swiss social and cultural life, and contacts with Swiss citizens;
article 2(2) allows cantonal authorities to require a knowledge test, but only if applicants can prepare
using appropriate tools or courses and can pass the test with the language skills required for
naturalisation. 18

Article 3 defines threats to internal or external security through concrete indicators such as terrorism,
violent extremism, organised crime and prohibited intelligence activity. Article 4 defines disregard of
public security and order. Article 5 identifies relevant constitutional values, including rule of law, the
democratic-liberal order, fundamental rights such as equality between women and men and freedom of
conscience and opinion, and obligations linked to military/civil service and compulsory schooling.
Articles 6 to 9 then govern language, economic life/education, family integration and personal
circumstances. 19

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM’s current handbook for applications from 1 January 2018 is the principal federal administrative
source. The handbook page for ordinary naturalisation lists a full integration analysis under article 12
and then treats familiarity with Swiss living conditions and security as separate headings; the chapter
also confirms that ordinary naturalisation is built on Swiss federalism and that authorities conduct
enquiries and an interview to assess suitability. 20

On the public-facing side, SEM’s ordinary-naturalisation page confirms the ordinary-naturalisation
federal test and expressly notes that cantons may stipulate additional integration requirements.
SEM’s third-generation facilitated-naturalisation page gives a useful official plain-language formulation
of the same article 12 criteria and concrete examples of public-order compliance, including paying taxes
on time, not being subject to debt collection proceedings, not having certified unpaid debts and not
having a criminal record. 21

The SEM handbook also supplies several drafting-relevant refinements. It states that the language
minima are B1 oral / A2 written, that cantons may set higher language levels, that participation in
economic life must exist both when the application is filed and at the time of naturalisation, that
social assistance in the three years before filing or during the procedure excludes naturalisation unless
fully repaid, and that cantons remain free to adopt stricter social-assistance rules. It also states that tax
arrears in the five years before filing can justify refusal of the federal naturalisation authorisation,
assessed by reference to definitive taxation rather than provisional assessments. 22

## Cantonal and communal practice notes

## F. Cantonal and communal practice notes

The following points are representative practice notes, not an exhaustive survey of all 26 cantons and
all communes.

In Zurich, official cantonal guidance requires, for ordinary naturalisation, knowledge of Switzerland, the
canton and the Zurich communal system, proved by a basic knowledge test. The canton also runs the
KDE language test and states the federal minimum of B1 oral / A2 written for German in the Zurich
context. City of Zurich guidance shows how this is operationalised: after the application is filed, the city
schedules an on-site knowledge test at the Stadthaus. 23

In Bern, official cantonal guidance states that ordinary naturalisation requires integration and
familiarity with life in Switzerland, district-language competence at A2 written / B1 oral, and a
citizenship test, with exemptions for persons who completed sufficient Swiss schooling or Swiss
secondary/tertiary education. The ordinary process page also states that the applicant is invited to a
personal interview at the commune after the dossier check. 24

In Geneva, the canton provides an official didactic platform, Connaître la Suisse et Genève, with
modules and a quiz specifically designed to prepare applicants’ knowledge of Switzerland and Geneva.
Although the quiz is not itself a guarantee of success, the official message is that knowledge of the
Confederation and canton is a live part of preparation. 25

In Vaud, cantonal law materially supplements the federal floor. The Vaud citizenship law says that the
commune evaluates participation in Swiss and Vaud social and cultural life and the applicant’s contacts
with the Swiss and Vaud population; it also requires the commune to test elementary knowledge of

Swiss and Vaud geographic, historical, political and social particularities, with questions balanced
between Swiss, cantonal and local life. Vaud law further states that the whole procedure is conducted in
French. A cantonal step-by-step official sheet says that, in ordinary naturalisation, the commune
prepares an enquiry report and conducts a hearing. Lausanne’s municipal directive then gives the
clearest operational example: 48 written questions in 60 minutes, a 70 per cent pass mark, oral testing
by interview on SPOP request, and an audition where needed—mandatory where the pre-advice is
negative. 26

In Ticino, official cantonal regulation and guidance place distinct weight on Italian and on local
contextual knowledge. The regulation requires proof of oral and written Italian under the federal article
6 test, and also proof of attendance on a course and success in a test on the geographic, historical,
political and social context of Switzerland and Ticino, subject to exemptions or dispensations in defined
cases. Ticino’s regulation also shows the evidential depth of local inquiry: residence certificates, Swiss
criminal-record extract, debt-enforcement records, declarations on payment of communal/cantonal/
federal taxes, confirmation of any social assistance in the last ten years, tax assessments, and evidence
of work or education over the last ten years. The municipality must gather information not only on
security and constitutional values, but also on social and cultural participation, relationships with Swiss/
Ticinese persons, economic activity or education, and support for the integration of spouse and minor
children. Ticino’s public guidance additionally states a stricter cantonal practice position of no social
assistance during the ten years before the application, at filing, or during the procedure, unless
repaid in full. 27

These examples support a conservative internal proposition: ordinary naturalisation is often won or
lost in local evidential practice, not only in abstract federal-law analysis. Federal law supplies the
baseline; cantonal and communal procedure determines how applicants are tested, what evidence is
collected, and how local integration is translated into an overall recommendation. 28

## Practical legal analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis

Language competence. Federal law fixes a minimum of B1 oral / A2 written in a national language.
Proof may be supplied through mother tongue, five years’ compulsory schooling in a national language,
secondary II or tertiary education in a national language, or an accepted language certificate. Cantons
may require more, and may in practice insist on the locally relevant language. Zurich, Bern, Vaud and
Ticino all operationalise this locally. Later content should therefore distinguish between the federal
minimum and the locally required language in practice. 29

Respect for public security and order. This is broader than “no convictions”. It includes serious or
repeated breaches of legal rules, willful non-fulfilment of important public or private obligations, certain
criminal-record entries and pending criminal proceedings. The handbook and SEM public guidance
show that tax compliance, debt-enforcement history, certified unpaid debts and criminal record
are all relevant. The handbook further indicates that the federal authorisation may be refused for tax
arrears in the preceding five years. This factor should be treated internally as one of the highest refusal-
risk clusters. 30

Constitutional values. Official sources make clear that this is not merely symbolic language. It includes
respect for the rule of law, the democratic-liberal order, fundamental rights and certain civic obligations,
including compulsory schooling and military/civil service duties where applicable. This criterion is
capable of independent work and should not be subsumed invisibly into “general integration”. 31

Participation in economic life or education. The federal test is not limited to current full-time
employment. A person participates in economic life where income, assets or legally claimable third-
party benefits allow them to cover living costs and maintenance obligations both at filing and at
naturalisation. Education or further training can also satisfy the criterion. SEM guidance accepts several
evidential pathways, including an existing job, self-employment evidence, economic independence if
not employed, genuine willingness to seek work, and certain family-care arrangements. The point is
self-sufficiency and responsible participation, not one rigid labour-market model. 32

Social assistance. At the federal level, receiving social assistance in the three years before filing or
during the procedure means the economic-life criterion is not met unless the assistance is fully repaid,
subject to the personal-circumstances moderation in article 12(2) and article 9. Some cantons adopt
stricter practice, and Ticino’s official guidance is notably stricter on this point. Later drafting should
always differentiate clearly between the federal three-year rule and stricter cantonal practice. 33

Familiarity with Swiss living conditions. This is a separate federal condition in ordinary naturalisation.
It combines civic knowledge, social and cultural participation, and contacts with Swiss persons.
Ordinance article 2 also allows cantonal knowledge testing, but only with fair preparation conditions. In
practice, cantons and communes often convert this into local tests or interviews. That makes this
criterion both substantive and evidential. It should not be treated as a mere “nice to have”. 34

Family integration and support obligations. This factor is often under-analysed. Federal law requires
the applicant to encourage and support the integration of the spouse/registered partner and minor
children under parental authority, by helping them acquire language, participate in economic life or
education, and take part in Swiss social and cultural life. Separate from that, article 7 also refers
expressly to the applicant’s ability to cover maintenance obligations. SEM guidance also notes that the
criterion can still be met where a family member is unwilling to integrate, provided the applicant
genuinely tried to provide support, and where a household has a traditional division of roles provided
the non-working spouse meets the relevant criteria. 35

Evidence. A conservative evidence set for internal content should include: language proof; residence/
history proof; criminal-record extract; debt-enforcement extract; tax evidence; social-assistance
certificate; employment, self-employment or education records; and materials showing social/cultural
participation and local ties. Ticino’s regulation is especially useful as an evidence map, while Bern, Vaud
and Lausanne materials show the practical importance of the interview/report stage. 36

## Drafting cautions and source control

## H. Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss naturalisation integration as a language test with extras. The federal statute
makes language only one of five listed manifestations of successful integration, and ordinary
naturalisation has the additional separate requirement of familiarity with Swiss living conditions. 37

Do not say that facilitated naturalisation and ordinary naturalisation have identical material tests. The
safer formulation is that facilitated naturalisation applies the same article 12 integration criteria and
the same security principle, but ordinary naturalisation alone expressly includes the separate article
11(b) condition of familiarity with Swiss living conditions. 38

Do not overstate any single factor as automatically decisive unless the official sources justify that
proposition. For example, the framework does justify strong statements about certain VOSTRA entries,

pending criminal proceedings, and social assistance unless repaid, but even there the statute and
ordinance require appropriate account of certain serious personal circumstances in relation to
language and economic-life criteria. 39

Do not omit cantonal and communal practice from ordinary-naturalisation drafting. Federal law
expressly leaves procedure to cantonal law, allows cantonal additional integration criteria, and official
local materials show that interviews, reports and civic-knowledge tests can be outcome-relevant. 40

Do not present tax compliance, debt, social assistance and maintenance as free-standing statutory
headings divorced from the legal framework. The cleaner analysis is: taxes and debt usually sit within
public security and order or the practical proof of compliance with important obligations;
maintenance and self-sufficiency sit within economic life; family support also has its own separate
integration heading under article 12(1)(e). 41

Do not write local-practice content as if it were nationally uniform. Zurich, Bern, Vaud, Geneva and
Ticino all show different language, testing and interview configurations. Later content should either stay
at federal level or label local examples as canton-specific or representative municipal practice. 42
