# Swiss C Permit Status and Ordinary Naturalisation Readiness in Switzerland

## Executive legal summary

## A. Executive legal summary. Ordinary naturalisation in Switzerland is a citizenship process governed

primarily by the Swiss Citizenship Act, not a simple extension of immigration status. For the ordinary
route, federal law requires the applicant to hold a settlement permit at the time of filing and to show
ten years’ residence in Switzerland, including three years in the five years immediately preceding the
application. Time spent in Switzerland between the ages of 8 and 18 counts double, but the actual
residence must still total at least six years. The federal licence also requires successful integration,
familiarity with Swiss living conditions, and no threat to Switzerland’s internal or external security.
Integration is specified through respect for public order, respect for constitutional values, language
ability, participation in economic life or education, and support for the integration of family members.

A Swiss C permit is therefore a necessary threshold condition for ordinary naturalisation, but it is not
a proxy for naturalisation readiness. SEM’s guidance is explicit that ordinary naturalisation is intended
as the “last step of integration” and therefore presupposes the most secure immigration status, namely
settlement. The same guidance also explains that a C permit only shows that, at the time it was granted,
the holder met the settlement-permit criteria under foreign nationals law. Naturalisation authorities
then carry out their own, separate and current assessment under citizenship law. In practical terms, a
person may hold a valid C permit and still be refused naturalisation because of tax arrears, enforceable
debts, social-assistance reliance, insufficient language evidence, weak familiarity with Swiss civic life,
public-order issues, or local residence and procedural problems. 2

The relationship between residence for the C permit and residence for naturalisation is close, but not
identical. Immigration law uses its own rules for granting, preserving, lapsing and re-granting a C
permit. Citizenship law separately defines which residence titles count towards naturalisation, how
provisional admission is treated, and when residence is interrupted. Under citizenship law, residence
counts if held with a B or C permit, with provisional admission counted at half, or with an FDFA
legitimation card or comparable title; L, G, N and S statuses do not count. Residence is normally treated
as abandoned on deregistration or after more than six months’ actual stay abroad, although short
absences with intent to return do not interrupt residence and a stay abroad of up to one year for work
or education may still count as a temporary departure under the Citizenship Ordinance. That means the
fact that a C permit survives, or is formally maintained, does not automatically mean the federal
naturalisation residence clock survives on the same basis. 3

That distinction matters especially for absences abroad. Under the AIG guidance, a C permit lapses on
deregistration or after six months’ actual stay abroad, but it may be maintained for up to four years if
an application is made in time and the foreign stay is genuinely temporary. By contrast, the citizenship
materials treat personal presence and the Swiss centre of life as fundamental, and the SEM handbook
states that an absence abroad for work or study lasting more than twelve months cannot be counted
towards the federal residence period. Accordingly, preserving a C permit during an overseas
assignment is not the same thing as preserving uninterrupted residence for ordinary naturalisation.

The ordinary naturalisation process is federalist. The Confederation sets minimum requirements and
grants the federal naturalisation licence, but cantons regulate procedure and may impose local
residence periods and additional integration expectations. Communes often play a decisive evidential
and interview role. Official cantonal and communal sources show materially different local frameworks:
Zurich requires two years in the same commune before filing and warns that a move before the
municipal inquiry is complete will end the process; Geneva requires two years in the canton including
the last twelve months and effective residence in Geneva throughout the procedure; Vaud requires two
complete years in the canton including the year before filing, while the commune may impose one year
locally; Ticino requires five years in the canton and three years in the commune, including the two years
before the application. These local layers are not mere administration; they materially affect timing and
readiness. 5

Finally, ordinary naturalisation is not automatic even where the formal federal criteria are met. SEM’s
own handbook states that the federal naturalisation licence does not create an entitlement to
naturalisation, and the canton may still refuse after the federal licence if later facts become known that
would have prevented an earlier positive assurance. Refusals must be reasoned, and citizenship
obtained by false statements or concealment of material facts may later be declared null. The
Citizenship Ordinance also imposes an ongoing duty on applicants to provide complete information and
to notify adverse changes in circumstances during the process. For drafting and planning purposes, the
correct proposition is therefore: a C permit opens the ordinary route, but readiness depends on a
wider residence, integration, compliance and local-procedure assessment that should be
prepared well before filing. 6

## Framework and propositions

## B. Structural overview of the C permit requirement in ordinary naturalisation.

Legal basis. The statutory starting point is Article 9 of the Swiss Citizenship Act, which makes
possession of a settlement permit a formal condition for the federal naturalisation licence in ordinary
naturalisation. Article 11 then adds the substantive federal conditions: successful integration, familiarity
with Swiss living conditions, and no threat to internal or external security. 7

Does the C permit requirement apply across the ordinary route? For ordinary adult applicants, yes.
SEM guidance also states that the special ordinary route for a person in a registered partnership with a
Swiss citizen still requires a C permit. The family context is more nuanced for included minors: SEM’s
handbook says an included child may be scrutinised individually where the child does not hold the same
residence title as the naturalising parent, so later drafting should avoid blanket statements about every
included child needing an identical permit status in all cases. 8

Why holding a C permit is necessary but not sufficient. SEM’s annex explains that the revised
citizenship law sought coherence with foreign-nationals law by requiring settlement status for ordinary
naturalisation, because citizenship is treated as the final integration step. But SEM’s Chapter 3 also
makes clear that the federal and cantonal authorities still examine integration, language, public order,
finances, and familiarity with Swiss conditions on the naturalisation file itself. 9

Residence history and counting. Naturalisation residence is not counted simply by asking whether the
person has qualified for or retained a C permit. Citizenship law has its own residence-counting rule: B
and C permits count in full; provisional admission counts at half; FDFA legitimation cards and
comparable titles also count; and deregistration or more than six months’ actual stay abroad normally

ends the residence period. Years spent in Switzerland between ages 8 and 18 count double for ordinary
naturalisation, subject to six years’ actual residence. 10

Absences, interrupted residence and partial relocation. Under citizenship law, short absences with
intent to return do not interrupt residence, and work or study abroad for up to one year can still be
treated as temporary departure. Under immigration law, a C permit can sometimes be maintained for
up to four years during a temporary overseas absence if requested in time. Those are different legal
ideas. A person may preserve the permit but still weaken or break the citizenship-law residence case,
particularly if the centre of life shifts abroad. Geneva’s official guidance expressly warns that lengthy
departures abroad or shifting the centre of interests out of Switzerland may lead to non-counting of
residence or closure of the application. 11

Integration, language and civic knowledge. Naturalisation adds substantive requirements beyond
settlement status. Language minima are B1 oral and A2 written in a national language for ordinary
naturalisation, subject to the recognised evidence routes and exemptions in the Citizenship Ordinance
and SEM guidance. Familiarity with Swiss living conditions includes knowledge of Swiss geography,
history, politics and society, participation in social and cultural life, and contacts with Swiss citizens.
Cantons may test this; Zurich, Vaud, Bern and Lausanne all describe canton or commune-level
knowledge testing in official materials. 12

Public order and financial compliance. Citizenship law treats tax arrears, unpaid debts, enforcement
proceedings, maintenance failures and social-assistance reliance as live naturalisation issues. SEM’s
handbook states that tax arrears can lead SEM to refuse the federal licence; unpaid debt-enforcement
entries above specified thresholds can be an obstacle; and receipt of social assistance in the three years
before filing or during the procedure is disqualifying unless fully repaid, subject to the personal-
circumstances clauses. Geneva and Ticino publish similarly strict, and in places stricter, practice points.
Holding a C permit does not wash these issues away. 13

Cantonal and communal procedure. The canton designates the filing authority. Cantons and, where
cantonal law so provides, communes examine the case first; only cases that can be supported locally
are transmitted to SEM for the federal licence; and after the federal licence the canton still takes the
final naturalisation decision. Refusals must be reasoned. Cantonal law controls much of the process,
and it may also impose local residence periods and additional integration requirements. 14

Refusal and post-grant vulnerability. The federal licence does not create a right to naturalisation. The
canton may still reject if adverse facts emerge after federal approval. If citizenship was obtained by false
statements or concealment of material facts, it may later be declared null by SEM or, for ordinary
naturalisation, by the canton under Article 36 BüG. Applicants also have a continuing duty to notify
materially adverse changes while the procedure is pending. 6

Downstream planning significance. In practice, the C permit should be treated as the moment when
citizenship planning becomes possible, not as proof that the file is ready. Early settlement permit grant
after five years, and C permits preserved during foreign stays, are two situations that can create false
reassurance. Naturalisation readiness still depends on citizenship-law residence, language proof, local
ties, clean tax and debt history, and stability throughout what may be a multi-stage and multi-year
process. 15

## E. Official guidance / administrative materials

The most important interpretive source located was SEM Handbook on Citizenship, Chapter 3:
Ordinary Naturalisation. It gives detailed official guidance on how SEM and cantonal authorities
should understand the federal criteria. For present purposes, its strongest points are that the applicant
must have a C permit to file, that SEM expects the permit to remain valid during the whole ordinary
process, that residence-title counting is governed by Article 33 BüG, and that tax, debt, social-
assistance, language, public-order and civic-familiarity issues are all examined as independent
citizenship matters. It is the main official source on the C-permit/naturalisation relationship beyond the
bare statute. 43

The SEM Annex to Chapter 3: Ordinary Naturalisation is short but important. It sets out the policy
rationale that ordinary naturalisation is the last integration step and therefore presupposes the most
stable immigration status, namely settlement. It is particularly useful when drafting conservative
explanatory text connecting foreign-nationals law and citizenship law without collapsing them into one
regime. 44

SEM’s official page “Ordinary naturalisation” provides the public-facing federal summary that the
canton may impose additional integration requirements and that the commune and canton complete
the local process before sending the file to SEM for the federal licence. It is less detailed than the
handbook, but useful for high-level structure and confirming that the federal licence comes after local
support has been formed. 45

SEM’s official page “Language requirements” is valuable because it expressly presents language proof
as a staged system applying across family migration, residence permits, settlement permits and
naturalisation. It confirms that language proof is required in both immigration and citizenship
procedures, but not at the same legal stage or on the same terms. It also points to SEM’s list of
recognised language certificates, updated on the page to 1 April 2026. 46

SEM’s official FAQ on residence and integration criteria is useful on the immigration side. It
summarises the AIG integration criteria relevant to residence and settlement matters, namely
participation in economic life or education, public-order compliance, respect for constitutional values
and language competence, and it emphasises that cantonal migration authorities assess cases
individually under the principle of proportionality. This is helpful when comparing the logic of
settlement law with the stricter and differently structured citizenship-law test. 47

SEM’s AIG Directives are the key official administrative materials on settlement-permit grant, loss,
maintenance abroad and re-grant. They explain the ordinary settlement time periods, the continuous-
presence logic, the possibility of maintaining a C permit during a genuine temporary foreign stay, and

the consequences of return after the permitted period. These directives are indispensable for
explaining why C-permit continuity and naturalisation continuity are related but not identical.   48

Official general guidance on ch.ch and SEM overview pages can support the proposition that procedure
varies and that communes may set their own rules within cantonal and federal bounds, but they should
remain secondary to the statutes, ordinances and SEM handbook for later drafting. 49

## Practical legal analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis. The most useful conceptual distinction for future drafting is that a C permit

is still part of immigration law, whereas naturalisation changes the person’s status into Swiss
citizenship itself. A settlement permit gives an indefinite right of stay, but it remains subject to the logic
of the AIG: it can lapse on deregistration or prolonged absence, and it may be revoked or downgraded
in certain cases. Naturalisation, by contrast, ends dependence on foreign-national status for residence
in Switzerland and is acquired only after a separate public-law decision on citizenship. That is why the
SEM annex describes ordinary naturalisation as the final integration step rather than as the automatic
next stage after settlement. 57

For that reason, the C permit is best understood as a gateway criterion. Without it, the ordinary route
cannot normally be started. With it, the applicant becomes formally admissible to the ordinary route,
but the substantive naturalisation analysis only begins at that point. This is also why a recently obtained
C permit does not necessarily mean the person is strategically ready to apply. The federal law does not,
in the sources reviewed, impose an express waiting period after grant of the C permit before filing. But
a newly issued C permit may coincide with a still-developing evidential record on language proof, local
integration, civic knowledge, tax compliance, debt clean-up, or post-absence residence continuity.
Readiness is therefore an assessment of the whole file, not a single permit milestone. 58

Residence continuity is one of the points most likely to be misunderstood if drafting focuses too heavily
on the C permit. The immigration-law question, “Has the C permit lapsed or been maintained?”, and the
citizenship-law question, “Can the federal residence period still be counted continuously?”, are not
identical. The AIG directives allow maintenance of the C permit during a genuinely temporary overseas
stay if requested in time. Citizenship law, however, has its own rules about residence counting,
interruption, temporary departure and centre of life. An applicant on an extended overseas posting may
therefore still be a C-permit holder but have a weaker or broken ordinary naturalisation residence case.
This is especially true where there has been deregistration, more than six months abroad, or a practical
relocation of the centre of interests away from Switzerland. 59

The comparison between settlement-permit criteria and citizenship criteria also needs careful
treatment. There is definite overlap. Both regimes care about integration, language, public order and
financial independence. But the overlap should not be mistaken for equivalence. Settlement law asks
whether the person is sufficiently integrated for indefinite residence under the AIG. Citizenship law asks
whether the person is sufficiently integrated, civically familiar and publicly trustworthy to be admitted
to the three-level Swiss citizenship structure of commune, canton and Confederation. The latter
includes citizenship-specific elements such as familiarity with Swiss living conditions, local knowledge
testing, and federal-citizenship investigations that go beyond the mere existence of settled status. 60

Public-order and financial-compliance issues are especially important because they often explain why a
C-permit holder is not yet naturalisation-ready. SEM’s citizenship handbook treats tax arrears,
significant debt-enforcement issues, maintenance failures and recent social-assistance dependency as
naturalisation obstacles. Criminal history is assessed through authority extracts on a detailed timetable,
not just by casual reference to a private record. In cantonal practice, Geneva publishes a debt threshold
for non-entry and Ticino publishes a much longer social-assistance look-back period than the federal
minimum. Those local materials do not change federal law, but they are highly relevant to practical risk
assessment and to cautious content generation. 61

Cantonal and communal procedure can materially affect both timing and substance. Zurich’s published
warning about moving before the municipal inquiry is complete, Geneva’s insistence on effective
residence in the canton during the process, Vaud’s combination of cantonal filing and communal
testing, and Lausanne’s description of the commune’s role in interviews and knowledge assessment all
show that the person’s local footprint remains important after the federal threshold is met. The SEM
handbook also states that federal naturalisation does not require local integration as such, but that
cantons may require local integration additionally. For internal drafting, this means that “C permit plus
ten years in Switzerland” is never a sufficient statement of readiness without checking the relevant
canton and commune. 62

A final point is vulnerability during and after the grant. The applicant must not only satisfy the rules at
filing; materially adverse changes can still matter later. SEM’s handbook says the canton may reject after
the federal licence if later facts emerge that would have prevented local support. The Citizenship
Ordinance requires applicants to notify later changes that could stand in the way of naturalisation. And
citizenship obtained by false statements or concealment can later be declared null. This makes pre-filing
planning, accurate disclosure, and continued compliance during the whole process legally significant
rather than merely advisable. 63

## Drafting cautions and source list

## H. Drafting cautions

- Do not describe the Swiss C permit as equivalent to eligibility for ordinary naturalisation. It is a
       threshold condition, not the whole legal test. 7
- Do not imply that all C-permit holders are naturalisation-ready. Residence continuity, language,
       civic familiarity, finances and local rules remain to be assessed. 64
- Do not confuse settlement-permit continuity under the AIG with residence continuity for
       naturalisation under the BüG. 65
- Do not say that preserving a C permit during an overseas absence automatically preserves the
       naturalisation residence clock. 66

- Do not flatten federal law, SEM guidance, and cantonal or communal practice into one
        undifferentiated rule-set. Distinguish black-letter law from official interpretation and local
        administration. 67
- Do not omit cantonal and communal residence requirements and mobility constraints. They can
        change whether an applicant is ready to file or should wait. 68
- Do not present naturalisation as automatic once the C permit and ten-year residence
        requirement are met. SEM’s own guidance says the federal licence does not create an
        entitlement to naturalisation. 69
- Do not omit tax, debt, social-assistance and maintenance issues from readiness analysis. 24
- Do not present language evidence generically. Use the Citizenship Ordinance routes, SEM’s
        recognised-certificates list, and any canton-specific demands. 70
- Do not assume every minor in a family application can be analysed by simply copying the
        parent’s C-permit position; SEM indicates case-by-case checking where the child has a different
        residence title. 17
- Do not invent a federal waiting period after recent grant of a C permit unless a current official
        source supports it. In the materials reviewed, no express federal waiting period was identified.

- Do not overlook nullity risk where citizenship was obtained through false statements or
        concealment of material facts. 27

## Open questions and limitations

No official federal source was identified in the materials reviewed that imposes an express post‑C
waiting period before an ordinary naturalisation application may be filed. That absence should not be
overstated into a positive entitlement to apply immediately in every case; it simply means the readiness
question remains substantive and fact-sensitive. Also, the interaction between included minor children
and residence-title requirements is not stated in a single simple federal rule in the materials reviewed;
SEM’s handbook instead points to case-by-case checking where the child’s residence title differs from
the parent’s. 81
