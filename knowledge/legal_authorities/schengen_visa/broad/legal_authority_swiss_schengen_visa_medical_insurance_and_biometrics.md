# Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Travel Medical

# Insurance and Biometrics Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

For Swiss Schengen Visa Type C applications, the core legal rules on travel medical insurance and biometric enrolment come principally from Schengen law, not from a separate Swiss-only code. The Visa Code requires applicants for a uniform visa for one or two entries to prove adequate and valid travel medical insurance, and it requires Member States to collect biometric identifiers consisting of a photograph and ten fingerprints. Switzerland applies these Schengen rules as a Schengen-associated state, and its Ordinance on Entry and the Granting of Visas expressly provides that it governs entry, airport transit and visa issuance only insofar as the Schengen association agreements do not provide otherwise. The same Swiss ordinance also provides that fingerprints of short-stay visa applicants are taken under the Swiss VIS Ordinance and may be used to establish identity under the FNIA. 1

The insurance rule is legally specific. For one- or two-entry uniform visas, the applicant must show insurance covering expenses linked to medical repatriation, urgent medical attention, emergency hospital treatment, or death during the stay. The insurance must in principle be valid throughout the Schengen territory, cover the full period of intended stay or transit, and provide minimum cover of EUR 30,000. For multiple-entry visas, proof is required only for the first intended visit, but the applicant must sign the standard declaration acknowledging the need to hold insurance for later stays as well. The Visa Code also says the insurance should, in principle, be taken out in the applicant's country of residence, though it may be obtained elsewhere, including by a third party on the applicant's behalf. Claims recoverability in a Member State is relevant to addequacy. Holders of diplomatic passports are exempt, and the Code also allows consulates to treat the requirement as satisfied where adequate cover may be presumed from the applicant's professional situation. 2

Official operational guidance adds important qualifications that matter in practice. The Commission's Visa Code Handbook states that the insurance need cover only the period of effective stay, not the full validity period of the visa and not the additional 15-day "grace period" that is often added to some short-validity visas. The Handbook aIso states that airport-transit visa applicants do not need topresent travel medical insurance, and that non-EU family members of EU/EEA and Swiss citizens benefiting from the free-movement lex specialis are exempt from presenting proof of such insurance. The Handbook further indicates that if insurance produced is inadequate, the application should not automatically be refused; the applicant should ordinarily be allowed to cure the deficiency before a final decision is taken. These are best treated as official operational guidance and practice points rather than as stand-alone Swiss black-letter rules. 3

The biometrics regime is equally structured. At the first application, the applicant must appear in person for collection of a photograph and ten fingerprints. If fingerprints were first entered into the VIS less than 59 months before the new application, they must be copied to the later application rather than taken again. However, recollection is permittedwhere there is reasonable doubt about identity, and Swiss consular practice also reflects recollection where previously captured prints are of insufficient quality. Children under 12 are exempt from fingerprinting. Persons for whom fingerprinting is physically impossible are also exempt, although the maximum number of prints should be taken where possible, and a temporary impossibility can require collection at a later application. Importantly,the photograph is still entered in the VIS for each application; personal appearance is not required merely for the photograph. 4

Swiss representation practice is broadly aligned with these rules. Official Swiss representation pages for the United Kingdom and Canada state that personal appearance is mandatory where fingerprints have not yet been collected, not necessary where usable fingerprints were captured within the previous 59months, and still may be required ifprint quality is insufficient. Those pages also state that children under 12 do not need fingerprint enrolment and that the representation may still require a personal interview after reviewing the file. One Swiss representation in Saudi Arabia publishes a checklist requiring proof of insurance valid for all Schengen States and covering the entire intended stay, with minimum cover of EUR 30,000. Those materials are reliable evidence of Swiss post-level practice, but they remain jurisdiction-specific procedural guidance rather than universal Swiss legislation. 5

VIS and EES must be kept distinct. VIS is the visa database used by consulates and border authorities for visa data exchange, biometric matching, identity verification and re-use of previously captured visa fingerprints. EES is a separate border-management system that records short-stay entries,exits and refusals of entry at the external border. As of 10 April 2026, the European Commission states that EES is fully operational across all Schengen countries, although some SEM pages still describe the system in rollout terms dating from October 2025. Official EES materials explain that the system stores different biometrics depending on whether the traveller needs a short-stay visa: for visa-required travellers, fingerprints are already stored in VIS and are not stored again in EES; the system stores only the facial image in that scenario. ETIAS is different again: it applies only to visa-exempt travellers, not Type C visa applicants, and official SEM and EU sources currently state that ETIAS is planned for the last quarter of 2026, with the exact date still to be announced. 6

## Structural overview

**Legal nature** **of** **the** **framework.** The controlling rules on insurance and biometrics for Swiss Type C visas are primarily Schengen rules under the Visa Code, supplemmented by the VIS legal framework and Swiss implementing provisions. Swiss law does not displace those Schengen norms; rather, the Swiss visa ordinance is expressly subordinated to the Schengen association agreements wwhere those agreements govern the point. 7

**Travel** **medical insurance** **requirement.** The black-letter rule sits in Article 15 of the Visa Code. It covers meddical repatriation, urgent medical attention, emergency hospital treatment and death; requires validity across the territory of the Member States; and sets the minimum sum insured at EUR 30,000. For multiple-entry visas, proof is required only for the first intended visit, with a declaration covering later visits. 2

**Insurance** **exemptions** **and** **qualifications.** Diplomatic passport holders are expressly exempt. The Code also allows the requirement to be treated as met where adequate coverage may be presumed from the applicant's professional situation. The Visa Code Handbook adds that proof is not required for airport-transit visa applicants and, under the free-movement lex specialis, for non-EU family members of EU/EEA and Swiss citizens falling within that regime. 8

**Biometrics rule.** Article 13 of the Visa Code requires a photograph and ten fingerprints at the first application, with re-use of visa fingerprints for 59 months. The same article sets exemptions for children under 12, persons physically unable to provide prints, certain heads of state or government and official delegations, and certain senior royals invited for official purposes. 9

<!-- 2 -->

**Personal appearance.** Personal appearance is tied to fingerprint collection, not to every application as such. Where reusable fingerprints exist in VIS, personal appearance is generally unnecessary unless there is an identity issue, a quality issue, or the representation requires interview or further checks. Swiss post-level guidance reflects that approach. 10

VIS, **EES and** **data** **handling.** VIS is the visa-application system used for data exchange,biometric verification and anti-abuse checks. EES is a border crossing system recording short-stay travel movements and some biometrics. For a visa applicant or visa holder, they serve different purposes and operate at different stages. ETIAS is separate again and does not apply to visa-required Type C applicants. 11

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

**Primary** **legal texts.** Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas is the principal legal instrument. Article 1 states that the Visa Code governs procedures and conditions for visas for intended stays not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. Article 10 links lodging of the application to presentation of the form, travel document, photograph, fingerprints where applicable, supporting documents, and, where applicable, proof of travel medical insurance. Article 13 governs biometric identifiers: photo plus ten fingerprints, first-application appearance in person, copying of fingerprints for 59 months, and exemptions. Article 15 governs travel medical insurance: scope of risks, territorial validity, minimum EUR 30,000 cover, MEV treatment, professional-situation qualification and diplomatic-passport exemption. Article 19 makes biometric collection part of admissibility, and Article 23 fixes the normal 15-day processing time, extendable to 45 days in individual cases. These provisions matter because they define both the legal minimum and the procedural logic of decision-making in Swiss Type C visa cases.22

<!-- 5 -->

Regulation (EC) No 767/2008 oon the Visa Information System establishes the VIS and the exchange of visa data between Schengen States. In Commission guidance, VIS is described as the system linking consulates and border crossing points, enabling biometric matching for visa examination, border verification and anti-abuse functions such as combatting "visa shopping". The 2021 VIS reform package, especially Regulation (EU) 2021/1134, is relevant because it updated the VIS architecture and aligned it with other EU information systems, including the Borders Code, the EES and ETIAS framework.For present purposes, the legal significance of the VIS regime is that the 59-month re-use rule operates through fingerprints already stored in VIS. 23

Regulation (EU) 2017/2226 establishing the Entry/Exit System is not the source of visa-application biometrics, but it is now operationally relevant because it records entries, exits and refusals of entry of short-stay third-country nationals and supports calculation of authorised stay. That matters to this topic because practitioners must distinguish consular VIS enrolment from border-stage EES registration and verification. The Swiss visa ordinance is relevant on the domestic side because Article 1 expressly states that the ordinance applies only insofar as the Schengen association agreements do not provide otherwise, and because it lists the Visa Code, VIS Regulation, EES Regulation and related Schengen instruments among the EU acts connected to Swiss implementation. Article 13 of the same ordinance expressly addresses fingerprints of short-stay visa applicants. 24

**Official guidance and administrative materials.** The Commission's 2024 replacement Visa Code Handbook I is the most useful operational guidance source. It states expressly that fingerprints already collected within the previous 59 months should be copied from VIS; that photos are provided with every application; that under-12s are exempt from fingerprinting; that travel medical insurance for the intended stay need not cover the additional 15-day grace period; that airport-transit applicants do not have to present proof of insurance; and that applicants should, where possible, be allowed to remedy inadequate insurance before final refusal. The Handbook is not itself a binding source of rigghts beyond the legislation, but it is highly material for internal drafting on practice, evidential handling and decision-quality. 25

The European Commission's public "Applying for a Schengen visa" page provides a concise and current official overview confirming that medical insurance, fingerprints, the 6-month/15-day filing window,and 15-day/45-day processing framework remain central parts of the Schengen application process. The Commission's VIS page explains the system's operationaI purpose and confirms that ten fingerprints and a digital photograph are collected from visa applicants and can be reused over a five-year period. Swiss federal guidance is comparatively lighter but still useful: SEM's "Entry with visa" page summarises entry conditions for visa holders; the SEM entry leaflet says proof of health insurance belongs in the file; SEM's ETIAS page warns that ETIAS is not yet live and is currently planned for the last quarter of 2026; and SEM's FAQ page still refers to EES being introduced since October 2025, which should be read alongside the newer Commission statement that EES became fully operational on 10 April 2026. 26

## Swiss representation notes and practical analysis

**Swiss** **representation** **/ consular** **practice** **notes.** Reliable Swiss representation pages show a largely harmonised practical message. The UK and Canada representation pages state that biometric capture of fingerprints and facial images applies to Schengen visa applicants, that data are kept in VIS for five years, and that where the applicant's fingerprints were collected within the last 59 months there is normally no need to provide fresh fingerprints or to appear in person solely for that purpose. The UK page adds a specific procedural requirement to provide a copy of a previous Schengen visa sticker issued after biometric rollout, and both the UK and Canada pages reserve the right to require a personal interview after examining the file. The Saudi Arabia checklist shows a post-specific evidential formulation of the insurance requirement: proof must cover the entire intended stay, all Schengen States, and the EUR 30,000 minimum. These are best treated as reliable but jurisdiction-specific practice notes, not universal Swiss black-letter rules. 27

<!-- 6 -->

**Practical legal analysis.** The insurance requirement should be drafted as an admissibility-and-merits issue, not as a mere travel-preparation point. In law, proof of insurance is one of the application elements to be produced "where applicable", and in practice it is part of ensuring that the short-stay traveller is properly coveredd against specified emergency risks while in the Schengen area. Internal drafting should therefore distinguish between the legal minimumn in Article 15 and the operational questions that often drive outcomes: whether the policy is geographically valid across Schengen, whether the cover period is long enough for the intended stay, whether the insurer is one against whom claims are realistically recoverable in a Member State, and whether the cover corresponds to the traveller's actual itinerary. The Commission Handbook's statement that inadequate insurance should ordinarily be curable before refusal is important for quality-control and appeal-risk analysis. 28

Biometric collection should likewise be drafted with careful distinction between the legal rule and the recurring operational exceptions. The black-letter position is simple: first application, in-person appearance, photograph plus ten fingerprints; later application within 59 months, copy the existing prints from VIS. The complications arise from identity doubts, poor-quality earlier enrolment, temporary physical impossibility, and post-level administrative requirements used to confirm that reusable biometrics genuinely exist. Swiss representation guidance shows how those complications are handled in practice. A statement such as "all Schengen applicants must attend in person" is therefore too broad; equally, a statement such as "once fingerprints have been given, no future appearance can ever be required" is also wrong. 29

VIS and EES must not be collapsed into one concept. VIS is a visa-adjudication and border-verification database; EES is a border-crossing records system for short-stay travellers. That distinction now matters more than before because EES is officially fully operational as of 10 April 2026 and is replacing manual passport-stamping with digital entry and exit records. Official EES guidance makes an additional point of practical importance: where the traveller needed a short-stay visa, their fingerprints are already in VIS and are not stored again in EES. For Type C drafting, the safer formulation is that visa biometrics are collected during the visa process and may later be checked at the border, while EES records and verifies short-stay travel movements at the external border. ETIAS, by contrast, is irrelevant to visa-required Type C applicants and should only be mentioned to explain that it concerns visa-exempt travel and has not yet started operating. 30

## Drafting cautions and source list

**Drafting** **cautions.** Do not say that insurance must cover the whole period of visa validity; the operational guidance is that it must cover the period of effective stay, and not necessarily any additional 15-day grace period. Do not say that every applicant must attend in person on every application; the law ties personal appearance to fingerprint collection, and prints already in VIS for less than 59 months are copied unless recollection is justified. Do not say that all applicants aged 12 and over are absolutely exempt once previously enrolled; poor-quality or doubtful biometrics can still trigger fresh collection. Do not blur the distinction between VIS and EES. Do not imply that ETIAS applies to visa-required Type C applicants. Do not present post-specific insurer lists, copy-of-old-visa requirements, or local

appointment practices as universal Swiss law. Do not rely on old EES or ETIAS rollout dates without checking current official material. 31

**Open** **questions** / **limitations.** Publicly accessible Swiss federal materials on this topic are thinner than the EU/Schengen materials because the decisive rules largely sit in the Visa Code and related Schengen instruments. Swiss post-level requirements also vary by place of residence, and official Swiss central guidance explicitly says that required documents and procedures differ depending on the applicant's country of residence. For country-specific later drafting, the relevant Swiss representation page and checklist should therefore always be checked in addition to the Schengen-wide authorities. 32
