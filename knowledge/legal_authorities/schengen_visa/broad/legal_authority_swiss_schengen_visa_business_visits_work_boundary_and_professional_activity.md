# Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Business Visits and Work Boundary

## Executive legal summary

Switzerland is not an EU Member State, but it is a Schengen-associated state. Since Switzerland's Schengen/Dublin participation took effect on 12 December 2008, Swiss representations abroad have issued Schengen visas (Type C) for short stays and national visas (Type D) for longer stays. A Swiss Schengen visa is therefore part of the common Schengen short-stay framework, not a separate Swiss-only visitor category. As a matter of Swviss official guidance, Type C covers stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period, while Type Dis for longer-term stays; crucially, Swiss guidance also states that where the planned stay involves gainful employment, a work permit is additionally required.

For business travel, the decisive legal issue is not the label used by the traveller, but whether the planned activity is merely a short non-gainful business visit or instead amounts to "gainful activity" under Swiss immigration law. The Visa Code requires the consulate to verify the purpose and conditions of the intended stay, sufficient means, compliance with entry conditions, and the applicant's intention to leave before visa expiry; it also allows refusal where the applicant has not justified the purpose of stay or where there are reasonable doubts about document authenticity, reliability of statements, or intention to leave. In parallel, the Visa Code makes clear that possession of a visa does not confer an automatic right of entry, and Swiss border guidance confirms that even visa holders must still satisfy entry conditions at the border. 2

Swiss official materials treat the concept of gainful activity broadly and objectively. SEM's directives explain that the concept is interpreted widely for third-country nationals, that the question is whether the activity is ordinarily carried out for remuneration and affects the Swiss labour market, and that unpaid or minimally compensated work can still count as gainful activity. SEM guidance further distinguishes between work "with job entry" for a Swiss employer or Swiss establishment, and work "without job entry", which includes self-employment and activity for an employer based abroad. That point is central to modern business-travel analysis: being paid abroad, or working for a foreign company, does not by itseIf place the activity outside Swiss work-authorisation rules. 3

On the non-gainful side ofthe line, SEM's 2025 annex on the concept of gainful activity identifies short business meetings as not constituting gainful activity. Its examples include representative executive visits, contract and mandate negotiations, contract signature, strategic meetings, information-only coordination meetings, and visits to prepare a future work stay. The same annex says that strictly theoretical or technical training without integration into a work process or productive activity may be regulated through the visa route, as may passive attendance at conferences and similar events, particularly where the person is not giving a presentation. 4

On the gainful side, the same SEM material classifies "training on the job", induction integrated into productive work, internships and trainee placements, repairs and maintenance, project assignments, and other productive or service-delivery activities as gainful employment. SEM's broader guidance also states that all gainful activity, whether paid or unpaid, remains subject to authorisation. For non-EU/ EFTA workers, the Swiss employer normally files the work-permit application with the competent

cantonal labour-market or migration authority; the canton makes a preliminary decision, approved cases are submitted to SEM for consent, and only then is visa authorisation transmitted to the Swiss representation abroad for visa issuance where a visa is required. Work may begin only after the required Swiss formalities have been completed. 5

Swiss practice also recognises short-duration work-authorisation routes. SEM's directives describe authorisations for gainful activity of up to four months within twelve months under Article 19(4)(a) VZAE, including so-called 120-day permits where the work is planned in advance and spread across multiple short stays. The directives state that a single stay between three and four months is generally issued with a Type D visa, but split short stays can be authorised within the 120-day architecture. Consular material from the Swiss Consulate General in Ho Chi Minh City likewise states that gainful activity of mnore than eight but less than 120 working days per calendar year can be handled as a Schengen visa combined with prior cantonal authorisation. That is an important drafting point: some short professional work in Switzerland is legally possible within a short-stay/Type C procedural framework,but only where Swiss work authorisation has been obtained first. 6

There is also a narrow permit-free tolerance in Swiss guidance for certain work "without job entry": SEM states that such activity may be carried out without a permit for up to eight days per calendar year, after which a permit is required. However, specific sectors require authorisation from day one, and SEM guidance expressly includes construction and related work, hospitality/cleaning, security, itinerant trade and the sex industry among the stricter categories. For EU/EFTA and certain posted-worker situations, a separate nootification regime exists for up to 90 working days per calendar year, but SEM states expressly that this notification procedure does not apply to all other categories of persons; it is not a general third-country business-visitor safe harbour. 7

No Swiss federal source found in this research creates a general remote-work exemption for Type C visitors. On the contrary, SEM's materials classify activity for an employer abroad as gainful activity without job entry, and the general concept of gainful activity is deliberately wide. The safest legal reading is therefore that sustained remote work from Switzerland-beyond trivial incidental communications during travel-carries real authorisation risk and should not be presented as automatically compatible with a business-visitor Type C stay. That conclusion is partly inferential, but it is a conservative inference from official Swiss sources, not speculation detached from the legal framework. 8

## Structural overview of the business-visit and work-boundary framework

### Legal nature of the short-stay route

A Swiss Schengen Type C visa is a short-stay Schengen visa, not a residence permit and not a free-standing Swiss work permission. Swiss official guidance describes it as permitting entry and stay of up to 90 days in any 180-day period, while national Type D visas are used for longer-term stays in Switzerland. Swiss border guidance also confirmms that the SEM does not issue visas: Swiss representations abroad issue and refuse visas, while work-permit approval is dealt with through Swiss labour-market and migration authorities.9

The Schengen layer and the Swiss labour-market layer therefore operate together. A business-travel visa analysis that stops at the Visa Code is incomplete, because even if the visa classification is short-stay, Swiss domestic law may still require prior work authorisation for the underlying activity. 10

## What Swiss official guidance treats as non-gainful business activity

SEM's annex on the concept of gainful activity is the clearest Swiss official source on the business-visit side of the line. It states that short business meetings are, by definition, not gainful activity. The examples given include executive representation, mandate and contract negotiations, contract signature, strategic meetings, information-only coordination meetings, preparatory visits before a future authorised work stay, and supervision linked to negotiations. 11

The same annex also treats purely theoretical or technical training without internship, productive tasks or integration into a working process as non-gainful. Its examples include seminars, theoretical instruction on newly sold machinery, sales training for foreign affiliate staff, ordinary classroom study, and participation in workshops concerning new application systems. Passive attendance at conferences and events, especially without giving a presentation, is also expressly treated as non-gainful. 12

### What crosses the line into gainful employment

Swiss official guidance is equally clear that productive participation crosses the line. It treats on-the-job training, operational induction integrated into a work process, internships, trainee stays, repair and maintenance work, project implementation, and a range of productive or service-delivery tasks as gainful activity. The examples are practical and important: training on Swiss systems under supervision so that the worker can maintain them later abroad, an internship in a multinational programme, machine maintenance in Switzerland, or implementing a programme at a Swiss headquarters are all treated as gainful. 13

The broader SEM directives reinforce the same logic by stating that every activity going beyond a mere favour and ordinarily carried out for remuneration is gainful activity, regardless of whether it is primary or secondary work. SEM's English guidance on admission criteria adds that all gainful activity, whether paid or unpaid, remains subject to a permit. 14

### Foreign employer cases, remote work and the eight-day rule

Swiss official guidance does not confine "gainful activity" to Swiss payroll cases. SEM expressly classifies activity for an employer based abroad as gainful activity without job entry. For that category, SEM states that the activity may be performed without a permit only up to eight days per calendar year; from day nine, a permit is required, and the decision must be awaited abroad. 15

This point matters for remote work and cross-border professional services. On the sources gathered here, there is no general rule that work becomes unregulated merely because it is done for a foreign employer or paid abroad. The safer view is that a traveller physically carrying out substantive work from Switzerland is inside the Swiss gainful-activity framework unless a specific exemption or route applies. That is especially so where the work is planned, recurring, productive, or linked to client delivery. 16

### Short-duration work authorisation architecture

For non-EU/EFTA nationals, SEM's procedure page explains that the employer files the application with the competent cantonal labour-market or migration authority. The canton examines the file under the FNIA/AIG, approved cases go to SEM for consent, SEM issues a formal decision, and only then is visa authorisation transmitted to the Swiss representation for visa issuance where the worker is visa-required. The worker may begin work only after registration following entry. 17

SEM's directives add a distinct short-duration route under Article 19(4)(a) VZAE for work of up to four months within twelve months, including 120-day permissions for pre-planned multiple short stays. These permissions are still subject tosubstantive labour-market review. Local Swiss consular material from Vietnam confirms a practical version of the same architecture: gainful activity of more than eight but less than 120 working days per calendar year requires prior cantonal authorisation and can then be processed as a Schengen visa case. 18

### EU/EFTA notification routes are separate

The Swiss notification procedure for short-term work is part of the Switzerland-EU/EFTA free-movement regime. SEM explains that it applies to EU/EFTA nationals employed in Switzerland for up to three months, posted workers from EU/EFTA-based companies, and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers; it expressly states that it does not apply to all other categories of persons. It also contains its own eight-day notification-free rule and 90-working-day ceiling. That regime should not be conflated with the ordinary third-country Type C business-visitor analysis. 19

### Primary legal texts

#### EU and Schengen instruments

**Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas (Visa Code).** Article 1 sets the basic scope: the Regulation governs visas for transit through, or intended stays on, the territory of the Member States not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. For Swiss Type C analysis, this is the starting point: the visa is a short-stay Schengen authorisation, not a residence title and not, by itself,a Swiss labour-market permission. 34

**Visa Code, Article** 21. This provision governs examination of a uniform-visa application. The consulate must assess whether the applicant meets the relevant entry conditions and, specifically, whether there is a risk of illegal immigration or a lack of intention to leave before expiry. For business-visit cases, Article 21 is what turns activity-characterisation into a refusal issue: if the declared "business" purpose is inconsistent with the real planned activity, the application is vulnerable even before Swiss work-permit law is reached. 35

<!-- 7 -->

**Visa Code, Article** **32.** This provision sets the refusal grounds. Refusal must follow, among other things, where the applicant does not justify the purpose and conditions of the intended stay, does not prove sufficient means, or where there are reasonable doubts about the authenticity or reliability of the supporting material or the applicant's intention to leave. That matters directly where professional activity has been understated or mislabelled. 36

**Visa** **Code, Article** **30.** This provision states that mere possession of a uniform visa or limited territorial validity visa does not confer an automatic right of entry. That principle is especially important in business/work-boundary cases because a traveller who arrives with a Type C visa but appears to be coming for unauthorised work can still face border difficulties. 23

**Visa** **Code, Article** **14** **and** **Annex II.** The Code permits the consulate to require supporting documents to verify the purpose and conditions of the journey; Annex II provides a non-exhaustive list. In Swiss practice, local business-visa checklists build on this structure by asking for a Swiss company invitation, travel arrangements, insurance, proof of employment status and funds. 37

**Regulation (EU) 2016/399 establishing a Union Code on the rulesgoverning the movement of** **persons** **across** **borders** **(Schengen** **Borders** **Code).** Article 6 contains the short-stay entry conditions for third-country nationals. The SwNiss SEM's entry guidance mirrors those conditions: valid visa or equivalent document, valid travel document, proof of purpose and circumstances, sufficient means, no SIS refusal alert, and no public-order or security threat. For business visitors, this matters because the border authority can reassess purpose and circumstances independently of the visa label. 38

### Swiss legislation and ordinances

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (FNIA/AIG), Article 11.** Swiss official guidance identifies Article 11 as the core porovision on permit duty for gainful employment. SEM's directives explain the article through a broad concept of gainful activity, and SEM's public guidance summarises the same principle by stating that foreign nationals may not work in Switzerland without a permit. For this topic, Article 11 is the doctrinal hinge betweenpermissible business visits and work requiring authorisation. 39

**FNIA/AIG, Articles** 18 **to** **24.** SEM's English labour-market guidance identifies these as the key admission criteria for third-state workers. The provisions cover the overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, salary and employment conditions customary in the region and sector, personal requirements, and accommodation. These articles matter whenever the proposed "business visit" is in fact gainful work that requires Swiss labour-market approval. 25

**FNIA/AIG, Article** **30.** SEM's English guidance identifies this as the provision governing exceptions to ordinary admission requirements. It is relevant because some short professional activities-such as certain internship/training structures or narrow international-company transfer situations-may be analysed through exceptions rather than the standard labour-market route, though those exceptions are regulated and should not be assumed. 25

**Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment (VZAE / ASEO / OASA), Articles 1 to 4.** SEM's directives explain that these provisions define the concept of gainful activity and allocate the decision on whether a concrete activity is "gainful" to the competent cantonal labour-market authority, with doubtful cases being referred to SEM. The official guidance makes clear that the concept is wide, objective and labour-market-focused. 40

<!-- 8 -->

**VZAE** **/ ASEO, Article** **14.** SEM's directives state that gainful activity without job entry may be exercised without a permit only up to eight days per calendar year, with a permit required from day nine, and that certain sectors require authorisation from day one. This provision is centralI to cross-border services and foreign-employer cases, including many remote-work fact patterns. 29

**VZAE** **/ ASEO, Article** **17(1).** SEM's directives state that for gainful activity without job entry requiring authorisation, the decision must be awaited abroad. Practically, that prevents travellers from entering on the assumption that the business/work boundary can be sorted out after arrival. 15

**VZAE** **/ ASEO,** **Article** **19(4)(a).** SEM's directives state that this provision allows authorisation for gainful activity of up to four months within twelve months, including a possible "max 120 days in 12 months" formulation for pre-planned non-continuous assignments. The directives also note that a single stay of at least three and at most four months is generally paired with a Type D visa, while split stays can be organised within the 120-day structure. 30

**VZAE** **/ ASEO, Article** **56(1).** SEM's directives note that between two permits of up to four months under this route, the foreign national must spend at least two months abroad. This matters for later drafting on back-to-back deployments and anti-rotation policy. 41

**Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance (VEV), Article 14.** SEM's 2025 annex on the concept of gainful activity expressly states that certain non-gainful short stays can be regulated through visa issuance within the meaning of Article 14 VEV in conjunction with the Visa Code annexes. The significance here is structural: Swiss visa law and Swiss work-authorisation law are linked at the classification stage, and SEM uses Article 14 VEV as the domestic bridge for non-gainful short-stay cases. 42

#### International commitments affecting short professional activity

**General Agreement on Trade in Services and Swiss market-access commitments.** SEM's directives explain that Switzerland has specific commitments for certain categories of service providers and "other essential persons". In particular, executives entering to establish a Swiss establishment, persons entering to conclude service contracts, and certain specialists from foreign firms without Swiss establishment performing engineering or IT service contracts may fall within a specific short-stay access route. SEM states that, for relevant A.2/B.1 categories, a Type C visa with a work confirmation may be issued for stays of up to three months. This is a specialised route, not a general business-visitor exemption. 43

### Official guidance and consular practice notes

#### Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important Swiss administrative source for this topic is **SEM's** FNIA **directives, Chapter** 4**"Stay with gainful activity",** together with the annex **"Concept of gainful activity".** Those materials do three critical things. First, they state that gainful activity is interpreted broadly and objectively. Second, they identify concrete examples of short business meetings that are not treated as gainful activity. Third, they identify concrete examples of productive or service-delivery tasks that are treated as gainful activity and therefore require authorisation. For internal drafting, this annex is probably the single most valuable Swiss source on the business/work boundary. 44

The second key Swiss source is **SEM's guidance on admission of non-EU/EFTA nationals to the Swiss** **employment** **market.** That guidance translates the labour-market side of the topic into operational

#### Swiss representation and consular practice notes

Local Swiss representation material is useful for evidence and procedure, but it should be read as local operational practice rather than black-letter law.

The **Embassy of Switzerland in London's Schengen Business Visa checklist** is **a** good example. **It** requires, among other things, a Swiss company invitation letter stating the purpose and exact dates of business travel and who bears costs, together with travel arrangements, insurance, proof of lawful residence, proof of professional status and proof of funds. This strongly illustrates how Swiss posts expect the non-gainful business narrative to be documented and why evidential coherence matters. However, it is explicitly non-exhaustive and residence-country specific. 46

**The Swiss Consulate General in Ho Chi Minh City's leaflet on a Schengen visa with a short-duration** **work** **permit** is important because it demonstrates a practical Swiss consular route for gainful activity of more than eight but less than 120 working days per calendar year. It saysthat any gainful activity is subject to prior cantonal migration authorisation, that the employer must take the steps in Switzerland beforehand, and that the application must include the migration authority's visa authorisation. This is local consular practice, but it aligns closely with SEM's procedure guidance and is highly useful for drafting nuance. 47

##### Practical legal analysis

The way Switzerland applies the Schengen short-stay framework in business-travel cases is legally layered. At the Schengen level, the core question is whether the person has justified the purpose and conditions of the stay and satisfied the visa and entry criteria. At the Swiss domestic level,the key question is whether the planned conduct is "gainful activity". Those two questions interact. If the planned conduct is actually productive work, then a Type C visa application framed merely as a business trip may fail under the Visa Code for lack of a truthful or adequately documented purpose even before the labour-market issue is fully analysed. 48

<!-- 10 -->

That is why the contract-negotiation / contract-performance distinction matters so much. SEM's annex treats representative visits, negotiations, contract signature and information-only meetings as non-gainful. But once the person is in Switzerland to perform the contract, implement systems, train on the job, repair equipment, join a project, or otherwise deliver productive value on Swiss territory, the case moves into gainful-employment analysis. The legal fault line is not "commercial purpose" versus "employment"; it is whether the stay remains a short, non-productive business visit or becomes actual labour-market participation. 49

Training requires particular care. Swiss official guidance explicitly allows theoretical and technical training without internship or productive integration to be treated as non-gainful and therefore potentially manageable through the short-stay visa route. But the same guidance explicitly classifies training on the job, practical induction in the work process, internships and trainee stays as gainful. Later legal content should therefore distinguish sharply between classroom-style instruction and operational training on Swiss systems, machinery or workflows. Flattening both into "training" would be legally misleading. 12

Conferences are similar. SEM expressly excludes passive participation, especially where the person is not giving a presentation. It does not, however, create a general safe rule for every kind of active conference role. A cautious internal drafting approach should therefore say that passive attendance is clearly on the non-gainful side, while paid speaking, active delivery of professional services, or activity resembling performance work requires closer analysis. That is a professional inference from the source structure, and it is the prudent one. 50

Remote work is the most delicate modern issue. No official Swiss federal source found in this research says that a foreign national on a Type C visa may freely perform remote work from Switzerland for a foreign employer. The signals point the other way: SEM classifies activity for an employer abroad as gainful activity without job entry, and the general concept of gainful activity is broad, objective and not dependent on where salary is paid. A legally conservative internal position is therefore that substantive remote work from Switzerland is risky unless clearly within a specific authorised framework; merely checking emails or taking incidental calls during ordinary business travel is not clearly regulated in the sources collected, so later drafting should avoid categorical statements either way. 8

The short-duration permit architecture is also more nuanced than many public explainers suggest. It is incorrect to say that every work case requires a Type D visa. SEM's directives and local consular practice show that some gainful activities can be handled within a short-duration authorisation structure, including 120-day permissions and, in some cases, a Type C visa combined with work confirmation. Equally, it is incorrect to say that a Type C visa itself authorises work. The precise legal analysis depends on duration, continuity, employer structure, whether the person is entering a Swiss job or rendering cross-border services, and whether the case falls within a special route such as the GATS-related categories described by SEM. 51

The GATS element is real but should be used carefully. SEM's directives recognise specific categories such as executives establishing a Swiss presence, persons entering to conclude service contracts, and certain engineering or IT specialists carrying out a service contract for a Swiss client. SEM says that for some of these short-stay essential-person categories, a Type C visa with work confirmation is used. This is not a broad "business visa" exemption for professional services generally; it is a narrow treaty-informed route within Swiss labour-authorisation practice. If later content is drafted for sophisticated corporate audiences, this point is worth preserving. 43

Finally, the compliance consequences are significant. SEM's official guidance states that illegal employmnent carries criminal penalties, and its brochure adds that illegal stay may lead to removal and an entry ban. Even aside from sanctions, once an applicant or traveller has misdescribed productive work as business, that fact can damage credibility for future visa applications because the Visa Code refusal structure explicitly focuses on reliability of statements and justification of purpose. In practice, business-visit analysis is therefore also credibility analysis. 52

##### Drafting cautioons

·Do not describe a Swiss Schengen Type C visa as permission to work in Switzerland. Swiss official guidance says that gainful employment additionally requires a work permit. 21

·Do not treat "business travel" as a single safe category. Swiss official guidance distinguishes sharply between short negotiations/information exchange and productive or service-delivery work. 53

·Do not assume that payment abroad or employment by a foreign company removes Swiss work-authorisation risk. SEM expressly classifies activity foran employer abroad as gainful activity without job entry. 15

·Do not equate theoretical training with oon-the-job training. The former can fall outside gainful activity; the latter is expressly treated as gainful. 12

·Do not say that all conference attendance is automatically safe. The official SEM exclusion is clear for passive attendance, especially without presenting; it is less explicit for active speakers or service-delivery roles. 28

·Do not state that all short professional work requires a Type D visa. SEM and Swiss consular practice show that some short authorised work can be handled through short-duration work-permit routes and, in some cases, a Schengen/Type C procedure with work connfirmation. 51

· Do not omit the eight-day rule, and do nnot omit that some sectors require authorisation from the first day. 7

·Do not ignore the labour-market layer for third-country workers. Qualifications, precedence, quotas and wage conditions remain relevant once the case is, in substance, work. 32

·Do not present remote work on a Type C visa as clearly permitted. No official Swiss carve-out was found in this research, and the official definition of gainful activity points toward regulation rather than exemption. 8

·Do not say that holding a visa guarantees admission at the border. The Visa Code and Swiss entry guidance say the opposite. 23

##### Open questions and limitations

The official Swiss materials collected here do not expressly define when trivial incidental tasks during travel -such as routine email checking or short internal calls-become legally relevant "gainful activity". The report therefore takes a conservative view and avoids claiming a general exemption for remote work. 16

The official SEM conference guidance clearly excludes passive attendance, especially without presentation, but it does not fully resolve every speaking or panel-participoation scenario. Later drafting should therefore avoid over-confident statements about active conference roles without closer fact analysis. 28

Consular document lists vary by residence country. The UK and Vietnam documents used here are reliable official Swiss sources, but they should be treated as examples of local practice and evidential expectations, not as globally exhaustive Swiss checklists. 54
