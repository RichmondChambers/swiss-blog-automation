# Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Refusals Appeals and Reapplications Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

For Swiss Schengen short-stay visas, the substantive refusal framework is set principally by the Schengen Visa Code, while the institutional and procedural route for contesting a refusal is governed by Swiss law. Under Article 32 of the Visa Code, a visa must be refused where the applicant presents a false, counterfeit or forged travel document, fails to justify the purpose and conditions of stay, fails to show sufficient means of subsistence, has already exhausted the available short-stay time, is the subject of an SIS alert forrefusal ofentry, is considered a threat to public policy, internal security, public heaIth or international relations, or fails to show adequate travel medical insurance where required. A visa must also be refused if there are reasonable doubts about the authenticity or truthfulness of supporting documents, the reliability of the applicant's statements, or the applicant's intention to leave the Schengen area before visa expiry. Article 21 makes clear that this is not a purely formal checklist: the authorities must assess entry conditions, illegal-immigration risk, security risk, document authenticity, means, and intention to leave.1

The refusal decision must be notified by means of the standard form in Annex VI to the Visa Code. The Code also reqquires that the applicant be told how to appeal. In Swiss law, Article 6 of the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration provides that, for a refusal of a visa for a stay not requiring a permit, the competent Swiss representation abroad issues the decision on a standard form on behalf of the State Secretariat for Migration or the Federal Department of Foreign Affairs. The same provision states that a written objection may be filed with the relevant authority within 30 days, and that Article 63 of the Administrative Procedure Act applies mutatis mutandis. Swiss official English-language guidance often calls this an "appeal", but the statute itself uses the terminology of a written objection. 2

Swiss official guidance is relatively specific about how that objection is made in practice. The SEM's public FAQ states that the refusal is communicated by a standard visa refusal form; the objection must be made in writing in German, French or Italian, with a statement of grounds, within 30 days of receipt of the refusal. The person concerned or an authorised representative may submit it to the SEM, and if no authorised proxy exists it may be submitted to a Swiss representation abroad. The same SEM guidance states that SEM will confirm receipt and request an advance on costs of CHF 200 under Article 63 APA, payable within 30 days on the payment slip sent by SEM. Official Swiss mission guidance gives the same essentials and, in some jurisdictions, adds that the visa applicant, a representative with power of attorney, a host in Switzerland, or a company expecting the traveller may lodge the objection; that the filing must be signed and dated; and that it may be sent either to the refusing representation or directly to SEM in Bern-Wabern. 3

The Administrative Procedure Act then supplies the general procedural backbone. Article 50 sets a 30-day appeal period. Article 52 requires the appeal petition to contain the application, grounds, details of evidence and signature, and to attach the contested decision and available supporting evidence. Article 49 allows the appellant to argue breach of federal law, incorrect or incomplete findings of fact,and inadequacy. Article 63 regulates procedural costs and advance payment. Article 58 also allows the lower instance to reconsider its contested ruling before making a formal response to the appeal. These provisions matter because Swiss visa refusals are often decided on documentary credibility, factual inference, or perceived return risk, so a legally effective challenge usually needs to identify both factual error and evidential correction. 4

As to subsequent judicial review, the Federal Administrative Court Act provides that the Federal Administrative Court hears appeals against "decisions" within the meaning of Article 5 APA, including decisions of federal departments and their subordinate or administratively affiliated units. The FAC's own public materials state that it judges the legality of rulings issued by federal authorities and that proceedings before it are generally governed by the APA unless the Court Act provides otherwise. Read together, those materials strongly support the conclusion that a SEM decision on a visa objection is subject to review by the Federal Administrative Court, although the Swiss public guidance located for this pack did not itself set out the full post-SEM appellate route in a visa-specific page. 5

On reapplications, the official Swiss public pages located concentrate on the formal objection route rather than laying down a dedicated "reapplication regime". What is clear from the EU side is that the VIS is designed to store visa-application data and decisions, including refusals, and the Commission's Visa Code Handbook I states that where a previous refusal appears in VIS the consulate should contact the Member State that decided the earlier case and that a new application should be linked to the prior application dataset. Practically, that means a fresh application is not invisible: it will be read against the refusal history, and any unresolved credibility, finances, purpose, document, or return-risk concerns are likely to persist. In legal content terms, the safest internal distinction is that an objection challenges the lawfulness or factual correctness of the refusal already made, whereas a reapplication seeks a new decision on a new or materially improved evidential record. The latter proposition is an inference from the structure of the regime and should be treated as such. 6

## Framework and propositions

### Structural overview

**Legal character** **of refusal.** A Swiss Schengen Type C refusal is not governed by a freestanding Swiss substantive refusal code. The substantive refusal grounds come from Article 32 of the Schengen Visa Code, which Switzerland apoplies as a Schengen-associated state through its Schengen-linked federal framework. Swiss law then organises who issues the refusal and what remedy lies against it. 7

**Decision-maker and** **form.** For short stays not requiring a Swiss permit, the competent Swiss representation abroad issues the refusal on a standard form on behalf of SEM or FDFA. At EU level,the refusal must be notified on the Annex VI standard form and must include information on the appeal procedure. 2

**Substantive** **refusal grounds.** The core legal grounds are inadequate purpose/conditions evidence, insufficient subsistence means, overstay of the 90/180 rule, SIS alert, public-policy or security concerns, missing travel medical insurance where required, and broader credibility concerns including false documents, unreliable statements, and doubts about intention to leave. Article 21 shows that these grounds are assessed through a risk-based examination of entry conditions and illegal-immigration/ security risk. 8

<!-- 2 -->

**Swiss** **remedy** **terminology.** Swiss official English pages commonly describe the remedy as an "appeal", but the current FNIA text uses "written objection". That distinction should be preserved in internal drafting, because the statutory remedy to SEM/FDFA is not the same procedural step as subsequent judicial review by the Federal Administrative Court. 9

**Time** **limit, language** **and** **content.** The key deadline is 30 days from notification. SEM guidance requires the filing to be in German, French or Italian, with grounds. APA Article 52 adds formal content requirements, and Article 63 governs the advance on costs. 10

**SEM handling.** SEM's public contact guidance states that appeals against Schengen and humanitarian visa refusals are examined by SEM and that examination usually takes several months, depending on the need for complementary investigations. 11

**Further review by the Federal Administrative Court.** The Federal Administrative Court hears appeals against decisions of federal administrative authorities and proceedings before it are generallysubject to the APA. In combination with VGG Articles 31, 33(d) and 37, this strongly indicates that a SEM objection decision can be judicially reviewed by the FAC. This is a legally strong inference from the legislation and court materials located. 12

**Reapplication** **risk.** The material located supports the proposition that previous refusals remain visible and relevant through VIS-linked records. What was not found in the located Swiss official material was a dedicated Swiss public rule setting out when reapplication is preferable to appealI or any formal Swiss "reapplication test" for Type C refusals. That point should therefore be handled as practice analysis, not black-letter Swiss law. 13

### Primary legal texts

**Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas.** Article 21 requires the decision-maker to verify entry conditions and conduct a risk assessment, including whether the applicant presents a risk of illegal immigration or security risk and whether the applicant intends to leave before visa expiry. It also requires verification of travel-document authenticity, purpose and conditions of stay, means of subsistence, SIS status, and insurance. This is the key legal basis for the evidential and credibility analysis that later feeds refusal decisions. 15

<!-- 5 -->

The same Regulation's Article 32 is the main refusal provision. It requires refusal where the applicant fails to meet one of the listed grounds and where there are reasonable doubts about document authenticity, truthfulness, statement reliability or intention to depart. Article 32(2) requires reasons to be notified on the Annex VI standard form; Article 32(3) grants a right of appeal under the nnational law of the deciding state. For Swiss practice, this is the substantive refusal rule that SEM and the Swiss representations must apply. 22

Article 30 of the Visa Code states that mere possession of a uniform visa or LTV visa does not confer an automatic right of entry. That matters in refusal and challenge drafting because a short-stay visa decision and a later border-entry decision are related but legally distinct. 14

Article 34 deals with annulment and revocation of an issued visa, including notification on the same Annex VI standard form and a right oof appeal. This is not the same as a refusal of an unissued visa, but the overlap matters when analysing whether the correct legal label is refusal, annulment or revocation. 23

**Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration.** Article 5 states the Swiss entry requirements: recognised identity documennt, visa or ETIAS where required, sufficient means, no threat to public security/order or international relations, no relevant expulsion or entry-ban measure, and a guarantee to leave if only a temporary stay is planned. In visa-refusal work this matters because Article 21 Visa Code examination cross-refers to Schengen-style entry conditions, and Swiss federal law states them in domestic form. 18

Article 6 is fundamental for Swiss refusal procedure. Paragraph 2 provides that, in short-stay cases not requiring apermit, the foreign representation issues the refusal on a standard form on behalf of SEM or FDFA.Paragraph 2bis provides for a written objection within 30 days to the relevant authority and imports APA Article 63 on costs. This is the clearest Swiss primary-law basis for the remedy after a Type Crefusal. 18

**Federal Act** on **Administrative** **Procedure.** Article 35 requires decisions to state grounds and directions on legal remedies. Article 44 says any ruling is subject to appeal. Article 48 sets standing. Article 49 defines review grounds: breach of federal law, incorrect or incomplete fact-finding, and inadequacy. Article 50 fixes a 30-day period for filing an appeal. Article 52 sets the appeal petition requirements. Article 55 deals with suspensive effect in general administrative procedure. Article 58allows the lower instance to reconsider its contested ruling before its formal response. Article 63governs procedural costs and advance payment. Together, these provisions supply the general Swiss procedural law for visa objections andany further judicial review. 4

**Federal Administrative** **Court** Act. Article 31 gives the FAC jurisdiction over appeals against "decisions" as defined in APA Article 5. Article 33(d) extends that to decisions of federal departments and their subordinate or administratively affiliated units. Article 37 provides that FAC proceedings are governed by the APA unless the Court Act provides otherwise. These provisions matter because SEM is a federal administrative unit, so they provide the statutory basis for onward review after the SEM stage. 24

## Official guidance and practice materials

### Official guidance and administrative materials

The SEM's public FAQ on entry is the most useful Swiss official administrative source for refusal challenges. It states that refusal is notified by a standard visa refusal form; that the appeal must be made in writing in German, French or Italian with a statement of grounds; that the filing deadlline is 30days from receipt; that the person concerned or an authorised representative may submit it to SEM; that if there is no authorised proxy it may be filed with a Swiss representation abroad; and that SEM will request an advance on costs of CHF 200 under Article 63 APA. It also sets out family-related advance-cost practice. 19

<!-- 6 -->

The SEM's contact page adds an important operational point: appeals against the refusal of Schengen and humanitarian visas are examined by SEM, and the examination usually takes several months depending on whether complementary investigations are needed. For internal drafting, this is a strong official source for explaining why appeal is a formal legal route but may not be a quick practical remedy for imminent travel. 11

At EU/Schengen level, the Commission's 2024 Visa Code Handbook I is not itself a binding source of rights, but it is official operational guidance under Article 51 of the Visa Code. It emphasises that only the underlying legal acts are legally binding, but it remains highly useful in showing how refusal grounds are operationalised, including credibility, reliability and intention-to-leave assessments. It also maps refusal-form grounds in practice and explains howprevious refusals appearing in VIS should be handled in later files. 25

The FAC's public procedural materials confirm that FAC proceedings generally follow the APA and that the Court reviews the legality of rulings made by federal authorities. These materials do not replace the Court Act but support the practical understanding of the review stage. 26

### Swiss representation and consular practice notes

Swiss mission pages are useful but should be treated as procedural/explanatory practice materials, not as substitutes for the Visa Code, FNIA or APA. Multiple Swiss representations state in materially similar terms that refusal reasons are notified by standard form and that the applicant has 30 days to appeal. UK,Pakistan and Sri Lanka mission pages repeat that high-level position and refer readers back to SEM for the appeal procedure. 27

The Switzerland-in-Ethiopia mission page is more detailed. It states that the visa applicant, a representative with written authority, the host in Switzerland or a company awaiting the traveller may lodge the appeal within 30 days; that it must be sent in an official Swiss language, signed and dated; and that it may be sent either to the refusing representation or directly to SEM in Bern-Wabern. It also states that SEM will issue a further decision subject to appeal after an advance payment. This is useful as an official consular description of current practice, but it should still be treated as explanatory mission guidance. 28

A point to handle cautiously is the identity of the Swiss authority. FNIA Article 6(2bis) still refers to objection to the relevant authority, "SEM or FDFA", whereas the current Swiss public guidance located for ordinary Schengen visa refusals points consistently to SEM. No present official Swiss source was located in this research set that clearly explains, for current Type C refusals,the residual categories in which FDFA rather than SEM is the operative objection authority. 29

#### Practical legal analysis

Swiss Schengen Type C refusal law is best understood as a layered system. The Visa Code supplies the substantive grounds and the obligation to use the Schengen standard refusal form; Swiss law supplies the institutional route by which the representation issues the decision and the applicant challenges it. That is why later legal drafting should not describe a refusal as if it were simply "consular discretion under Swiss practice". The representation is applying an EU/Schengen refusal code within a Swiss procedural framework. 30

<!-- 7 -->

Because Article 21 requires a risk assessment, and Article 32 allows refusal where there are reasonable doubts about document authenticity, statement reliability or intention to leave, many refusals in practice are really credibility refusals even when they are framed under more concrete headings such as unclear purpose or insufficient means. A weak itinerary, unexplained deposits, a sponsor with unclear status, inconsistent employment evidence, or vague travel plans can all culminate in a refusal because the file fails to tell a coherent, verifiable story. That is not free-floating discretion: it is the Article 21/32assessment mechanism in operation. 31

The standard refusal form matters more than it may appear. It is the formal vehicle required by Annex VI, and it should tell the applicant both the refusal reason and how to challenge it. However, standard-form reasons can be terse. For later drafting, the safest approach is to treat the ticked box as the legal category and the file evidence as the true battleground. That means an objection should not merely restate sympathy or future travel plans; it should address the exact refusal category with targeted documents and legal argument. Relevant EU case law also shows that the right of appeal under Article 32 is tied to effective judicial protection and the scope of judicial review. 32

Appeal and reapplication are not interchangeable. The formal Swiss route is a written objection within 30 days to the competent Swiss federal authority, followed by onward judicial review on the Swiss administrative-law model. A fresh application, by contrast, seeks a new merits decision.The sources located do not set out a dedicated Swiss rule saying when one must reapply rather than object, so the strategy point here is an informed professional inference: appeal is generally the stronger route where the refusal is legally or factually wrong on the material already before the authority, whereas reapplication is often more practical where the original file was genuinely weak and can now be materially repaired. In either case, the applicant must assume that the earlier refusal and much of the earlier data remain visible through VIS-linked records. 33

The onward FAC stage should also be understood accurately. The legislation and the Court's own materialIs make the route reasonably clear: the FAC reviews federal administrative decisions and generally applies the APA. That means a SEM visa decision is not insulated from judicial scrutiny.At the same time, judicial review is still review of an administrative record and administrative decision-making. Where the real problem is that the original application was incomplete, unconvincing or poorly documented, a factually improved new application may be pragmatically stronger than prolonged litigation. That is strategy, not black-letter law, and should be presented as such. 5

### Drafting cautions

·Do not say that Swiss visa refusals are governed only by Swiss domestic law. The substantive refusal grounds come from the Schengen Visa Code; Swiss law governs the issuing authority and remedy route. 34

·Do not describe the first Swiss remedy only as an "appeal" without qualification. Swiss official English guidance uses that word, but FNIA Article 6(2bis) speaks of a written objection. 9

·Do not omit the 30-day deadline, and do not imply that it is flexible. The statute and Swiss guidance both anchor the challenge route to 30 days from notification/receipt. 35

·Do not suggest that English-language submissions are sufficient as of right. The Swiss official guidance located requires German, French or Italian. 36

<!-- 8 -->

·Do not tell users that a refusal can be overcome simply by "explaining better". The law requires hard proof on purpose, means, insurance, documents and intention to leave, and it allows refusal on broader credibility doubts. 8

·Do not conflate refusal of an unissued visa with annulment or revocation of an issued visa,or with refusal of entry at the border. They are related but legally distinct processes. 37

·Do not say that the embassy itself decides the appeal. The located Swiss public guidance points to SEM as the authority examining appeals against Schengen visa refusals. 38

·Do not treat reapplication as a clean restart. The material located shows that refusals remain visible through VIS-related records and that later files are linked to earlier ones.(13

·Do not overstate what was found on reapplications. No dedicated Swiss official public rule on "reapplication strategy" was located in this research set; any appeal-versus-reapplication advice should therefore be identified as professional inference and not as black-letter Swiss law. 39

·Do not ignore cost-advance requirements. SEM's own guidance states that it usually requests a CHF 200 advance on costs under **APA** Article 63. 20
