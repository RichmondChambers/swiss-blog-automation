# Border Checks Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

For short stays in Switzerland, the core legal architecture for third-country nationals is Schengen law, applied in Switzerland through the Schengen association framework and Swiss domestic law. Swiss law expressly provides that the visa procedure and rules on entry and exit apply only so far as the Schengen Association Agreements do not provide otherwise, and the Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance states that it governs entry, airport transit and visas, but only insofar as the Schengen association instruments do not displace it. Swiss federal guidance also states that all Schengen member states apply the same entry requirements at the external border. ①

The operative entry conditions for a short stay are those in Article 6 of the Schengen Borders Code. For a stay of no more than 90 days in any 180-day period, a third-country national must have a valid travel document meeting the Schengen validity rules, a valid visa if one is required, evidence of the purpose and conditions of the intended stay, sufficient means of subsistence for the stay and return or onward transit, no SIS alert for refusal of entry, and no disqualifying threat to public policy,internal security, public health or the international relations of a Schengen state. Swiss SEM guidance restates those same conditions for entering Switzerland. 2

A Schengen visa is therefore not an unconditional admission document. The Visa Code states that mere possession of a uniform visa or a visa with limited territorial validity does not confer an automatic right of entry. Swiss representation guidance and the European Commission's official visa FAQ both emphasise the same practical point: at the border, a traveller may still be asked to produce supporting material such as proof of funds, medical travel insurance where relevant, and return-travel evidence, so applicants should carry copies of the documents submitted with the visa application. 3

Border control is not limited to looking at the passport and visa sticker. Under Article 8 of the Schengen Borders Code, third-country nationals are subject to thorough checks on entry and exit. These checks include verification of the Article 6 conditions, examination of supporting documents about destination and purpose, scrutiny of means of subsistence, and consultation of the relevant databases. Official EU and Swiss materials explain that these checks rely in particular on systems such as SIS and VIS, and now also EES. VIS allows border guards to verify that the visa holder is the rightful holder, including by biometric verification. EES, which became fully operational across the participating Schengen countries on 10 April 2026, records short-stay entries, exits and refusals of entry and replaces passport stamping for the affectedcategories of non-EU travellers. 4

The evidential questions that matter most at the border are practical and credibility-driven. Annex I to the Schengen Borders Code gives examples of supporting documents that may be requested, including invitations, hotel reservations, travel itineraries, return or round-trip tickets, proof of participation in events, and documents showing the business, study or other short-stay purpose. Swiss SEM guidance likewise points to invitation letters, hotel reservations and return or onward tickets. Swiss official guidance also gives a practical benchmark for means of subsistence in Switzerland: ordinarily CHF 100per day, or CHF 30 per day for students with a valid studdent permit. Those Swiss amounts are administrative guidance rather than a directly stated figure in the Borders Code itself. 5

If a traveller does not satisfy the entry conditions, refusal follows as a border-law consequence, not merely as a visa-office concern. At Schengen level, refusal of entry must be based on a substantiated decision stating the precise reasons. In Swiss law, where entry is refused, the border authority issues a return decision; and where controls are temporarily reintroduced at the Swiss border and entry is refused, the authority must issue a reasoned and appealable decision using the Schengen standard form in Annex V Part B. Swiss law further provides that such a refusal may be enforced immediately and that an appeal does not have suspensive effect. For this pack, the safest internal drafting position is that border refusal remedies exist, but they are structurally narrower and more urgent than ordinary visa-application remedies. 6

## Structural overview of entry conditions

**Legal** **frame.** Switzerland applies Schengen external-border rules through its Schengen association arrangements. Swiss domestic law defers to those agreements for visa procedure and entry/exit,while the Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance is the principal implementing ordinance on the Swiss side. The Schengen Borders Code supplies the core entry and border-check rules. 1

**Valid** **document and** **visa** **position.** The starting point is a recognised travel document that satisfies the Schengen validity criteria. Where the traveller is visa-required, a valid visa is needed. Swiss SEM guidance also recognises that a valid Schengen residence permit or valid Schengen type D visa can satisfy the entry side of the visa requirement for travel to Switzerland. 7

**Purpose, conditions** **of stay** **and** **means.** Border guards may require the traveller to justify the purpose and conditions of the intended stay and to show sufficient means for the intended stay and return or onward transit. Schengen law gives examples rather than an exhaustive list of acceptable proof. Swiss guidance specifically mentions invitation letters, hotel reservations and return or onward tickets. 8

**Accommodation** **and** **onward** **plans.** There is no separate black-letter Schengen rule saying that a hotel booking is always mandatory. Rather, accommodation evidence is one form of supporting material used to verify the purpose and conditions of stay. The same is true of return or onward travel: it is evidence that helps show the connditions of stay and lawful exit, not an isolated stand-alone requirement divorced from the Article 6 framework. 5

**Insurance.** Travel medical insurance is clearly a Visa Code requirement for applicants for a uniform short-stay visa for one or two entries. Swiss SEM guidance also lists medical insurance among the conditions that visa-required third-country nationals should meet on entry. The careful internal position is that insurance is primarily a visa-law requirement, but it remains relevant at the border because the traveller may be asked to evidence the conditions that supported visa issuance. 9

**Databases and** **security** **checks.** Entry checks are integrated with SIS, VIS and now EES. SIS matters because an alert for refusal of entry is itself an Article 6 ground for refusing entry. VIS matters because it allows the border authority to verify the authenticity of the visa and the identity of the visa holder, including through biomnetrics. EES matters because it now records entry, exit and refusal-of-entry data for the relevant short-stay categories and automatically supports stay calculation. 10

**Refusal** of **entry** **and** **remedy.** Refusal of entry follows if the Article 6 conditions are not satisfied and no relevant derogation applies. At Schengen level, refusal must be reasoned. In Swiss law, border

## Primary legal texts

**Regulation (EU) 2016/399, Schengen Borders Code, Article 6.** This is the central short-stay entry provision1. In substance, it requires a compliant travel document, a visa if required, justification of purpose and conditions of stay, sufficient means for the stay and return or onward transit, no SIS refusal alert, and no threat to public policy, internal security, public health or international relations. It also contains the key travel-document validity criteria. For Swiss Schengen analysis, this is the default border-admission test. 12

**Schengen** **Borders** **Code, Article** **6(3) and Annex** I. Article 6(3) links the traveller's burden of proof to supporting documents, and Annex I gives the practical documentary examples. Those include invitations from hosts or businesses, hotel bookings, travel itineraries, return or round-trip tickets, tickets or enrolments for events, and documents showing study, business or private-visit purposes. This matters boecause many "document requests" at the border are not free-standing legal obligations; they are evidential ways of testing the Article 6 conditions. 21

<!-- 6 -->

**Schengen** **Borders** **Code, Article** 8. This governs border checks. For third-country nationals, checks on entry and exit are thorough rather than minimal. The material gathered here shows that the checks comprise verification of the Article 6 conditions and, in practice, include examination of supporting documents and consultation of databases such as SIS and VIS. This provision is the legal bridge between the abstract entry conditions and the practical questioning/document scrutiny done by border officers. 22

**Schengen Borders Code, Article 14 and Annex V Part B.** **If** a third-country national does not fulfil the entry conditions and does not fall within a relevant derogation, entry is refused. The decision must be substantiated and state the precise reasons. The Schengen refusal form in Annex V Part B remains important in Swiss implementation, because Swiss law expressly refers to it in the reintroduced-control context. 6

**Schengen Borders Code, Article 2 definition of "threat to public health".** The Code defines the public-health limb by reference to diseases with epidemic potential under the International Health Regulations and other infectious or contagious parasitic diseases where protection rules apply to Member State nationals. That matters because "public health" is a legal ground of refusal, but it is not an open-ended discretion. 23

**Regulation** **(EC)** No **810/2009,** Visa **Code, Article** **15.** For applicants for a uniform visa for one or two entries, adequate and valid travel medical insurance must cover repatriation for medical reasons, urgent medical attention, emergency hospital treatment and death during the stay. This matters to border analysis because insurance is one of the conditions behind lawful visa issuance and Swiss guidance treats it as something a visa-required traveller may have to show on entry. 9

**Visa Code, Article30.** The legal effect is simple and important: a Schengen visa does not itself create an automatic right of entry. This is the single most useful corrective against overstatementin client-facing or internal drafting. 24

**Regulation** **(EU) 2017/2226** **establishing** **EES.** The EES Regulation creates the system that records entry, exit and refusal-of-entry data for the relevant category of short-stay non-EU nationals crossing the external borders. Official current EU material confirms that the system is fully operational and replaces passport stamping for those categories. For Swiss analysis, EES is now part of the normal border-control environment, not a merely future project. 25

**Swiss Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 5.** Swiss entrylaw mirrors and supplements the Schengen framework. Article 5 requires a recognised identity document, a visa or ETIAS travel authorisation where required, the required financial means, no threat to public security/ order or Switzerland's international relations, and no applicable entry ban or expulsion measure; for temporary stays it also requires a guarantee of departure. This matters because Swiss domestic law still frames the border-control decision fromn the Swiss side, even though Schengen law remains central. 26

**Swiss Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 7.** Article 7 states that entry and exit are governed by the Schengen Association Agreements, requires the border authority to issue a return decision if entry is refused, and provides that where controls are temporarily reintroduced and entry is refused the authority issues a reasoned, appealable decision on the Schengen Annex V Part B form, enforceable immediately andd without suspensive effect of appeal. This is the key Swiss text on border refusal consequences. 26

**Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance, Article 1.** The official Fedlex text states that the ordinance governs entry into Switzerland, airport transit and the issuance of visas to foreign nationals, and applies only insofar as the Schengen Association Agreements do not provide otherwise. This is the clearest Swiss implementing gateway provision. 27

**Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance, Article 28.** The official Fedlex text states that entry into and departure from Switzerland are governed by the Schengen Borders Code, subject to customs law and implementing customs provisions. This is especially important for Swiss-border drafting because it explains why Schengen person-entry law and Swiss customs practice coexist. 28

## Official guidance andI consular practice notes

Swiss federal guidance is relatively strong on the practical content of border checks. The SEM's entry FAQ breaks down the requirements separately for visa-exempt and visa-reqquired third-country nationals. For visa-exempt third-country nationals, SEM states that they must prove the purpose and circumstances of their stay, have sufficient financial resources for the stay and return or onward transit, not be the subject of a SIS alert for refusal of entry, not pose a threat to Swiss security, public order, public health or the international relations of a Schengen state, and not be subjectto an expulsion order or other Swiss entry ban. The same FAQ then gives concrete examples of documents that may be asked for, including a return or onward ticket, an invitation letter and a hotel reservation. 19

For visa-required third-country nationals, SEM says they must hold a valid C visa, D visa or Schengen residence permit, explain the purpose and conditions of their stay, show sufficient means, have medical insurance, not be subject to a SIS refusal alert, not pose the relevant security/public-order/public-health/international-relations threat, and not be subject to expulsion or exclusion measures. The same official FAQ states that at the Swiss border travellers may be questioned about their financial means and may be turned away if they cannot show sufficient means, their place of stay or the purpose of the visit. It then gives the Swiss benchmark of CHF 100 per day, reduced to CHF 30 per day for students with a valid student permit. 19

The SEM's "Entry with visa" and general entry pages restate the same basic logic in a more consolidated form: entry requirements vary by nationality, purpose and length of stay; visa-required third-country nationals must have a valid visa or equivalent status document, a compliant travel document,evidence of purpose/circumstances, sufficient funds, and no SIS or security/public-order/public-health disqualification. 29

Swiss federal customs/border guidance is narrower but useful. The Federal Office for Customs and Border Security notes that entry requires a recognised travel document and, in some cases,a visa,while directing travellers to SEM for the operative immigration-law rules. SEM's Schengen information page further explains the Swiss practical nuance that, because Switzerland is outside the EU Customs Union, customs and goods inspections conntinue at Swiss borders and persons may also still boe checked where necessary in connection with those inspections or for personal protection. 30

On consular practice, recent official Swiss representation pages are helpful but should be treated as procedural or evidential guidance rather than black-letter law. FDFA's central "travel to Switzerland" and "visa requirements" pages distinguish Schengen short-stay and national long-stay visas and direct travellers to SEM entry rules. Representation pages for the United Kingdom and Germany state that an application for a Schengen visa for Switzerland should be accompanied by the required documentation, that biometrics are captured and retained in VIS for five years, and that as a general rule a visa decision is taken within 15 days. Those pages are useful to show how Swiss authorities operationalise the Schengen framework, but they do not themselves create the border-entry condditions. 31

A particularly useful representation practice note appears on the official Swiss representation site for Vietnam. It states that an issued visa does not automatically entitle the holder to enter the Schengen area and that, at border checkpoints or during other checks within Schengen, the traveller may have to show the visa and provide additional documentation such as proof of sufficient means, compulsory medical travel insurance and the return flight ticket. It therefore recommends carrying copies of the application documents. This is not new law, but it is a reliable official statement of how the legal distinction between visa issuance and border admission is applied in practice. 32

At EU level, the Commission's official border-crossing page explains that the Schengen Borders Code provides the common rules on external border checks, entry requirements and short-stay duration, and that the Practical Handbook for Border Guards provides operational guidance. The Commission's official short-stay visa FAQ likewise states that a short-stay visa does not automatically entitle entry and that additional documentation may be requested at the border. 33

## Practical legal analysis

The first internal drafting point is that Swiss border admission for Schengen short stays is not primarily a consular matter. Visa issuance and border admission are legally connected but distinct. The visa office assesses the application ex ante under the Visa Code. The border authority then assesses, at the moment of crossing, whether the entry conditions are actually met. That is why Article 30 of the Visa Code says visa possession does not create an automatic right of entry, and why official Swiss and EU guidance tell travellers to carry copies of their supporting documents even after the visa has been issued. 3

The second point is that "entry conditions" are broader than a checklist of passport and visa. Article 6SBC bundles document validity, immigration status, credible purpose, sufficient resources and security screening into one cumulative test. In practice, purpose, accommodation, itinerary and finances are assessed together. A hotel booking may support both purpose and accommodation; a return ticket may support temporary intent and theavailability of means to depart; an invitation letter may support destination, accommodation and the social explanation for the trip.That is why Annex I uses illustrative examples rather than a closed list. 34

The third point is that Swiss official practice goes further than the bare text of the Borders Code in certain concrete respects, but usually as guidance rather than as a new legal threshold. The CHF 100-per-day and CHF 30-per-day figures are good examples. They are plainly useful for practical assessment and should be treated as an official Swiss benchmark for sufficiency of means,yet they are not themselves the wording of Article 6 SBC. The same care applies to accommodation and return-travel evidence.They are highly relevant and often expected, but they are evidential tools used to test the Article 6 conditions, not independent statutory conditions floating on their own. 35

The fourth point is that the border officer's enquiry is now much more system-based than older passport-stamp-era explanations suggest. VIS allows verification of the visa and the identity of the holder, including by biometrics. SIS matters because an entry-refusal alert is itself an Article 6disqualifier. EES matters because, asof 10 April 2026, the system is fully operational and records entries, exits and refusals of entry for the covered categories while replacing passport stamping. For internal drafting, that means credibility problems, previous-travel inconsistencies and overstay questions are more likely to be visible at the border than older content models may assume. 36

The fifth point is that travel medical insurance requires careful phrasing. It is clearly an issuance requirement under the Visa Code for many uniform-visa applications. Swiss SEM guidance also lists it among the conditions that visa-required travellers should meet in order to enter Switzerland. But the Borders Code's Article 6 does not list it in the same way that it lists the travel document, visa, means and SIS/security grounds. The safe internal formulation is therefore: insurance is a visa-law requirement and a live border-practice issue for visa-required travellers, but it should not be described as an autonomous Schengen-Borders-Code entry condition for every traveller. 37

The sixth point is that Swiss domestic law matters most when describing consequences and enforcement. FNIA Article 7 ties refusal of entry to a return decision and makes clear that, where border controls are temporarily reintroduced and entry is refused, the decision is reasoned, appealable and based on the Schengen standard form, but may be enforced immediately and without suspensive effect. In internal drafting, that means border refusal should not be treated as functionally identical to an ordinary visa refusal by a consulate. The procedural posture, the immediate practical consequences and the urgency are different. 26

The seventh point is specifically Swiss: statements about "the Swiss border" need to distinguish Schengen immigration checks from customs controls. Schengen removes routine checks on persons at internal borders as a rule, but Switzerland remains outside the EU Customs Union. Official SEM guidance therefore says customs and goods inspections continue and persons may also be checked where necessary in connection with them. Content that says there are "no border checks" between Switzerland and neighbouring Schengen states is too broad and should be corrected. 11

## Drafting cautions

·Do not say that a Swiss or Schengen short-stay visa guarantees entry; it does not. 24

·Do not reduce the border test to "passport plus visa"; Article 6 also requires credible purpose, sufficient means and no SIS/security/public-health disqualification. 38

·Do not present hotel bookings or return tickets as invariably mandatory in every case;they are examples of supporting evidence used to verify the conditions of stay. 39

·Do not present Swiss means thresholds as statutory Schengen law; the CHF 100/CHF 30 figures are Swiss official guidance. 19

·Do not omit the travel-document validity rules, especially the "three months after intended departure" and "issued within the previous tenyears" criteria for ordinary short-stay Schengen entry. 13

·Do not describe travel medical insurance as a universal standalone Borders Code entry condition; describe it as a clear Visa Code requirement and a border-practice issue for visa-required travellers. 37

·Do not collapse visa issuance and border admission into one decision. The visa office and the border authority perform different legal functions. 40

·Do not ignore SIS, VIS and EES when discussing modern border checks; database and biometric verification are now central to the system. 41

·Do not say that there are neverchecks at Swiss internal borders; Schengen person-check rules and Swiss customs controls must be distinguished. 11

·Do not treat border-refusal remedies as interchangeable with ordinary visa-refusal appeal routes; Swiss law allows immediate enforcement and removes suspensive effect in the reintroduced-control setting. 26
