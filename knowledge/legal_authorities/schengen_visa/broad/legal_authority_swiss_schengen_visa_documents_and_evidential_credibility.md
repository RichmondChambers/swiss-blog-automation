## Executive legal summary

A Swiss Schengen visa application for a short stay is governed first by the Schengen Visa Code and then, for Swiss implementation points, by Swiss entry and visa rules. At the level of black-letter law, the core evidential architecture is compact: the application must be lodged with the required form, travel document, photograph, biometric data where applicable, fee, supporting documents, and proof of travel medical insurance where applicable; admissibility is then checked before the merits are examined. On the merits, the decisive questions are whether the applicant meets the relevant external-border entry conditions, whether the stated purpose and conditions of stay are made out, whether sufficient means exist, whether the file credibly shows an intention to leave before visa expiry, and whether there is any illegal-immigration, security or document-fraud concern. Switzerland applies those Schengen rules directly for Type C visas, while the Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance supplements them on fingerprints, sponsorship undertakings, travel medical insurance and related guarantees. 1

The documentary burden is therefore broader than a simple checklist. The Visa Code requires documents showing the purpose of the journey, accommodation or the means to cover it, means of subsistence for the trip and return, and material allowing the consulate to assess whether the applicant genuinely intends to leave the Schengen area before the visa expires. Annex II to the Visa Code makes clear that the possible proofs are non-exhaustive and vary by travel purpose: business trips may require invitations, evidence of trade relations and employment status; tourism and private visits may require accommodation and itinerary material; study or training can require enrolment evidence; medical travel requires confirmation from the treating institution and proof of funds for treatment. The European Commission's Visa Code Handbook emphasises that the object of these documents is not formalism for its own sake, but assessment of entry conditions, immigration risk and security risk.2

For Switzerland, the file's credibility is often as important as the mere existence of documents. Article 21of the Visa Code requires verification of the applicant's justification for the purpose and conditions of stay, sufficient means, relevant SIS alerts, public-policy and security concerns, and valid travel medical insurance. It also requires special attention to the risk of illegal immigration and to whether the applicant intends to leave before expiry. The Visa Code Handbook develops that into a practical evidential test: consulates must assessthe authenticity and reliability of documents and statements, may interview the applicant, may request additional documents in justified cases, and should treat the depth of examination as dependent on nationality, local circumstances, profile and travel history. That means that internally consistent evidence matters: itinerary, accommodation, invitation, host details, bank statements, employer letter, leave approval, family situation and previous travel history should point in the same direction, rather than merely existing in isolation. 3

The most common evidential pressure points are travel-document validity, recent passport photograph, insurance, funds, accommodation, and ties to the country of residence. The travel document must normally extend at least three months beyond the intended departure, contain at least two blank pages, and have been issued within the previous ten years. For one-or two-entry visas, insurance must cover the entire intended stay; for multiple-entry visas, it must cover at least the first intended stay and the applicant must sign the declaration acknowledging the need for insurance on future stays. The minimum cover is EUR 30,000 and must extend throughout the Schengen area. Swiss public guidance repeats those requirements and adds a practical point: the consulate decides whether a Swiss declaration of sponsorship is needed where the applicant's own funds are insufficient or doubtful. Under Swiss law that sponsorship can cover living costs, medical costs and return costs up to CHF 30,000, and it is irrevocable once effective. 4

Official Swiss guidance also shows that not every supporting document has the same legal quality.A letter of invitation is evidential rather than dispositive: SEM states that it may be requested in the visa process or at the border, that it should contain specified factual details, and that it proves the reason for the visit, but it does not amount to a binding financial undertaking. The binding financial undertaking, where required, is instead the Swiss declaration of sponsorship. Consular checklists then add jurisdiction-specific practice. For example, Swiss representations in China publish purpose-specific lists requiring, depending on the case, recent bank statements, employer letters, translations into English, notarial relationship certificates with apostille, former Schengen visas, residence-permit copies, invitation letters, detailed medical-clinic letters and, for some business-training scenarios, even a work permit. Those local lists are best treated as procedural and evidential practice in a particular jurisdiction, not universal Swiss black-letter law. 5

Where credibility fails, refusal follows. Under Article 32 of the Visa Code, a visa must be refused if entry conditions are not met, required insurance is missing, or there are reasonable doubts about the authenticity of supporting documents, the truth of their contents, the reliability of statements, or the applicant's intention to leave in time. Switzerland uses the standard refusal form and SEM guidance states that an appeal must be filed within 30 days, in German, French or Italian, normally with an advance on costs. In practice, therefore, documentary weakness has two consequences: it can defeat the application on the merits, and it can shape whether a later appeal is worth pursuing or whether a better-documented reapplication is more sensible. 6

## Structural overview of documentary evidence

### Application form, admissibility and travel document

Every applicant must submit a signed Schengen visa application form;persons included in another travel document must still submit a separate form, and a minor's form must be signed by a person exercising parental authority or legal guardianship. Swiss public guidance makes the Schengen form available centrally and notes that representation websites may also provide local-language versions. Admissibility depends on timely lodgement, the presence of the core form/photo/travel-document elements, biometric capture and payment of the fee. The passport or travel document must normally be valid for at least three months after intended departure, contain two blank pages, and have boeen issued within the previous ten years. 7

### Photograph and biometrics

The Visa Code requires a photograph and, for most applicants, biometric enrolment consisting of a facial image and ten fingerprints. The first application generally requires personal appearance; later applications may reuse fingerprints already entered in the VIS if first enrolled less than 59 months

earlier, but the consulate may still recollect if identity doubts exist or earlier prints are unusable. Children under 12 are exempt from fingerprinting. Swiss representation guidance in the United Kingdom and China reflects that framework and adds the practical point that personal appearance may still be required if earlier fingerprints are of insufficient quality. Representation photo sheets typically apply ICAO-style requirements: recent colour photo, correct dimensions, neutral background, clear face and no obstructing accessories save limited religious exceptions. 8

### Purpose, itinerary and accommodation evidence

Article 14 of the Visa Code requires purpose-of-journey documents, accommodation evidence or proof of means to cover accommodation, and material relevant to intention to leave. Annex II provides examples rather than an exhaustive list. The type of evidence is purpose-specific: tourism commonly calls for accommodation and itinerary material; porivate visits may call for a host invitation and proof of relationship; business travel may call for a company invitation, evidence of trade or work links and proof of employment status; medical travel may call for confirmation from the Swiss medical institution and proof of funds for treatmment. The Handbook adds an important practice point: a paid return ticket should not normally be required at lodgement,although reservations or equivalent travel planning may be requested and exceptional post-issuance confirmation may sometimes arise. 9

### Financial means, sponsorship and ties

Financial evidence matters in two different ways. First, it shows that the trip is properly funded. Secondly, it helps show stability and intention to return. The Handbook states that proof may include recent bank statements, credit-card statements, traveller's cheques, salary slips, certificates of employment, proof of sponsorship or private accommodation, or prepaid hotel reservations. It also stresses that evidence of intention to leave is often tied to socio-economic stability in the country of residence,such as employment, financial position, family links and property. Swiss law allows the Swiss authorities to require a declaration of sponsorship from a solvent natural or legal person domiciled or established in Switzerland where sufficient means need to be demonstrated; the undertaking can also be requested at the border in some cases. SEM likewise explains that a declaration of sponsorship may be required if the applicant lacks sufficient funds or if there is doubt on that point. 10

### Translation, legalisation and local format issues

Neither the Visa Code nor Swiss public guidance creates a universal rule that every supporting document must always be translated or legalised.The Commission Handbook instead states that authentication, legalisation or apostillation should be required only in individual and exceptional cases, although it may become systematic locally if a particular document type is frequently falsified and all consulates in that location follow the same practice. Local Swiss checklists then show how this plays out in practice: Swiss representations in China require some employer letters in English or in Chinese with English translation; some notarial family-relationship and parental-consent documents must carry an apostille; and certain local household-registration documents are accepted without translation. This is a strong example of why later drafting must distinguish general law from local evidential practice. 11

## Primary legal texts

### Visa Code

The principal legal source is Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas. For documentary requirements, the key provisions are Articles 10 to 15, Article 19, Article 21, Article 32 and Annex II. Article 10 sets the minimum components of a lodged application. Article 11 governs the form. Article 12 sets the travel-document validity rules. Article 13 governs photograph and fingerprint capture, including the 59-month re-use rule and exemptions for children under 12. Article 14 defines the four core documentary categories for a uniform visa: purpose, accommodation, means, and intention to leave. Article 15 governs travel medical insurance. Article 19 draws the line betwveen admissibility and inadmissibility. Article 21 governs substantive examination, including risk assessment and authenticity checks. Article 32 governs refusal, including doubts about documents and intention to leave. Annex II gives the non-exhaustive examples of supporting documents by journey type. These

<!-- 7 -->

provisions form the backbone of Swiss short-stay evidential analysis because Swiss short-stay visa procedure is tied directly to Title III of the Visa Code. 34

### Schengen Borders Code

Regulation (EU) 2016/399, the Schengen Borders Code, matters because the Visa Code's documentary assessment is expressly linked to the external-border entry conditions. Article 6 sets the conditions for a short stay of no more than 90 days in any 180-day period and requires, in substance, a valid travel document, a valid visa if required, justification for the purpose and conditions of stay, sufficient means of subsistence, absence of relevant SIS alerts and no threat to public policy, internal security, public health or international relations. Visa Code Article 21 makes those entry conditions central to visa examination, so Swiss documentary analysis fora Type C visa is not confined to consular formalities; it is anticipatory border-entry analysis. 35

### Swiss Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration

At Swiss federal level, the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act is relevant because it frames entry requirements and the Swiss allocation of visa powers. Article 5 requires foreign nationals entering Switzerland to hold a recognised identity document and visa where required, possess necessary financial means, not threaten public security or international relations, and provide a guarantee of departure if only a temporary stay is planned. Article 6 provides that visas are issued by Swiss representations abroad or by authorities designated for that purpose. These provisions matter because they show that, from the Swiss domestic-law perspective as well, financial means and temporary-stay credibility are basic entry predicates. 36

### Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance

For Swiss implementation, the Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance is the critical supplement. Article 12 states that the conditions and procedures for short-stay and airport-transit visas are governed by Title III of the Visa Code and supplemented by Articles 13 to 19 of the Ordinance. Article 13 refers fingerprint collection for short-stay visas to the Swiss VIS regime. Articles 14 to 16 govern the Swiss declaration of sponsorship: when it may be requested, who may act as guarantor, what costs it covers, its amount, duration and examination by cantonal or communal authorities. Article 17 requires proof of travel medical insurance in accordance with Visa Code Article 15, subject to specified exemptions. Article 18 allows other equivalent guarantees, including Swiss bank guarantees. These provisions are the main Swiss black-letter additions to the Schengen evidential scheme for Type C visas. 37

### Other primary legal material relevant to this pack

No separate Swiss primary-law text on documentary translations, invitation letters or local checklist content was found in the public materials reviewed for this pack. Those matters are handled chiefly through Visa Code guidance, local Schengen cooperation and Swiss representation practice materials rather than through a single Swiss statute or ordinance provision. Appeal-cost guidance in the materials reviewed is given by SEM with reference to Article 63 of the Swiss Administrative Procedure Act,but the consolidated primary text of that Act was not separately examined for this pack. 38

## Official guidance and administrative materials

Swiss official guidance is unusually useful for turning the legal framework into operational evidential expectations. SEM's visa-form page confirms the central role of the Schengen application form for short stays and distinguishes it clearly from the national Type D form; it also notes that local-language

<!-- 8 -->

variants may exist on representation websites. The one-page SEM factsheet on entry to the Schengen area / visa procedure summarises, in official Swiss language, that applicants should enclose their travel document, proof of health insurance and any other documents supporting the purpose of the visit, and that the embassy or consulate decides whether a declaration of sponsorship is needed. 39

SEM's public entry guidance further restates the evidential structure in border-law terms. It says that a visa-required third-country national must hold a valid C or D visa or a Schengen residence permit, must have a recognised travel document satisfying the ten-year / three-month criteria, must provide evidence of the purpose and circumstances of stay, must have sufficient financial means or lawful access to them, and must not fall within SIS refusal or public-order/security grounds. That guidance is useful because it aligns Swiss public-facing explanations with the Visa Code and Borders Code. 40

The invitation-letter factsheet is especially important for internal drafting. SEM explains that the letter may be requested during the visa process or on entry, that it should be written by the prospective host, ordinarily in a Swiss official language, and that it should include host/applicant identification details, duration of stay, date and signature. It can mention that the host will bear costs, but SEM expressly says that the invitation letter does not amount to proof of a binding financial commitment. That makes it strong purpose-and-host evidence, but weaker than a formal sponsorship undertaking for poroving means. 29

The declaration-of-sponsorship factsheet is the main Swiss administrative source on financial guarantees. SEM describes it as an official document proving that third-country nationals have sufficient financial means for Switzerland, explains that it may be requested by a Swiss embassy or consulate if own funds are insufficient or doubtful, identifies the categories of permitted guarantor, and states that the guarantee covers sickness, accident, return transport and living costs up to CHF 30,000. It also notes that approval of the guarantee does not in itself create an entitlement to a visa. 30

At EU level, the European Commission's 2024 Visa Code Handbook I is the most useful official interpretive source for evidential issues. It is not itself a binding legislative act, but it supplies operational guidance of high value. It explains that supporting documents exist so that authorities can assess entry conditions and possible immigration/security risk; that harmonised lists are locally adapted; that paid return tickets should not normally be demanded at lodgement; that recent bank statements with movement over a period of time are a common way to prove means; that proof of employment, family ties and property are relevant to intenntion to leave; that authentication or apostille should generally be exceptional; and that document authenticity, statement reliability and overall coherence must be assessed carefully. 41

VIS-related official guidance also matters to documentary analysis. The Commission's VIS materials explain that the system exists to facilitate the visa procedure, prevent visa shopping, support fraud detection and assist checks at borders and in the territory; the Commission's VIS overview also states that data are kept in VIS for five years. That matters because prior applications, previous refusals, and earlier biometrics remain visible to decision-makers and can affect the credibility assessment of a nnew file. 42

## Swiss representation and consular practice notes

The clearest official Swiss representation material reviewed comes from the Swiss representations in China. The China "Application for Schengen visa and procedure" page links purpose-specific checklists for business, tourism, family/friends and medical travel, identifies VFS as the filing channel, notes the standard 15-day processing period with possible extensions, states that biometrics are generally

collected every 59 months from first recording, and states that refusal reasons are communicated on a standard form with a 30-day appeal period. These points are largely procedural and explanatory rather than creating new legal rules, but they are reliable evidence of how the Swiss network operationalises the Visa Code in that jurisdiction. 43

The China family/friends checklist is useful for evidential detail. It requires the visa form, passport photograph, passport, insurance, invitation letter, financial-guarantee material where relevant, proof of relationship with the sponsor, recent bank statements, employer material and copies of earlier Schengen visas and residence permits. It also shows local-format practice: some family and minor documents must be in notarial form and apostilled; employer letters may require English or English translation; and the checklist warns that additional documents may still be requested and that submission of the listed documents does not guarantee visa issuance. This is evidential and procedural practice, not black-letter law of universal application. 44

The China tourism, business and medical-treatment checklists show the same point in other factual settings. Tourism requires round-trip reservation, accommodation proof and a travel programme; business requires business licences, employer letters, an invitation from the Swiss-side organiser, and in some cases a work permit where training is "on the job" or work in the applicant's own company is planned; medical treatment requires a Swiss clinic or doctor letter confirming treatment details and that treatment costs have already been covered in advance. These documents are plainly purpose-specific elaborations of the Article 14 / Annex II structure. 45

Representation guidance in the United Kingdom provides a useful biometrics note. It states that if fingerprints were collected within the last 59 months, new fingerprints are usually unnecessary and personal appearance is not needed, but also warns that fresh appearance may still be required if prior fingerprints are insufficient or if the representation wants a personal interview after file review. That is an important operational qualification for later drafting. 18

The South Africa photo-requirements sheet is a further example of representation-level technical practice. It gives detailed dimensions and image-quality criteria consistent with ICAO-style standards: recent photo, 35-40 mm width, close-up head-and-shoulders framing, neutral background, clear eyes and neutral expression. Again, the legal significance is procedural and evidential rather than legislative, but these are the sorts of requirements that defeat otherwise adequate applications if overlooked. 46

### Practical legal analysis

In Swiss Type C practice, documentary sufficiency is less about accumulating paper than about producing a coherent, testable narrative that answers every question implicit in Articles 14 and 21 of the Visa Code. A good file therefore tells one story across multiple evidential channels: who the applicant is, what exactly they will do, where they will stay, who pays, why the timing makes sense, why the documents are genuine, and why they will leave on time. The law itself points towards this holistic method because it links the supporting-document categories to border-entry conditions and to an explicit risk assessment of illegal immigration and timely departure. 47

That is also why invitation letters and itineraries should be described carefully in later drafting. A host invitation can be powerful evidence of purpose, personal connection and accommodation arrangements, but SEM expressly treats it as a reason-for-visit document rather than as a legally binding assumption of financial liability. Likewise, travel reservations are evidential rather than constitutive: the Handbook says a paid return ticket shouldnot normally be required when lodging the application. Internally, that means later content should resist the temptation to turn such documents

<!-- 10 -->

into absolute prerequisites. They are important because they make the applicant's account plausible, not because they are magic forms. 48

Financial evidence is often the evidential hinge of the whole case. It does double work: it demonstrates present ability to fund the trip and it also helps reveal socio-economic stability in the country of residence. The Handbook's emphasis on bank statements shoowing movement over time is particularly important. A file showing only a late lump-sum credit is weaker than one showing stable salary history, consistent balances and lawful funding arrangements. Where the applicant relies on a host or sponsor, the Swiss declaration of sponsorship is stronger than a vague promise in an invitation letter because Swiss law defines who may guarantee, what is covered and up to what amount. But even a valid Swiss sponsorship does not automatically cure every other weakness: SEM expressly says that approval of the guarantee does not create a right to a visa. 49

Ties to the country of residence remain one of the most sensitive areas of evidential credibility.Annex II and the Handbook identify return reservations, proof of financial means in the country of residence, employment, property and family ties as relevant to intention to leave. In practice, these documents should be read cumulatively. Stable employment with approved leave, dependent family members remaining at home, an existing residence status, prior compliant travel, and a short logically timed itinerary all reinforce each other. Conversely, an invitation to stay with a friend,weak banking history, vague itinerary, missing leave approval and no evidence of settlement in the country of residence will often be read together as a credibility problem even if each individual gap seems arguable on its own. 50

Translation, notarisation and legalisation require especially careful later drafting. The most defensible legal statement is not that they are always required, but that they may be required depending on the jurisdiction, document type and local fraud environment. The Handbook says apostille/legalisation should generally be exceptional, yet local practice can become stricter where fraud patterns justify it. Swiss representation checklistsin China show exactly that dynamic, with apostilled notarial family documents for some applicants and English translations for some employer documents. For internal drafting, the safest position is to distinguish between the baseline legal rule and the representation-specific evidential practice that may apply in the applicant's country of residence. 11

Credibility is also affected by prior applications and digital records. The Visa Code requires consultation of the VIS in each case, and the Commission's VIS materials emphasise that the system is designed to facilitate the visa procedure, prevent visa shopping and fight fraud. The Handbook notes that, where an earlier refusal is recorded in VIS, the consulate may contact the Member State that took the earlier decision to understand the underlying circumstances. Since VIS data are retained for five years, a prior refusal, problematic explanation or earlier biometric record is not an invisible historical fact. For internal content generation, this means that reapplications should be analysed against the reality that previous documentary weaknesses are often reviewable by the next decision-maker. 51

The immediate refusal ground most closely tied to documentary weakness is Article 32. It squarely covers doubts about authenticity, truth of contents, reliability of statements and intention to leave, in addition to straightforward failures like lack of insurance. For strategy, that often matters more than whether the published checklist was technically complete. An applicant may have "every document" and still fail because the documents do not make sense together; equally, a technically incomplete file may survive if the missing item is peripheral and the core story is credible. Where refusal is plainly driven by curable evidential defects, a carefully rebuilt reapplication may be more practical than arguing an appeal on the existing record, although Swiss law preserves the 30-day appeal route. 52

## Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

·Do not reduce Swiss Type C documentary assessment to a generic checklist; the governing legal test is whether the evidence proves the border-entry conditions, the purpose and conditions of stay, sufficient means, and timnely departure. 47

·Do not state that a complete set of listed documents guarantees visa issuance. Swiss representation checklists expressly say the opposite. 33

·Do not say that an invitation letter itself proves financial sponsorship or creates a binding obligation; SEM says it does not. 29

·Do not say that a Swiss declaration of sponsorship is always required; it is a tool that may be required where own funds are insufficient or doubtful. 53

·Do not present paid return tickets as a universal filing requirement; the Commission Handbook says they should not normally be required at lodgement. 54

·Do not universalise one embassy's local checklist as if it were the law for all jurisdictions; local Schengen cooperation and fraud conditions matter. 55

·Do not say that every foreign document must always be legalised or apostilled; the safer rule is that this is generally exceptional unless local conditions justify stricter practice. 31

·Do not say that previous biometrics always remove the need for personal appearance; representations may still require new fingerprints or an interview. 56

·Do not treat bank balance alone as enough. Movement over time, source of funds, employment evidence and broader stability are all relevant. 57

·Do not omit minor-specific evidence where relevant, including parental consent and guardianship/family-status material. 58

·Do not ignore prior refusals or prior visa history in later drafting; VIS retention and consular consultation can make old credibility issues visible again. 59
