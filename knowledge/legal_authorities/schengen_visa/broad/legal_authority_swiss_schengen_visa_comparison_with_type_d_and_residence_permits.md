# Swiss Visa and Residence Route Comparison

## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration routing is built on a basic division between short-stay Schengen mobility and Swiss domestic residence permission. A **Type** C **Schengen** **visa** is the ordinary instrument for stays of **up** **to** **90** **days** in **any** **180-day** period for purposes such as tourism, private visits, business, sports or cultural participation, and short study attendance. An **airport** **transit visa** **Type** **A** is narrower:it authorises only passage through the **international transit** **area** of an airport and does **not** authorise entry into Switzerland or the Schengen territory. A **Type** **D** **national** visa is the entry route for **stays** **exceeding** **90days,** especially for gainful employment, education or vocational training, and family reunification. **Swiss residence** **permits** are not visas at all: they are the domestic stay titles issued by the competent cantonal authorities, ordinarily after the underlying residence admission has been approved. 1

For internal advisory work, the decisive routing questions are therefore: **what** is **the** **real purpose** of **stay,is there any gainful employment, will the stay exceed 90 days, is prior cantonal approval** **required,** and **is the person relying on a visa, a residence permit, or visa-free entry.** Swiss law further makes two especially important points. First, anyone who wishes to **work** in Switzerland requires a permit **irrespective** **of** **the** **duration** **of** **stay.** Secondly, for a stay in Switzerland of **more** **than** **90** **days,** a residence permit is required and, **in** **principle,** the residence application must be filed **before** **entry,** including for persons who are otherwise visa-exempt for long stays. 2

The practical distinction between **business** visits **and** **work** is therefore crucial. Swiss official guidance treats **business** as a Type C short-stay purpose, but separates **gainful employment** into the permit system. That means a short business meeting, conference, client visit or negotiations may fall within the Type C route, whereas hands-on service delivery, productive work, or remunerated activity usually triggers work authorisation questions, even if the stay is very short. For long-stay work or study, the route is not merely "apply for a different visa"; it is a **cantonal** **residence/work** **authorisation** **process** followed, where needed, by a D visa for entry. 3

Using the wrong route has both **procedural and** **substantive** consequences. A visa may be refused if the applicant does not justify the true purpose and conditions of stay, lacks sufficient means, or raises doubt about an intention to leave before expiry. Someone who enters for a temporary stay and then applies for a longer stay must, as a rule, **wait abroad** for the decision unless the canton permits them to remain. Unlawful stay and work without authorisation are penalised under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, and employers face separate sanctions for employing foreign nationals without a permit. A visa also does not create an automatic right of entry at the border. 4

## Primary legal texts

The primary legal architecture is a **Swiss-Schengen** **hybrid.** The FNIA itself says that Swiss provisions on the visa porocedure and on entry and exit apply only so far as the **Schengen** **Association** **Agreements** do not provide otherwise, and it expressly ties visa requirements to the EU **Visa** Code.For citation work, that means the controlling texts are often a combination of **Swiss** **federal statute** and **Schengen** **secondary** **legislation.** The official English translation of the FNIA is expressly stated to be **for** **information** **only** and to have **no** **legal force,** so any contentious point should ultimately be checked against the authentic Fedlex text in an official Swiss language. 21

## Official guidance and administrative materials

For operational advice, the most useful official material is the **SEM** **FAQ-Entry.** It states, in one place, the short-stay purposes for Type C, the long-stay purposes for Type D, the rule that a D-visa application is forwarded to the relevant **cantonal migration** **office,** the normal C-visa processing window, the fact that D-visa cases are handled by the cantons, the visa-free entry effect of Schengen residence permits, and the airport-transit definition that excludes entry into Switzerland. It is the best single administrative source for the routing analysis required here. 26

<!-- 5 -->

The **FDFA** **visa** **requirements page** gives the high-level public-facing taxonomy that Swiss representations use: Type C for stays up to 90 days in 180 days and Type D for longer stays, with the express warning that **gainful employment** additionally requires a **work** **permit.** The **SEM Visa** **Application** **Form** page reinforces the division by maintaining distinct forms for Schengen short stays and national Type D long stays "subject to authorisation". 27

On the residence side, SEM guidance states that anyone who **works** during the stay in Switzerland, or remains for **more** **than** **three** **months,** requires a permit, and that permits are issued by the **cantonal** **migration** **offices.** The SEM's residence-permit materials and the biometric permit page confirm the practical permit categories most relevant to this pack: L short-term residence, B residence, and C settlement. 8

For work, SEM and ch.ch guidance is especially useful because it separates the visa issue from the labour-market issue. SEM's "Working in Switzerland" pages say plainly that a foreign national cannot work without a permit; for non-EU/EFTA nationals, the admission system is quota-based and focused on highly qualified workers, with tests such as economic interest, labour-market precedence, and locally customary salary and conditions. ch.ch also makes the post-entry point clear: even where a worker has the relevant immigration clearance, they may have to register wwith the commune and cannot start work beforehand. 28

## Swiss representation and consular practice notes

Swiss representation pages are not substitutes for statute or ordinance, but they are very valuable evidence of **how** **the** **route** **is operationalised** in real cases. The United Kingdom pages, for example, show a mature consular structure with distinct purpose-based short-stay checklists for **business,study** **trip, tourist,** and **visit** categories; a general **15-day** Schengen processing rule; biometrics generally refreshed every **59** **months; and** **a** **30-day** appeal reference. Those pages are strong support for internal drafting that distinguishes between short-stay purposes even within the same Type C framework. 29

The UK national-visa materials are equally useful for long-stay routing. They provide separate **student,** **marriage,** and **dependant** checklists and state that the application is **forwarded to the competent** **cantonal authorities** for decision and that the representation may only issue the visa **after** **approval** boy those authorities. The same page gives a working estimate of 8-12 **weeks,** while SEM centrally says that one to several months should be expected depending on canton and purpose. 30

The airport-transit pages show howlocal representations add **jurisdiction-specific** **practice** **notes** without changing the substantive law. The UK page, for instance, says travellers resident in the UK should carry a **share** **code,** that a valid **UK** **visa** does not itself exempt the holder from Swiss airport-transit requirements, that a qualifying UK **eVisa/residence** **status** may do so, and that a routing through more than one Schengen airport may require a **Schengen** **tourist** **visa** because the traveller will cross the Schengen border. That is precisely the sort of point that should appear in an internal pack as a **practice** **note,** not as a universal rule. 31

For medical-treatment cases, representation checklists are particularly useful because theyshow the evidence expected for a **short-stay Type** **C** **medical route.** An official Swiss embassy checklist requires, among other things, a medical certificate confirming the need for treatment in Switzerland, confirmation from a Swiss hhospital that the treatment can be provided, proof of an appointment, proof of **advance** **payment,** proof of accommodation, and ordinary short-stay supporting material such as travel medical insurance and financial means. A UK representation also provides a **fast-track** **procedure** for serious urgent medical reasons. 32

### Practical legal analysis

The correct Swiss instrument depends first on the **short-stay** **/** **long-stay** **divide.** If the intended stay is within the Schengen short-stay framework, the analysis begins with Type C or visa-free short-stay entry rules. Once the stay exceeds **90** **days** in **any** **180-day** **period,** the legal centre of gravity moves out of the Schengen short-stay system and into the Swiss residence-permit regime. Swiss official guidance also makes clear that periods authorised under a residence permit or long-stay visa do not count towards the normal 90-in-180 short-stay allowance. 33

**For tourism** **and** **private** **visits,** the answer is comparatively straightforward. SEM lists tourism and visiting family or friends as classic **Type** C purposes, and the FDFA describes Type C as the vehicle for entry and stay of up to 90 days in 180 days. As with all short-stay analysis, entry remains conditional on purpose evidence, sufficient funds, and-where applicable-medical insurance. The route is therefore visitor-style and temporary, not residential. 13

The more difficult line is **business** **visits versus** **work.** Swiss official guidance expressly **listsbusiness** as a Type C purpose. That comfortably covers classic visitor-type activities such as meetings, conferences, negotiations and similar non-productive commercial presence. But Swiss domestic law simultaneously provides that anyone who wishes to **work** in Switzerland needs a permit, regardless of how long the stay is, and SEM guidance reiterates that a foreign national is not permitted to work without a permit. In other words, "business" in the visa taxonomy is not the same thing as "gainful employment" in the work-permit taxonomy. Short work engagements may therefore require a **work** **permit plus** **a** **short-stay** **entry** **route,** while long work engagements move onto the **D-visa** / **permit** route. 34

That distinction becomes sharper when nationality-specific visa rules are added. The SEM nationality guidance shows that some otherwise visa-exempt nationals must still obtain a visa for **gainful** **occupation** once certain thresholds are crossed, and in some categories even for work **not** **exceeding** **eight days.** SEM's work leaflet adds that, as a basic rule, transnational services of up to **eight days** **per** **calendar** **year** may be permit-exempt, but a visa may still be required and some sectors require a work permit **from** **the** **first** day. The practical lesson is that an internal opinion should never say "short work is fine on a business visa" without first separating **permmit,** visa, **sector, nationality, and** **duration.** 35

**For** **study,** Swiss guidance supports a two-track analysis. The SEM FAQstates that a Type C visa may be used for attending school or to study where the stay remains within the short-stay framework.The FDFA's public guidance also offers short-term language studies as a Type C example. But for education or vocational training as a **long-stay** purpose, the SEM FAQ says a **Type** **D** **visa** is required, and FNIA article 27 then supplies the substantive admission criteria: confirmation from the educational institution,suitable accommodation, adequate financial means, and fulfilment of the relevant personal and educational requirements. ch.ch adds the practical residence-permit material usually expected from students, including a study plan, proof of admission, a CV, and a declaration that the person will leave Switzerland at the end of studies. 36

For **medical treatment,** the short-stay route exists, but it is evidence-heavy. Consular medical-treatment checklists show that a **Type** **C** **medical application** is built around proof of need for treatment, a Swiss medical-provider confirmation, appointment evidence, advance payment, insurance, and proof of accommodation; urgent cases may be fast-tracked by the representation.For **longer** **treatment,** the relevant Swiss domestic legal basis is FNIA article 29, which permits admission for medical treatment provided financing and return are guaranteed. Read with the rule that stays longer than 90 days require a Swiss permit, the better internal view is that planned medical treatment exceeding the short-stay framework should be analysed as a **long-stay** **admission/residence** **case,** not simply as an extended visitor matter. That last step is an inference from the statute and the official short-stay medical materials, but it is a strong one. 37

**For** **prior** **cantonal authorisation,** the authorities are unusually clear. Long-stay residence permits are issued by the **cantons.** D-visa applications are forwarded by the Swiss representation to the competent **cantonal migration** **office** for examination, and the representation can issue the visa only after approval. For work, both SEM guidance and representation pages emphasise that the employer or applicant must proceed through the competent cantonal authorities. This means the real legal decision-maker in many long-stay cases is not the consulate but the cantonal migration and, where relevant, labour-market authority. 38

The distinction between **Type** **D** and a **Swiss** **residence** **permit** should therefore be stated with care. The D visa is an **entry** **authorisation** **for** **a** **long** **stay.** The residence permit is the domestic title that evidences the person's actual Swiss status, such as L, B, or C. Swiss law requires registration and permit formalities, and SEM's biometric-permit guidance confirms that the permit card indicates the holder's status and, when shown with a valid passport, enables visa-free travel within Schengen.Internal drafting should avoid collapsing these instruments into one another. 39

Finally, the **consequences** **of using** **the** **wrong** **route** are not merely administrative inconvenience. Under the Visa Code, a visa must be refused if the applicant does not justify the purpose and conditions of stay, lacks sufficient means, has already exhausted the authorised short-stay time, or gives rise to doubt about the authenticity of documents or the intention to leave on time. Under Swiss domestic law, a person who enters for a temporary stay and later seeks a longer stay generally has to wait **abroad** for the decision. Unlawful stay and working without authorisation are criminally punishable, and employers face separate liability for employing foreign nationals without a permit. Even where a visa is held, border entry may still be refused because a visa does not itself create an automatic right of entry. 4

### Drafting cautions and source list

When drafting internal advice, the first caution is to keep **four questions** **separate**: the person's **nationality/free-movement status, the purposeof** the stay, the **duration** of the stay, and whether there will be any **gainful employmment.** The FNIA itself limits its application where EU/EFTA free-movement arrangements provide otherwise, so a Swiss/EU/EFTA analysis and a third-country analysis should never be collapsed into a single proposition. 21

The second caution is not to confuse a **visa** with **a** **permit.** A Type C visa is a short-stay travel authorisation. A Type D visa is a long-stay **entry** visa. A Swiss residence permit is the domestic title that governs the actual stay in Switzerland. Likewise, do not confuse a visa with a **work** **authorisation**:Swiss law requires work permission independently of the visa question. 40

The third caution is to treat **"business"** as a visa-purpose label, not as a synonym for "lawful work without permit". Swiss official guidance admits business under Type C, but the same official framework insists that work requires a permit. If the factual matrix includes service delivery, productive output,

local remuneration, or the taking up of employment, the analysis should move immediately into work-authorisation territory. 41

The fourth caution is to preserve the official language of uncertainty where the authorities themselves use it. Two examples matter here: long-stay residence applications are, in **principle,** to be made before entry,including by long-stay visa-exempt nationals; and the **eight-day** service exception is only a basic rule, subject to visa rules and sectoral exceptions. Internal packs should therefore avoid categorical wording where the official material is qualified. 42

The fifth caution is to distinguish **law** **from** **consular logistics.** Representation pages are excellent evidence of local process, documentary expectations, VFS outsourcing, biometrics, local eVisa/share-code practice, and rough processing times, but they should not be elevated above the FNIA, the Visa Code, or the Borders Code. They are best cited as **practice** **notes** rather than as primary legal authority. 43

### Open questions and limitations

This pack is strong on the **core routing rules** and on current **SEM/FDFA operational guidance,** but it is not a substitute for checking the **authentic Fedlex text in an official Swiss language** where a point is genuinely contentious, because the English FNIA translation is expressly non-binding. It also does not attempt a country-by-country digest of every nationality-specific visa exception or every cantonal practice variation; those remain fact-sensitive and should be checked at file level. 47
