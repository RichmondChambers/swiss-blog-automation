# Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Permitted Purpooses and Short-Stay Limits Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

A Swiss Schengen visa type C is the Schengen short-stay visa used for intended stays in the Schengen area of no more than 90 days in any rolling 180-day period. In Swiss official guidance, it is the visa for short stays such as tourism, visits, short-term language studies and comparable short-duration travel; Swiss guidance also expressly states that a stay involving gainful employment requires a work permit in addition. Swiss law further distinguishes a short stay without gainful activity from gainful activity,and defines "gainful activity" broadly enough to include work that is usually remunerated even where it is done without pay. That is the central legal boundary: a type C visa is a short-stay travel authorisation under Schengen rules, but it is not, by itself, a work authorisation or a residence title. 1

The permitted purpose analysis is therefore two-layered. At Schengen level, the Visa Code governs the issuing of short-stay visas and the Schengen Borders Code governs border-entry conditions and the documents that may be required to justify the purpose and conditions of stay. Those instruments do not create a closed, exhaustive statutory list of "approved purposes"; rather, they regulate intended short stays and require the applicant or traveller to evidence the stated purpose. The supporting-document lists are expressly non-exhaustive. Border and visa materials expressly recognise business trips, study or other training, tourism or private reasons, and political, scientific, cultural, sporting or religious events as standard short-stay categories. Swiss official materials add concrete examples such as visits to relatives or friends, short-term language studies, conferences and sporting or cultural events. 2

For tourism and private visits, the legal position is straightforward: those are paradigm type C purposes, provided the trip remains temporary and the traveller can justify accommodation, itinerary, means and onward departure. For business trips, the position is more qualified. Schengen border rules clearly accept attendance at meetings, conferences, trade-related events, fairs and similar commercial contacts as business-trip purposes. However, Swiss immigration law separately requires authorisation for gainful activity regardless of duration, and cross-border service work is specifically regulated. In practice, a short business visit sits safely within type C territory only where the person is attending meetings or events, or otherwvise remaining on the "visitor" side of the line, rather than carrying out Swiss work requiring labour-market authorisation. Where the traveller will actually perform work or provide services in Switzerland, the legal analysis must move beyond the visa label to Swiss work-permit rules. 3

Short study and theoretical training can also fall within type C territory, so long as the stay remains within short-stay limits. Border rules expressly recognise journeys for study or other types of training and refer to enrolment at a teaching institution for vocational or theoretical courses. Swiss federal law separately provides for admission for education or training, and Swiss official guidance treats short-term language study as a type C example. Once the intended study ortraining moves beyond the short-stay framework, the analysis shifts to Swiss long-stay rules and uusually to a type D/national-visa and residence-permit route. 4

Medical treatment is an accepted Swiss admission purpose, but it is evidentially ddemanding.Swiss statute expressly provides that foreign nationals may be admitted for medical treatment if financing and re-departure are secured. Official Swiss consular materials for medical-treatment visas require evidence from a doctor, confirmation that treatment can be provided in Switzerland, proof of an appointment, proof of advance payment, and evidence of accommodation and funds. That makes medical type C cases legally viable, but also credibility-sensitive. 5

The short-stay limit is Schengen-wide and rolling, not country-by-country and not a fixed "90 days per trip". SEM's official calculator guidance states that third-country nationals may in principle enter the Schengen area for a maximum of 90 days within 180 days; the period is "rolling", assessed by looking back 180 days from any given day. Swviss law also makes clear that for stays without gainful activity no permit is needed only up to three months, and that where a visa grants a shorter authorised stay,that shorter grant controls. For States operating the Entry/Exit System, the Schengen Borders Code now states that the 90/180 calculatioon is treated as a single period for those States. 6

Finally, the practical lesson for later drafting is that type C purpose analysis is never only about labels. The authorities examine whether the stated purpose is genuine, whether the documents match it, whether the applicant has sufficient means, and whether there is a credible intention to leave before the visa expires. The Court of Justice in Koushkaki confirms that the visa examination includes the risk of illegal immigration and the apoplicant's intention to leave before expiry; Swiss law separately requires assurance of secure departure where only a temporary stay is planned. Future content should therefore avoid treating "tourism", "business", "study" or "medical treatment" as self-proving categories. The legal question is whether the trip remains a genuine, evidenced and temporary short stay,or whether it crosses into residence or work requiring additional authorisation. 7

## Structural overview of permitted purposes

### B. Structural overview of permitted purposes

**Legal** **nature** **of the** **type** **C** **visa.** The Visa Code establishes the procedures and conditions for issuing visas for intended stays not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. Swiss federal guidance describes the Schengen visa type C as the visa for entry and stay up to 90 days in a 180-day period, while the nationaI visa type D is for longer stays in Switzerland. The type C framework is therefore about temporary short presence, not settlement. 8

**Temporary** **nature** **and** **short-stay** **limits.** The Borders Code applies the 90/180 rule across the Schengen area, and SEM's official calculator guidance explains that the 180-day period is rolling and must be recalculated on every day of stay. This is a Schengen-wide cap, not a Swiss-only cap. If the visa sticker authorises fewer than 90 days, the visa grant is the operative limit for that visa holder. 9

**Tourism** **and** **private** **visits.** Tourism and private/family visits are classic type C purposes. Border rules expressly treat tourism and private reasons as recognised categories, and Swiss public guidance likewise identifies tourism, visits to relatives or friends and similar temporary stays as short-stay type C cases. The required evidence usually concerns accommodation, itinerary, invitation, relationship, finances and insurance. 10

<!-- 2 -->

**Business, conferences, fairs** **and** **events.** Border rules recognise business trips through invitations to meetings, conferences or events linked to trade, industry or work, documents showing trade or work relations, and tickets for fairs and congresses. As a matter of careful legal inference, that covers the ordinary business-visitor pattern, including meetings and negotiations, provided the activity remains a visit rather than Swiss gainful activity. If the traveller will actually work, provide services or otherwise engage in activity falling within Swiss "Erwerbstätigkeit", Swiss labour-authorisation rules become decisive. 11

**Cultural and** **sporting** **events.** Border rules also recognise political, scientific, cultural, sporting and religious events, with invitations, tickets, enrolments or programmes as supporting evidence.Swiss official guidance specifically mentions sporting and cultural events as examples of short-stay type C use. 12

**Short study** **and** **theoretical training.** The Schengen border framework recognises journeys for study or other training, including vocational or theoretical courses. Swiss law separately allows admission for education or training, and Swiss federal guidance expressly includes short-term language studies in the type C category.Longer study falls outside short-stay architecture and into national-visa/residence territory. 13

**Medical** **treatment.** Swiss statutory law expressly permits admission for medical treatment if financing and return are secured. Official Swiss representation material for medical type C cases requires medical evidence, Swiss-hospital confirmation, proof of appointment and proof of payment or funding. This is therefore a recognised purpose, but a purpose with especially heavy evidential demands. 5

**Work** **boundary.** Swiss law requires a permit for gainful activity irrespective of the duration of stay and defines gainful activity broadly, even including activity ordinarily done for remuneration if unpaid. Swiss visa law also states that a short-stay visa can be issued for a short stay "with or without work authorisation" in Switzerland, which shows that the visa and the work permission are separate concepts. For some visa-exempt nationalities and some sectors, Swiss ordinances impose visa or permit requirements from the first day of gainful activity, while other cross-border work is tolerated only up to the eight-day threshold. 14

## Primary legal texts

### D. Primary legal texts

The legal architecture for this sub-topic is best understood by moving from Schengen instruments to Swiss implementing law.

**Visa** **Code.** Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishes the procedures and conditions for issuing visas for intended stays on the territory of the Member States not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. Its

<!-- 6 -->

definitions distinguish short-stay visas from airport-transit visas. Article 14 requires documents indicating the purpose of the journey, accommodation, sufficient means, and information allowing an assessment of the applicant's intention to leave before visa expiry; the supporting-document list in Annex II is non-exhaustive. Article 21 links the visa examination to compliance with entry conditions and to the assessment of illegal-immigration risk and intention to depart. For Swiss type C analysis,these are the central Schengen issuing rules. 27

**Schengen** **Borders** **Code.** Regulation (EU) 2016/399 governs the entry conditions for third-country nationals for intended stays of no more than 90 days in any 180-day period. The person must hold a valid travel document and, if required, a visa; justify the purpose and conditions of stay; have sufficient means of subsistence; not be the subject of an SIS alert for refusal of entry; and not be regarded as a threat to policy, security, health or international relations. Annex I contains a non-exhaustive list of supporting documents for business trips, study or training, tourism/private reasons, and political/ scientific/cultural/sporting/religious events or other reasons. For States operating EES, the Code now treats the 90/180 calculation as a single period across those States. These rules matter because even a correctly issued visa does not displace border scrutiny of purpose and conditions. 28

**Swiss Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration.** The current consolidated AIG states that the provisions on the visa procedure and on entry and exit apply only insofar as the Schengen association agreements do not provide otherwise. Article 5 sets Swiss entry conditions: recognised travel document, visa or ETIAS authorisation if required, sufficient means, no security/order problem, and assurance of secure departure for temporary stays. Article 10 states that no permit is needed for a non-gainful stay of up to three months, unless the visa itself authorises a shorter period. Article 11 provides that anyone wishing to engage in gainful activity in Switzerland needs a permit regardless of the duration of stay, and defines gainful activity broadly enough to include ordinarily remunerated activity even if unpaid. Article 26 regulates admission for temporary cross-border services; Article 27 governs admission for education and training; Article 29 governs medical treatment and requires financing and re-departure to be secured. These are the core Swiss statutory provisions for purpose analysis. 29

**Swiss** **Ordinance** **on** **Entry and** **Visa** **Issuance.** The OEV states that it governs entry, airport transit and visa issuance, subject to Schengen association rules. Article 8 sets the Swiss short-stay visa requirement and creates Swiss-specific rules for gainful activity by certain otherwise visa-exempt nationals, including first-day or ninth-day visa obligations and stricter rules in listed sectors. Article 11 states that a short-stay visa is issued for a short stay in Switzerland with or without work authorisation. Article 12 imports the Visa Code's shoort-stay issuing rules into Swiss practice and supplements them with Swiss provisions on fingerprints, sponsorship, financial guarantees, travel medical insurance and fees. Articles 14 to 18regulate the Swiss declaration of sponsorship, its scope, the CHF 30,000 guarantee amount, and alternative bank guarantees. These provisions matter because they show how Switzerland operationalises Schengen short-stay law and, crucially, how it treats gainful activity as a distinct legal question. 30

**Swiss Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Gainful Employment.** The VZAE provides that foreign nationals without gainful activity do not need a permit fora stay of up to three months within six months after entry, but must continue to meet the entry conditions throughout that permit-free stay.It also requires registration if the purpose changes, and visa extension if departure within the visa period becomes impossible or another purpose is pursued. Article 14 sets the special Swiss rule for cross-border service provision or temporary work for a foreign employer: a permit is needed when the activity lasts more than eight days in a calendar year, while certain sectors require authorisation regardless of duration. These provisions matter because they show that the short-stay framework does not eliminate domestic controls on what the traveller actually does in Switzerland. 31

## Official guidance and Swiss consular practice

### E. Official guidance / administrative materials

Swiss federal guidance is consistent on the practical categories of type C use. FDFA's central visa page says type C is for entry and a stay of up to 90 days in 180 days and gives tourism, visits and short-term language studies as examples; it also states that a stay involving gainful employment requires a wwork permit. The SEM page on Schengen entry explains that Switzerland has been part of Schengen since 12Decemnber 2008 and describes the Schengen framework as facilitating short stays of up to 90 days in a 180-day reference period for tourists, visitors and business travellers. The official ch.ch portal says that, for stays of up to 90 days, the Schengen application form is used for tourism, family visits, study purposes and participation in cultural or sporting events, but if the person wishes to work in Switzerland a work permit must also be requested. Together, these materials are not legislation, but they are high-value official explanations of the purpose architecture. 32

SEM's official stay-calculation page gives strong administrative guidance on the short-stay limit. It explains that third-country nationals may generally enter the Schengen area for a maximum of 90 days within 180 days, that entry day counts as day one and exit day as the last day,and that the period is "sliding" or rolling. It also links to the official EU stay calculator. This guidance is especially useful for future drafting because it explicitly corrects the common errors of treating the rule as country-specific or as 90 days per journey. 33

At EU/Schengen level, the 2024 Visa Code Handbook is not binding legislation, but it is official Commission operational guidance for consular staff. It confirms that the common visa policy regulates visas for intended stays not exceeding 90 days in any 180 days period and that only the underlying legal acts have binding force. The 2022 Practical Handbook for Border Guards likewise confirms that Switzerland is one of the Schengen States for external-border purposes and that the handbook is guidance, not a source of new legal rights or obligations. These materials are particularly helpful when explaining practice-oriented points conservatively. 34

### F. Swiss representation / consular practice notes

Accessible official local Swiss representation material supports the general legal framework but must be treated as local evidential and procedural practice, not universal black-letter law.

For family or friends visits, the Embassy of Switzerland in Nepal's official checklist requires, among other things, a host invitation with the host's details, the invited person's details, duration of stay, relationship and purpose; a copy of the host's identity or residence permit; evidence of occupation; accommodation evidence; several months of bank statements; Schengen medical insurance; and flight reservation.It also states that the embassy may request additional material, verify authenticity, send the case to Swiss federal authorities for decision, and refuse on the basis of false, incorrect or incomplete declarations or forged documents. This is a strong official illustration of how private-visit cases are evidenced and scrutinised. 35

For medical treatment, the Embassy of Switzerlandin Saudi Arabia's official leaflet requires a medical certificate confirming the need for specific treatment in Switzerland, confirmation that the treatment can be provided by a Swiss hospital, proof of an appointment, proof of advance payment, accommodation evidence, and proof of financial means beyond the treatment itself. The same leaflet explains that VFS Global collects applications and biometrics, but the visa decision is taken by the Swiss Embassy only. Again, this is not global law, but it is probative of the evidential intensity of Swiss medical-purpose type C cases. 36

Two cautionary points follow from these materials. First, Swiss representation practiceis local and document-specific; one should not universalise a Nepal or Saudi checklist into a worldwide mandatory list. Secondly, official Swiss representation materials consistently reinforce that border authorities may recheck the traveller's situation and that completeness, authenticity and consistency of documents matter materially. 37

## Practical legal analysis

### G. Practical legal analysis

The most important analytical point is that "permitted purpose" under Swiss type C law is not a simple list of labels. The Schengen legal framework is built around temporary intended stays, with the applicant expected to prove the purpose and conditions of stay and the authority expected to assess whether the stated purpose is credible and compatible with the short-stay framework. That architecture is visible in Article 14 and Article 21 of the Visa Code, in Annex I to the Borders Code, and in Swiss statutory requirements that a temporary entrant have sufficient means and give assurance of timely departure. For internal drafting, this means that a purpose category never answers the case on its own; it is only the starting point. 38

Tourism and visiting family or friends are the least conceptually difficult categories because they match both Schengen and Swiss public guidance. The legal issues there are mostly evidential: accommodation, host details, relationship where relevant, itinerary, funds and insurance. Refusal risk usually arises not because those purposes are legally controversial, but because the evidence does not support the narrative or because the authority doubts the intention to depart. The Nepal checklist illustrates this point well: private-visit cases are document-heavy and credibility-sensitive even though the underlying purpose category is ordinary. 39

Business travel requires more careful drafting. On the one hand, the border rules clearly recognise meetings, conferences, fairs and work-related events as business-trip scenarios, so it would be wrong to say that a Swiss type C visa is "tourism only". On the other hand, Swiss domestic law requires authorisation for gainful activity irrespective of duration and defines gainful activity broadly. The result is that "business visit" and "work" are not interchangeable. A safe formulation is that type C supports attendance at meetings, negotiations, conferences, trade fairs and similar visitor-type business activity, but does not by itself authorise employment, service provision or other gainful activity in Switzerland. Where the activity is operational rather than purely visit-based, a separate Swiss labour-authorisation analysis is essential. 40

That boundary is further complicated by Swiss-specific work and visa rules for short cross-border activity. VZAE Article 14 uses an eight-day threshold for certain temporary cross-border services or temporary work for a foreign employer, while requiring authorisation from day one in sectors such as construction, hospitality, cleaning, surveillance/security, travelling trades, the sex industry and landscape gardening. OEV Article 8 and its annexes overlay visa consequences for listed visa-exempt nationalities ingainful-activity scenarios. The internal drafting consequence is that any statement such as "visa-exempt nationals can do short work trips without a visa" is too crude unless it is very carefully qualified by nationality, sector, duration and whether a notification or permit is required. 22

<!-- 9 -->

Short study and training require a similar distinction between short-stay presence annd longer-term educational residence. The Schengen short-stay framework plainly contemplates study and other training, including theoretical courses, and Swiss guidance explicitly includes short-term language study in type C usage. But Swiss AIG Article 27 is a broader admissions basis for education and training generally, and longer or residence-like study falls outside type C architecture. Safe drafting should therefore say that short courses, language stays and comparable temporary training may sit within type C if the 90/180 ceiling and short-stay character are respected, whereas longer studies ordinarily move into national-visa and residence-permit analysis. 13

Medical treatment is legally available but unusually stringent in proof terms. Swiss statute requires secured financing and re-departure, and official medical checklists require treatment-need evidence, hospital acceptance, appointment confirmation and proof of payment. In practical terms, this means medical-purpose type C cases should boe drafted as a coherent bundle: medical necessity, Swiss treatment availability, funding, accommodation and post-treatment departure. The purpose is permissible, but the evidential burden is higher than for leisure travel. 5

The "non-residence" character of type C should also remain explicit. A short-stay visa is for temporary presence within the Schengen 90/180 framework. Swiss law exempts non-gainful stays of only upto three months from permit requirements, and Swiss guidance places stays above 90 days into national-visa territory. Even where a person holds a type C visa lawfully, it does not convert the stay into residence, and it does not allow the traveller to remain indefinitely by recasting the purpose after arrival. VZAE requires registration or extension processes where the purpose changes or departure within the visa period becomes impossible. 41

## Drafting cautions

### H.Drafting cautions

·Do not describe the purpose categories for type C visas as a closed statutory list. The supporting-document lists in the Visa Code and Borders Code are expressly non-exhaustive. 24

·Do not say that a Swiss type C visa is only for tourism. Official Swiss materials also recognise visits, business travel, short-term language study and participation in cultural or sporting events. 32

·Do not say that "business travel" automatically permits work. Swiss law requires authorisation for gainful activity regardless of duration. 42

·Do not imply that unpaid activity is automatically outside work-permit rules. Swiss law defines gainful activity broadly enough to include work usually done for remuneration even when unpaid. 20

·Do not flatten meetings, negotiations, fairs and conferences into "employment". They are recognised short-stay business-trip scenarios, but only so long as the activityremains on the visitor side of the Swiss work boundary. 43

·Do not state that visa-exempt nationals can always undertake short work in Switzerland without a visa or permit. Swiss rules for gainful activity by certain visa-exempt nationals and sectors are more restrictive and sometimes start from day one. 44

·Do not describe the 90/180 rule as 90 days per trip or per country. It is rolling and Schengen-wide. 45

·Do not forget that the visa sticker may authorise fewer than 90 days. In that case the authorised visa duration governs. 46

<!-- 10 -->

·Do not describe short study or short training as automatically requiring a type D visa. Short courses may fit within type C; the decisive issue is whether the stay remains a true short stay within the 90/180 framework. 47

·Do not describe type C as a residence permit or as suitable for long-term settlement. Swiss official guidance reserves type D for longer stays. 16

·Do not present medical-treatmnent cases as ordinary tourist cases. Swiss law and official practice require secured financing and re-departure plus medical and hospital evidence. 5

·Do not omit the credibility assessment. The authority must assess the reality of the purpoose, sufficient means, immigration risk and intention to leave before visa expiry. 48

·Do not treat local embassy checklists as universal worldwide law. They are official local practice materials and may vary by post. 49

#### Open questions / limitations

No single official Swiss source was found during this research that exhaustively classifies every borderline "business visitor" scenario against "gainful activity" for all fact patterns. The legal materials clearly establish the broad work-authorisation rule and the recognised Schengen business-trip category, but difficult cases such as technical hands-on work, intra-group assignments, unpaid productive activity or hybrid conference/service roles still require fact-specific analysis against Swiss labour-authorisation rules and, where relevant, cantonal practice.
