# Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Legal Framework and Schengen Architecture

## Executive legal summary

A Swiss Schengen visa of type C sits within the common Schengen short-stay framework, not a standalone Swiss-only visa regime. Switzerland is not an EU Member State, but it is a **Schengen-associated** **state.** By the EU-Swiss Schengen Association Agreement, Switzerland is associated with the implementation, application and development of the Schengen acquis; Swiss official material correspondingly describes the Schengen area as covering most EU Member States plus the four associated states, including Switzerland, and notes that Swiss participation has been operational since 12 December 2008. The result is that the core law on short-stay visas for Switzerland is primarily Schengen/EU law as applied through Switzerland's association arrangements, supplemented by Swiss federal legislation and ordinances. 1

The central EU instrument is the **Visa** **Code.** In its current form it governs the procedures and conditions for issuing visas for transit through, or intended stays in, the territory of the Member States not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. The Visa Code defines a visa as an authorisation for an intended stay not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period or for airport transit; it also distinguishes a **uniform visa** valid for the whole Schengen area, **a visa with limited territorial validity,** and an **airport** **transit** **visa.** Swiss law mirrors that architecture in the Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance, which defines type C, type A and type D visas and expressly subordinates Swviss regulation to any conflicting provisions in the Schengen Association Agreements. 2

For Swiss practice, the critical distinction is between **type** C, **type** A, **and** **type** D. Swiss official visa guidance says that type C is for entry and stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period, type D is for longer-term stays in Swvitzerland of more than 90 days, and airport transit visas concern passage through the international transit area without entry into Switzerland. A type C visa is therefore a short-stay entry permit within Schengen law; it is **not** a residence permit and does not by itself amount to authorisation for longer-term residence in Switzerland. 3

Visa issuance and border admission are legally distinct. The Swiss representation abroad generally issues or refuses a short-stay visa, while actual admnission at the external border remains subject to the **Schengen** **Borders** **Code.** The Visa Code itself emphasises that mere possession of a visa does **not** confer an automatic right of entry. Official Swiss entry guidance likewise states that visa-requiring third-country nationals must still satisfy border-entry conditions, including possession of a valid travel document, proof of purpose and conditions of stay, sufficient means, and absence of SIS alerts or security objections.4

Territorially, a normal Swiss-issued type C visa is generally valid throughout the Schengen area unless the visa itself shows limited territorial validity. Swiss official guidance states that category C visas are generaly valid for all Schengen states, including Switzerland, unless otherwise noted. That is why a Swiss Schengen visa normnally allows onward travel across Schengen; however, it does not displace the "competent state" rules at the application stage. Under the common rules, the competent state is the sole destination, or, for multi-state travel, the main destination; if no main destination can be determined, responsibility falls to the first state of entry. 5

The short-stay limit is **Schengen-wide** **and rolling,** not country-by-country. Official Swiss and European Commission guidance states that visitors are usually allowed a maximum of 90 days within any 180-day period across the Schengen area, counting back 180 days from each day of stay; holders of long-stay visas or residence permits are notsubject to this short-stay rule in the same way for the residence authorised by those documents. This is a structural feature of Schengen law, not a Swiss local counting rule. 6

Swiss implementation is principally found in the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration** and the **Ordinance** on **Entry** **and** **Visa** **Issuance.** The FNIA states the entry requirements, confirms that visas are issued by Swiss representations abroad, and provides a written objection route against refusals of visas for permit-free stays. The Swiss ordinance then links short-stay entry conditions directly to Article 6 of the Schengen Borders Code, sets out Swiss-specific visa duty rules and exceptions,and supplements the Visa Code on matters such as declarations of sponsorship and fingerprints. Swiss official guidance also makes clear that the SEMM does **not** itself issue visas; Swiss representations abroad do so, whereas type D applications feed into cantonal residence-permit processes. 7

From an internal drafting perspective, the safest description is therefore that a Swiss type C visa is a **Schengen short-stay entry permit processed by Switzerland under the common Schengen visa** **acquis** **and** **Swiss** **implementing** **law.** It may enable travel across the Schengen area, but it does not guarantee admission at the border, does not authorise residence beyond the short-stay limit, and does not authorise gainful employment unless a separate work authorisation applies. 8

## Structural overview of the legal framework

### Legal nature of the type C visa

Under the Visa Code, a visa is an authorisation for an intended stay of no more than 90 days in any 180-day period or for airport transit. Swiss implementing legislation defines a **type** **C** **Schengen** **visa** as a document issued by a Schengen state certifying that the holder meets the conditions for a short stay; it may be uniform for all Schengen states or territorially limited. Swiss official guidance describes type C as the visa for short stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period. 9

### Switzerland's Schengen position

Switzerland participates in Schengen by association, not by EU membership. The association agreement expressly associates Switzerland with the implementation, application and development of the Schengen acquis. Swiss official sources accordingly describe the Schengen area as including EU states plus the four associated states and record Swiss operational participation since 12 December 2008. Internal drafting should therefore describe Switzerland as a **Schengen-associated** **state,** not an EU Member State. 1

#### Visa requirement and exemptions

The underlying Schengen rule on who needs a short-stay visa is set by Regulation (EU) 2018/1806, which determines the third countries whose nationals are subject to, or exempt from, the visa requirement. Swiss law implements that structure through the Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance and official SEM nationality lists. Swiss official guidance also states that holders of a valid Schengen residence permit or

a valid type D visa,together with a recognised travel document, are exempt from the short-stay visa requirement for Switzerland. Swiss rules additionally contain important gainful-employment qualifications for some otherwise visa-exempt nationals. 10

### Competent state and place of application

Under common Schengen rules, the competent state is the state of sole destination; for multi-state trips it is the state of the main destination, usually the longest stay; and if equal stays make that indeterminate, the first country of entry becomes competent. The application must generally be lodged with the consulate having territorial responsibility for the place of lawful residence. Swiss official guidance and Commission guidance are aligned on those points. Representation arrangements between Schengen states can shift the practical receiving post, but not the underlying legal framework. 11

### Borders, territorial validity and national visas

A standard Swiss category C visa is generally valid throughout the Schengen area unless it is marked as territorially limited. Type A airport transit visas are limited to transit through the international airport transit area and do not amount to entry into Switzerland. Type D national visas are for Swiss stays exceeding 90 days and are tied to Swiss national admission procedures, usually including cantonal authorisation; the Commission's border guidance likewise notes that long-stay visas and residence permits for visits exceeding three months remain subject to national conditions. 12

### Swiss administrative and IT architecture

The SEM's own guidance states that the SEM does not issue visas; Swiss representations abroad issue or refuse them. For short-stay refusals, Swiss law places the refusal on a standard form on behalf of the SEM or FDFA, and Swiss guidance provides for a written objection to the SEM within 30 days. VIS supports the exchange of visa data between Schengen consulates and border points, while EES now records short-stay entries and exits electronically and detects overstays. ETIAS, by contrast, is a future travel-authorisation layer for visa-exempt travellers and is not yet in operation. 13

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

### Primary legal texts

**The Agreement between the European Union, the European Community and the Swiss** **Confederation on the Swiss Confederation's association with the implementation, application and** **development** of **the Schengen** **acquis** is the constitutional gateway for Swiss participation. Article 1associates Switzerland with the activities of the EU and European Community in the fields covered by

<!-- 5 -->

the Schengen acquis and its further development. This matters because it explains why Schengen visa and border instruments, although EU instruments, form the backbone of Swiss short-stay visa law. 27

**Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas** is the core short-stay visa instrument. Article 1 sets the scope: procedures and conditions for issuing visas for intended stays not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. The definitions distinguish the visa itself, the uniform visa, the limited territorial validity visa, and the airport transit visa. As a matter of architecture, this is the main source for what a type C visa legally is. 28

**Regulation (EU) 2016/399, the Schengen Borders Code,** supplies the entry architecture. Article 6 lays down the entry conditions for intended stays of no more than 90 days in any 180-day period; official EUR-Lex and Commission summaries identify the familiar elements: valid travel document, visa if required, justification of purpose and stay conditions, sufficient means, no SIS alert for refusal of entry, and no threat to public policy, security, public health or international relations. Its importance is that visa issuance never exhausts the admission inquiry. 29

**Regulation** **(EU) 2018/1806** identifies which third-country nationals are visa-required and which are visa-exempt for short stays. Swiss visa architecture depends on this Schengen-wide list, subject to Swiss implementation and Swiss-specific exceptions for gainful activity. 30

**Regulation (EC) No 767/2008 on the Visa Information System** establishes VIS. Its purpose includes facilitating visa procedures, preventing forum shopping, combating fraud, facilitating checks at external borders, and helping identify persons who no longer fulfil entry or stay conditions. This matters because biometric and data-sharing aspects of Swiss short-stay visa practice sit within this common Schengen infrastructure rather than a purely Swiss database architecture. 31

**Regulation (EU) 2017/2226 on the Entry/Exit System** underpins the new border-record architecture for short stays. The Commission's current official EES material states that EES registers non-EU nationals travelling for short stays, records entries, exits and refusals of entry, and as of 10 April 2026 replaced passport stamping for participating states, enabling automatic overstay detection. For Swiss analysis, that materially changes evidential and compliance visibility at the external border. 32

At Swiss statutory level, the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (FNIA/AIG/LEI), SR** **142.20,** is the primary domestic law. Article 5 sets Swiss entry requirements; article 6 provides that visas are issued by Swiss representations abroad and that refusals for permit-free stays are issued on a standard form on behalf of SEM or FDFA; article 6(2bis) provides for a written objection within 30 days; article 7 states thhat border crossing and border controls are governed by the Schengen Association Agreements; article 10 addresses permit-free stays without gainful employment of up to three months; and article 11 states that work in Switzerland requires a permit irrespective of duration.These provisions supply the core Swiss domestic architecture around the Schengen rules. 33

The **Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance (VEV/OEV), SR 142.204,** is the main Swiss implementing ordinance. It states that the ordinance governs entry, airport transit and visa issuance, subject to the Schengen Association Agreements; it defines short stay, long stay, airport transit, type C,type A and type D visas; it ties short-stay entry conditions to article 6 of the Schengen Borders Code; it identifies visa duty for short stays by reference to Regulation 2018/1806; it provides special visa-duty rules for gainful activity; it gives the basic domestic route for long-stay and airport-transit visa duty; and it states that procedures and conditions for issuing short-stay and airport-transit visas follow Title III of the Visa Code, supplemented by Swiss ordinance provisions such as fingerprints and declarations of sponsorship. 34

For appeals, the **Federal Act on Administrative Procedure (APA/VwVG/PA), SR 172.021** is central. Article 50 sets a 30-day appeal period after notification of a ruling, and article 63 governs procedural costs and cost advances. The English text is explicitly informational only and not legally authoritative, but it is still a useful official translation for internal orientation. 35

The **Federal Administrative Court Act (VGG/LTAF/TAF Act), SR 173.32** provides the general jurisdictional rule that the Federal Administrative Court hears appeals against administrative decisions within the meaning of the APA. In visa matters, that supports the representation practice note that a negative SEM objection decision can be followed by an appeal to the Federal Administrative Court. ## Official guidance and administrative materials

Swiss official guidance is unusually useful for architecture because SEM and FDFA pages clearly draw the line between short stays and long stays. SEM's FAQ states that a category C visa is for stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period and is valid for the Schengen area; category D is for stays exceeding 90days; and persons intending to pursue gainful employment require both a valid Schengen visa anda work permit. FDFA's central visa page says the samne in slightly different terms and adds that type D issuance is subject to cantonal migration-authority authorisation. 37

On jurisdiction, the European Commission's current "Applying for a Schengen visa" page is the clearest official summary. It states that one applies to the consulate of the country intended to be visited, or, for several Schengen countries, the country of longest stay, or if stays are equal the first country to be visited; and as a generaI rule the application must be made to the consulate with territorial responsibility for the country of lawful residence. SEM's FAQ says materially the same. 38

On border architecture, the European Commission's "Border crossing" page explains that the Schengen Borders Code contains the common rules on external border checks, entry requirements and duration of short stays, while the Visa Code harmonises visa issuance. SEM's "Entry with visa" page then translates those entry conditions into Swiss-facing guidance for travellers. Read together, those sources give the clearest structural picture of why visa issuance and border admission must not be conflated. 39

On counting rules, the Commission's short-stay calculator page and SEM's duration-of-stay FAQ are the practical official sources. Both confirm that short stay means a maximum of 90 days within any 180-day period and that the counting exercise is rolling. The Commission further notes that long-stay visas and residence permits are not entered into the calculator because those longer authorised stays are not governed by the short-stay cap in the same way. 40

On smart-border systems, the Commission's VIS, EES and ETIAS pages are the key official administrative materials. VIS connects consulates and border crossing points and facilitates checks and fraud prevention; EES now records short-stay entries and exits and automatically detects overstayers; ETIAS is still not operational and is expected to begin only in the last quarter of 2026. Swiss SEM guidance on EES points in the same direction and expressly says EES automatically calculates duration of stay and replaces manual passport stamping. 41

## Swiss practice notes and practical legal analysis

### Swiss representation and consular practice notes

Swiss representation websites are useful for **procedure** **and** **evidence,** but they are not all-purpose statements of black-letter law. The UK representation page explains the mechanics of representation agreements between Schengen states: if a state lacks its own visa section in a country or region, another Schengen state may handle applications under a representation agreement. That is best treated as an official **procedural/practice** **explanation,** not the source of the competence rule itself. 42

Swiss representation pages also help on biometrics. Representation guidance in the UK, Singapore, Canada and the United States states that Schengen visa applicants generally provide biometric data, that VIS stores thosedata for five years, and that if fingerprints were previously collected within 59months, personal appearance may not be necessary in a later applicatioon. These are reliable official practice points, but they should still be described as **operational** **implementation** **of** VIS **rules,** not as the core legal definition of the visa.43

An official Swiss Embassy note from New Delhi sets out the objection route after refusal, annulment or revocation: appeal to SEM in an official Swiss language, 30 days from notification, CHF 200 advance under APA article 63, and, if SEM confirms the negative decision, a further appeal to the Federal Administrative Court within 30 days. Because this is representation-specific explanatory material rather than a central SEM legal circular, it is best treated as an **official practice** **note** **consistent** with **the** **statutory** **architecture,** rather than as the sole authority for the appellate path. 44

### Practical legal analysis

In practice, Switzerland applies short-stay visa law through a layered structure. At the top are the Schengen acquis instruments themselves: the Visa Code for visa issuance, the Schengen Borders Code for admission at the external border, Regulation 2018/1806 for visa-required and visa-exempt nationalities, and the VIS/EES/ETIAS instruments for the data and travel-authorisation architecture. Switzerland applies those rules because it is bound through the Schengen association framework, while domestic Swiss law fills in institutional competences, permit interfaces and certain national supplements. 45

That layered structure matters because many recurring drafting errors come from treating a Swiss type C visa as though it were a purely Swiss nationaI permission. It is not. A Swiss representation may issue the visa, but the visa's territorial logic is Schengen logic. That is why a Swiss-issued category C visa will normally permit movement throughout Schengen unless marked otherwise, and why the stay cap is counted on a Schengen-wide basis. At the same time, the visa remains only a permission to seek entry subject to border checks, not an irrevocable admission decision. 46

The distinction between **type** **C** **and** **type** **D** is equally important. Type C is built for temporary short stays; type D belongs to the national long-stay sphere and is tied to Swiss residence authorisation procedures. Swiss official material makes plain that stays beyond 90 days require a residence permit and that type D applications are channelled through cantonal migrationauthorities. Internal drafting should therefore resist any language suggesting that a type C visa can be "converted" into residence as a routine matter or that it performs the same legal function as a long-stay visa or residence permit. 47The work boundary is another high-risk area. Swiss law separates a permit-free stay without gainful employment from work, which requires authorisation irrespective of duration. Swiss public guidance repeats that those entering to pursue gainful employment need both the appropriate entry document and a work permit. In addition, Swiss visa-duty rules contain special work-related visa requirements for some otherwise visa-exempt nationals. The safest internal approach is therefore to treat "business visit", "short stay" and "work authorisation" as distinct categories unless the underlying authority clearly overlapos them. 48

The IT systems now materially affect the operational environment. VIS means visa history and biometric data are available to Schengen consulates and border officers for verification and fraud control. EES, now fullyoperational, replaces passport stamps with electronic records of entries, exits and refusals of entry for short-stay travellers and automatically detects overstays. ETIAS, however, should still be described cautiously as a future requirement for visa-exempt travellers, because the official position remains that it is not yet in operation and will begjin only in the last quarter of 2026. 26

### Drafting cautions, open questions and source list

### Drafting cautions

Do not describe Switzerland as an EU Member State when explaining the source of Schengen visa law; describe it as a **Schengen-associated** **state** applying the Schengen acquis through association agreements. 49

Do not describe a Swiss category C visa as a residence permit, settlement status, or immigration permission for stays beyond the short-stay framework. It is a short-stay Schengen visa; longer stays move into type D and residence-permit territory. 47

Do not describe a visa as conferring unconditional entry. Border admission remains subject to the Schengen Borders Code entry conditions, and mere possession of a visa is not enough on its own. 50

Do not write the 90/180 rule as "90 days per country", "90 days per trip" or "90 days per six calendar months". It is a **rolling** **Schengen-wide** calculation. 40

Do not collapse type A, type C and type D into one generic "Swiss visa" category. Airport transit, short-stay Schengen travel and long-stay national residence serve different legal functions. 51

Do not assume visa-exempt nationals are free from Schengen legal constraints. They still remain subject to entry conditions, the short-stay cap, and, in some Swiss work scenarios, special visa-duty rules. 52

Do not suggest that ordinary employment is permitted on a type C visa alone. Swiss law requires work authorisation irrespective of the period of employment. 53

Do not omit the competent-state and territorial-responsibility rules when explaining where to apply. Main destination and place of lawful residence remain central structural concepts. 38

Do not state ETIAS commencement as though it were already live. The official position, as at 30 April 2026, is that ETIAS is **not yet operational** and is expected in the last quarter of 2026. 54

## Open questions and limitations

This pack is strongest on **legal architecture.** It does not attemnpt a full refusal-ground analysis under the Visa Code, a full evidential-document matrix by travel purpose, or a full review of Swiss Federal Administrative Court visa case law. Those topics would usually justify separate packs. 55

Some Swiss legislative materials were most accessible through official translations or mirror copies hosted via Refworld or similar databases. Those translations themnselves state that English is **for** **information** **purposes** only and that the legally operative Swiss texts are the German, French and Italian versions. Any publication-grade legal drafting should therefore verify article wording against the official Swiss-language Fedlex text current on the date of use. 56

The further appeal route from SEM to the Federal Administrative Court is strongly indicated by official Swiss representation material and the general jurisdiction provisions of the Federal Administrative Court Act, but I have treated it conservatively as a well-supported procedural point rather than a fully exhaustively traced visa-specific route through every possible statutory cross-reference. 57
