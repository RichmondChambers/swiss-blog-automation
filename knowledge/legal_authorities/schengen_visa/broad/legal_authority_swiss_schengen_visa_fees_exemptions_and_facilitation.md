# Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Fees, Exemptions and Facilitation Arrangements

## Executive legal summary

For Swiss Schengen visas for stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period, the core fee rules are set primarily by the Schengen Visa Code, which Switzerland appliesas a Schengen-associated state, and secondarily by Swiss implementing law and Swiss consular practice. The present fee architecture is therefore not a purely domestic Swiss construct: the principal fee amounts, the basic categories of mandatory and optional fee waivers, and the rules on external-service-provider charges are set at Schengen level, while Swiss law and Swiss representations govern how those amounts are collected in practice, including local-currency payment and post-specific administrative handling. 1

As of the current framework, the standard Schengen visa fee is EUR 90 for applicants aged 12 and over, and EUR 45 for children aged six to below 12. Children under six are exempt from the visa fee. The European Commission confirmed that these amounts have applied since 11 June 2024, when the general Schengen visa fee rose from EUR 80 to EUR 90 and the child fee rose from EUR 40 to EUR 45. 2

The Visa Code also creates mandatory fee waivers for a defined set of categories. These include children under six; school pupils, students, postgraduate students and accompanying teachers travelling for study or educational training; researchers travelling for scientific research or a scientific seminar or conference; and certain young representatives of non-profit organisations participating in qualifying seminars, conferences, sporting, cultural or educational events. In addition, the current Commission handbook summarising the Visa Code framework treats non-EU family members of qualifying EU citizens and of Swiss citizens as falling within a zero-fee category in the relevant privileged-family context. 3

Beyond those mandatory exemptions, the Visa Code allows further optional waivers. Member States may waive the fee for children aged six to below 18, for holders of diplomatic and service passports, and for participants aged 25 or less in qualifying non-profit events. Member States may also waive or reduce the fee in individual cases where this serves cultural or sporting interests, foreign-policy or development-policy interests, other vital public interests, humanitarian reasons, or international obligations. These are not automatic entitlements for every applicant in the category;they depend on how the competent State applies the discretion permitted by Article 16(5) and 16(6).4

For family members benefiting from Schengen free-movement privilege, the fee issue must be analysed together with the submission channel. The Commission handbook states that non-EU family members benefiting from the privileged regime must be able to lodge directly at the consulate without cost, and cannot be forced to use a charged external provider. Swiss representation pages in practice reflect this by stating that family members of Swiss, EU or EFTA citizens may submit directly at the responsible Swiss embassy or consulate; some Swiss fee pages also expressly state that visas for family members of Swiss or EU/EFTA nationals areissued free of charge. This matters because a service provider may still levy a handling fee where the applicant voluntarily uses that channel or where the post-specific system operates through an outsourced centre. 5

External service-provider fees are a separate category from the visa fee itself. Under Article 17 of the Visa Code, a service fee may be charged by an external service provider, but ordinarily it must not exceed half of the basic visa fee, irrespective of visa-fee waivers or reductions. The consolidated Visa Code also allows higher ceilings in certain third countries where the competent State has no consulate and is not represented by another State, in principle up to EUR 80 and exceptionally up to EUR 120. Swiss representation practice confirms that additional VFS or similar service fees may be charged on top of the visa fee; some Swiss posts expressly note that such a fee remains payable even where the underlying visa is free of charge. 6

Visa fees are normally charged in euro, in the national currency of the third country, or in another currency usually used in the place of application. Swiss representation pages consistently state that the amnounts are payable in the currency determined by the Swiss representation, that they are due when the application is lodged, and that they may be adjusted in line with exchange-rate fluctuations. Post-specific Swiss fee tables then show the operative local-currency equivalents. 7

As a rule,the visa fee is not refundable. That remains true if the applicant withdraws the case or the visa is refused. The principal exceptions are where the consulate turns out not to be competent after lodgement, or where the application is inadmissible in the relevant procedural sense; in those situations the visa fee must be reimbursed. The Commission handbook adds an important practical point: if the application was lodged through an external provider at the wvrong consulate, the service fee is not reimbursed even though the visa fee is. 8

Finally, visa facilitation agreements remain relevant, but they must be handled carefully in Swiss work. Switzerland states through SEM that, because of Schengen participation, it concludes visa facilitation agreements with countries that have already concluded similar agreements with the EU, in order to preserve harmonised visa practice. SEM lists Swiss agreements with Armenia, Azerbaijan, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Georgia, Moldova, Russia, Serbia and Ukraine, while Fedlex records that the Swiss-Russia agreement was fully suspended in 2022. Official Swiss fee tables also show a reduced-fee "visa according agreement VFA" or country-specific reduced-fee category. However, facilitation agreements only matter where the person still requires a visa; they do not override later visa-waiver arrangements for visa-free biometric passport holders. 9

## Structural overview of fees and exemptions

## Governing instruments

The governing hierarchy is straightforward. The Visa Code sets the common Schengen fee framework, including the standard fee, reduced child fee, mandatory and optional waivers, refund rule, and service-fee rules for external providers. Swiss law then implements collection at national level, including by providing that short-stay and airport-transit visa processing is fee-bearing under Article 16 of the Visa Code and the Swiss fee ordinance. Official Swiss representation pages repeatedly identify the Visa Code together with the Swiss Ordinance on fees under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals as the instruments governing Schengen visa fees. 10

### Standard fee architecture

The standard Schengen visa fee is EUR 90, with EUR 45 for children aged six to below 12, and no visa fee for children under six. Those amounts are the baseline and apply unless displaced by a mandatory waiver, an optional waiver or reduction, a facilitation agreement, or the special aggravated-fee

mechanism linked to readmission cooperation under Article 25a. Official Swiss fee tables convert the EUR amounts into local currency at the relevant post. 11

## Mandatory waivers and reductions

Mandatory zero-fee categories include children under six,school/study-related groups, researchers, and certain young representatives of non-profit organisations. The current Commission handbook also places non-EU family members of qualifying EU citizens and Swiss citizens within a zero-fee line in the privileged-family context. The only mandatory reduced fee in the standard architecture is the EUR 45child fee for ages six to below 12. 3

## Optional waivers and individual-case reductions

Member States may additionally waive fees for children aged six to below 18, diplomatic and service passport holders, and young participants in qualifying non-profit events. They may also waive or reduce the fee in individual cases for public-interest, cultural, sporting, humanitarian, foreign-policy, development-policy or international-obligation reasons. These points should be described as discretionary or qualified, not as universal entitlements. 4

## Local currency and service fees

The Visa Code permits charging the visa fee in euro or local currency, with regular review using the ECB exchange rate; Swiss representation pages state that amounts are payable in the currency determined by the representation and may be adjusted for exchange-rate fluctuations. External service-provider fees are legally distinct from the visa fee. They are ordinarily capped at half of the basic visa fee, though special higher ceilings exist in certain representation-gap cases. Swiss consular pages confirm that outsourced channels may add a VFS fee. 12

## Facilitation agreements

SEM states that Swiss visa facilitation agreements are used to harmonise Swiss short-stay visa practice with EU arrangements. Those agreements typically simplify documents,lower or remove fees for certain categories, speed up processing and improve access to multiple-entry visas. Swiss official material lists signed agreements and official Swiss fee tables reflect reduced-fee agreement categories where a visa is still required. 13

## Primary legal texts

### Regulation establishing the Schengen Visa Code

The principal legal source is Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas, in its current consolidated form. Article 16 is the core fee provision. It sets the standard fee at EUR 90,the reduced fee at EUR 45 for children aged six to below 12, and states the mandatory fee waivers and the categories for optional waivers or individual-case reductions. It also provides that the fee may be charged in euro or local currency and is generally non-refundable except in the specified competence and admissibility situations. These are the controlling rules for Swiss Type C fee analysis because Switzerland applies the common short-stay visa policy as a Schengen-associated country. 26

<!-- 7 -->

Article 17 of the Visa Code governs the separate service fee charged by an external service provider under an outsourcing arrangement. It requires the service fee to be proportionate, ordinarily caps it at half of the basic visa fee, preserves special higher ceilings in certain representation-gap scenarios, and requires that applicants retain a possibility of direct lodging at the consulate. For Swiss practice this matters because many posts use outsourced visa-application centres, but the external service fee is not the same as the legal visa fee and must be kept conceptually distinct. 27

## Refund and competence provisions under the Visa Code framework

The refund rule sits across Article 16(7) and the competence/admissibility provisions. Article 16(7) states the basic non-refund rule, while the Commission handbook explains its interaction wvith misfiled applications: if the consulate discovers after lodgement that it is not competent, the visa fee must be reimbursed, but any service-provider fee is not.This is especially relevant to Swiss practice where applicants may incur both a visa fee and a VFS-style handling charge. 28

## Swiss Ordinance on Entry and the Issuance of Visas

At Swiss level, the Ordinance on Entry and the Issuance of Visas (VEV; SR 142.204) is the key implementing ordinance for entry and visa procedure. Article 19 states that a fee is charged for processing an application for a short stay or airport-transit visa under Article 16 of the Visa Code and under the Swiss fee ordinance. For present purposes, its importance is that it formally connects Swiss Type C processing to the Schengen fee architecture and to the Swiss fee ordinance. 29

## Swiss Ordinance on Fees under the Foreign Nationals legislation

Swiss representation pages identify the Ordinance on fees under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals/ Foreign Nationals and Integration legislation (GebV-AIG; SR 142.209) as the domestic fee instrument used together with the Visa Code. An official Fedlex amending text also reproduces Article 1 of that ordinance, showing that it applies to decisions and services under the AIG, the Free Movement Agreement, the EFTA Convention, the Schengen association agreements, and the UK acquired-rights agreement. That matters because Swiss fee collection and local-currency implementation are not based on the Visa Code alone; they sit within a broader domestic fee framework. 30

## Free Movement Agreement context for family-member fee privilege

For privileged family-member cases, the crucial treaty context is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU. The Commission handbook's Swiss-specific operational section explains that Swiss consulates use Annex I to that agreement to define family members and to grant "every facility" in visa issuance; it also states that fammily members of EU citizens defined by Article 3(2) of Annex I to the AFMP are exempt from paying the visa fee under Swiss national legislation, and that such applicants are not required to produce proof of personal means, invitations, sponsorship or accommodation in the ordinary way. This is central to Swiss fee analysis because it explains why family-member cases are structurally different from ordinary short-stay applications. 31

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The Commission's 2024 Visa Code handbook is the most useful official guidance document for fee analysis. It consolidates the current fee table, distinguishes mandatory from optional waivers, explains local-currency calculation, records the general non-refund rule, describes reimbursement where the

wrong consulate receives the application, explains family-member privileged processing, and includes a dedicated operational section for Swiss consulates applying the AFMP. 32

The Commission's 13 June 2024 notice is important for current drafting because it confirms that, from 11 June 2024, the baseline fee rose to EUR 90 and the child fee to EUR 45, while Cabo Verde's fee under the relevant EU facilitation agreement rose to EUR 67.50 and fees under other EU facilitation agreements remained EUR 35. This notice is not itself the legal text, but it is a useful official confirmation of the current operative amounts. 33

SEM's visa facilitation page is the key Swiss official guidance on facilitation arrangements. It states that visa facilitation agreements simplify short-stay visa issuance and may include shorter processing times, lower or waived fees for specified categories, and multiple-entry visas; it also states that Switzerland concludes such agreements with countries that have already concluded similar agreements with the EU, to ensure harmonised Schengen handling. 24

The Council's official infographic usefully complements SEM by describing the typical content of visa facilitation agreements at EU level: simplified supporting documents, reduced or no fees for certain categories, faster processing, and multiple-entry visas of longer validity. It also records current EU-level suspensions, which is useful context when checking whether a facilitation arrangement still has operative effect. 34

## Swiss representation and consular practice notes

Official Swiss representation pages consistently state that Schengenn visa fees are governed by the Visa Code together with the Swiss fee ordinance, that the amounts are payable in the currency determined by the representation, that payment is due when the application is submitted, and that the fee is not refunded if the application is withdrawn or refused. That is a reliable statement of Swiss administrative practice across posts. 35

Post-specific fee tables then show how the general rules operate in practice. The official Swiss India fee PDF lists local INR equivalents for the adult fee, child fee, a reduced "visa according agreement VFA" category pegged to EUR 35, and an Article 25a-aggravated adult category pegged to EUR 135. The official Swiss U.S. fee page lists local USD amounts and, on that page, expressly identifies free categories including children under six and certain family members of Swiss and EU/EFTA nationals, and a reduced-fee category for nationals of Armenia, Azerbaijan, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Georgia, Moldova, Serbia and Ukraine. These pages are useful practice evidence, but they remain post-specific and should not be elevated over the Visa Code. 36

On submission channels, the official Swiss representation pages for the United Kingdom and Lebanon state that family members of Swiss, EU or EFTA citizens are entitled to submit directly at the responsible embassy or consulate. That reflects the privileged-family rule and is important because direct submission may avoid outsourced handling charges that would otherwise arise. 37

On service-provider fees, official Swiss representation pages also show the limits of generalisation. The Swiss Canada page states that VFS Global charges an additional service fee for visa applications processed through the visa acceptance centre. The Swiss Egypt page is even more explicit: it states that even where the visa itself can be issued free of charge, the external-service-provider fee must still be paid, and gives a post-specific handling fee figure. These are best understood as local procedural practice, not universal substantive law. 38

### Practical legal analysis

In Swiss Typoe C work, fee analysis should start from the premise that thne decisive legal architecture is Schengen-wide, not purely Swiss. The main fee amounts and waiver categories come from Article 16 of the Visa Code, while Swiss instruments mostly perform an implementing and collecting function. That means later drafting should ordinarily cite the Visa Code first for entitlement questions and then use Swiss sources for how the fee is collected, converted into local currency, or operationalised at the relevant post. 39

The most common analytical error is to collapse three distinct issues into one: whether a person needs a visa at all, whether a person who still needs a visa must pay the standard fee, and whether a person using an outsourced appointment channel must pay an extra service fee. Those are different questions. A family member of a qualifying Swiss, EU or EFTA national may still need a visa if visa-exempt documentation is absent, but in the privileged channel the visa should generally be fee-free; if that same person voluntarily uses a charged external provider instead of the direct-consulate route, a service charge may still arise. Conversely, a national covered by a facilitation agreement may pay a reduced fee but still need the visa and may still incur a service fee. 40

A second recurring risk is over-stating waivers for minors. The current hard rule is not that all children are free. The legally clear structure is: under six are free; six to below 12 pay the reduced EUR 45 fee; and six to below 18 may be waived only if the competent State chooses to exercise the optional power in Article 16(5). Later content should therefore never say that "minors" as a whole are automatically fee-exempt. 41

Local-currency drafting also needs caution. Swiss representations do not usually restate the euro-denominated legal architecture in every page; instead they publish local-currency tables updated according to exchange-rate movements. The correct legal statement is therefore that the underlying fee is a euro-denominated Schengen fee collected in the local currency determined by the Swiss representation, not that the USD, INR or EGP figure is itself the autonomous legal fee. 7

Wrong-State or wrong-post filing has fee consequences and should be flagged in drafting. If the wrong consulate receives the application and this is discovered after lodgement, the visa fee should be returned, but any outsourced service fee is ordinarily lost. In practical terms, that makes competence analysis important not only for jurisdiction and refusal risk, but also for cost exposure. 20

Facilitation agreements need the most nuanced treatment. Switzerland's SEM makes clear that the country uses facilitation agreements to mirror EU arrangements and maintain harmonised Schengen practice, and official Swiss fee tables still show reduced-fee facilitation categories. But many of the historically covered countries later obtained broader visa-waiver arrangements for biometric passport holders. The correct drafting approach is therefore conditional: where a visa is still required for a nationality or passport type covered by a facilitation agreement, the agreement may reduce the fee and simplify procedure; it should not be assumed that the agreement is the applicant's operative regime without first checking whether the traveller is already visa-free. 42

## Drafting cautions

·Do not state that a Swiss Schengen visa fee is "set by Switzerland alone"; for Type C visas the controlling fee architecture is Schengen-wide, with Swiss law implementing and collecting it. 14

·Do not say that all minors are automatically fee-free. Children under six are mandatorily free; ages six to below 12 pay EUR 45; waivers for ages six to below 18 are discretionary. 43

<!-- 10 -->

·Do not treat service-provider fees as part of the visa fee itself.They are distinct, separately regulated charges. 21

·Do not imply that a visa-fee waiver automatically eliminates a VFS or other external-provider fee. Some Swiss posts expressly say the service fee still applies unless the applicant uses the direct-consulate route available in privileged cases. 44

·Do not describe children, service-passport holders, or young event participants as universally exempt without checking whether the exemption is mandatory or merely optional under Article 16(5). 4

·Do not present facilitation-agreement countries as automatically paying a reduced visa fee in every case. First check whether the traveller is already visa-exempt, and then whether the relevant Swiss facilitation agreement is in force and applicable. 45

·Do not say the application fee is refundable if the visa is refused or withdrawn. The default rule is non-refundability. 46

·Do not ignore competence errors: if the wrong consulate is used, the visa fee may be reimbursed but the service fee may still be lost. 20

·Do not freeze local-currency figures in generic content without dating them and tying them to the competent Swiss representation. Exchange-rate adjustments are part of the official practice. 47

·Do not generalise post-specific free categories such as CERN or UN official-trip treatment into universal black-letter law unless a natioonal source is identified. Treat such items as consular practice unless independently anchored in primary law. 48
