# Swiss Schengen Visa 90/180-Day Rule and

# Overstay Risk

## Executive legal summary

A Swiss Schengen Type C visa, and visa-free short-stay travel to Switzerland, sit inside a Schengen-wide short-stay regime rather than a Switzerland-only counting system. The core legal rule is that a short stay may not exceed 90 days in any rolling 180-day period. That standard appears in the Visa Code for short-stayvisas and in the Schengen Borders Code as a general entry-condition rule for third-country nationals seeking stays of no more than 90 days. Swiss domestic law does not replace that rule; instead, Swiss legislation and the Swiss visa ordinance expressly defer to the Schengen Association Agreements where Schengen law governs visa procedure and entry.

The counting rule is rolling, notcalendar-based. For every day of presence, the authorities look backwards over the preceding 180 days and count how many days of stay have already been used in the Schengen area. The day of entry counts as the first day of stay and the day of exit counts as the last. Periods spent under a residence permit or a long-stay visa are excluded from that arithmetic. Swiss official guidance mirrors the Schengen rule and explains it in the same way, including for visa-free travellers. 2

The practical consequence is that all Schengen short-stay days count together; they are not calculated separately for Switzerland, France, Italy or any other Schengen state. A multiple-entry visa does not create a fresh 90-day allowance on each trip. It merely allows repeated entries within the visa's validity, subject to the same cumulative cap. If the visa sticker authorises fewer than 90 days, that lower visa-specific limit controls. Swiss official calculator examples expressly treat several entries under a multiple-entry visa as one cumulative short-stay calculation. 3

The rule applies both to visa-required travellers and to visa-exempt travellers. Swiss SEM guidance states that the short-stay calculator is for third-country nationals in the Schengen area regardless of visa-obligation status, and the official EES material applies the same 90/180 framework to travellers with short-stay visas and to travellers who do not need a visa. 4

As of 10 April 2026, the Entry/Exit System is fully operational across the Schengen countries using it, includingSwitzerland. The EES electronically records short-stay entries, exits and refusals of entry, includes an automated calculator of remaining authorised stay, and automatically identifies overstayers. That materially reduces reliance on passport stamps and increases the visibility of previous overstays for future border checks and immigration decisions. 5

Overstay risk has twvo layers. First, there is the arithmetic layer: if a person has already used 90 days in the relevant 180-day window, a fresh visa must be refused under the Visa Code and entry may be refused at the external border because the Schengen entry conditions are no longer met. Secondly, there is the Swiss enforcement layer: Swiss law provides for removal orders where a foreign national lacks the required authorisation or no longer fulfils the entry conditions, criminalises unlawful stay, and allows SEM to impose entry bans, in particular where the person has not left within the prescribed period or has been punished for offences including unlawful stay under Article 115 FNIA. 6

The short-stay calculator is useful but not determinative. Both the European Commission's official calculator and the Swiss SEM calculator describe themselves as technical aids whose results do not themselves confer a right to stay. Border and migration authorities remain able to reach a legally different conclusion in an individual case. 7

Finally, exceeding the 90/180-day limit does not create a single universal Schengen penalty rule saying that re-entry is impossible for a fixed number of months. The arithmetic position may later improve as older days fall outside the rolling 180-day window, and Swiss official examples show that re-entry may again become mathematically possible after enough time has passed. That does not erase the original overstay, however. The overstay can still remain visible in EES and can still support Swiss or other Schengen enforcement consequences such as removal, fines or monetary penalties, detention under national law where applicable, future visa refusal, or entry bans under Swiss law. 8

### Structural overview of the 90/180-day rule

**Legal basis.** The rule is anchored at Schengen level. The Visa Code defines a short-stay visa as an authorisation for intended stays not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. The Schengen Borders Code uses the same formula as the key entry condition for third-country nationals seeking short stays. Swiss domestic law and the Swiss visa ordinance operate subject to the Schengen Association Agreements, so Swiss practice follows the Schengen rule rather than a separate national counting regime. 1

**Rolling** **reference** **period.** The 180-day period is not fixed by calendar half-years. It is a "moving" or rolling reference period assessed backwards from each day of stay. Swiss SEM guidance states expressly that a retrospective check is made on any given day to determine how many days the person has spent in the Schengen area during the previous 180 days. 9

**Schengen-wide** **counting.** The rule counts short-stay days across the Schengen area as a single pool, not country by country. The official EES FAQ states that the 90 days in any 180 days are calculated as a single period for all European countries using the EES, and the list of those countries includes Switzerland. As of 10 April 2026, EES is fully operational across thoose countries. 10

**How days** **are** **counted.** Entry day and exit day both count. Periods of stay authorised under a residence permit or a long-stay visa are excluded. Swiss SEM guidance repeats the same counting method on its calculator pages. 11

**Multiple-entry** **vrisas.** A multiple-entry visa does not authorise 90 days per trip. Under Article 24 of the Visa Code, a uniform visa may allow one, two or multiple entries, but the period of validity is separate from the authorised stay; where a visa is valid for more than six months, the permissible stay remains 90 days in any 180-day period. Swiss SEM's official example on multiple enntries applies the cumulative cap across several visits. 12

**Visa-free** **and** **visa-required** **travellers.** The same arithmetic governs both categories. Swiss SEM states that the calculator is intended for visa-required and visa-exempt third-country nationals alike, and EES guidance does the same. 4

**EES** **interaction.** EES records short-stay entries, exits, refusals of entry and certain biometric data, contains an automated calculator of remaining authorised stay, and automatically identifies records with no exit following expiry of the authorised period and other overstays. Overstay data are retained longer than ordinary compliant travel histories. 13

**Overstay** **consequences.** If the 90/180 cap is exhausted, a visa must be refused and entry can be refused at the border. If the person nevertheless remains unlawfully in Switzerland, Swiss law provides for removal orders, criminal liability for unlawful stay, and possible entry bans. Official EES guidance also warns that national systems may impose removal, detention, fines, or future re-entry consequences. 14

## Primary legal texts

**Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas.** For this pack, the most important provisions are Article 2(2)(a), Article 24 and Article 32(1)(a)(iv). Article 2(2)(a) defines a visa for an intended stay notexceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. Article 24 distinguishes visa validity from authorised stay, allows one, two or multiple entries, and makes clear that where a visa is valid for more than six months, the duration of stays remains 90 days in any 180-day period. Article 32(1)(a)(iv) makes exhaustion of the 90/180 entitlement an express visa-refusal ground. These provisions matter because they show that the 90/180 cap is not merely a border-control concept; it is also embedded in visa issuance and refusal. 27

<!-- 4 -->

**Regulation (EU) 2016/399, Schengen Borders Code.** Article 6(1) sets the entry conditions for short stays, using the same 90/180 formula. Article 6(2) supplies the crucial mechanics: entry day is the first day of stay, exit day is the last, and residence-permit or long-stay-visa periods are excluded from the short-stay calculation. Article 14 provides that a third-country national who does not fulfil all Article 6(1) conditions shall be refused entry. In current consolidated text, Article 6(1a) also confirms that the 90-day period is calculated as a single period for the Member States operating EES; because EES is now fully operational across those countries, this is the operative present-day framework. These provisions matter because they convert the arithmetic into an enforceable border condition. 28

**Regulation (EU) 2017/2226 on the Entry/Exit System.** Article 3 defines an overstayer as **a** third-country national who no longer fulfils the short-stay time conditions. Article 11 requires EES to include an automated calculator of maximum authorised stay. Article 12 requires EES automatically to identify records without exit data immediately after authorised stay expires and to generate a list of identified overstayers for competent national authorities. Article 13 creates a web service for travellers to check remaining authorised stay. Article 34 sets retention periods: compliant exit-linked records are generally retained for three years, but if there is no exit record following expiry of authorised stay, the data are stored for five years after expiry. These provisions matter because they turn what used to be stamp-based approximation into structured, searchable border data. 29

**Swiss Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration.** Article 2(4) states that the provisions on visa procedure and on entry and exit apply only insofar as the Schengen Association Agreements do not provide otherwise, which is the key Swiss domestic deference clause. Article 5 lays down Swiss entry requirements. Article 64 requires removal orders where the person lacks a required permit or no longer satisfies the entry conditions. Article 64d sets the ordinary departure deadline at seven to thirty days, with accelerated enforcement in specified cases. Article 67 empowers SEM to impose entry bans on removed foreign nationals, including where they did not leave by the deadline or were punished for conduct under Article 115. Article 115 criminalises unlawful stay, including remaining in Switzerland after expiry of a permit-free or authorised stay. These provisions matter because they are the Swiss enforcement bridge between the Schengen short-stay rule and Swiss migration sanctions. 30

**Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Granting of Visas.** The ordinance states that it governs entry into Switzerland, airport transit and visa issuance, and applies insofar as the Schengen Association Agreements do not provide otherwise. The current provision map shows dedicated articles on short-stay entry conditions, short-stay visa obligation, application of the Visa Code, fingerprints and declaration of sponsorship. The ordinance therefore functions as Swiss implementing legislation around the Schengen visa and entry framework rather than as an alternative counting code. 31

## Official guidance and Swiss representation notes

**Official Swiss** **federal guidance.** SEM publishes a short-stay calculator, "rules for calculating the length of stay", and worked examples for legal stays, multiple-entry visas and overstays. The guidance states that third-country nationals may in principle enter the Schengen area for a maximum of 90 days within a 180-day period; that entry day and departure day both count; and that a rolling 180-day period applies. SEM also makes clear that the calculator is intended for both visa-required and visa-exempt third-country nationals and that the calculator's results are not legally binding. The official examples are especially useful for later drafting because they illustrate how prior overstay affects future date-planning without changing the underlying law. 32

<!-- 5 -->

**Official** EU **/ Schengen** **guidance.** The European Commission's short-stay calculator states that it checks compliance with the 90/180 rule, counts back 180 ddays from each day of stay, and excludes periods spent under a long-stay visa or residence permit. The "Travel to Europe" / EES official pages explain that the system applies to non-EU nationals travelling for short stays, records entries, exits and refusals of entry, and identifies overstayers. They also state that if a person overstays,consequences depend on national law and may include removal, administrative fines or detention, and prevention of future re-entry. As of 10 April 2026, the Commission states that EES fully replaces passport stamping in the participating countries, including Switzerland. 33

**Swiss** **representation** **/ consular** **practice** **notes.** Swiss representation pages are broadly consistent with SEM's federal materials. They describe the Schengen Type C visa as a short-stay visa for up to 90days in a 180-day period and direct applicants or travellers to the SEM or official short-stay calculator. The International Geneva guidance is particularly explicit that the visa holder must check the validity of the visa and the remaining authorised length of stay in the Schengen area, and it describes that point as varying with previous Schengen travel history. These notes are best understood as procedural and explanatory practice mmaterial, not black-letter law. 34

## Practical legal analysis

Switzerland applies the Schengen 90/180-day rule in two layers. The first layer is supranational: the Visa Code and the Schengen Borders Code fix the maximum duration of any short stay and determine when a further visa or entry request must fail. The second layer is Swiss: once the short-stay entitlement has been exceeded, Swiss domestic law supplies the machinery for removal, deadlines to leave,criminal liability for unlawful stay, and entry bans. That division matters for drafting. The arithmetic question is primarily Schengen law; the enforcement response is primarily Swiss law. 35

The rolling character of the rule is often the point most likely to be misstated. Neither the Visa Code nor the SBC creates a country-specific Swiss allowance, a calendar half-year allowance, or a "90 days per trip" allowance. Instead, lawful presence must still be tested on each day against the immediately preceding 180 days. That is why an apparently short visit can still become unlawful if earlier Schengen stays remmain inside the same rolling window. It is also why a traveller who has overstayed is not subject to a universal legal rule requiring a fresh 180-day absence before re-entry: the correct question is always how many prior Schengen days still fall inside the relevant rolling window on the proposed date of entry. 36

Multiple-entry visas generate a separate but related drafting trap. Article 24 of the Visa Code permits visas of long validity and multiple entries, but it does not enlarge the total quantum of lawful short-stay time. Swiss official example 4 is useful because it shows a holder of a 90-day multiple-entry visa making several visits, with only the visits falling inside the relevant 180-day reference period being counted. The correct drafting formulation is that a multiple-entry visa facilitates repeated border crossings during its validity; it does not displace the cumulative 90/180 ceiling. 21

EES materially changes overstay-risk analysis. In the stamping era, arithmetic disputes often turned on stamp legibility, missing stamps and reconstruction of travel. Under EES, entries, exits, refusals of entry and certain biometric identifiers are recorded electronically; the system includes an automated stay calculator, identifies no-exit records after expiry of authorised stay, and places identified overstayers on a list for competent authorities. Since 10 April 2026, the Commission states that EES is fully operational across the EES countries, including Switzerland. For internal content generation, that means future drafting should assume much higher record visibility for porior short-stay movements and overstays. 37

<!-- 6 -->

A prior overstay can therefore have two distinct future effects. The first is mathematical: after enough earlier days have fallen out of the rolling 180-day window, a further short stay may again become arithmetically possible. SEM's official example 5 illustrates precisely that point. The second is reputational and enforcement-related: the earlier overstay does not disappear merely because the arithmetic later allows a new visit. EES can retain overstay-linked data for five years where no exit record follows expiry of authorised stay, and Swiss law can still have produced a removal order, an Article 115offence, or an Article 67 entry ban. Future visa or border credibility assessments may therefore remain adversely affected even where the rolling window has later "opened up" again. 38

## Drafting cautions

·Do not describe the rule as **90 days per trip, 90 days in Switzerland, or 90 days per Schengen** **country;** it is a single rolling Schengen-wide short-stay calculation. 39

·Do not describe the rule as operating by **calendar** **half-years.** The relevant test is the preceding 180 days viewed from each day of stay. 40

·Do not omit that **entry day and exit day both count.** 17

·Do not imply that a **multiple-entry** **visa** gives a new 90-day allowance on each entry. It does not. 41

·Do not omit that, if the **visa sticker** **authorises fewer** **days,** the visa-specific authorised stay controls. 42

·Do not treat **visa-free** **travellers** as outside the 90/180 framework; the same arithmetic applies to them. 19

·Do not say the official **calculator** **is** **legally** **binding.** It is expressly only a technical aid. 43

·Do not state that EES is merely forthcoming. As of **10 April 2026** it is **fully** **operational,** and drafting should reflect that present status. 44

·Do not tell readers that a prior overstay is "cured" simply because enough time later passes to allow fresh arithmetic capacity. The earlier overstay can still remain visible and can still have Swiss-law consequences. 45

·Do not present **future** **re-entry** **timing** after an overstay as a pure legal entitlement divorced from enforcement history. Arithmetic feasiboility, visa discretion, border admission, and any existing Swiss entry ban are separate questions. 46
