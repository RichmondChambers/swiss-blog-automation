# Swiss Schengen Visa Requirement, Exemptions and Competent State Authority Pack

## A. Executive legal summary

Switzerland is not an EU Member State, but it participates in the Schengen system as an associated state under the EU-Swiss Schengen association agreement. In practice, that means that short-stay visa requirement rules for entry into Switzerland are principally Schengen rules, not a standalone Swiss visa code: the core legal architecture comes from the Visa Code, the Schengen Borders Code, and the common Schengen visa-list regulation, while Swiss federal legislation, ordinances, SEM guidance and FDFA consular materials organise Swiss implementation and procedure. Official Swiss guidance itself reflects this architecture by explaining that, since Switzerland's adherence to the Schengen agreements took effect on 12 December 2008, Swiss representations abroad issue Schengen visas (Type C) for short stays and national visas (Type D) for longer stays. 、

Who needs a Swiss Schengen visa for a short stay is determinedd first by the common Schengen visa lists. Regulation (EU) 2018/1806 harmonises which third-country nationals are visa-required and which are visa-exempt for stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period, through Annex I and Annex II respectively. Switzerland operationalises that common rule through SEM's nationality-based overview in Annex CH-1, List 1, and SEM expressly indicates that applicants should check the nationality-specific Swiss list; it also flags that there are additional nationality-neutral or category-specific rules in Annex CH-1, List 2. 2

Visa exemption is not the same as unrestricted entry. Visa-exempt travellers must still satisfy the Schengen entry conditions at the external border. Equally important, a person who would otherwise be visa-required for a short stay is generally exempt from needing a new short-stay visa if they already hold a valid residence permit issued by a Schengen state, or a valid Schengen long-stay visa (Type D), together with a recognised travel document. Both the Schengen Borders Code and Swiss SEM guidance reflect this. SEM also states expressly that a Category C or D visa issued by another Schengen state is valid for Switzerland during its period of validity. 3

There is, however, an important Swiss qualification: SEM's nationality lists show that some otherwise visa-exempt categories may still need a visa where gainful activity is involved. Swiss public materials therefore do not support a simplistic statement that "visa-exempt nationals never need a visa for short stays"; the answer can change if the intended activity is paid work or falls within a listed sectoral derogation. 4

The competent Schengen state is determined by Article 5 of the Visa Code. If Switzerland is the sole destination, Switzerland is competent. If several Schengen states are involved, competence lies with the state of the "main destination", assessed by length or purpose of stay. Only if no main destination can be identified does the first external-entry state become competent. Official EU guidance restates the

same rule: where stays are of equal length, the application goes to the consulate of the first Schengen country to be visited. This is why "first entry" is a fallback, not the general rule. 5

Territorial jurisdiction is a separate question from competent state. The default rule is that the applicant must apply at the consulate of the competent state in whose jurisdiction the applicant legally resides. Swiss official guidance mirrors that Schengen rule by stating that visas are issued by the Swiss representation covering the applicant's place of residence, and SEM's short guidance says visa-required persons should, in principle, lodge with the Swiss representation competent for their place of domicile. Visa Code Article 6 nonetheless allows an exception where the applicant is legally present but not resident in the consular district and justifies filing there; Swiss administrative materials also reflect a comparable exception in Swiss practice. 6

Switzerland may itself represent, or be represented by, another Schengen state under a representation arrangement. Swiss consular pages explain that where a state lacks its own visa section in a country or region, Schengen visa handling may be delegated to another Schengen state, and the European Commission maintains a current overview of consular presence and representation arrangements. 7

If an applicant lodges with the wrong state or wrong competent post, that is not merely a technicality. Official Commission guidance states that where the receiving state is not competent, the application and supporting documents should be returned, the fee reimbursed, and the applicant redirected to the competent consulate. In practice, getting competence wrong can therefore cost time, create credibility problems in itinerary assessment, and expose the file to "visa shopping" concerns. 8

Family-member cases require particular care. The public Swiss materials located in this research do not establish a blanket exemption merely because the applicant is a family member of a Swiss, EU or EFTA national. On the contrary, SEM's "entry without visa" guidance says that a third-country family member of an EU/EFTA citizen from a visa-required nationality must hold a valid C visa, while Swiss representation guidance provides procedural facilitation by allowing family members of Swiss, EU or EFTA nationals to submit directly at the responsible embassy or consulate. At the same time, EU free-movement law exempts holders of a valid Article 10 residence card from the visa requirement, and the Schengen Borders Code treats such residence cards as residence permits. The safest drafting position is therefore that family-member status alone is not a general Swiss published visa waiver, but separate residence-card and free-movement regimes may independently remove the visa requirement in some cases. 9

## B. Structural overview of visa requirement and competent state

**Legal source** **hierarchy.** The top layer is Schengen law: Regulation (EU) 2018/1806 on who is visa-required or visa-exempt; the Visa Code on which Schengen state is competent and where the application must be lodged; and the Schengen Borders Code on entry conditions and exemptions for holders of residence permits and long-stay visas. Switzerland applies those rules as a Schengen-associated state through the EU-Swiss association agreement, and Swviss domestic law and administrative materials then organise Swiss implementation. 10

**Visa** **requirement and** **exemptions.** The starting point is nationality, as defined by Annex I and Annex II to Regulation (EU) 2018/1806 and operationalised in Switzerland through SEM's Annex CH-1,List 1.That is not the whole picture. SEM also points to Annex CH-1, List 2 for special rules regardless of nationality, and the Commission separately publishes national derogations from the visa requirement. Swiss SEM guidance expressly states that holders of a valid Schengen residence permit or valid Schengen Type D visa, together with a recognised travel document, are exempt from visa requirements. 11

<!-- 2 -->

**Competent** **state.** The competent state is determined by destination logic, not applicant preference.If Switzerland is the only destination, Switzerland is competent. If the trip covers several Schengen states, competence goes to the state of the main destination as measured by length or purpose of stay. If no main destination exists, the state of first external entry becomes competent. Official EU guidance repeats the same structure. 5

**Territorial jurisdiction and place of application.** Once the competent state is identified, the application should normally be filed at that state's consulate in thhe place where the applicant legally resides. Article 6 of the Visa Code allows a narrower exception for persons legally present but not resident in that district if they justify lodging there. Swiss official guidance mirrors the residence-based filing rule; SEM's short guidance uses the language of the Swiss representation competent for the applicant's domicile. 12

**Swiss representation and Schengen representation agreements.** Swiss FDFA pages explain that Swiss representations issue visas for applicants in their district of residence, but also that Schengen visa handling can be delegated to another Schengen state where Switzerland lacks its own visa section. The Commission's official overview of consular presence and representation arrangements is the cross-Schengen operational reference. 13

**Consequences** **of** **filing** in **the** **wrong** **place.** The Visa Code is strict on competence, and official Commission guidance says that if the receiving state is not competent, the application and documents should be returned, the fee reimbursed, and the applicant redirected. That makes competence a substantive filing issue rather than a mere administrative preference. 8

## E. Official guidance, administrative materials and Swiss representation practice

The most useful Swiss federal guidance starts with SEM's entry pages. SEM directs users to the nationality-based overview in Annex CH-1, List 1 to determine whether a visa is required, and expressly points to Annex CH-1, List 2 for special rules regardless of nationality. SEM's "entry with visa" page restates the border conditions for visa-required third-country nationals and expressly includes holders of valid Schengen residence permits and valid Type D visas among those who may enter without obtaining a fresh Swiss short-stay visa. SEM's short guidance sheet also states that visa-required persons should in principle apply to the Swiss representation competent for their domicile, while visa-free travel remains subject to border questioning on purpose and means. 32

FDFA's central visa pages add the Swiss consular architecture. They state that visas for foreign nationals are issued by the Swiss representation covering the applicant's place of residence; distinguish Type C (up to 90 days in 180 days) from Type D (more than 90 days); and note that supporting documents and local procedure vary by applicant's country of residence. This last point matters because the legal competence rules are Schengen-wide, but filing logistics remain locally administered. 33

On the EU side, the Commission's "Applying for a Schengen visa" page restates the operative competence rules in a practical form: apply to the consulate of the country you intend to visit; if visiting more than one Schengen country, apply where you will spend the longest time; if the stays are of equal length, apply to the first country you will visit; and, as a general rule, use the consulate with territorial responsibility for the country where you are legally resident. The Commission's visa-policy page also emphasises that all 29 Schengen countries apply the same visa rules. 34

The Commission also maintains two particularly useful official administrative tools for this topic: an overview of consular presence and representation arrangements under Article 8 of the Visa Code, and a table of national derogations from the visa requirement. These are important where the black-letter rule is common across Schengen, but operational responsibility or derogations differ by state. 35

## F. Swiss representation / consular practice notes

Swiss representation pages are useful for practice, but they should be treated as procedural and explanatory material rather than autonomous black-letter law. A Swiss representation page for the United Kingdom explains that where a state lacks its own visa section, Schengen visa handling may be delegated to another Schengen state under a representation agreement, and that the appointed representation may handle applications within its own competence. That is a practical illustration of Visa Code Article 8. 36

The same Swiss representation guidance also states that family members of Swiss, EU or EFTA nationals are entitled to submit directly at the responsible embassy or consulate of Switzerland. That is a procedural facilitation point. It does not, by itself, establish a general exemption from the substantive visa requirement for all third-country family members from visa-required nationalities. 37

Finally, Swiss federal pages repeatedly stress that required documents and filing procedure differ depending on the applicant's country of residence. For internal drafting, that means global propositions about competence can be stated at framework level, but any operational checklist must still be local-post specific. 38

## G. Practical legal analysis

Switzerland applies the commnon Schengen visa requirement architecture, not because it is an EU Member State, but because it is bound into the Schengen acquis as an associated state. For a Swiss short-stay authority pack, that means the correct analytical sequence is: first identify the Schengen rule; then identify the Swiss implementing material; and only then identify local Swiss consular practice. That sequence avoids two common errors: overstating Swiss autonomy on Schengen Type C competence questions, and overlooking Swiss-specific implementation nuances such as SEM's nationality tables and local-post filing channels. 39

The competent-state rule matters because it allocates legal responsibility for the application. "Main destination" is not synonymous with "first airport" and not always synonymous with simple arithmetic either. Article 5 of the Visa Code uses both length **and** **purpose** of stay. A Switzerland-centred business conference with ancillary tourism elsewhere can therefore point to Switzerland even if the raw number of nights is higher in another state, provided Switzerland is the real main destination in purpose terms. Conversely,where no state stands out by purpose or length, first external entry becomes the fallback allocator. 40

Territorial jurisdiction is equally important. The applicant does not generally choose the "most convenient" or "most lenient" Schengen consulate. The default is the consulate of the competent state in the district where the applicant legally resides. Swiss public guidance tracks this through the language of the Swiss representation covering the place of residence or domicile. The non-resident exception exists, but it is an exception requiring justification, not a parallel free-choice route. Swiss administrative material reflecting Article 22(3) VEV points in the same direction. 41

This is why applying to the wrong state creates real legal and practical risk. Official Commission guidance indicates that a wrongly lodged application should be returned with supporting documents, the fee reimbursed, and the applicant redirectedd. Even before that formal consequence, an itinerary that appears engineered to fit a preferred state rather than the legally competent state can undermine credibility and invite "visa shopping" concerns. For internal drafting, competence should therefore be treated as an evidential question as much as a jurisdictional one: the travel plan, accommodation, invitation evidence and purpose evidence should all converge on the same main-destination story. 8

Exemptions also require careful framing. The cleanest short-stay exemption categories are those grounded in the Schengen Borders Code and reflected in SEM guidance: holders of valid Schengen residence permits and holders of valid Schengen long-stay visas do not ordinarily need an additional Swiss Type C visa for short-stay entry, though they still need a valid recognised travel document and must satisfy the underlying border-entry conditions. This is a short-stay visa exemption point, not a statement that all immigration restrictions disappear. 42

Family-member exemptions are the least cleanly expressed in the public Swiss materials located here. EU free-movement law clearly gives an exemption for holders of an Article 10 residence card, and the Borders Code's definition of residence permit recognises those cards. But Swiss SEM public guidance also states that a visa-required third-country family member of an EU/EFTA citizen must hold a valid C visa, while Swiss consular guidance gives family members direct filing access at the responsible post. The defensible drafting approach is therefore to separate three issues: family relationship, residence-card status, and filing facilitation. They are related, but not interchangeable. 43

A final Swiss nuance is that the visa requirement question cannot always be answered by nationality alone. SEM's lists expressly note visa obligations relating to gainful occupation for certain otherwise visa-exempt categories. Any internal content that speaks about exemptions should therefore avoid implying that visa-free nationality automatically covers short-term work or all remunerated activity in Switzerland. 4

## H. Drafting cautions

·Do **not** describe Switzerland as an EU Member State; describeit as a Schengen-associated state. 14

·Do **not** **say** that "Swiss visa rules" alone determine who needs a Type C visa; start with the Schengen-wide visa-list regulation and then add Swiss implementation. 44

**·Do** **not** reduce competence to "first entry"; first entry is only the fallback where no main destination can be determined. 19

·Do **not** ignore the "purpose of stay" limb of the main-destination rule; it is not purely a nights-counting exercise. 45

·Do **not** equate citizenship with correct filing location; the ordinary rule is filing in the district of legal residence. 46

·Do **not** imply that applicants can freely choose another Swiss post just because they are temporarily present there; non-resident filing requires justification and acceptance. 22

·Do **not** state that visa-exempt nationals never need visas for short stays; Swiss derogations for gainful activity can change the answer. 4

·Do **not** suggest that holders of valid Schengen residence permits or Schengen Type D visas necessarily need a new Swiss Type C visa for a short stay. 17

·Do **not** treat family-member status, residence-card status, and direct-consulate access as the same thing.They areanalyticallydistinct. 9

·Do **not** assume that a country-specific Swiss representation page is globally generalisable; local filing channels and documentary requirements vary by country of residence. 38
