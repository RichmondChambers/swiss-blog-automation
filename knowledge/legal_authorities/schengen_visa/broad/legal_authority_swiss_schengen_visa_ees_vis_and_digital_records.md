# Swiss Schengen EES VIS and Digital Border

# Records Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

The Entry/Exit System, the Visa Information System and the surrounding interoperability architecture now form the core digital record environment for short-stay Schengen travel. For Swiss Schengen practice, the key distinction is functional: VIS is the consular and visa-decision record, **EES** is the border-movement and stay-calculation record, and **ETIAS** is a separate pre-travel authorisation tool for visa-exempt travellers, not a visa system. The core EES legal basis is Regulation (EU) 2017/2226, read with the Schengen Borders Code and the Commission start-operations decision of 30 July 2025; the Commission and eu-LISA state that EES started on 12 October 2025 and became fully operational across all Schengen countries on 10 April 2026. 1

For scope, EES covers third-country nationals admitted for **short** **stays** and certain non-EU family members of EU/free-movement persons who lack the relevant residence card or residence permit; it also covers refusals of entry. It does **not** apply to everyone. In particular, holders of long-stay visas and residence permits are outside its core short-stay scope, and free-movement family members who hold the prescribed residence card are excluded. For those who are covered, EES records entry, exit and refusal-of-entry data and replaces manual passport stamping with digital registration. 2

Biometric handling is different in EES depending on visa status. For travellers subject to the Schengen visa requirement, the EES individual file contains alphanumeric identity and travel-document data plus a **facial image** at the border; the visa-related record can then pull specified visa data from VIS. For visa-exempt third-country nationals, EES records both a **facial** **image** **and** **fingerprints,** subject to explicit exemptions such as children under 12 and persons for whom fingerprinting is physically impossible. Official EU traveller guidance reflects the same split: visa-required travellers generally already supplied fingerprints in the visa process, whereas visa-exempt travellers give both face and fingerprint data for EES registration.3

EES is also a stay-calculation and overstay-detection tool. Regulation (EU) 2017/2226 requires an automated calculator indicating the maximum authorised stay and obliges border authorities to inform the traveller of that remaining duration. EU guidance makes explicit that the "90 days in any 180 days" rule is calculated as **one** **single** **period** **across** **all EES** **countries.** Where no exit is recorded after the authorised stay expires, EES flags the record and places the person on the EES list of identified overstayers; overstayer data are retained longer than ordinary compliant travel histories. 又

VIS remains the key digital record for Swiss short-stay Schengen visa applications. The Commission describes VIS as the system linking Schengen consulates abroad with all external border crossing points, allowing the exchange of visa data and biometric matching primarily through fingerprints. The current VIS Regulation provides that an application file is stored for a maximum of five years. Swiss representation guidance mirrors that framework in operational terms: biometric data are kept in VIS for five years, and fingerprints taken for a previously issued Schengen visa can usually be reused for a subsequent application within 59 months, subject to quality and interview exceptions. 5

Access is structured rather than general. Visa authorities may consult EES when examining and deciding visa applications, including use of the automated calculator and the applicant's prior entry/exit history. Law-enforcement access to EES and VIS exists, but only under tightly conditioned routes: specific case, necessity and proportionality, reasonable grounds that the consultation will contribute to the prevention, detection or investigation of terrorist offences or other serious criminal offences, and central-access-point control. Europol access is likewise conditional. 6

For Swiss rollout and practice, the State Secretariat for Migration states that EES has been gradually introduced in the Schengen area since 12 October 2025, that Switzerland implemented the legislative changes and national EES Ordinance, and that EES replaces passport stamping and calculates duration of stay automatically. SEM also states that travellers have rights of access, correction and deletion in respect of EES data. On the ETIAS side, SEM and the official EU ETIAS site both state that ETIAS is **not** **yet** **operational** and is planned for the **last quarter** **of 2026;** no applications are being collected as at 30April 2026. 7

## Structural overview and legal propositions

### Structural overviewv of digital border records

In practical Swiss Schengen work, these systems should be read as a layered digital record structure. **VIS** sits at the application and decision layer: it stores the visa application, biometric enrolment and the visa decision, and it is available to visa authorities, border authorities and certain other authorities within defined legal limits. **EES** sits at the border-movement layer: it records entry, exit and refusal-of-entry events and calculates remaining authorised stay and overstays. **ETIAS** sits at a pre-travel authorisation layer for visa-exempt nationals and does not replace either VIS or EES. The architecture is increasingly interoperable, with EU law and officiaI materials referring to shared query and identity components such as the Common Identity Repository and shared biometric matching service. 8

### Primary legal texts

#### Primary legal texts

The principal **EES** instrument is **Regulation** **(EU) 2017/2226,** which establishes the system, defines its scope, regulates data entry and use, and sets the law-enforcement, retention and data-protection rules. The practical commencement date was then fixed by **Commission** **Implementing** **Decision** **(EU)** **2025/1544,** which set **12** **ODctober** **2025** as the date from which EES would start operations. 36

The principal **border-code** instrument is the **Schengen Borders Code, Regulation (EU) 2016/399.** Even before EES, it provided the canonical "90 days in any 180 days" short-stay rule in Article 6 and the stamp-based entry/exit verification model in Articles 11 and 12. That background remains legally important because EES is best understood as the digital successor to those manual-stamping functions for covered short-stay travellers. 37

The core **VIS** instrument is **Regulation** **(EC)** **No** **767/2008,** now titled as the VIS Regulation for short-stay visas,long-stay visas and residence permits. For Swiss short-stay work, the live operational propositions remain that VIS exchanges visa data, supports biometric matching and retains application files for a maximum of five years. 38

<!-- 4 -->

The major VIS reform measure is **Regulation** **(EU) 2021/1134,** which amended the VIS Regulation, broadened the architecture to cover long-stay visas and residence permits, and repealed **Council** **Decision** **2008/633/JHA** on VIS law-enforcement access. This matters because older commentary may still cite the repealed 2008 decision, whereas the current authority base sits in the amended VIS framework. 39

For the visa-application procedure itself, the relevant procedural code is the Visa **Code, Regulation (EC)** **No** **810/2009.** EUR-Lex material for the Visa Code confirms the personal-appearance rule for first biometric collection and the integration of facial image and ten fingerprints for storage in VIS; Swiss consular practice on the 59-month reuse rule sits squarely within that visa-code/VIS architecture. 40

**The** **ETIAS** instrument is **Regulation** **(EU) 2018/1240,** but ETIAS should be kept doctrinally separate from EES and VIS. It is a travel-authorisation regime for visa-exempt travellers and, according to the official EU and Swiss sources reviewed here, is planned for the **last** **quarter** of **2026** and is not yet receiving applications. 41

For the broader digital-record architecture, **Regulation** **(EU) 2019/817** on interoperability is relevant because it establishes the framework for shared identity and query functions across borders-and-visas systems, including the Common Identity Repository. That is why EES and VIS should no longer be treated as wholly separate silos in legal drafting. 42

On the Swiss side, the legal gateway is Switzerland's Schengen association. The Schengen Borders Code itself records the relevant association instrument as **Council** **Decision** **2008/146/EC** on the conclusion of the EU/EC/Swiss Confederation agreement associating Switzerland with the implementation, application and development of the Schengen acquis. SEMM's EES page then records the Swiss legislative implementation path, including the Federal Council dispatch of 21 November 2018 and the 10November 2021 adoption of amendments to the AIG and the national EES Ordinance. 43

## Official guidance and Swiss practice notes

## Official guidance and administrative materials

For traveller-facing and operationally current EES material, the most useful administrative sources are the European Commission's EES pages and news releases and eu-LISA's rollout notices. Those materials confirm that EES records the traveller's name, travel-document data, biometric data, and entry/exit/ place data; records refusals of entry; and, from 10 April 2026, replaces passport stamping for covered travellers while enabling automatic overstay detection. They also confirm the phased start on 12October 2025 and full deployment on 10 April 2026. 44

For VIS, the Commission's dedicated VIS pages remain the best official administrative overview.They explain that VIS links consulates in non-EU countries with all external border crossing points of the Schengen States, that it processes data and decisions relating to short-stay visa applications, and that it supports biometric matching primarily through fingerprints. eu-LISA's current VIS material reinforces that description and situates VIS in the broader digital-border architecture. 45

For timeline questions on ETIAS and the reformed VIS, the official picture as at 30 April 2026 is asymmetrical. ETIAS has a published operational horizon in the last quarter of 2026 and is expressly said not to be in operation yet. By contrast, the Commission's annual VIS state-of-play reporting said that reporting would continue until a start-date decision for revised VIS is adopted, and official planning material still placed revised VIS readiness in **autumn** **2026.** That is important because practitioners can easily overstate the present operational reach of the amended VIS legal package. 46

## Swiss representation and consular practice notes

SEM's EES page is the key Swiss administrative source. It states that EES has been gradually introduced throughout the Schengen area since 12 October 2025, that EES registers all third-country nationals entering and leaving the Schengen area, calculates duration of stay automatically and replaces the manual practice of passport stamping, and that travellers have rights to know whether their personal data are being processed and to seek correction or deletion. The same page also records Swiss implementation dates, including the Federal Council's 2018 dispatch and the 2021 national implementing measures. 18

Because the English SEM page still refers to a "soft opening" expected to complete in April 2026, it should be read together with the later Commission and eu-LISA confirmation that the system became fully operational on 10 **April 2026.** In other words, the Swiss page is authoritative for Swiss implementation history, but EU-level rollout status should be updated from the later Commission/eu-LISA notices. 47

Swiss representation pages give especially useful operational detail for VIS biometrics. The FDFA representation page for the United Kingdom states that all Schengen representations collect biometric data consisting of finger scans and facial image for Schengen visa applications; that the data are kept in VIS for five years; that if fingerprints were collected within the last 59 months, fresh fingerprints and personal appearance are normally unnecessary; that poor-quality prior fingerprints can trigger re-attendance; that children under 12 are exempt from fingerprinting; and that the Swiss representation still reserves the right to require a personal interview. 10

Those same Swiss practice points appear consistently across other Swiss representation pages, including country pages stating that previously captured fingerprints are reusable if the earlier visa bore theVIS marking, again subject to quality and interview exceptions. For a Swiss authority pack, that does not create new law, but it is valuable evidence of how the VIS biometric-retention and reuse rules are being operationalised in Swiss consular work. 48

## Practical legal analysis

### Practical legal analysis

For **Swiss Schengen** **visa** **applications,** VIS remains the operative digital application record. The applicant's facial image and fingerprints are colected into VIS, retained for up to five years, and in ordinary Swiss consular practice the fingerprints can usually be reused for a later application within 59months. That means repeat Swiss short-stay applicants often fall into a two-stage evidential pattern: the new application is assessed against both the fresh documentary case and the existing VIS biometric/ application history. 32

EES changes that visa-analysis landscape because it adds a reliable digital border-stay history that visa authorities may consult directly from VIS. Article 24 of the EES Regulation allows visa authorities to search EES from VIS, consult the individual file, entry/exit records and refusal-of-entry records, and use the automated calculator when examining new visa applications. The practical implication is clear: past compliance with Schengen short-stay rules is now easier to verify systemically when a later Swiss visa

<!-- 6 -->

application is assessed. That conclusion is an inference from the cited legal permissions, but it is a strong one. 29

At **theborder-check** stage, EES is now the central record for EES-covered travellers. Before EES, the Schengen Borders Code relied heavily on passport stamps and the absence/presence of those stamps to verify duration of stay. After the EES transition, and especially after full operation from 10 April 2026, the evidential centre of gravity for covered short-stay travellers shifts from the passport page to the digital record. In practical terms, that means a Swiss adviser should expect arguments about lawful stay,number of days used and overstay to be tested first against EES data rather than manual stamp reconstruction. That is again an inference, but it follows directly from the legal replacement of stamping and the automated-calculator design. 49

**For** **stay** **calculation,** the older Schengen Borders Code still supplies the underlying 90/180 rule, but EES operationalises it electronically. EU gguidance states that the 90/180 period is calculated as a single period across all EES countries, and the Regulation requires the automated calculator to inform border authorities and, through them, the traveller. For Swiss practice, that is important because Swiss entry advice is no longer just about what happened at the Swiss frontier; it is about the traveller's aggregate EES history across the participating states. 50

For **overstay** **detection,** the new framework is stricter and more documentary. If there is no exit record immediately following expiry of the authorised stay, EES flags the record and the personis entered into the list of identified overstayers. Overstay-related data are retained for five years from the expiry of authorised stay, and the Regulation expressly provides a correction/erasure route where the person shows that the excess stay resulted from unforeseeable and serious events, acquisition of a legal right to stay, or error. Swiss practitioners should therefore frame genuine overstay-dispute cases as data-accuracy and rectification cases, not simply as requests for sympathetic discretion. 51

A second important nuance is **scope** **discipline.** EES is not the digital record system for every kind of Swiss migration or visa case. Holders of long-stay visas and residence permits are outside the ordinary short-stay EES scope, and some family members enjoying free-movement rights are excluded when they hold the prescribed residence card. That matters because Swiss work often spans C-visa, D-visa and residence matters; the governing digital-record consequences are not identical across those categories. 20

**On** **law-enforcement access,** precision matters. EES and VIS law-enforcement access is real, but both systems build it around tightly framed conditions: serious-crime or terrorism purpose, necessity and proportionality, reasonable grounds that the consultation will contribute, and central-access-point verification. For EES there is even a specific urgency route requiring ex post review within seven working days. So while these systems undeniably create a richer enforcement data environment, they are not designed as unrestricted police-search tools. 52

Finally, there is a **timing** point for drafting on VIS and ETIAS. The reformed VIS legal package is in place, but official Commission materials still describe revised VIS operational readiness as planned for autumn 2026, while ETIAS is planned for the last quarter of 2026 and is not yet collecting applications. As at 30April 2026, that means a careful Swiss authority pack should distinguish between: the already-live short-stay VIS; the already-live EES; the legally adopted but not yet fully operational revised VIS architecture; and the not-yet-operational ETIAS regime. 53

## Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

·Do **not** say that EES applies to "all travellers" or "all passport holders". It applies to covered **short-stay** **third-country** **nationals** and certain free-movement family members without the relevant residence card or residence permit, while excluding others such as long-stay visa holders and residence-permit holders. 19

·Do not say that every EES-covered traveller gives fingerprints at the border. For ordinary visa-required travellers, EES stores a facial image at the border and can import specified visa data from VIS; fingerprints at the border are principally an EES issue for visa-exempt travellers and certain exception cases. 3

·Do **not** treat the absence of a passport stamp as determinative proof of compliance or non-compliance for EES-covered travellers after 10 April 2026. The operative record is now the EES entry/exit history and automated stay calculation. 54

·Do not collapse VIS and ETIAS into one concept. VIS is a visa-data system for Schengen visa applications and decisions; ETIAS is a future pre-travel authorisation for **visa-exempt** travellers and is not yetoperational. 55

·Do **not** write as though all "revised VIS" features are already operational in April 2026. Official Commission material still places revised VIS readiness in autumn 2026 and continues annual state-of-play reporting until a start-date decision is adopted. 56

·Do not overstate Swiss consular automation. Even where prior fingerprints exist within 59months, Swiss representations may still require fresh attendance if print quality is insufficient or if an interview is considered necessary. 10

·Do **not** describe law-enforcement access as general or routine. For both EES and VIS it is conditioned by necessity, proportionality, serious-crime purpose and central-access-point control. 52

·Do **not** omit dates in Swiss rollout drafting. The technically safest formulation is that EES started progressive operations on **12 October** **2025** and became fully operational on 10 **April 2026,** while SEM's own explanatory pages record the Swviss legislative implementation path and the earlier expectation of completion in April 2026. 57
