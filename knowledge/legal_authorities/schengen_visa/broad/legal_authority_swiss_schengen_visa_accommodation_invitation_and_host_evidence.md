# Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Accommodation,

# Invitation and Host Evidence Framework Pack

## Executive legal summary

For Swiss Schengen visa purposes, accommodation evidence is not a peripheral checklist item. Under the Schengen Visa Code, supporting documents must show the purpose of the journey, the applicant's accommodation or means to cover accommodation, sufficient means of subsistence, and information enabling an assessment of whether the applicant intends to leave the Schengen area before visa expiry. Switzerland applies those Schengen rules through Swiss representations abroad and the State Secretariat for Migration, while the Schengen Borders Code independently governs what can still be checked at the external border. In practice, that means that accommodation evidence matters both at visa-issuance stage and, potentially, again on entry. 1

For tourism or private visits, the legal and administrative materials recognise several acceptable accommodation models: a host's invitation where the applicant will stay privately, a hotel or other lodging reservation, a rental agreement, or another appropriate document indicating the accommodation envisaged. However, the 2024 Visa Code Handbook expressly warns that a hotel reservation is a weak way to prove purpose of travel because it can often be cancelled free of charge. Swiss and Schengen authorities therefore look for a coherent narrative: dates,itinerary, accommodation, host details, and financial arrangements should align rather than merely exist as isolated documents. 2

Where the applicant will stay with a private host in Switzerland, an invitation letter is usually the central piece of accommodation evidence. Swiss SEM guidance states that the letter is written by the host,must be in an official Swiss language, need not follow a special form, and need not be officially certified.At minimum it should state that the host has invited the applicant, give the full personal details of both host and visitor, identify the duration of stay, give the date of writing, and bear the host's signature.It may also mention relationship, reasons for thevisit, and whether the host will cover costs. But the same Swiss factsheet is explicit that the invitation letter proves the reason for the visit; it does not by itself create a binding financial undertaking. 3

That financial point is crucial. In Swiss practice, accommodation by a host and sponsorship by a host are related but legally distinct. If the applicant lacks sufficient funds, or if the representation doubts that the applicant has sufficient funds, a Swiss embassy or consulate may require a formal **Declaration** **of** **Sponsorship.** Swiss SEM guidance describes this as an official document by which a Swiss citizen, a foreign national holding a residence or settlement permit, or a registered legal entity undertakes to cover certain costs up to CHF 30,000. The declaration is examined by the competent cantonal or communal authority, which checks the guarantor's solvency; even an approved declaration does not create a legal right to visa issuance. 4

The evidential burden in accommodation cases is therefore broader than "show where the person sleeps". EU official guidance says that, where host data have not already been verified by the authorities, the applicant should also provide a copy of the host's ID card or passport biodata page, the host's residence permit, and proof of residence such as property title deeds or rental agreements; proof of income may also be relevant. Swiss representation checklists reflect the same approach in local practice, often asking for a signed invitation letter plus the host's passport and/or residence permit, while tourism checklists may require proof of accommodation for every night of the planned Schengen stay. 5

Accommodation defects commonly translate into one of three refusal routes under Article 32 of the Visa Code: failure to justify the purpose and conditions of the stay, failure to showv sufficient means of subsistence including accommodation, or reasonable doubts about the authenticity or reliability of the documents or statements and the applicant's intention to leave before visa expiry. The Court of Justice has confirmed that a uniform visa may be refused where there is reasonable doubt about intention to leave, assessed in light of both country conditions and the applicant's individual circumstances. Swiss Federal Administrative Court case law likewise treats timely departure as a key entry condition and places the evidential burden on the third-country national, while recognising that factual errors and new evidence can matter on appeal.6

If refusal occurs, Switzerland provides a written refusal form and a written objection route. Official SEM guidance states that the appeal must be lodged within 30 days, in German, French or Italian, and may be filed by the applicant or an authorised representative such as a host in Switzerland. The SEM requests an advance on costs of CHF 200 and decides the objection; Swiss representation material additionally identifies further recourse to the Federal Administrative Court after a negative SEM decision. In accommodation cases, an appeal is most useful where the authority misread the evidence, ignored documents, or based itself on a materially wrong factual premise. Where the real problem is that the file was thin, inconsistent or incomplete, a fresh application with a better-evidenced accommodation narrative is often the more practical route. That last point is a professional inference from the refusal grounds, the Swiss appeal framework, and recent Swiss case law. 7

## Structural overview of accommodation and host evidence

Accommodation evidence sits at the intersection of Schengen law and Swiss implementation. At Schengen level, accommodation forms part of the supporting-document regime for visa applications and of the documentary-evidence regime for border checks. At Swiss level, SEM guidance,Swiss representations' checklists, and the Swviss Declaration of Sponsorship process convert that framework into operational evidential requirements. 1

**For hotel** or **commercial** **lodging,** the applicant is expected to show a booking or equivalent lodging document. The Visa Code Handbook also recognises rental agreemnents and property title deeds as appropriate accommodation evidence. Swiss representation practice often sharpens this into a requirement for accommodation evidence for every night of the Schengen trip, sometimes with reservation numboers or proof of prepayment. 8

**For** **private** **host accommodation,** the central document is the invitation letter. Swiss federal guidance treats the invitation as proof of the reason for the visit and sets minimum content requirements, while EU guidance says invitations should be personalised and, where the host's data are not already verified, should be backed by identity, status and residence evidence for the host. This is why a bare one-line letter is weak even if technically an invitation exists. 9

**For host** **evidence,** the most important elements are usually the host's identity, lawful status in Switzerland where relevant, address, capacity to accommodate, relationship to the applicant, and the internal consistency of dates and itinerary. Official Swiss representation checklists commonly ask for a copy of the host's passport and/or residence permit. The EU Handbook goes further, stating that proof of residence and proof of income may also be appropriate where host data have not yet been verified. 10

<!-- 2 -->

**For sponsorship,** Swviss law uses a separate formal mechanism. A host may merely provide accommodation; that does not automatically prove that the host will lawfully or financially support the visit. If the representation considers the applicant's own means insufficient or doubtful, it may require a Declaration of Sponsorship, which is then verified by Swiss communal or cantonal authorities. The invitation letter and the sponsorship declaration are therefore not interchangeable documents. 11

Accommodation issues are also relevant at the **border** **stage.** The Borders Code allows border guards to ask for supporting documents concerning lodging, itinerary and return travel. Swiss SEM guidance adds that even visa-exempt visitors may find it useful to carry a personal invitation from the host when entering Switzerland, although the invitation is not mandatory. A granted or visa-free entry status does not remove the need to justify the planned stay if questioned. 12

### Primary legal texts

<!-- 5 -->

The core Schengen text is **Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas.** For accommodation disputes, the most important provisions are Article 14, Article 21, Article 32 and Annex II. Read together, they create the basic architecture: applicants may be required to produce documents showing the purpose and conditions of stay, accommodation or means to cover it, subsistence,and intention to leave; the consulate must assess entry conditions and risk; and the application must be refused if the applicant fails to justify the purpose and conditions of stay, fails to show sufficient means, or leaves reasonable doubts about document reliability or intention to depart on time. 22

The key border-stage instrument is **Regulation (EU) 2016/399, the Schengen Borders Code,** especially Article 6 and Annex I. Article 6(1)(c) requires the third-country national to justify the purpose and conditions of the intended stay and to have sufficient means of subsistence; Article 6(3) points border guards to Annex I, which expressly lists invitations from hosts, documents from establishments providing lodging, itinerary documents and return tickets as possible evidence. Article 6(4) also recognises host guarantees or declarations of sponsorship where national law provides for them. 19

On the Swiss side, the relevant statutory anchor is the **Federal Act** on **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration.** Official Swiss Federal Administrative Court case law states that a third-country national must offer a guarantee of timely departure and cites Article 5(2) FNIA in that regard. The same case law also confirms that Swiss visa refusals are appealable to the Federal Administrative Court after the SEM stage and that the applicant bears the evidential burden regardling intention to leave, judged in light of all circumstances. 23

Foor Swiss appeal procedure, official Swiss representation material identifies **Article** 6 **paragraph** **2bis** **FNIA and Article 18 of the Ordinance on Entry and Visa Procedure** as the rules governing objection against refusal, annulment or revocation of a visa, while **Article** **63** **of the** **Federal Act** on **Administrative** **Procedure** is cited as the legal basis for the advance on costs. The same official material states that objections go to the SEM, must be in German, French or Italian, and may be filed by the applicant or an authorised representative such as the host or inviting company in Switzerland. 24

A further interpretive layer comes from Court of Justice case law. In **Koushkaki,** the Court held that a uniform visa must be issued only where the Visa Code conditions are satisfied and there is no reasonable doubt that the applicant intends to leave the Member States before visa expiry. In El **Hassani,** the Court confirmed that Article 32(3) of the Visa Code requires an effective remedy and that applicants refused a visa must have access to a judicial appeal under national law. These decisions matter because accommodation evidence often affects precisely the two issues they discuss:whether the declared private or tourism stay is credible, and what remedy exists if the refusal decision is wrong. 25

A limitation should be stated openly. In the official Swiss materials consulted, the Swiss statutory and ordinance provisions were identifiable through SEM guidance, Swiss representation appeal notices and Federal Administrative Court decisions, but the consolidated Fedlex article text for each specific provision was not directly extractable in the accessible web interface used here. For that reason, the Swiss-law wording below is given conservatively as a close paraphrase rather than as a verbatim citation. 26

### Official guidance and consular practice

Swiss federal administrative guidance is unusually important in this topic. The SEM **factsheet** on **letters** **of invitation** explains what a Swiss invitation letter is for, what minimum content it should contain, that it should be in German, French or Italian, and that it does not amount to a binding promise to pay.The **SEM information sheet on Declaration of Sponsorship** explains when the formal sponsorship route may be required, who can act as guarantor, the CHF 30,000 ceiling, and the communal or cantonal solvency check. The SEM **Entry** **to** **the** **Schengen** **Area** **/ Visa** **Procedure** sheet adds a border-stage perspective, noting that a personal invitation can be helpful when visiting someone in Switzerland but is not mandatory, and that a declaration of sponsorship may be required if financial means are lacking. The SEM **FAQ-Entry** gives the operative Swiss appeal guidance: 30-day deadline, language requirements, advance on costs, and host representation. 27

<!-- 6 -->

EU official guidance adds several important qualifications that are easy to miss if one reads only the statute. The 2024 Visa Code Handbook says invitations should be personalised and that there is no legal basis in the Visa Code for national invitation forms as such. It also states that, where host information has not already been verified, the applicant should be ready with identification, residence and housing evidence for the host, and possibly proof of the host's income. Most importantly for drafting, it says that hotel reservations are a weak means of proving purpose because they may be cancelled easily; a rental agreement, property right, organised-trip confirmation or other document may therefore carry more evidential weight in context. 28

Reliable Swiss representation material confirms that local application practice is more granular than the black-letter law. The **Swiss diplomatic and consular representations in Canada** checklist for a visitor visa requires a signed invitation letter stating the visitor's full name, dates and duration of stay, the host's contact details, and the relationship; it also asks for a copy of the host's passport and/or residence permit. In the same Canada materials, tourism applications require a hotel reservation with a reservation number for every night in a Schengen country. That is best treated as a local evidential implementation of the general Schengen requirement, not as universal hard law for every post. 29

**The Consulate General of Switzerland in Hong Kong** tourism checklist gives a second reliable illustration. It requires accommodation evidence for every night intended to be spent in a Schengen country and distinguishes between hotel confirmation, chalet/apartment rental contract or ownership evidence, and an invitation letter where the applicant will stay with family or friends. Again, that is a local procedural and evidential specification of the common legal framework, not a substitute for the Visa Code itself. 30

For Swiss remedy practice, the **Embassy** **of** **Switzerland** to **India** **and** **Bhutan** appeal sheet is useful as an official consular explanation rather than black-letter law. It states that a refused, annulled or revoked visa is based on a formal decision; that Article 6 paragraph 2bis FNIA and Article 18 OEV govern objections; that the host or inviting company in Switzerland may act as authorised representative; that the objection must be in a Swiss official language; and that, after a negative SEM decision, a further appeal lies to the Federal Administrative Court. Because this is representation-specific explanatory material, it should be used as evidence of Swiss administrative practice and procedure, not as the sole statement of Swiss law. 31

### Practical legal analysis

Accommodation evidence in Swiss Schengen visa work should be analysed as part of a **coherence** **assessment,** not as a single isolated document test. The Visa Code and the Handbook tie accommodation to four interlocking issues: purpose of journey, means of subsistence, credibility of the supporting file, and intention to leave. That is why a file with a hotel booking but no plausible itinerary, or with an invitation letter that does not match the stated relationship, dates or means, remains vulnerable even if it is formally "complete". 32

<!-- 7 -->

**In** **private** **host** cases, the accommodation story is usually stronger when the file can answer five practical questions without contradiction: who is the host, what is the host's status in Switzerland, where exactly will the applicant stay, why this host is hosting this applicant, and who is paying for what. EU guidance expressly contemplates host-ID, residence-permit and housing evidence where host data are not already known; Swiss representation checklists follow that logic. The result is that a host's legal status and address are not merely "nice to have" in many cases, but central to evidential credibility. 33

**In tourism** cases, practitioners should resist overvaluing cancellable bookings. The Handbook's warning that a hotel reservation is weak proof of purpose is legally important because it explains why posts often ask for more: itinerary detail, travel bookings within Schengen, package confirmations, rental contracts, or evidence of advance payments. The more the accommodation material is revocable, generic or last-minute, the more the broader file must do the credibility work. That is a source-based inference from the Handbook and the official representation checklists. 34

It is also critical to distinguish **accommodation-only hosting** from **financial sponsorship.** Swiss SEM guidance is explicit that an invitation letter may mention the host paying costs, yet the invitation itself is not legally binding proof of a financial commitment. Where the representation wants a legally meaningful undertaking because the applicant's own funds are inadequate or doubtful, it may require a Declaration of Sponsorship instead. The declaration is a Swiss administrative instrument, verified by communal or cantonal authorities, with its own eligibility rules and consequences. Treating an invitation letter as if it automatically substituted for a Swiss sponsorship declaration is legally wwrong. 35

**The** **border** **dimension** should also shape drafting. The Borders Code permits border guards to ask for supporting documents concerning lodging, itinerary and return travel, and Swiss SEM guidance says that an invitation letter may still be useful for visa-exempt visitors visiting someone in Switzerland. So accommodation evidence is not just a consular filing issue. A visa sticker, or visa-free status, does not eliminate the traveller's need to justify the conditions of stay if asked at entry. 12

As to **refusal risk,** accommodation problems usually map on to one of three legal pathways. First, the authority may say the applicant has not justified the purpose and conditions of stay. Second, where accommodation costs are not credibly covered, the authority may say sufficient means of subsistence have not been shown. Third, and often most seriously, the authority may say the documents or statements are not reliable, or that there are reasonable doubts as to the intention to leave. Koushkaki confirms that this "reasonable doubt" analysis is lawful and fact-sensitive. Swiss Federal Administrative Court case law similarly stresses the applicant's evidential burden and the centrality of return credibility. 6

**On** **appeals,** Swiss official guidance allows the applicant or an authorised Swiss host to object within 30days, in a Swiss official language, with an advance on costs. The Federal Administrative Court's visa case law shows two points of real practical value: first, hosts can indeed have standing in appropriate cases; second, the court may scrutinise whether the administration relied on a materially incorrect factual premise, and new evidence can matter. That tends to make appeals worthwhile where the refusal rests on administrative misreading or demonstrable factual error. Where the refusal simply reflects an under-evidenced or internally inconsistent accommodation narrative, a fresh application is often more efficient. That final contrast is a professional inference drawn from the Swiss appeal framework and the kinds of issues visible in recent Swiss visa cases. 36

## Drafting cautions

·Do not describe a host's invitation letter as a binding promise to fund the trip; Swiss SEM guidance says the invitation proves the reason for the visit, but does not itself amount to proof of a binding financial commitment. 3

·Do **not** collapse "private accommodation" and "sponsorship" into one category; in Switzerland, a formal Declaration of Sponsorship is a separate instrument that may be required when funds are insufficient or doubtful. 4

·Do **not** say that Swiss law requires an invitation letter to be notarised or to follow a prescribed form; the SEM factsheet says the opposite. 3

·Do **not** present hotel bookings as conclusive proof of purpose; the Visa Code Handbook expressly treats hotel reservations as weak evidence because they are easily cancelled. 18

·Do **not** omit host identity and status evidence in private-stay cases where the host is not already verified; EU guidance specifically points to host ID, residence permit and proof of residence, and often income evidence. 15

·Do **not** treat local Swiss representation checklists as if they wwere universal black-letter law; they are best used as local procedural and evidential practice layered on top of the Schengen legal framework. 37

**·** **Do** **not** forget that accommodation evidence can still matter at the border. Border guards may request lodging, itinerary and return documents, and the SEM says even visa-exempt visitors may find a host invitation helpful. 38

·Do **not** discuss accommodation evidence without discussing itinerary consistency. Official local checklists often require accommodation evidence for every night or throughout the planned Schengen trip, which reflects the broader purpose-and-conditions assessment. 39

**·Do** **not** state refusal grounds in vague discretionary terms; anchor them to Article 32 grounds: unjustified purpose/conditions, insufficient means, or reasonable doubts about authenticity, veracity, reliability or intention to leave. 40

·Do **not** present appeal and reapplication as interchangeable. Swiss guidance fixes a strict 30-day objection deadline and language requirements; whether appeal or reapplication is preferable depends on whether the problem is an administrative error or a curable evidential gap. 41
