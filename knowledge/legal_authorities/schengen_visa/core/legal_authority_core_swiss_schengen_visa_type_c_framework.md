# Core Swiss Schengen Visa Type C Framework Pack

This pack is a source-grounded internal legal authority summary for Swiss Schengen short-stay visas as
at 30 April 2026. It is built primarily from EU / Schengen legislation, Swiss federal law and ordinances,
and official SEM / FDFA / European Commission materials. Where Swiss law is cited in English, note that
official English translations are provided for information only and have no legal force; the binding texts
are in Switzerland’s official languages. 1

## Executive legal summary
A Swiss Schengen visa of category C is a short-stay Schengen visa. In Swiss implementing law it is
defined as a visa sticker issued by a Schengen State certifying that the holder meets the conditions for a
short stay, and it may be either a uniform visa valid throughout the Schengen area or a visa with limited
territorial validity. A category A visa is different: it covers airport transit only and does not allow the
traveller to leave the international transit area. A category D visa is a national long-stay visa for stays
over 90 days. Switzerland applies this structure as a Schengen-associated state, and Swiss federal
legislation expressly makes visa procedure and entry rules subject to the Schengen Association
Agreements. 2

A Swiss-issued Schengen visa is not a right of unconditional admission. Under the Visa Code, mere
possession of a visa does not automatically confer entry. At the external border, the Schengen Borders
Code conditions still have to be met: the traveller must hold a valid travel document, have a visa if
required, justify the purpose and conditions of stay, show sufficient means of subsistence, not be the
subject of a refusal-of-entry alert, and not pose a relevant public policy, security, health or international-
relations threat. Swiss SEM guidance repeats the same point in practical terms. Accordingly, the visa is
best understood as prior authorisation to travel to the border and seek admission, not as a guarantee
that border authorities will admit the holder. 3

Who needs a visa is determined by the Schengen common visa-requirement lists and Swiss
implementing rules. Switzerland’s Visa Ordinance ties short-stay visa obligation to Regulation (EU)
2018/1806 and exempts, among others, holders of valid Schengen residence permits and valid long-
stay visas issued by a Schengen State. SEM guidance says the same in plainer form: holders of residence
permits issued by Schengen States may enter Switzerland without a visa for up to 90 days in the last 180
days, and persons holding a valid Schengen residence title or valid Schengen type D visa are generally
treated as visa-exempt for short entry purposes. Visa exemption does not remove the need to satisfy
border-entry conditions or the Schengen-wide short-stay cap. 4

The core time limit is the well-known but often misstated “90 days in any rolling 180-day period” rule. It
is Schengen-wide, not Switzerland-only, and the days are counted across all Schengen States. SEM
guidance explains that both the day of entry and the day of exit count, and that on each day of presence
one looks backwards 180 days to see whether the total stay has exceeded 90 days. The European
Commission’s short-stay calculator is the official checking aid. A Swiss category C visa therefore cannot
lawfully be described as “90 days per trip”, “90 days in Switzerland plus more elsewhere”, or “90 days per
calendar half-year”. 5
Applications are assessed on both formal sufficiency and credibility. Under the Visa Code, consulates
check compliance with the Schengen Borders Code entry conditions and assess whether there is a risk
of illegal immigration, a security risk, or reasonable doubt about the applicant’s intention to leave
before the visa expires. Supporting documents are therefore not a routine box-ticking exercise: purpose
of travel, finances, accommodation, sponsorship, insurance, previous travel history, and ties to the place
of lawful residence must form one coherent narrative. Official Swiss materials reinforce this by requiring
evidence of purpose and circumstances of stay, means of subsistence, insurance, and—where needed—
declarations of sponsorship or invitation letters. CJEU case law under the Visa Code also confirms that
competent authorities have a real evaluative margin where credibility and intention to leave are in
doubt. 6

A type C visa is for short stays; it does not itself authorise residence in Switzerland, and it does not itself
authorise gainful employment. Swiss federal law states that no permit is required for a stay without
gainful employment of up to three months, but any gainful employment in Switzerland requires a
permit irrespective of duration, unless a separate exemption or notification regime applies. That
matters especially for “business visit” cases: visas may be issued for business purposes, meetings and
events, but productive work in Switzerland engages Swiss work-authorisation rules. After refusal, the
formal Swiss remedy is a written objection to SEM or FDFA within 30 days, in German, French or Italian,
with costs advanced under Article 63 APA as applied by the FNIA. A fresh application may still be
strategically preferable in some fact patterns, but that is a practice judgement rather than a substitute
for the statutory remedy. EES and VIS now make border movements and biometric history materially
more visible, which in turn increases overstay detection and the evidential significance of prior
applications and travel conduct. 7

## Structural overview of the Swiss Schengen visa framework
Legal nature of the type C visa. The Swiss category C visa is a Schengen short-stay visa for intended
stays not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period. Under Swiss law it is a visa sticker certifying that the
holder meets the conditions for a short stay; it may be uniform for the whole Schengen area or
territorially limited. It is distinct from the type A airport transit visa and from the type D national long-
stay visa for stays over 90 days. 8

Visa requirement and exemptions. Visa obligation follows the Schengen common lists, as
incorporated into Swiss law through Regulation (EU) 2018/1806. Swiss rules also exempt certain
categories, including persons holding valid Schengen residence permits or valid Schengen long-stay
visas. Visa-exempt travellers remain subject to the Schengen short-stay cap and to border-entry
conditions. 9

Competent State and place of application. The competent State is the State of sole destination; for
multi-State travel it is the State of the main destination by length or purpose of stay; if no main
destination can be determined, competence falls to the State of first intended external-border entry. As
a general rule, the application must be lodged with the consulate having territorial jurisdiction over the
place where the applicant is legally resident. Swiss consular pages also show that local filings may be
restricted to persons lawfully resident in the country of filing, and that Switzerland sometimes uses
representation arrangements with other Schengen States. 10

Permitted purposes and the work boundary. Official Swiss sources list tourism, family or friend visits,
sports and cultural events, business, and school or study among the category C purposes. But Swiss
federal law separately provides that gainful employment in Switzerland requires a permit irrespective of
duration. The safe legal distinction is therefore between short-stay visit purposes and work requiring
separate Swiss labour authorisation or a notification route. 11

Ninety-day rule and entry conditions. The short-stay ceiling is Schengen-wide and rolling. SEM
guidance states that entry and exit days both count and that the 180-day look-back is recalculated on
every day of presence. Border admission additionally depends on the Schengen Borders Code
conditions: travel document, visa if required, purpose, means, no SIS refusal alert, and no relevant
threat. 12

Evidence and procedure. Applications are ordinarily lodged no earlier than six months and at least 15
days before the intended journey. Core documents include the application form, passport, photograph,
medical insurance, and supporting evidence for purpose, accommodation, finances and intention to
return. Swiss materials also explain the role of invitation letters and declarations of sponsorship, while
warning that local consular requirements may vary. 13

Fees, biometrics and processing. Official Swiss guidance presently states EUR 90 for adults and EUR 45
for children aged 6–12, with exemptions for some applicants including family members of EU/EFTA
citizens. Biometrics are generally collected from age 12 and reused for 59 months if usable. Standard
processing is 15 days, extendable up to 45 days; consultation of other Schengen States can add time.
Refusals, appeals and reapplications; EES / VIS / ETIAS; distinction from type D visas. Refusals are
notified using the standard Schengen refusal form and must specify reasons. In Switzerland, the formal
first remedy is a written objection to SEM or FDFA within 30 days, in German, French or Italian, with an
advance on costs. EES now records short-stay entries, exits and refusals of entry and, from 10 April
2026, replaces passport stamping in EES countries; VIS stores visa and biometric data and allows
fingerprint reuse; ETIAS will apply only to visa-exempt travellers and is officially scheduled to start in the
last quarter of 2026, with no action required yet. Type D visas remain the correct route for Swiss stays
over 90 days or other long-stay authorised purposes such as employment or family reunification. 15

## Primary legal texts
Regulation (EC) No 810/2009 establishing a Community Code on Visas (Visa Code). This is the
primary legal instrument governing Schengen short-stay visas and airport transit visas. On the material
gathered, the most important provisions for Swiss analysis are: the scope and definition of visas for
intended stays not exceeding 90 days in any 180-day period; Article 3 on airport transit visas and
exemptions; Article 5 on the competent Member State; Article 6 on territorial competence by legal
residence; Article 9 on filing window; Article 13 on biometrics; Article 14 and Annex II on supporting
documents; Article 15 on travel medical insurance; Article 21 on examination of applications against
border-code conditions and illegal-immigration / security risk; Article 24 on single- and multiple-entry
visas; Article 25 on limited territorial validity; Article 30 on no automatic right of entry; and Article 32
plus Annex VI on refusal and appeal. For Swiss content generation, this Regulation supplies the default
Schengen rule-set unless Swiss implementing law or Schengen-association arrangements require a
specifically Swiss lens. 42

Regulation (EU) 2016/399 establishing the Schengen Borders Code. The Schengen Borders Code sets
the external-border entry conditions that Swiss authorities must still apply even where a visa has
already been issued. The most important points are the short-stay entry criteria in Article 6: valid travel
document, visa if required, justification of purpose and conditions of stay, sufficient means of
subsistence, absence of SIS refusal alerts, and absence of relevant threat. It also provides the legal
architecture for refusal of entry at the border through the standard form in Annex V Part B. In Swiss
analysis, this is the indispensable companion to the Visa Code because consular issuance and border
admission are legally distinct stages. 43

Regulation (EU) 2018/1806 on visa-required and visa-exempt third countries. Switzerland’s Visa
Ordinance expressly uses this Regulation as the nationality-list basis for short-stay visa obligation and
exemption. It matters because country coverage can change and later drafting must always check the
current official list rather than rely on static nationality examples. 20

Regulation (EU) 2017/2226 on the Entry/Exit System and related later rollout measures. EES is the
legal basis for electronic recording of short-stay entries and exits of non-EU nationals. Official
Commission material explains that EES records the person’s identity data, travel document details,
fingerprints, facial image, dates and places of entry and exit, and refusals of entry; from 10 April 2026 it
replaces passport stamping in EES countries and enables automatic overstay detection. For Swiss
Schengen visa analysis, EES matters because border history will be more readily verifiable and overstays
harder to conceal. 44

Regulation (EC) No 767/2008 on the Visa Information System and Regulation (EU) 2021/1134
reforming VIS. VIS is the central Schengen visa data system. According to the Commission’s VIS page, it
connects Schengen consulates and external borders, stores application and decision data, supports
biometric checks, and retains data for five years. Fingerprints may be reused for later applications over
that period. For Swiss work, VIS matters because prior visa history, biometrics, and adverse records are
more visible across Schengen than older paper-based assumptions might suggest. 45

Swiss Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (FNIA / AIG), especially Articles 5, 6, 10, 11
and 115. Article 5 states the Swiss entry requirements, including visa or ETIAS where required, means,
lack of threat, and the need to provide a guarantee of departure for temporary stays. Article 6 governs
visa issuance and provides that refusal of a short-stay visa is notified on a standard form, with a written
objection to SEM or FDFA within 30 days; it also applies Article 63 APA mutatis mutandis. Article 10
confirms that stays without gainful employment of up to three months do not in themselves require a
Swiss permit, but longer stays do. Article 11 states that gainful employment requires a permit
irrespective of duration. Article 115 criminalises unlawful stay after expiry of authorised stay and
unauthorised work. These are the core Swiss domestic provisions for distinguishing short-stay entry,
residence, work and remedies. 46
Swiss Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance (VEV), especially Articles 1 to 8. The VEV is Switzerland’s
key implementing ordinance for entry, airport transit and visa issuance. It defines short stay, long stay,
type C, type A and type D visas; makes short-stay entry conditions turn expressly on Article 6 of the
Schengen Borders Code; sets the travel-document validity rules; incorporates the common Schengen
nationality list; exempts certain long-stay-visa and residence-permit holders from visa obligation;
provides that means of subsistence may be shown by cash, bank funds, a declaration of sponsorship or
other security; and authorises limited-territorial-validity visas for Switzerland on humanitarian or
national-interest grounds in certain cases. In practical Swiss analysis, the VEV is the bridge between
Schengen law and Swiss administration. 47

Swiss Administrative Procedure Act. This pack did not separately retrieve the current consolidated
APA text, but FNIA Article 6(2bis) expressly applies Article 63 APA mutatis mutandis to visa objections,
and SEM guidance states that SEM normally requests an advance on costs of CHF 200 after receiving
the objection. For current drafting, the safe point is that APA cost-advance rules are engaged at the
Swiss objection stage. 48

Schengen association instruments. This pack did not separately retrieve the full treaty text of the EU–
Swiss Schengen Association Agreement, but both the FNIA and the VEV expressly state that Swiss visa
procedure and entry rules apply subject to the Schengen Association Agreements, and the European
Commission identifies Switzerland as one of the four non-EU countries forming part of the Schengen
area. That is sufficient for core-framework purposes. 19

## Official guidance and consular practice notes
Swiss official guidance is operationally important because many legally material propositions are
expressed there more clearly than in the black-letter texts. SEM’s FAQ and entry pages state that a
category C visa is for stays of up to 90 days in any 180-day period; that it is generally valid throughout
Schengen unless geographically limited; that Schengen-residence-permit holders may enter without a
visa; that travel medical insurance of EUR 30,000 is required for Schengen visa applications; that refusal
decisions are communicated by standard form; and that the objection must be made within 30 days in
German, French or Italian. SEM’s stay-calculation page explains the rolling 180-day method. Its
sponsorship factsheet explains when a declaration of sponsorship may be required and the CHF 30,000
cap. Its invitation-letter factsheet explains what such a letter proves and what it does not prove. Its work
pages explain that foreign nationals are not permitted to work without a permit and that short-term
work regimes may instead depend on separate labour-law notification systems. 49

Swiss representation material adds useful procedural and evidential detail, but it should be treated as
jurisdiction-specific practice rather than universally applicable black-letter law. Official FDFA pages for
Swiss representations abroad commonly say that Schengen visa applications with main destination
Switzerland are processed through the Swiss online visa system and, in many places, through VFS
Global; that standard processing is 15 days; that consultation of other Schengen States may add up to
14 days; that biometrics are generally collected every 59 months from age 12; and that refusals are
followed by a 30-day right to appeal / objection. They also show two practical features that later drafting
should respect: first, representation agreements are real and can shift the operational filing post to
another Schengen State; secondly, local pages sometimes lag behind the current Visa Code timetable—
for example, mainstream EU and Swiss guidance now uses a six-month earliest filing rule, but one
Singapore representation page still referred to an earliest filing point of three months. 50

For internal drafting purposes, the safest hierarchy is therefore: first the Visa Code and Schengen
Borders Code; next the FNIA and VEV; then SEM central guidance; and only then local representation
pages as jurisdiction-specific evidence of procedure, providers, or documentary practice. Where a local
Swiss representation page conflicts with updated Schengen legislation or broad SEM guidance, the
conflict should be flagged and the live jurisdiction-specific instruction rechecked before publication or
advice. 51

## Practical legal analysis
Swiss Schengen visa law is best understood as layered law. The Visa Code governs who should examine
the short-stay visa application, how and when it is lodged, what evidence may be required, when
biometrics are taken, and when refusals must follow. The Schengen Borders Code governs whether the
traveller may actually enter at the border. Swiss domestic law then plugs those Schengen rules into
Swiss institutions, remedies and enforcement. This layered structure is the reason one cannot answer
Swiss short-stay questions from a consular checklist alone. 52

A Swiss-issued Schengen visa normally permits travel throughout the Schengen area because it is
ordinarily a uniform visa, but that does not displace either the Schengen-wide stay calculation or the
border-entry inquiry. The same visa can be used for movement across Schengen only so long as the
traveller remains within the authorised number of days and continues to satisfy border conditions when
checked. That is why later content should distinguish carefully between validity area, validity period,
and duration of authorised stay. Those are different legal ideas, and confusion between them is a
common source of drafting error. 53

The competent-State rule is more than a filing convenience. It is the anti–visa-shopping mechanism
built into the Visa Code. Applying to the wrong State can produce rejection, referral or deeper credibility
concerns if the itinerary appears engineered to reach a more favourable consulate. This matters
particularly for Switzerland because Swiss practice relies heavily on territorial-jurisdiction rules and, in
some countries, representation arrangements or external service providers. Applicants who are only
visiting, rather than lawfully resident in the country of filing, may be unable to lodge there at all. 54

Evidence is assessed holistically. The legal test is not just whether some documents exist; it is whether
the decision-maker is satisfied on purpose, means, insurance, accommodation, and intention to leave,
without reasonable doubt. Swiss invitation-letter and sponsorship materials illustrate the distinction
neatly: an invitation letter can explain relationship, itinerary and host support, but it is not a binding
financial undertaking; a declaration of sponsorship is a formal financial-security instrument, but even
that does not legally vouch for departure. The evidential lesson is that purpose, finances,
accommodation and onward-return intention must all fit together. Prior refusals, past overstays, forged
or doubtful documents, and inconsistent statements go directly to credibility. 55

The work boundary is especially important. “Business” is an accepted category C purpose in official
Swiss guidance, but Swiss federal law separately says that gainful employment in Switzerland requires a
permit irrespective of length. The legal consequence is that a category C visa can legitimately cover
short business travel—meetings, negotiations, attendance at events and comparable visit activity—but
it does not collapse Swiss labour-authorisation law. Later content should therefore avoid treating
“business visitor” and “worker” as interchangeable categories, and should resist making broad claims
about remote or productive work unless there is specific Swiss authority for the exact scenario. 56

The Swiss remedy structure also needs careful language. Official Swiss materials commonly use the
language of “appeal”, but the legal mechanism identified in the FNIA is a written objection to SEM or
FDFA within 30 days, in German, French or Italian, with an advance on costs under APA rules. That
formal remedy is not the same thing as sending extra papers informally to a consulate. Equally,
reapplication is not the same thing as objection. A new application may be the more practical course
where the refusal is document-driven and the factual record can be materially improved; by contrast,
where the refusal is based on a clear legal or evidential error already on the file, the statutory objection
route may be preferable. That final choice is practice judgment, not a one-size-fits-all rule, and this pack
did not locate a single current Swiss central source laying down a Switzerland-wide reapplication policy.
EES and VIS materially change the risk environment. VIS centralises visa and biometric history and
allows reuse of fingerprints over five years. EES now records short-stay entries, exits and refusals of
entry and has replaced passport stamping in EES countries from 10 April 2026. For Swiss Schengen visa
analysis that means prior travel conduct, overstay history, identity consistency and earlier applications
are now much more visible across the Schengen system. For visa-exempt travellers, ETIAS remains
prospective only: official EU material says it will start in the last quarter of 2026 and no action is
currently required. 57

## Drafting cautions and open questions
Later legal content should be drafted with the following cautions in mind:

      • Do not describe a Schengen visa as an unconditional right to enter Switzerland or the Schengen
        area. A visa holder must still satisfy border-entry conditions, and the Visa Code itself says the visa
        does not confer automatic entry. 3
      • Do not describe the 90/180 rule as 90 days per trip, per country, or per calendar half-year. It is a
        Schengen-wide rolling calculation. 26
      • Do not imply that time is counted separately for Switzerland and the rest of Schengen. A Swiss-
        issued uniform visa ordinarily operates across the Schengen area, and the stay cap is
        aggregated Schengen-wide. 58
      • Do not suggest that a type C visa permits residence in Switzerland. Stays over three months
        require the appropriate long-stay route, permit or authorisation. 59
      • Do not treat ordinary business visits and work authorisation as the same thing. Swiss law
        requires a permit for gainful employment irrespective of duration. 60
      • Do not omit the main-destination rule or the first-entry fallback where no main destination can
        be determined. 61
      • Do not ignore territorial jurisdiction and lawful-residence filing rules. Some official Swiss pages
        expressly refuse local filing by persons who are only visitors in the filing country. 62
      • Do not rely on a generic worldwide Swiss document list without checking jurisdiction-specific
        practice. Local Schengen-cooperation lists and local representation pages can differ, and some
        official pages appear to lag behind current Visa Code timing rules. 63
      • Do not treat invitation letters as if they were sponsorship declarations. The two documents serve
        different evidential functions. 64
      • Do not treat appeal / objection and reapplication as interchangeable. The former is a statutory
        remedy with a strict deadline and language requirements; the latter is a fresh procedural
        attempt and is mainly a practice strategy question. 48
      • Do not state ETIAS start dates without checking current official EU material. As of 30 April 2026,
        the official position is “last quarter of 2026” with no exact start date yet published. 65
      • Do not make categorical claims about remote work on a type C visa without specific Swiss
        authority on the fact pattern. This pack confirms the general permit requirement for gainful
        employment, but did not retrieve a Swiss central source specifically resolving all remote-work
        permutations. 60
### Open questions and limitations. This pack did not separately retrieve: the full current treaty text of the
EU–Swiss Schengen Association Agreement; the current consolidated text of the Swiss Administrative
Procedure Act provision applied by FNIA Article 6(2bis); a current Swiss central source expressly setting
out the full post-SEM judicial route in ordinary Schengen-visa objections; or a full jurisdiction-by-
jurisdiction collection of local Schengen-cooperation supporting-document lists relevant to Swiss filings.
Those gaps do not prevent a reliable core framework, but they should be checked if later drafting needs
detailed appellate mapping or highly localised filing guidance. 66
