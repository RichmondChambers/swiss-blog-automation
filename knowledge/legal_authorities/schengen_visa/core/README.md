# Core Swiss Schengen Visa Legal Authority Packs

This folder contains core legal authority packs for the Swiss Schengen visa blog automation workflow.

Core packs are used by `generate_and_publish.py` to ground legal analysis, drafting and validation for blog posts that require an overview of the Swiss Schengen visa framework before moving into broad, route-specific or issue-specific detail.

## Current Core Pack

### `legal_authority_core_swiss_schengen_visa_type_c_framework.md`

This pack should provide the foundational legal architecture for Swiss Schengen Type C visas, including the main legal framework, permitted short-stay purposes, 90 / 180-day limits, entry conditions, competent state rules, application procedure, evidence requirements, financial means, medical insurance, refusal grounds, appeals and recurring drafting cautions.

## Purpose of the Core Folder

Use this folder for authority packs that explain the overall Swiss Schengen visa framework.

Core packs should be broad enough to support general blog posts across multiple Schengen visa scenarios, including:

tourism; family and private visits; business visits; short professional activity; conferences and events; medical visits; transit-related short stays where relevant; invitation and host evidence; accommodation; financial means; travel medical insurance; biometrics; border checks; refusals and appeals; overstay risks; comparison with Type D visas and Swiss residence permits.

## Legal Framework to Cover

A core Swiss Schengen visa pack should explain the relationship between:

the Schengen Borders Code; the Visa Code; the Convention Implementing the Schengen Agreement where relevant; Swiss implementation legislation and ordinances; SEM guidance and official procedure materials; consular practice; competent state rules; short-stay entry conditions; Type C visa rules; 90 / 180-day calculation; permitted purposes of stay; financial means and sponsorship; travel medical insurance; biometric enrolment; document reliability; refusal grounds; appeal routes; border control discretion; EES, VIS and digital record issues where relevant.

## Recommended Structure for Core Packs

Each core pack should, where possible, include:

an executive legal summary; a structural overview of the Swiss Schengen visa framework; a table of legal propositions; primary legal texts; official guidance and administrative materials; key case law, where relevant; consular or authority practice notes, where relevant; practical legal analysis; drafting cautions; source list; open questions or limitations.

## Legal Proposition Categories

Legal propositions should distinguish clearly between:

entitlement; eligibility only; discretion; facilitation; guidance; procedure; evidential practice; consular practice; border practice; unresolved or practice-sensitive points.

Do not describe guidance as law. Do not describe visa eligibility as an entitlement to entry. Do not describe consular practice as uniform Schengen law. Do not describe border admission as guaranteed by possession of a visa.

## Core Analysis Points

A core Swiss Schengen visa authority pack should preserve the key distinctions that determine the legal analysis, including:

applicant nationality; visa requirement or exemption; residence country; competent Schengen state; main destination; first entry; purpose of travel; length of stay; single-entry or multiple-entry visa; 90 / 180-day calculation; prior Schengen travel history; previous refusals; overstay history; financial means; sponsorship; accommodation; invitation evidence; host evidence; travel medical insurance; biometrics; document credibility; intention to leave; ties to country of residence; risk of unlawful stay; border control discretion; EES, VIS and digital records; appeal or reapplication strategy.

## Relationship with Other Folders

Use the core pack for foundational framework points.

Use `broad/` for recurring thematic issues that apply across several Schengen visa scenarios, such as entry conditions, permitted purposes, 90 / 180-day rules, financial means, accommodation, invitation evidence, document credibility, medical insurance, biometrics, refusals, appeals, EES, VIS and comparison with Type D visas or residence permits.

Use `specific/` for packs focused on a specific applicant category, nationality group, factual profile or travel scenario, such as family visitors, business visitors, repeat travellers, applicants with prior refusals, applicants with weak ties, applicants relying on Swiss hosts or applicants at risk of overstay.

Use `issue_specific/` if added for narrower risk issues or complex sub-categories, such as overstay calculation errors, insufficient funds, unreliable documents, unclear purpose of stay, weak return evidence, host credibility problems, medical insurance gaps or business-visit / work-boundary issues.

## Terminology

Use both English and Swiss / Schengen legal references where useful.

Preferred public-facing terminology includes:

Schengen visa; Swiss Schengen visa; Type C visa; short-stay visa; 90 / 180-day rule; permitted purpose of stay; tourism; family visit; private visit; business visit; invitation letter; host declaration; accommodation evidence; financial means; sponsorship; declaration of sponsorship; travel medical insurance; biometrics; VIS; EES; border checks; entry conditions; refusal; appeal; reapplication; competent Schengen state; main destination; first entry; Type D visa; residence permit; SEM; consulate; Schengen Borders Code; Visa Code.

Avoid using informal terminology that suggests a Schengen visa guarantees entry or creates a right of residence.

## Important Drafting Cautions

A Swiss Schengen visa should not be treated as permission to reside in Switzerland.

The analysis usually depends on the applicant’s nationality, residence country, competent Schengen state, purpose of travel, length of stay, prior Schengen history, financial means, accommodation, invitation or host evidence, medical insurance, document reliability, intention to leave, overstay risk, border control issues and any previous refusals.

Authority packs in this folder should help prevent public blog posts from overstating rights, implying that a Type C visa permits residence or work, treating possession of a visa as guaranteed entry, or flattening consular and border discretion into uniform outcomes.
