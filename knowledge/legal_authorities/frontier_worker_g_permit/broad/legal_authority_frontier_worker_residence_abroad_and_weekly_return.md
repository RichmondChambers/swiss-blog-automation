

## Swiss Frontier Worker G Permit Authority Pack
on Residence Abroad and Weekly Return
Executive legal summary
Swiss frontier-worker status under a G permit is, in immigration-law terms, a status built on residence
outside Switzerland coupled with authorised work in Switzerland. For EU/EFTA nationals, the legal
structure comes primarily from the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing
practice: a frontier worker is a person resident in one contracting state who works in the other and
returns to the place of residence as a rule daily, or at least once a week. SEM’s current public
guidance states the same in practical terms and adds that EU/EFTA frontier workers may live anywhere
in the EU/EFTA and work anywhere in Switzerland; the key condition is the weekly return to the foreign
residence.
For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the position is materially narrower. SEM’s current guidance states that third-
country frontier workers must be resident in a foreign border zone, work in the neighbouring Swiss
border zone, return abroad at least once a week, hold a durable right of residence in a neighbouring
state, and have been resident in the neighbouring border zone for at least six months; labour-market
controls also apply. The current SEM AIG guidance and the September 2025 AIG instruction update
reflect the same structure and emphasise that where the work focus moves outside the permitted
border-zone framework, a frontier-worker route is no longer the correct route.
The safest internal drafting position is therefore that a G permit is not simply an employment
authorisation with a foreign mailing address attached. It is a cross-border status that presupposes a
real foreign residence and a real return pattern. Official sources do not publish an exhaustive federal
checklist for testing “effective residence abroad” in disputed cases, but they do show that authorities
care about more than a declared address: Swiss-resident applicants in Geneva must first declare
departure abroad before applying for a frontier-worker permit; foreign-address changes must be
reported; and persons who stay in Switzerland during the week must register locally as weekly residents
rather than treating Swiss overnight stays as invisible. Those features strongly support a conservative
practice view that a foreign address alone is not enough where the facts suggest that the person is, in
substance, living in Switzerland.
Published evidence requirements are also asymmetrical. For EU/EFTA frontier workers, several cantonal
materials set out only limited filing requirements at first issue, often essentially identity and
employment documents. By contrast, Geneva’s published third-country frontier-worker process
expressly requires a municipal proof of domicile in neighbouring France showing more than six months’
stay, together with a durable foreign residence title and labour-market evidence. That difference should
not be overread: the lighter published paperwork for EU/EFTA applicants does not mean that cantons
are legally bound to accept a merely formal address if the factual pattern contradicts frontier-worker
status.
The practical risk point for content drafting is straightforward. If a person keeps or regularly uses
accommodation in Switzerland, spends most nights in Switzerland, moves family life to Switzerland, or
otherwise appears to have shifted the centre of life into Switzerland, the frontier-worker analysis

Structural overview of residence-abroad and return
requirements
EU and EFTA cases
Under the AFMP, an employed frontier worker is a national of a contracting party who resides in one
contracting state, works in the other, and returns to the place of residence as a rule every day or at least
once a week. The same weekly-return structure appears for self-employed frontier workers. SEM’s
current public material restates that rule and adds the present Swiss practice point that EU/EFTA
frontier workers no longer face border-zone limits: they may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA and work
anywhere in Switzerland, provided that the foreign residence is maintained and the worker returns
there at least weekly.
EU/EFTA frontier workers therefore remain, conceptually, non-resident workers. SEM’s VFP directives
underline that frontier workers who stay in Switzerland during the week must register with the
competent commune as weekly residents, but no additional immigration permit is required alongside
the G permit. That point matters because it confirms that Swiss overnight presence can be tolerated
within frontier-worker status, but only as a secondary weekly stay consistent with continued foreign
residence.
Non-EU and non-EFTA cases
For third-country nationals, the governing structure is stricter. SEM’s current guidance states that
frontier workers must have residence in the foreign border zone, work in the neighbouring Swiss
border zone, return weekly to the principal foreign residence, hold a durable residence right in a
neighbouring state, and have lived in the neighbouring border zone for at least six months. The 2025
SEM AIG instruction update further states that if the work focus is moved permanently to the border
zone of another canton, a new frontier-worker permit is required, and if the work site is outside the
border zones, the person must instead hold a short-stay or residence permit for employment under the
## AIG.
The SEM AIG guidance also summarises the continuing border-zone treaty framework for third-country
frontier workers and records weekly return as the operative return obligation under Article 35 AIG. It
emphasises that labour-market rules must be applied strictly and that only exceptionally may
temporary work outside the border zone be authorised.
Formal address and actual residence
The reviewed official sources do not contain a single federal paragraph saying, in terms, “a formal
foreign address is insufficient”. Even so, the legal architecture points that way. The law and treaty
materials require residence abroad and weekly return abroad; Geneva requires prior departure from
Swiss residence status before a G application; changes of foreign address must be reported; and non-
daily returns require commune registration of the weekly stay in Switzerland. Read together, those
materials support a conservative practice proposition that the relevant question is the real cross-
border living pattern, not merely the name of an address written on the application.

Authorities and practice materials
Primary legal texts
For EU/EFTA nationals, the primary legal anchor is the AFMP. Its frontier-worker provisions define an
employed frontier worker as a person residing in one contracting state and working in the other while
returning to the place of residence as a rule daily or at least once a week; self-employed frontier
workers are treated in parallel. The treaty also distinguishes frontier-worker permits from ordinary
residence permits, which is significant for status characterisation.

For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the decisive federal statutory materials are Articles 25, 35 and 39 AIG. Article
25 governs admission as a frontier worker by reference to durable residence rights in a neighbouring
state, prior residence in the neighbouring border zone and work in the Swiss border zone; Article 35
governs the G permit itself and makes weekly return abroad explicit; Article 39 addresses work outside
the original border-zone framework and the consequences of shifting the focus of work. Article 35 also
makes clear that extension rights depend on the absence of revocation grounds.
A useful general procedural text is the VZAE rule that no entitlement can be drawn solely from acts such
as school enrolment, purchase of property, renting a dwelling, signing an employment contract or
founding a business. That is not a frontier-worker-specific provision, but it is highly relevant when
reviewing attempts to use Swiss housing or settled Swiss life facts to “support” a G-permit narrative.
Official guidance and administrative materials
SEM’s current public G-permit pages remain the clearest concise official statements of the operative
differences. The EU/EFTA page states that frontier workers stay in an EU/EFTA state, work in Switzerland,
and return to their foreign principal residence daily as a rule or at least weekly; it also states that there
are no more border zones for EU/EFTA frontier workers. The non-EU page states the foreign-border-
zone residence requirement, durable-neighbour-state residence status, six-month residence
requirement, labour-market controls and weekly return abroad.
SEM’s one-page frontier-worker factsheet for EU/EFTA nationals is brief but operationally helpful. It says
that residence must be in an EU/EFTA state, the employer or self-employed activity must be based in
Switzerland, and the frontier worker must return as a rule every day or at least once per week to the
place of residence outside Switzerland.
The SEM VFP directives add two important administrative points. First, changes to employer, business
seat, work address or foreign address must be reported to the authority responsible for the frontier-
worker card. Secondly, frontier workers who remain in Switzerland during the week must register with
the competent communal authority as weekly residents; no additional immigration permit is required.
The directives also state, with reference to BGE 151 II 213, that frontier workers do not “stay” in
Switzerland within the meaning of the AFMP family-rights regime.
The SEM AIG guidance is especially helpful for third-country nationals. It states that third-country
border-zone rules continue to matter, that EU/EFTA frontier workers are not subject to those border
zones, and that temporary work outside a third-country frontier-worker’s border zone is exceptional.
The September 2025 AIG instruction change then clarifies that if the work focus moves permanently to
another canton’s border zone a new G permit is required, and if the work lies outside the border zones a
short-stay or residence permit under the AIG is required instead.
The Federal Supreme Court’s July 2024 media release in case 2C_158/2023 is not a frontier-residence
case in the narrow sense, but it is still useful authority on the legal nature of frontier-worker status. The
Court held that the non-AFMP spouse of a French frontier worker could not derive an independent right
to work as a frontier worker in Switzerland from the spouse’s status, reinforcing that frontier-worker
status is narrower than ordinary residence-based free-movement status.

Geneva’s EU/EFTA frontier-worker page states that the applicant must be domiciled in an EU/EFTA state,
employed by a Swiss employer and return at least one day per week to the foreign domicile. It also
states that a person who previously held a Swiss residence permit in Geneva must first declare
departure abroad before lodging a frontier-worker application. The published document list for EU/EFTA
frontier workers is sparse: identity document, photograph, and employer attestation or employment
contract.
Geneva’s third-country frontier-worker page is much more explicit on evidence. It requires, among
other things, a justificatif de domicile in neighbouring France issued by the mairie and attesting to
more than six months’ stay, a copy of the durable foreign residence title, a signed employment contract,
civil-status records, and labour-market recruitment evidence. It also states the weekly-return
requirement expressly.
Basel-Stadt’s frontier-worker page and official form emphasise that the route is for persons who live
abroad and work in the canton. Basel-Stadt expressly states that changes to the foreign residence
address and any Swiss weekly-stay location must be reported, and the official form states that frontier
workers who stay in Switzerland during the week must register with the competent local authority as
weekly residents.
Basel-Landschaft’s official information sheet states, in plain terms, that EU/EFTA frontier workers need
only return weekly to the foreign place of residence and that anyone with an official second residence in
Switzerland must register there as a weekly resident.
Ticino’s official materials are also useful. The public page describes permit G as the route for a foreign
national who wishes to carry on gainful activity in Switzerland without transferring residence. The
warning sheets for both EU/EFTA and third-country frontier workers state that the worker may stay
overnight in Swiss communes but must leave Switzerland at least once per week and notify the
commune where he or she stays overnight. Ticino’s sheets also require notification of changes to the
foreign address and warn that permits may be revoked if obtained through false statements or if a
condition of the permit is not met. For third-country workers, the sheet adds that the permit loses
validity if the foreign domicile is moved outside the border strip.
Vaud’s published G1 and G2 forms show the relative thinness of published EU/EFTA evidence
expectations. For employed frontier workers, the forms essentially require identity and contractual
documents; for changes, the G2 form contains a specific category for changement d’adresse à
l’étranger. For self-employed frontier workers, Vaud asks for business-premises lease documents,
explanations of the planned business steps and proof of financial resources.
Fribourg’s official frontier-worker declaration form states that the permit is valid throughout
Switzerland, that return to the foreign domicile must occur at least once a week, that changes in work
location, employment or foreign address must be notified, and that a worker who does not return
abroad every day must declare presence to the Swiss commune of stay.
Practical legal analysis
The most defensible internal analytical position is that residence abroad is constitutive of frontier-
worker status. That proposition is explicit for third-country nationals and embedded in the treaty
definition for EU/EFTA nationals. A G permit should therefore be described as a status for a person
whose principal residence remains abroad while Swiss work is carried out on a cross-border basis.

The return requirement is best analysed as both a legal minimum and a factual reality test. For EU/
EFTA cases, official texts use the formulation “as a rule daily, or at least once a week”. For third-country
cases, Article 35 AIG and current SEM guidance state the weekly minimum directly. Daily commuting is
therefore not mandatory, but a pattern of returning less frequently than weekly is incompatible with
the published legal standard. Where daily return does not occur and Swiss overnight stays occur during
the week, the correct administrative treatment is local weekly-stay registration, not silence.
On actual residence versus declared address, official Swiss sources are more suggestive than
exhaustive. They do not publish a single federal matrix of “residence indicators” for disputed G-permit
files. But the combination of rules is telling: Geneva requires declaration of departure from Swiss
residence before a frontier-worker application; foreign-address changes must be notified; weekly Swiss
stays must be registered; and frontier-worker status is treated by federal guidance and case law as
distinct from staying in Switzerland under ordinary free movement. On a conservative reading, that
means the authorities are entitled to assess whether the foreign residence is real, not merely declared.
The best officially grounded evidence of genuine residence abroad, from the reviewed materials, is
relatively narrow but concrete: an official foreign domicile certificate or attestation from the foreign
municipality; a durable foreign residence permit where the person is a third-country national; identity
documents; a signed Swiss employment contract or employer attestation; and, on renewal or change
filings, existing G-permit documents and in some cantons a recent payslip. Where there are Swiss
overnight stays, the commune registration of the weekly stay is also important. Those are the items that
appear in the actual forms and pages reviewed.
For higher-risk factual patterns, the official sources are thin, so the content system should be explicit
that any wider evidential list is inferential rather than codified. In practice, if a file is likely to be
scrutinised because the person appears to spend substantial time in Switzerland or maintains a
substantial Swiss living setup, it is prudent to think in ordinary residence-proof terms: lease or
ownership documents abroad, utility use, municipal registration, household and family location,
children’s schooling, and commuting records can all help test whether the foreign residence is genuine.
That is not a published Swiss immigration checklist in the sources reviewed; it is a conservative
evidential inference from the legal requirement of real residence abroad and real weekly return.
The principal consequences of failure are refusal, non-renewal, revocation, or the need to move to a
different permit category. Article 35 AIG conditions extension on the absence of revocation grounds.
Ticino’s official warnings say that permits may be revoked for false statements or for failure to satisfy an
imposed condition, and that non-observance may be fined; Fribourg states that the authorisation ends
with cessation of the activity. Geneva’s departure requirement illustrates the same structural idea from
another angle: once the person is in truth resident in Switzerland, frontier-worker status is the wrong
immigration box.
Drafting cautions
Do not write that a foreign postal address by itself is enough for G status. The legally safer formulation
is that the person must maintain a genuine foreign residence and a real return pattern abroad, with
Swiss weekly accommodation treated as secondary and, where required, registered.
Do not collapse together legal residence, factual residence, address registration and commuting
practice. The sources reviewed treat those as separate things: foreign domicile, foreign-address
reporting, Swiss weekly-stay registration, and the return obligation all appear separately in official
material.

Do not mix EU/EFTA and third-country rules. EU/EFTA frontier workers currently have country-wide
occupational and geographical mobility in Switzerland and are not confined to border zones; third-
country frontier workers remain tied to neighbouring-state residence, border-zone logic and labour-
market controls.
Do not imply that a G permit can safely coexist with effective residence in Switzerland. Geneva’s
published process requires prior departure from Swiss residence status before a frontier-worker filing,
and federal guidance treats frontier-worker status as distinct from ordinary residence-based presence
in Switzerland.
Do not import tax-residence or tax-frontier-worker thresholds into immigration content. SEM expressly
states that the tax-law and immigration-law concepts of “frontier worker” do not coincide.
Do not manufacture unofficial hard thresholds for Swiss overnight presence. The reviewed immigration
materials do not publish a general permissible number of nights or days in Switzerland for G-permit
holders beyond the return formulations noted above. Where facts are close to the line, internal content
should say so and present the issue as fact-sensitive.

Open questions and limitations
The reviewed official sources do not publish a comprehensive federal checklist for proving “genuine
residence abroad” in contested EU/EFTA G-permit cases. Published materials are strongest on the legal
rule itself, the weekly-return rule, reporting duties and some filing requirements; they are comparatively
sparse on how authorities weigh competing residence indicators in difficult factual files. That is why the
discussion above on lease, utility, family and school evidence is framed as a conservative evidential
inference, not as a codified list.
I did not identify, in the materials reviewed, a published federal precedent squarely deciding a classic
“paper foreign address but effective Swiss residence” G-permit fact pattern. The analysis on that point is
therefore built from the black-letter residence-abroad and return rules, plus the official reporting and
weekly-stay registration materials, rather than a single flagship case directly on disguising Swiss
residence under a G permit.
Official English translations of Swiss legislation are informative only and have no legal force. For any
later production drafting that needs quotation-level precision, the German, French or Italian
consolidated texts should be checked directly.
