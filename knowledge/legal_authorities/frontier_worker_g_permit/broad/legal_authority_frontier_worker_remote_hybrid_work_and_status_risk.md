

Swiss legal authority pack on frontier-worker
remote and hybrid work status risk
Executive legal summary
For EU/EFTA nationals, Swiss frontier-worker status is built on three legal elements: the person resides
in an EU/EFTA state, works in Switzerland, and returns to the main residence abroad as a rule
every day or at least once a week. Under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, the
frontier-worker document is a specific title, not a Swiss residence permit, and the permit is valid
throughout Switzerland; there are no longer border-zone limits for EU/EFTA frontier workers. Official
SEM pages and directives repeat those points consistently.
For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the framework is materially narrower. Admission as a frontier worker
requires a durable right of residence in a neighbouring state, at least six months’ residence in the
neighbouring border zone, and work within the Swiss border zone. Weekly return to the main
residence abroad is required; the permit is generally issued for one year; labour-market rules apply; and
profession/job mobility is restricted more tightly than under the EU/EFTA regime.
On the specific question of remote work, hybrid work and home office, the key finding is that Swiss
immigration sources are thin. The reviewed federal migration materials do not set any general
immigration-law percentage of home working abroad that automatically preserves G-permit status, nor
any federal immigration-law percentage that automatically destroys it. The official materials instead
continue to emphasise the classic frontier-worker markers: residence abroad, work in Switzerland,
weekly return, declared employer/workplace, and notification of relevant changes. That means status
risk is chiefly factual and evidential, not governed by a published immigration “safe harbour”
percentage. This is an inference from the reviewed SEM pages, SEM directives, SEM circular and
cantonal materials, and should be drafted as such.
Official sources do, however, say something important about Swiss weekly stays. SEM guidance states
that frontier workers who stay in Switzerland during the week must register with the competent
communal authority at the place of stay, and that for EU/EFTA frontier workers no additional
foreign-nationals permit is required alongside the frontier permit. Ticino says the same in practical
terms: a frontier worker who does not return home daily must notify the commune where they stay in
Switzerland, but must still return to the main residence abroad at least weekly. So occasional
overnight stays in Switzerland are not, by themselves, incompatible with frontier-worker status,
provided the foreign main residence remains genuine and the weekly-return condition is maintained.
The harder cases are the genuinely hybrid ones. Where a frontier worker spends a material part of
working time at a foreign home office, Swiss immigration materials do not publish a bright-line rule.
The legal logic remains that the person must still look like someone who works in Switzerland while
residing abroad, not someone who merely has a Swiss employer while the substantive work is
performed mainly elsewhere. The risk is therefore strongest where Swiss workplace attendance
becomes weak, the commuting pattern thins out, and the file contains little objective sign of real
work being performed in Switzerland. That conclusion is clearest for non-EU/EFTA cases, because the

A separate risk arises where the person starts working from Switzerland rather than from the foreign
residence. The SEM’s 2022 circular on home office is not a frontier-worker circular, but it is the clearest
federal immigration text on how remote work is classified. It says that an EU/EFTA national physically in
Switzerland, working from Swiss home office for an employer established abroad and with no Swiss
labour-market connection, is treated under immigration law as non-economically active, not as a
worker in Switzerland. For third-country nationals, by contrast, any home-office activity with an effect on
the Swiss labour market is treated as gainful employment and the competent canton is the canton of
the company seat. The same circular expressly says that immigration, tax and social-security concepts
are not identical. That is crucial: telework percentages seen in tax or social-security materials must not
be copied into immigration content as though they were G-permit rules.
The most conservative drafting position for internal content is therefore this: do not say that any fixed
level of remote work is automatically acceptable, and do not say that home office abroad
automatically defeats G status. Instead, say that Swiss immigration materials continue to test
frontier-worker logic through residence abroad, work in Switzerland, weekly return, and disclosure of
changes; that Swiss overnight stays may be compatible if properly notified and clearly secondary; and
that cases where the Swiss element becomes only nominal carry renewal and classification risk,
especially for non-EU/EFTA nationals.
Structural overview of remote and hybrid work risks
The first structural question is where the person’s main residence remains. Swiss frontier-worker
materials consistently require the residence to remain abroad and the person to return there at least
weekly. Cantonal practice materials reinforce that focus by requiring the foreign address to be declared
and updated, by asking about any second address in Switzerland, and by demanding proof of durable
residence abroad in third-country cases. If a Swiss flat or room begins to look less like a declared weekly
stay and more like the person’s real centre of life, the case starts to move away from the basic frontier-
worker model. The official texts do not provide a federal “centre of life” test specifically for G permits,
but the residence-abroad requirement is explicit and the practical materials clearly treat the foreign
address as a live permitting fact.
The second structural question is where the work is actually being performed. For EU/EFTA frontier
workers, the treaty model is residence in one contracting state and salaried activity in the other. For
third-country frontier workers, the statutory model is even tighter, because the work must be
performed in the Swiss border zone. That means hybrid work performed from the foreign residence is
not automatically incompatible, but the more the work pattern shifts away from a real Swiss worksite
and towards the foreign home office, the more the factual fit with “working in Switzerland” becomes
contestable. Swiss migration sources do not publish a quantitative threshold for that shift.
The third structural question is whether the Swiss presence is still only a weekly stay or has become
a residence pattern. Official SEM guidance for EU/EFTA frontier workers says that staying in
Switzerland during the week is compatible with frontier status if the person registers locally; Ticino says
the same. Vaud’s application form goes further and expressly asks whether the person has a secondary
residence in Switzerland, and if so where. That does not amount to a substantive approval of any
particular pattern of Swiss living, but it does show that Swiss weekly accommodation is an expected
practical issue rather than a forbidden fact. The risk is therefore not the existence of any Swiss

The fourth structural question is whether a remote-work issue is really an immigration issue at all.

rules, not migration-law rules. Immigration content should therefore use such materials only to prevent
confusion, not as authority for G-permit validity.

Primary legal texts
For EU/EFTA nationals, the legal starting point is Article 7 of Annex I to the AFMP, which defines the
employed frontier worker as a person residing in one contracting party, working in the other, and
returning home “as a rule every day, or at least once a week”. The same provision says frontier workers
do not need a residence permit, although the state of employment may issue a specific title that is
valid across its territory. The Swiss implementing ordinance, OLCP Article 4, mirrors the permit
architecture by providing for EU/EFTA short-stay, residence and frontier permits, and by stating that the
EU/EFTA frontier permit is valid throughout Switzerland. OLCP Article 9 then ties frontier-worker
procedures back to the ordinary foreign-national arrival/authorisation rules and the notification
procedure for short employment.
For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the anchor provision is Article 25 AIG/FNIA, which limits frontier-worker
admission to foreign nationals with a durable right of residence in a neighbouring state who have lived
for at least six months in the neighbouring border zone and who work in the Swiss border zone. The
federal statutory picture is therefore geographically anchored in a way that the EU/EFTA regime no
longer is. The SEM’s third-country permit page tracks the same rule and adds the practical

Two OASA/VZAE provisions are especially relevant to remote and hybrid scenarios. Article 1a OASA/
VZAE defines employed work broadly as any activity for an employer based in Switzerland or abroad,
regardless of where the salary is paid. That matters when analysing Swiss home-working by third-
country nationals. Article 3 OASA/VZAE separately defines a cross-border service as a time-limited
service performed in Switzerland by a person or undertaking domiciled abroad. Those provisions help
explain why some “remote work” scenarios may stop looking like frontier-work cases and begin to look
instead like Swiss work, Swiss home working, or cross-border service provision.
A further operational provision is Article 16 OASA/VZAE on weekly stays away from the main home.
It requires registration when a person stays during the week in another place for more than three
months in a calendar year without moving the centre of life there. Although not drafted specifically for
frontier workers, SEM’s OLCP directives expressly apply the weekly-stay registration logic to frontier
workers staying in Switzerland during the week. That is the main positive legal footing for occasional
overnighting in Switzerland without immediate reclassification.
For third-country frontier workers, the SEM’s 2026 AIG directives are particularly important in
consolidating the statutory rules. They state that only exceptional, temporary activity outside the
border zone may be approved for frontier workers, and that if the work is outside the border zones a
short-stay or residence permit for gainful employment is required instead. Those directives are not
legislation, but they are the main official administrative explanation of how AIG Article 25 is applied in
practice.
Official guidance and administrative materials
The most useful federal official materials for EU/EFTA cases are the SEM’s G-permit page, the SEM
frontier-worker factsheet, and the OLCP directives. Read together, they establish the classical criteria
—residence abroad, work in Switzerland, weekly return, nationwide validity, and employer registration
on the card—and they add two practical points: frontier workers staying in Switzerland during the week
must register locally, and employer/address changes must be notified. None of those materials,
however, states an immigration-law telework percentage.
For non-EU/EFTA cases, the SEM’s third-country G-permit page and the AIG directives are the central
official sources. They emphasise the neighbouring-state residence right, six-month residence in the
foreign border zone, work in the Swiss border zone, weekly return, labour-market scrutiny, and
restricted mobility. Again, nothing in these reviewed immigration materials says that a specified
proportion of home working abroad is automatically consistent with frontier-worker status. The closest
they come is to insist on the underlying factual model and, for third-country nationals, to warn that
activity outside the border zone is normally only exceptionally tolerated.
The most important federal text on home office as an immigration-law category is the SEM circular of
5 April 2022 on remote work, immigration-law qualification, the AFMP and third-country nationals. Its
principal value for this topic is twofold. First, it shows that the SEM is willing to classify physical work in
Switzerland differently depending on whether there is a Swiss labour-market connection. Secondly, it
expressly warns that the concepts used in foreign-nationals law differ from those used for tax and
social-security purposes. The circular is therefore indispensable when drafting cautionary language
about remote work from Switzerland and about the limits of using non-immigration telework
thresholds in immigration content.

A practical contrast appears in the Basel-Stadt HR note on home office for frontier workers resident in
France or Germany. That note contains telework limits—such as a 40% annual cap for certain France-
based staff and a requirement of at least one day per week or five days per month at the operational
workplace for Germany-based staff—but it expressly organises those limits under tax law and social-
security law headings. It is therefore useful as a warning against category confusion, not as a
migration-law authority on G permits.
Cantonal practice notes
Geneva is the clearest canton in openly restating the legal conditions. For EU/EFTA frontier workers,
Geneva says the person must be domiciled in an EU/EFTA state, employed by a Swiss company, and
return at least one day per week to the foreign home. Geneva also requires notification of employer
changes and states that the foreign address must be updated within 14 days. For non-EU/EFTA frontier
workers, Geneva requires durable residence rights in a neighbouring state, six months’ regular domicile
in the neighbouring border zone, weekly return to the foreign home, and—importantly for evidence—
submission of municipal proof of domicile abroad for more than six months together with a durable
foreign residence title.
Ticino is the clearest canton on Swiss overnight stays. Its practice page states that a frontier worker
who does not return daily to the home address must notify the commune in which they stay overnight
in Switzerland, and that in all cases the frontier worker must return to the main residence abroad at
least once per week. Ticino also requires timely notification of changes to the data shown on the permit,
including the foreign address, and says that the start of each new gainful activity and any job change
must be notified before the new work begins.
Basel-Stadt also contains useful practice indicators. Its public migration page restates that EU/EFTA
frontier workers usually return daily or at least weekly to their foreign residence, and says that changes
of foreign residence and any Swiss weekly-stay location are notifiable. The Basel application form asks
for the foreign residential address, the Swiss worksite (Einsatzort), and reproduces the rule that
frontier workers who stay in Switzerland during the week must register with the competent commune,
without needing an additional immigration permit next to the frontier permit. Those materials do not
regulate remote work percentages, but they make clear where the canton’s evidential attention lies.
Vaud is useful mainly for evidence design. Its G-permit form asks for the foreign address, whether the
worker already has a Swiss permit history, whether the worker has a secondary address in
Switzerland, and the address in Vaud where the activity is exercised. In other words, Vaud’s official
form already frames the file around the exact facts that become sensitive in remote/hybrid cases:
foreign residence, second Swiss accommodation, and a concrete Swiss place of work.
Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
The safest analysis starts by separating three scenarios. The first is the classic frontier-worker
pattern: foreign main residence, Swiss employer/worksite, presence at the Swiss workplace, weekly
return abroad, and any Swiss overnight stays declared as weekly stays. Official sources support that
pattern. The second is the hybrid abroad pattern: some days worked from the foreign residence and
some in Switzerland. Official sources do not ban that pattern in terms, but neither do they validate any
percentage. The third is the Swiss home-working pattern: work is physically performed from
Switzerland. That third pattern is where the SEM’s 2022 circular becomes relevant and where the case
may drift away from frontier-worker logic into a different immigration category.

For hybrid work from the foreign residence, the legally conservative position is that the arrangement
is not automatically fatal, but it does carry status risk as the Swiss element weakens. The reason is
simple: the official regime is built around work in Switzerland by someone residing abroad. No official
immigration text reviewed here says that the worker may perform most of the job abroad and still
necessarily qualify as a frontier worker. The more the work pattern points to a Swiss employer with only
occasional Swiss attendance, the more the case looks weak at renewal or modification stage. This is
especially so for third-country nationals, because the statute requires activity in the Swiss border zone
and SEM guidance allows out-of-zone activity only exceptionally and temporarily.
For remote work from Switzerland, the main immigration question is no longer simply frontier-worker
status, but whether the person is in fact presenting as someone based in Switzerland. A G permit
presupposes residence abroad; the SEM circular shows that physical work from Swiss home office can
trigger a different immigration classification. At the same time, official frontier-worker guidance permits
weekly stays in Switzerland with communal registration. The practical distinction is therefore qualitative,
not numeric: a declared and secondary weekly stay is one thing; a Swiss accommodation that has
become the operational base of life and work is another. Official immigration sources do not define the
tipping point, so internal content should describe this as a status and evidence risk, not as a settled
bright-line rule.
For occasional overnight stays and regular Swiss accommodation, the right message is balanced.
Swiss sources do not say that every overnight stay in Switzerland defeats G status. On the contrary, SEM
and Ticino expressly contemplate weekly stays in Switzerland, and Vaud asks whether the worker has a
Swiss secondary address. The real legal concern is whether the person still maintains the foreign
main residence in substance, continues to return there at least weekly, and remains truthfully
describable as a frontier worker rather than a Swiss resident worker.
For reduced commuting, the sources support one limited positive point: daily commuting is not
mandatory if the person still returns to the main residence abroad at least once a week. That means a
reduction from daily commuting to weekly commuting is not, by itself, disqualifying. But if reduced
commuting is really a symptom of work being done mainly from the foreign home office, then the
problem is no longer commuting frequency as such; it is the weakening of the “work in Switzerland”
element and, in some cases, the move away from a Swiss worksite that the cantonal materials continue
to require or ask the worker to identify.
The minimum evidence set supported by official materials is narrower than many employers assume.
The materials clearly require the foreign address, and in third-country cases may require proof of
foreign domicile and a durable foreign residence title. They also require or request the Swiss
employer, the Swiss worksite/place of activity, and disclosure of any secondary Swiss address or
weekly-stay location. They also require prompt notification of employer changes, foreign-address
changes, and cessation of activity. What the reviewed sources do not provide is a closed federal
evidential list proving weekly commuting or acceptable telework shares. Internal content should
therefore present commuting/work-pattern evidence as a case-management need, not as a published
formal checklist.
Future remote-work drafting for frontier workers should therefore be framed in four cautions. First,
separate EU/EFTA from non-EU/EFTA from the outset, because the legal intensity of the status risk is
very different. Secondly, distinguish immigration from tax, social security, payroll and employment
law every time remote-work percentages are mentioned. Thirdly, avoid formulae such as “up to x%
home working is fine” unless a canton has issued an immigration-specific rule—which the reviewed
materials did not reveal. Fourthly, use the language of factual fit, notification duties and renewal
risk, not “automatic validity” or “automatic invalidity”.
