# Swiss legal authority pack on frontier-worker reality loss and status change from G permits

## Executive legal summary

In Swiss immigration law, a G permit is **frontier-worker authorisation,** not Swiss residence status. For EU/EFTA nationals, the Agreemnent on the Free Movement of Persons defines an employed frontier worker as a person who **resides in one contracting party and works in the other,** returning to the place of residence as a rule daily or at least weekly; frontier workers **do** **not require** **a** **residence** **permit,** though the state of employment may issue a special permit. SEM's current public guidance states the same point in operational terms: EU/EFTA frontier workers stay in an EU/EFTA state, work in Switzerland, and return to their foreign main residence daily or at least weekly. For third-country nationals, SEM describes the G permit as available only to persons who **live** **in** **the** **foreign** **border** **zone,** work in the adjacent Swiss border zone, and return to their foreign main residence at least weekly.1

If a person **moves** **residence** **to** **Switzerland,** or no longer genuinely maintains a foreign main residence with the required pattern of return, the factual basis for frontier-worker status is undermined. Swiss official materials also separate the frontier-worker route from the Swiss residence route: all foreign nationals who remain in Switzerland for more than three months need a Swiss residence permit; EU/EFTA workers with employment over three months must apply through the commune of residence, and a B permit is issued for an unlimited contract or one lasting at least 365 days, while an L permit covers shorter employment over three months. That means that an actual move to Switzerland is not merely a later renewal issue; it points to a different legal category. 2

Official sources do recognise one important nuance: a frontier worker may **stay** **in** **Switzerland** **during** **the** **week** without losing G status, provided the person still has the foreign main residence and still returns there at least weekly. SEM's OLCP directives say such frontier workers must notify the competent commune of the place of stay, and Ticino states expressly that a frontier worker who does not return homne daily must notify the commune where the person sleepsin Switzerland, while still returning at least once a wveek to the principal home abroad. This supports a conservative distinction between **weekkly Swiss accommodation** and an actual **transfer of residence.** 3

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the normal consequence of a genuine move to Switzerland is that the person should be handled under the resident route, usually L or B depending on the duration of employment, or under a non-active residence route where the conditions are met. I did **not** find any official federal source creating a general safe harbour under which someone who has actually moved to Switzerland can simply continue on a G permit until the next renewal cycle. The official materials instead point to prompt registration and notification obligations when residence or permit facts change. That point should therefore be drafted as **a conservative inference from the available authorities,** not as a verbatim federal rule. 4

**For third-country** **nationals,** the position is materially stricter. SEM's federal guidance describes a binary admissions system: EU/EFTA nationals benefit from facilitated labour-market access under free movement, whereas third-country nationals may be admitted to Swiss employment only in restricted numbers and only if sufficiently qualified. SEM's general work guidance further states that the future employer must obtain the work authorisation and that the person may work only after the required process is completed. **I** found **no official source establishing a general automatic "conversion" from** **G to Swiss residence status forthird-country frontier workers.** The safer internal position is that once frontier-worker facts fall away, a fresh Swiss residence/work authorisation under the ordinary third-country regime may be required, with no assumption of continuity. 5

The risk profile is real for both worker and employer. SEM's directives state that the foreign-national permit must be presented to the employer without being asked, that repeated employer breaches can lead to administrative sanctions and cost liability, and thatunlawful residence or gainful activity without authorisation falls under the criminal provisions of the FNIA/AIG framework. For EU/EFTA frontier workers, SEM's OLCP directives also require changes of employer, business seat or professional address, and the **foreign** **address** to be notified to the competent canton **before** the new activity begins; sanctions may follow for non-notification.6

## Structural overview of loss of frontier-worker reality

The legal structure is best understood by separating three questions.

**First,frontier-worker reality** is factual. The core facts are residence abroad, work in Switzerland, and the required return pattern to the foreign principal residence. For EU/EFTA nationals, those elements come directly from the free movement rules and SEM guidance. For third-country nationals, SEM additionally ties G status to the foreign border zone and the Swiss border zone. If those facts no longer exist, the person may still be working lawfully in Switzerland only if some **other** Swiss immigration status now covers the situation. 7

Secondly, **communal registration** is its own layer. Official Swiss registration guidance says that when a person moves, the new commune of residence must be notified, generally within 14 days. SEM's work guidance says that persons taking up Swiss residence for work must apply through the commune of the future place of residence. Registration therefore matters as public-law evidence that Swiss residence has been taken up, even though it is not itself the same thing as immigration entitlement. That distinction is an analytical inference from the official materials and should be maintained in drafting. 8

Thirdly, **immigration** **authorisation** must match the actual facts. A G permit fits cross-border worker facts. A Swiss resident worker generally needs the resident route instead. The clearest official illustrations come from cantonal practice: Vaud uses different forms for frontier workers and for EU/ EFTA residents working more than three months "hors frontaliers"; Geneva runs separate procedures for "reside and work" and for frontier permits; Zurich presents separate pages for G permits and for EU/ EFTA residence with work; Ticino likewise separates G from B. 9

On the present authorities, loss of frontier-worker reality is therefore usually triggered by one or more of the following: an actual move to Switzerland, Swiss communal registration as a resident, failure to maintain the foreign main residence, or failure to continue the required weekly return. A mere Swiss weekday room or secondary accommodation does **not** automatically collapse G status if the foreign main residence remains genuine and the weekly return continues; official sources expressly contemplate that arrangement. 10

### Primary legal texts and official guidance

The most important **primary** **legal texts** are these.

The **Agreement** on **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons** is the anchor for EU/EFTA analysis. Annex I, article 7 defines employed frontier workers; article 13 does the same for self-employed frontier wworkers; article 6 deals wwith residence permits for employed persons who reside in the host state; and the mobility provisions distinguish resident-worker mobility from frontier-worker mobility. Those provisions are enough to show why a person cannot be both a genuine frontier worker and, at the same time, someone whose actual residence has shifted into Switzerland. 19

The **FNIA/AIG** **framework** is the anchor for third-country froontier workers and for sanctions. SEM's current directives summarise the third-country G architecture by reference to articles 25, 35 and 39 AIG, including six months' residence in the neighbouring border zone, weekly return, and permit controI for job changes. The same directives also summarise article 91 AIG on presentation of permit documents to the employer, article 122 AIG on administrative sanctions against employers, and the unlawful-stay/ unlawful-work offence structure. 20

<!-- 5 -->

The **OLCP** matters operationally for EU/EFTA frontier workers. The clearest machine-readable official restatement located is in SEM's 2026 OLCP directives, which cite article 9(3) OLCP for advance notification of changes and refer to sanctions for non-compliance. Those directives also explain the communal declaration duty for frontier workers who stay in Switzerland during the week. 21

The most useful **official administrative** **materials** are the SEM permit pages, SEM directives, and ch.ch guidance.

SEM's current pages on **G EU/EFTA, G non-EU/EFTA, B EU/EFTA,** and **Working in Switzerland are** especially useful because they state the operational categories in simple form: which route is for frontier workers, which is for residents, what contract lengthpoints to B rather than L, and how registration works when residence is taken up in Switzerland. 22

SEM's **OLCP** **directives** and **AIG** **directives** are the strongest current official interpretive materials located for this topic. They provide the best federal guidance on advance-notification duties,weekly-stay treatment, third-country border-zone restrictions, permit presentation to employers, and sanctions. For internal drafting, these directives should be treated as the main federal administrative framework where the browser-accessible consolidated legislative text is not easily machine-readable. 23

The federal/cantonal portal ch.ch is important for the residence-registration layer. It states that foreign nationals staying in Switzerland for more than three months need a permit and that a move must generally be registered with the new commune within 14 days. That is not a substitute for analysing permit entitlement, but it is important evidence that Swiss residence cannot simply be ignored once it exists factually. 24

### Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** treats G and Swiss residence as separate tracks. Its frontier-worker page says that people who want to work in Switzerland but continue to live abroad can obtain a G permit, and for third-country nationals it reiterates the border-zone and weekly-return requirements and the need for a labour-market pre-decision for starting work, changing jobs or moving into self-employment. A separate Zurich page for EU/EFTA residents states that those working in Switzerland for more than 90 days must register personally with the commune of residence within 14 days. 25

**Geneva** likewise runs separate permit channels for "frontier work" and for "reside and work in Geneva". Its OCPM frontier-worker FAQ says that where a G holder moves domicile to another canton, the permit should be cancelled by returning the relevant form, completed by the employer, because frontier-worker activity in Geneva has ceased. Geneva's employer portal separately states that any EU/EFTA national with a Swiss employment contract of more than three months must apply for a residence permit with work activity.Geneva also expressly says that British nationals holding a G permit who wish to settle in Switzerland are treated as third-country cases unless they fall within acquired-rights rules. 26

**Ticino** states the point with unusual clarity. Its G guidance says the permit is for the foreign citizen who intends to carry on gainful activity in Swvitzerland **without** **transferring** **residence.** Ticino also says that a frontier worker who does not return home daily must notify the commune where the person sleeps in Switzerland, but in every case must return at least once a week to the principal domicile abroad. Its EU/ EFTA B-permit application guidance separately states that the application must be sent within 14 days from entry, and workers must send it **before** starting activity. 27

**Vaud** provides useful evidence of administrative classification. The G1 frontier-worker form for Europeans asks for the **foreign** **address** and, separately, whether the person has a **secondary** **residence in Switzerland.** The T1 form, by contrast, is expressly for a **UE/AELE residence title for** **activity of more than three months in Vaud (excluding frontier workers),** asks for the person's current Swiss address, and states that the commune receives the form at registration. That is strong cantonal practice evidence that Swiss residence and frontier-worker status are administered as distinct legal tracks. 28

### Practical legal analysis

The safest internal analytical model is to separate **facts, registration,** and **permit** **category.**

A person can lose frontier-worker reality because the **facts** have changed before the permit card changes. The legal category is built oon where the person genuinely lives and how the cross-border pattern is actually maintained. If the Swiss address becomes the real home, or if the weekly returns to the foreign principal residence stop being genuine, the fact pattern that justified G status has changed even if the document has not yet expired. That conclusion is straightforward for both EU/EFTA and third-country cases because the definitions themselves depend on residence abroad plus return. 18

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the practical position is comparatively structured. If the person has taken up Swiss residence and will continue working in Switzerland for more than three months, the official resident-worker route is L or B depending on the contract. If the person is residing in Switzerland without work, SEM's B guidance recognises the non-active B route where sufficient means and insurance are shown. The difficult point is timing: SEM's general work page says the person must register within 14 days of arrival and before starting wwork, while the AFMP text says completion of residence-permit formalities must not stop the applicant immediately taking up emnployment under the contract. The conservative drafting position is therefore that an EU/EFTA mover should **switch** **promptly to the resident route and not assume that the old G authorisation remains the correct** **category once residence has shifted to Switzerland.** 29

For **third-country** **nationals,** the practical position is more restrictive and the official materials are thinner on direct conversion language. SEM's public guidance says third-country labour admission is limited to qualified workers in restricted numbers, and the employer must obtain the work authorisation. Swiss official materials located for this pack do **not** establish a general entitlement for a third-country G holder who moves into Switzerland to be "converted" administratively to a B permit.The safer view for internal use is that the person may need a fresh residence/work authorisation under the ordinary third-country regime, and shouldnot be described as having an automatic in-country status-change right merely because they previously held G status. 5

**On temporary stays, dual accommodation and trial moves,** the official sources support only a narrow safe proposition. A Swiss weekly accommodation can coexist with G status if the foreign main residence remains real and the weekly return continues; SEM's directives and Ticino's official guidance say so, and Vaud's G form expressly allows for a Swiss secondary residence to be declared. What I did **not** find is any official federal statement that a "trial move" to Switzerland may be ignored while the person keeps using G status, where the reality has already moved beyond secondary accommodation and the foreign main residence is no longer genuine. Thatis a genuine drafting limitation and should be acknowledged in any product copy. 10

As to **employee** **and** **employer obligations,** the minimum high-confiddence propositions are these. The employee must present the permit to the employer without being asked. For EU/EFTA frontier workers, employer changes and foreign-address changes must be notified tothe canton before the new activity begins. Ticino says a new activity and a change of workplace must be notified before the new engagement; Geneva's FAQ shows a form-based cancellation practice when frontier-worker status in that canton ends. Employers who persist in employing workers who are not authorised can face administrative sanctions, future-admission restrictions and cost liability, while unlawful residence/work carries criminal risk on the worker side as well. 30

**For** **renewal,** the key legal point is simple even if federal materials do not spell it out in one sentence: renewal on a G basis presupposes that the person is still a frontier worker. EU/EFTA sources require residence abroad and weekly return; third-country sources require residence in the neighbouring border zone and weekly return. A person whose foreign residence is no longer genuine is therefore on weak ground for renewal as a frontier worker, because the category itself no longer matches the facts. 7

### Drafting cautions

Do not describe a G permit as a Swiss residence permit. The authorities treat it as a frontier-worker permit whose logic depends on foreign residence, even though the document itself is a Swiss-issued immigration authorisation. 31

Do **not** collapse three concepts into one: **factual residence, communal registration,** and **immigration** **authorisation.** Official materials treat them as related but distinct layers. Internal content should do the same. 32

Do not say or imply that a person who has moved to Switzerland can simply wait until renewal.The safer and better-supported position is that residence and address changes trigger prompt registration and notification obligations, and that the correct permit route should be regularised without assuming the old G category still fits. Where you say this, present it as a conservative conclusion from the sources rather than as a quoted statutory sentence. 4

For **EU/EFTA** cases, avoid categorical "conversion" language. Prefer: "where a frontier worker actually takes up residence in Switzerland, the case normally moves onto the Swiss resident route, usually L or B depending on the employment duration, subject to the ordinary EU/EFTA conditions." ³3

For **third-country** cases, be even more conservative. Prefer: "if the frontier-worker facts no longer exist, a new Swiss residence/work authorisation may be required under the ordinary third-country regime; no general automatic conversion right was identified in the official sources reviewed." 5

When discussing **secondary accommodation or weekday stays in Switzerland,** say only that official sources permit this where the foreign main residence remains genuine and the weekly return continues, with local overnight-stay notification where required. Do not stretch that into a general permission for an undeclared relocation. 10

Keep **tax** **and** **social-security** **consequences** outside the main rule statements. They may arise as adjacent risk context, but the immigration authorities reviewed here focus on residence facts, permits, labour-market admission and notification duties, not on tax residence or social-security coordination. 34
