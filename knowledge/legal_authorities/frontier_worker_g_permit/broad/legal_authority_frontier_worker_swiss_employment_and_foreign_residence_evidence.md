

Swiss frontier-worker evidence pack
Executive legal summary
A. Executive legal summary. Swiss G-permit evidence sits on two different legal tracks, and internal
content should keep them separate. For EU/EFTA frontier workers, the controlling framework is the
Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. The official SEM materials and Annex I of that Agreement
describe a frontier worker as a person resident in an EU/EFTA state who works in Switzerland and
returns to the foreign residence as a rule daily, or at least once a week; the canton of employment may
issue a special permit, usually for the duration of a contract of more than three months and less than
one year, or for five years where the work lasts at least one year. For third-country frontier workers,
the controlling framework is the FNIA/AIG route: the person must have a durable right of residence in a
neighbouring state, must have resided for at least six months in the neighbouring border zone, must
work in the Swiss border zone, and must return abroad at least once a week. Labour-market controls
also apply to that route.
The legal basis for requiring evidence is not a canton-created checklist alone. Federal law imposes a
duty to cooperate in migration proceedings: applicants and relevant third parties must provide
accurate and complete information about facts essential to the regulation of stay and must submit the
evidence required without delay. The implementing ordinance further indicates that the admission
requirements are treated as obviously fulfilled only where the filed documents establish the legal or
treaty basis of the claim and the applicant complies with that duty; the same ordinance also allows
authorities to require original identity documents.
On the employment side, the core official evidence is ordinarily the employment contract or employer
attestation. For free-movement cases, SEM guidance repeatedly ties permit duration to the duration of
the contract and expects the written employment evidence to identify the employment relationship.
Cantonal forms and pages commonly ask not only for the contract, but also for the Swiss employer’s
identity, the Swiss work address, the role, the start date and the workload; some also ask for salary
data, but that is not universal.
On the residence and commuting side, the law requires a real foreign residence and the continuing
reality of frontier-worker status. Official canton practice is not uniform. Some cantons publicly ask for a
foreign domicile certificate or mairie attestation, especially where residence abroad is central to
eligibility; others publish forms that collect the foreign address but do not expressly list a domicile
certificate as a standard attachment for all EU/EFTA first applications. If the worker stays in Switzerland
during the week, official sources say that this is compatible with frontier-worker status, but the stay
must be notified to the Swiss commune where the person is staying and the person must still return to
the foreign main residence at least weekly. Just as importantly, Swiss law does not treat paperwork as
self-curing: a contract, a rental arrangement or a business formation step can support an application,
but they do not independently create an immigration entitlement if the facts are weak.

Primary legal texts
D. Primary legal texts. The first primary source is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons.
Annex I, article 7 defines the employed frontier worker and states that the worker returns to the foreign
residence daily as a rule, or at least once a week. It also provides that no residence permit is required as
such, although the authorities of the state of employment may issue a special permit, and that on
extension the worker must furnish proof of actually pursuing an economic activity. Article 8 then
preserves occupational and geographical mobility. Article 13 performs the same function for self-
employed frontier workers.
The second primary source is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration for the non-free-
movement route. Article 25 sets the entry conditions for cross-border commuters. Article 35 requires
weekly return to the foreign residence, provides that the permit is time-limited and extendable, and
after five uninterrupted years gives a right to extension subject to there being no revocation grounds.
Article 39 governs employment mobility for G-permit holders, including job change, canton change and
change to self-employment. Article 40 confirms cantonal competence to grant G permits and, where the
person has no direct right to work, requires a cantonal employment-market preliminary decision. Article
90 supplies the general procedural duty to cooperate and to file the required evidence.
The third primary source is the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. Article 1a
defines employed activity broadly, including work for a Swiss or foreign employer. Article 6 states that
admission requirements are treated as obviously met where the filed documents demonstrate the legal
basis of the claim and the applicant complies with the duty to cooperate. Article 6(2) is especially
important for evidence drafting: it says that steps such as renting accommodation, buying property,

address before the new activity begins.
Official guidance and administrative materials
E. Official guidance / administrative materials. SEM’s public-facing factsheets and FAQ materials are
the clearest federal administrative statements for ordinary G cases. They state that EU/EFTA frontier
workers must reside in an EU/EFTA state, have a Swiss employer or Swiss-based self-employed activity,
and return to the foreign residence daily as a rule or at least weekly. They also confirm that week-stays
in Switzerland are allowed, provided the person registers with the commune of stay. The same
materials tie the permit’s duration to the employment contract and say that the employer name is
written on the permit.
SEM’s VFP directives provide the densest official material on the evidence side. They state that the
employment documents must show the employer’s and employee’s particulars, the duration of the
relationship and the degree of employment, because only then can the authority assess whether
worker status exists and which permit category is correct. They also say that where the filed
employment declaration or employer’s certificate is obviously inconsistent with the actual economic
circumstances, the permit may be refused or withdrawn. This is one of the strongest official statements
that frontier-worker evidence is about factual reality, not form alone.
The same directives clarify two further points that matter for evidence packs. First, a G permit is not for
a worker posted by a foreign service provider. Secondly, for EU/EFTA frontier workers, changes relating
to the employer, the company seat, the business address or the foreign address must be notified to the
competent authority before the new activity begins, and failure to observe that duty may be sanctioned.
For third-country cases, the AIG directives confirm that border-zone treaties continue to define the
relevant zones, that the labour-market authority must be involved at least by preliminary decision, and
that labour-market rules must be strictly applied. Their border-zone table also summarises the six-
month foreign residence requirement, weekly return, labour-market dependency, and the restrictions
on job change in the first five years.
The SEM home-office circular of 5 April 2022 is relevant, but only with care. It addresses the
immigration characterisation of home office performed from Switzerland, especially for persons
working for foreign employers or engaging the Swiss labour market. It does not set a general federal
rule for classical frontier workers who live abroad and perform some work remotely from abroad. The
circular is therefore useful mainly as a warning that immigration, tax and social-security
characterisations can diverge, and that internal content should not create unsupported immigration
percentage rules for hybrid frontier-worker patterns.

Cantonal practice notes
F. Cantonal practice notes.Geneva publishes the most detailed public frontier-worker pages. For EU/
EFTA employed frontier workers it asks for a valid identity document, photograph and employer
attestation or contract; it says the person must be domiciled in an EU/EFTA state, employed by a
company domiciled in Switzerland, and return at least once a week. For non-EU/EEA workers it goes
much further, demanding a civil-status document, a mairie-issued proof of residence in neighbouring
France showing more than six months’ stay, a durable foreign residence title, a signed employment
contract, labour-market search evidence, the candidate’s CV and qualifications, and an employer letter
explaining the role and economic interest. Geneva also publishes separate pages for employer change
and for third-country workers leaving Geneva to live in neighbouring France while keeping the same
Geneva job; that latter route requires evidence of two uninterrupted years with the same employer and
a receipt for the French residence-title application, with a copy of the French residence title to follow at
renewal.
Vaud publishes a first-application form for European frontier workers and a document-list PDF. The
form asks for the foreign address, explicitly asks whether the person also has a secondary residence in
Switzerland, identifies the Swiss employer, and asks for the type of activity, date of entry into service,
activity rate and the Swiss place of work in the canton. The attached public document list requires
identity proof and a signed work contract for employed frontier workers, but, unlike Geneva or
Fribourg, does not in that published list expressly require a foreign domicile certificate for all EU/EFTA
employed first applications. That makes Vaud a useful reminder that public documentary practice
differs by canton.
Fribourg publishes a simpler but very clear public checklist. The worker must provide the frontier-
worker declaration/application form, a passport photo, a valid identity document, an attestation of
domicile abroad, and either the employment contract or an employer attestation specifying the
duration of the engagement and the level of employment. Fribourg also states that a frontier worker
may either return daily abroad or take accommodation in Switzerland, in which case the worker must
return abroad at least once a week and notify the Swiss commune of stay.
Ticino publishes current new-permit and renewal/mutation pages, together with a standing warning
sheet. The new-permit page says the frontier worker who does not return daily must notify the Swiss
commune where he or she sleeps, and in all cases must return to the principal residence abroad at least
once a week. The renewal/mutation page requires renewal fourteen days before expiry and notification,
within fourteen days, of changes such as the foreign address or civil status. The warning sheet states
that the worker and employer must notify all changes in the circumstances underlying grant or renewal,
that changes in foreign residence address must be reported within fourteen days, that job changes
must be notified with a copy of the employment contract, and that the permit may be revoked for false
information or breach of permit conditions. The warning sheet is old, but it remains publicly posted and
is still useful as evidence of the canton’s administrative logic.

form requires, for example, the worker’s foreign address, the employer, the Swiss assignment place, the

Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
G. Practical legal analysis. The safest internal view is that a G-permit file has three evidential pillars:
Swiss employment, foreign principal residence, and continuing frontier-worker reality.
Employment evidence is the most straightforward pillar. Official materials repeatedly anchor ordinary
cases in a written contract or employer attestation. What matters most is not just the existence of a
contract, but whether the file shows the elements that the authorities use to categorise the permit:
employer identity, duration, start date, role and workload. Swiss worksite evidence is also important
because several official forms ask for the precise Swiss place of activity, and SEM’s public materials
describe frontier workers as working in Switzerland.
Residence evidence is more uneven. The legal rule is clear enough: the worker must truly live abroad,
and for third-country nationals that foreign residence must meet tighter border-zone and durable-
status requirements. What varies is the documentary packaging. Geneva and Fribourg publish official
demand for an attestation of foreign domicile, and Geneva’s non-EU materials specifically ask for mairie
proof of more than six months’ stay and a durable foreign title. By contrast, Vaud’s and Aargau’s public
EU/EFTA forms collect the foreign address but do not, in the materials reviewed, set out the same
standard demand for a foreign municipal certificate in every first application. For internal drafting, the
correct approach is therefore to treat official municipal residence evidence as the strongest published
proof where available, and to describe foreign lease documents, title deeds or utility bills only as
corroborative documents, especially when the foreign residence is newly created or likely to be
scrutinised.
Commuting evidence requires the most restraint. The legal obligation is obvious: daily return as a rule
or at least weekly for EU/EFTA, and at least weekly for the FNIA route. But the official immigration
materials reviewed do not identify a general federal filing rule requiring routine train tickets, toll logs or
petrol receipts in standard G-permit applications. What the official materials do say is that if the worker
stays in Switzerland during the week, that Swiss stay must be notified to the commune of stay. The
better internal position is therefore that routine commuting documents are fact-sensitive
corroboration, not a universal checklist item. Adjacent tax materials, such as Zurich’s Gre-3 non-return
certificate, can help evidence actual patterns, but they remain tax-side documents rather than standard
immigration filings.

For renewals and post-grant compliance, federal language matters. The AFMP route expressly links
extension to proof that the frontier worker is actually pursuing an economic activity, and SEM’s VFP
directives warn that if the employment declaration is plainly inconsistent with the real economic
circumstances, the permit can be refused or withdrawn. For third-country cases, the file must continue
to satisfy the underlying border-zone and labour-market logic, and mobility remains constrained before
five years of uninterrupted work. In practice, that means that payslips, payroll records, AHV evidence
or tax-side material may become useful corroboration for ongoing reality, especially on renewal or
where facts are disputed, even though the ordinary initial public checklists often focus on the contract
rather than recent payroll.
Remote or hybrid work is the area where published immigration authority is thinnest. Federal SEM
materials for frontier workers still assume work in Switzerland and, in the forms reviewed, ask for a
Swiss work address or employer location. SEM’s home-office circular, however, addresses a different
problem: people physically in Switzerland and working from Switzerland, especially for foreign
employers or in arrangements with Swiss-market impact. It does not create a published federal
immigration safe harbour for a frontier worker who lives abroad and carries out a material part of the
employment remotely from abroad. Internal evidential content should therefore avoid categorical
statements such as “up to 40% remote work is always acceptable” unless a canton has published
immigration-specific guidance to that effect.
H. Drafting cautions.
Do not write as if there were one uniform Swiss G-permit regime. EU/EFTA frontier workers and
third-country frontier workers sit on materially different legal foundations with different
residence, border-zone and labour-market conditions.
Do not convert canton-specific published documents into universal federal rules. A foreign
domicile certificate is clearly required in some published canton materials, but not in every
public EU/EFTA form reviewed.
Do not imply that a signed contract, a Swiss room, a Swiss secondary address, or any other
single document automatically proves frontier-worker status. Federal ordinance text and SEM
guidance both point the other way: paperwork supports a claim, but the authorities may test the
underlying reality.
Do not describe the appearance of an employer name on an EU/EFTA G permit as meaning that
every employer change requires a fresh substantive immigration approval. Federal free-
movement law preserves occupational mobility, even though notification duties remain.
Do not present tax or social-security documents as core immigration evidence unless an official
form or canton page actually asks for them. They are often adjacent evidence instead: useful to
corroborate ongoing employment or return patterns, but not a universal immigration filing
standard.
