A. Executive legal summary
EU/EFTA frontier-worker cases in Switzerland are analysed under the Agreement on the Free Movement
of Persons and its implementing framework, not under the ordinary third-country labour-admission
regime. At treaty level, an employed frontier worker is a national of a contracting party who resides in
one contracting party and works in the other, returning to the place of residence as a rule daily or at
least weekly; frontier workers do not need a residence permit, although the state of employment may
issue a special permit. Swiss administrative practice reflects that rights-based structure: EU/EFTA
frontier workers may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA area, work anywhere in Switzerland, and enjoy
professional and geographical mobility; no border-zone or prior border-zone residence requirement
applies. For employment of three months or less in a calendar year, the route is generally the online
notification procedure rather than a G permit.
Non-EU frontier-worker cases are different in kind. They are assessed under the Foreign Nationals and
Integration Act and related domestic rules. Under Article 25 AIG/FNIA, a foreign national may only be
admitted as a cross-border commuter if they have a permanent right of residence in a neighbouring
state, have lived for at least six months in that neighbouring border zone, and work within the Swiss
border zone. They must return to their residence abroad at least once a week. Swiss official guidance
adds that initial third-country G permits are usually issued for one year, are ordinarily limited to the
border zone of the issuing canton, and require permission for job change or change to self-
employment.
The labour-market rules for non-EU frontier workers are also route-specific and should not be
overstated. Article 25 paragraph 2 AIG expressly disapplies Articles 20, 23 and 24 AIG. Read
conservatively, that means the ordinary Art. 20 quota rule, the general Art. 23 personal-qualification
rule, and the Art. 24 housing rule are not the operative admission tests for G-permit frontier-worker
admission. However, SEM’s AIG directives make clear that Articles 21 and 22 AIG still matter and must be
applied strictly: the labour-market authorities must be involved at least by preliminary decision, and
priority for the domestic/EU/EFTA labour market plus Swiss salary and working-condition compliance
remain central.
This creates the core comparative point for drafting: EU/EFTA G-permit cases are not domestic labour-
market admissions at all, while non-EU G-permit cases are domestic labour-market admissions but
under a specific frontier-worker carve-out, not under the full standard third-country worker
framework. The most common legal error is therefore to import EU/EFTA free-movement assumptions
into non-EU cases. The second most common error is the opposite one: to import the full non-EU L/B
permit assumptions, including annual quota and “highly qualified worker” language, into third-country
G-permit cases despite Article 25 paragraph 2 AIG.

D–E. Primary legal texts and official guidance / administrative
materials
D. Primary legal texts
The primary treaty source is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. For frontier workers, the
key provisions are Annex I Articles 7 and 13, which define employed and self-employed frontier workers
as persons resident in one contracting party and economically active in the other, returning daily as a
rule or at least weekly. Annex I Article 8 and Article 14 then anchor occupational and geographical
mobility; Article 9 and Article 15 address equal treatment. For short-term cross-border services, Annex I
Article 17 is the key route and is distinct from frontier-worker status.
For non-EU frontier workers, the core federal source is the AIG/FNIA. Article 25 defines admission.
Article 35 governs the permit itself, including weekly return, time-limited status, and the five-year right
to extension. Article 39 governs work outside the border zone, canton change, employer change, and
change to self-employment. Article 40 confirms cantonal competence and the need for an employment-
market preliminary decision where the person does not already have a right to work. Article 21 contains
the precedence rule, and Article 22 the wage and working-condition rule, both of which remain
operative for third-country G cases.
The federal implementing structure is completed by the VFP/OLCP for EU/EFTA cases and by the treaty-
defined border-zone agreements for non-EU cases. SEM’s AIG directives list the relevant cross-border
agreements with Germany, France, Italy, Austria and Liechtenstein and emphasise that exact
recognised foreign border localities should be checked with the labour-market authorities of the
adjoining Swiss cantons.
E. Official guidance and administrative materials
SEM’s public EU/EFTA G-permit pages are concise but legally important. They plainly state the operative
contemporary rule: EU/EFTA frontier workers may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA area, work anywhere in
Switzerland, return daily as a rule or at least weekly, and receive five-year permits for indefinite or
longer-term employment. The same pages confirm the notification-only route for activity of up to three
months in a calendar year.
SEM’s public non-EU G-permit pages are equally important because they restate the domestic frontier-
worker conditions in a way fit for operational drafting: foreign border-zone residence, work in the

The two SEM directive sets are what turns those public pages into a workable authority pack. The 2026
VFP/OLCP directives explain that EU/EFTA frontier workers no longer face border zones, no longer need
prior residence in a border zone, and enjoy comprehensive professional and geographical mobility; they
also say clearly that the frontier-worker permit is unavailable for workers posted by a foreign service
provider. The 2026 AIG directives explain that, for third-country G cases, the border zones stem from the
frontier-worker agreements, that Articles 21 and 22 AIG must be strictly observed, that labour-market
authorities must participate by preliminary decision, that temporary work outside the border zone is
only exceptional, and that temporary work-agency lending is generally unavailable on first issue.
Finally, SEM’s short-term notification page is indispensable for keeping route boundaries clean. It
confirms that EU/EFTA citizens taking up employment in Switzerland for up to three months are
generally in the online notification regime, and it gives the separate rules for posted workers and
service providers. That page should be used as a guardrail against incorrectly funnelling short EU/EFTA
work or service provision into the G-permit analysis.
F–G. Cantonal practice notes and practical legal analysis
F. Cantonal practice notes
Geneva publishes unusually clear operational guidance. For third-country salaried frontier workers, the
employer must file with the OCPM about six to eight weeks before the proposed start date; the worker
may only work after the permit is issued; the permit is tied to a specific employer; and the route cannot
be used simply to facilitate a job search. Geneva also states that the applicant must have durable
residence rights in a neighbouring country, six months’ residence in the neighbouring border zone, and
weekly return abroad. On labour-market proof, Geneva expressly requires the employer to show that
the vacancy was announced promptly to the cantonal employment office and that recruitment efforts
were made in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area.
Geneva also illustrates how third-country self-employed frontier-worker cases can be evidentially
heavier than the federal texts alone might suggest. Its official page requires durable residence in
France, six months in the neighbouring border zone, weekly return, Swiss economic-interest
justification, adequate autonomous income, and financial/operational evidence for the business.
Geneva further states that these applications are examined by the OCIRT labour office and submitted
for opinion to the cantonal tripartite commission, with a stated twelve-week handling period.
Basel-Stadt makes the procedural split between EU/EFTA and third-country cases very visible. Its official
frontier-worker page says EU/EFTA applicants may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA area and work
anywhere in Switzerland, with five-year validity for longer engagements and notification for work up to
ninety days. The same page then states, separately, that third-country nationals need a durable right of
residence in a neighbouring state, six months in the border zone, and compliance with labour-market
rules; their G permit is issued for one year and job change or shift to self-employment is subject to
authorisation. Basel-Stadt further channels EU/EFTA mutation filings through the Migrationsamt, while
sending third-country G applications, extensions, employer changes, conversions and extra part-time
job requests through the Amt für Wirtschaft und Arbeit.

Ticino highlights day-to-day compliance obligations. Its service page states that a frontier worker who
does not return home daily must notify the municipality where they stay overnight in Switzerland,
although in all events they must return to the main residence abroad at least weekly. Ticino also
requires the start of every new gainful activity and any job change to be notified before the new

based employer, the page adds a very practical evidential step: identity-document checking at
designated gendarmerie posts, with the certified copy then attached to the application.
Vaud’s public materials are thinner in narrative form but still useful as practice evidence. The canton
publishes dedicated G-permit forms and a document-matrix showing which supporting documents
must accompany frontier-worker applications depending on the applicant’s situation. That is a reminder
that even where the federal legal test is uniform, document packages and filing architecture remain
canton-specific.
G. Practical legal analysis
For EU/EFTA cases, the legal analysis should start with the AFMP question: is the applicant an EU/EFTA
national residing in an EU/EFTA state and taking up genuine employment or self-employment in
Switzerland? If yes, the next questions are practical rather than labour-market restrictive: is the activity
over three months or within the notification regime; what is the contract duration; what Swiss
employment or Swiss business-seat evidence is available; and what needs to be notified on mutation.
Border-zone residence, prior residence in a neighbouring zone, and domestic worker priority are the
wrong tests for this route.
For non-EU cases, the analysis should start instead with the statutory gate in Article 25 AIG:
neighbouring-state durable residence right, six-month residence in the neighbouring border zone, and
work in the Swiss border zone. Only once those route-specific conditions are met should the labour-
market review be built out, including priority and Swiss pay/working conditions, and then the specific
permit-control issues such as job-change consent, self-employment consent, or exceptional activity
outside the border zone. In practice, the evidence bundle is likely to need proof of the foreign status,
proof of the exact foreign locality and border-zone residence history, proof that the Swiss worksite lies
within the authorised Swiss border zone, and labour-market evidence from the employer.
A particularly important analytical nuance is that third-country G cases are not simply “ordinary third-
country employment permits for people living abroad”. The general non-EU work-permit page says
third-country workers can ordinarily be admitted only if they are well qualified, and it describes the
standard labour-market search and salary control. But Article 25 paragraph 2 AIG carves frontier
workers out of the general Art. 20 quota rule and the Art. 23 qualification rule. The legally precise
position is therefore narrower and more route-specific: third-country G cases remain restricted and
labour-market controlled, but the controlling restrictions are the frontier-worker residence/border-zone
conditions plus Articles 21 and 22, not the whole standard L/B permit architecture.
The final practical point is route separation. EU/EFTA frontier-worker analysis should be kept distinct
from cross-border service-provider analysis. SEM’s own materials say a frontier-worker permit may not
be issued to workers posted by a foreign undertaking, and the up-to-ninety-day service/short-
employment rules operate through the notification procedure. If content blurs those routes, it will
produce the wrong legal test and the wrong filing path.

H. Drafting cautions
Do not write as though “frontier worker” means the same thing for all nationalities. For EU/EFTA cases,
current Swiss practice abolishes border zones and prior border-zone residence; for non-EU cases,
border zones and six months’ prior residence in the neighbouring border zone remain fundamental.
Do not write that non-EU G cases are analysed “like EU/EFTA cases but with extra documents”. That is
wrong. EU/EFTA cases depend on treaty-based free movement. Non-EU cases depend on domestic
admission under the AIG/FNIA, with labour-market control, weekly return, and route-specific border-
zone requirements.
Do not write that all third-country work-permit assumptions apply unchanged to third-country G
permits. Article 25 paragraph 2 AIG expressly disapplies Articles 20, 23 and 24 AIG. A statement such as
“third-country frontier workers must fit the annual quota and highly-qualified-worker rules” would
therefore be legally misleading unless tied to some separate route outside Article 25.
Do not overstate non-EU mobility. Third-country G permit holders may work temporarily outside the
border zone only on a limited basis, need prior approval to move the centre of their work to another
canton, and need authorisation for job changes until they reach the five-year statutory entitlement
point. Nationwide free mobility is an EU/EFTA concept, not a third-country G-permit concept.
Do not equate employer change in EU/EFTA cases with employer change in non-EU cases. EU/EFTA
materials point to notification/mutation of changes before the new activity and to cantonal
announcement procedures. Non-EU materials point to substantive authorisation review tied to Articles
21 and 22 AIG.
Do not forget edge cases. British nationals are not automatically EU/EFTA cases after Brexit; preserved-
rights analysis may still matter. Likewise, outdated material on Croatian transitional restrictions is now
unsafe, because SEM says Croatia has been on full free movement since 1 January 2025.
Open questions and limitations. Official public materials are strong on the headline rules but less
complete on some operational details. In particular, canton-by-canton evidential expectations for third-
country self-employed frontier workers, temporary work outside the border zone, and exact locality-by-
locality border-zone mapping remain fragmented and often delegated to cantonal labour-market
authorities. SEM’s own directives say detailed questions on recognised foreign border localities should
be taken to the bordering cantons. Liechtenstein also sits within a special treaty setting that is not fully
unpacked in the present pack. Those points should be treated as matters for targeted follow-up before
publishing highly granular filing guidance.
