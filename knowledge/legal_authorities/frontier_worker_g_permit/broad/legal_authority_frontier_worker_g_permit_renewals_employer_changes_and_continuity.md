Executive overview
A. Executive legal summary
Swiss frontier-worker status is legally structured in two different ways, and this distinction drives almost
every renewal and change question. For EU/EFTA nationals, the regime is treaty-based, rooted in the
Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and implemented through the Swiss ordinance on free
movement; official SEM guidance identifies, for frontier workers, Annex I AFMP Articles 7, 13, 28 and 32
together with OLCP provisions including Article 4(3), Article 4(3bis) and Article 9(3). For non-EU / third-
country nationals, the regime is narrower and derives from the FNIA/AIG, especially Articles 25, 35 and
39, together with the border-zone agreements and the general labour-market admission rules.
For EU/EFTA frontier workers, continuity of status depends on three elements remaining true: the
person must continue to reside in an EU/EFTA state, must continue to work in Switzerland, and must
continue to return to the foreign main residence as a rule daily or at least weekly. Official SEM materials
also confirm that EU/EFTA frontier workers have occupational and geographical mobility throughout
Switzerland, and that border zones no longer apply to them. A G permit is generally required only
where the employment lasts more than three months; if the contract lasts between more than three
months and less than one year, the permit duration tracks the contract, while contracts of one year or
more, or indefinite contracts, normally generate a five-year permit.
For non-EU frontier workers, continuity is materially tighter. The person must continue to hold a
durable residence right in a neighbouring state, must have the required residence connection to the
recognised foreign border zone, must work within the Swiss border zone, and must return at least
weekly to the foreign main residence. The permit is usually valid for one year and is restricted to the
border zone of the issuing canton. Official SEM materials expressly state that changing employer or
profession requires authorisation, and the SEM directives add that renewals remain dependent on
labour-market conditions, with a statutory claim to extension after five years of uninterrupted
employment.
Renewal should not be described as automatic in either regime. Official materials consistently frame
renewal as an application or filing supported by current evidence. For EU/EFTA cases, the evidence base
is usually light but real: identity document, current employer attestation or contract, and change-related
documents if personal details have altered. Cantonal forms sometimes ask for more, such as a copy of
the current G permit and a recent payslip. For non-EU cases, the evidence is usually heavier and can

Employer changes and workplace changes must be handled conservatively. For EU/EFTA frontier
workers, the federal rule is one of professional and geographical mobility, but official SEM guidance still
requires advance notification of changes of employer, enterprise seat or professional address, and
foreign address to the competent canton before the new activity starts. Cantonal practice then
operationalises that rule by requiring change filings and updated cards or records. For non-EU frontier
workers, the position is much stricter: change of employer, change of profession, and in practice other
material changes to the authorised employment can require a fresh authorisation and a renewed
labour-market examination.
Continuity becomes fragile where employment is interrupted. The official materials gathered do not set
out a broad, general “retention” route for a G permit during extended periods without qualifying
employment. Instead, they assume continuing employment or a timely new filing. Ticino expressly says
that if the worker stopped the frontier activity more than six months ago and/or the permit has expired,
a new permit application is required; St Gallen says, for third-country cases, that the permit ends when
the employment ends and that the employer must report the actual end date and return the original
permit. This should be reflected in drafting by treating continuity after breaks as fact-sensitive and by
avoiding broad claims that frontier-worker status simply continues through unemployment.
B. Structural overview of renewal and change rules
At federal level, the legal structure is layered. The first question is always which personal regime
applies. EU/EFTA frontier workers are governed primarily by the treaty-based free movement
framework and its Swiss implementing ordinance. Third-country frontier workers are governed
primarily by the FNIA/AIG, the admission ordinance and the old-style border-zone arrangements
preserved for that category. SEM guidance then explains how those rules are to be applied
administratively, and cantons operationalise them through forms, checklists, mutation notices and
work-canton practice.
Within that structure, the practical sequence for any renewal or change question is:
whether the person still satisfies the underlying frontier-worker conditions;
whether the current permit is still the correct immigration category;
whether the change is merely an update/notification or instead requires a fresh
authorisation; and
what documentary evidence the competent canton asks for to prove continuity.
For EU/EFTA cases, the decisive continuity indicators are foreign residence in the EU/EFTA area, ongoing
Swiss employment or self-employment, and the required return pattern. For non-EU cases, there is an
additional geographic constraint: foreign border zone, Swiss border zone, and continued satisfaction of
labour-market conditions. That is why cantonal practice is much more form-heavy and restrictive in
non-EU files.

Legal and administrative authorities
D. Primary legal texts
The primary legal texts fall into two clusters.
For EU/EFTA nationals, the controlling framework is the Agreement on the Free Movement of
Persons and the Swiss Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons. The SEM OLCP directives
expressly identify, for frontier workers, Annex I AFMP Articles 7, 13, 28 and 32 together with OLCP
Article 4(3), Article 4(3bis) and Article 9(3) as the operative legal bases. The same SEM directives also
confirm that the same approach extends to EFTA nationals through the amended EFTA Convention,
subject to Liechtenstein-specific arrangements.
For non-EU nationals, the core federal text is the FNIA/AIG, especially Articles 25, 35 and 39, as
explained in the SEM AIG directives. Article 25 is the entry point for admission as a frontier worker; the
SEM materials connect Article 35 to weekly return and to renewal/extension questions; and Article 39 is
the key rule on job changes and cross-cantonal border-zone activity. The SEM directives also place these
rules within the wider labour-market admission system and list the bilateral border-worker
arrangements that still define the relevant border zones for third-country cases.
The border-zone treaties/arrangements with neighbouring states remain legally relevant for third-
country frontier workers. The SEM AIG directives list the relevant instruments for Germany, France, Italy,
Austria and Liechtenstein and use them to explain that border zones continue to matter for third-
country G permits, even though they no longer matter for EU/EFTA frontier workers.
E. Official guidance and administrative materials
Three SEM materials are especially load-bearing for this topic.
The first is the SEM public page on the EU/EFTA G permit, which gives the clearest official summary of
the ongoing conditions for EU/EFTA frontier-worker status, the abolition of border zones, the rule on
daily or weekly return, and contract-based validity periods.
The second is the SEM EU/EFTA frontier worker factsheet, which adds two operationally useful points:
it states expressly that the permit may be renewed if the employment continues, and that the
employer’s name appears on the permit card. It also confirms that unemployed frontier workers
normally claim unemployment benefits in their state of residence, which is useful background but
should not be over-read into a general immigration retention rule.
The third is the pair of detailed SEM directives: the OLCP directives for EU/EFTA cases and the AIG
directives for third-country cases. These are the most precise official sources on change-of-employer
notifications, sanctions for failing to notify, the continued relevance of border zones for third-country
frontier workers, movement to another canton’s border zone, and the relationship between renewal
and labour-market conditions.
F. Cantonal practice notes
The cantonal materials gathered are not merely procedural; they also reveal how continuity is evidenced
in practice.

Geneva provides a clear split between EU/EFTA and non-EU frontier workers. For an EU/EFTA change of
employer, Geneva requires filing 14 days before the new employment, with a valid ID, the new
employer’s attestation or signed contract, and the certificate ending the previous employment. For
renewal with the same employer, Geneva says the employer usually receives an expiry notice one month
before expiry; if not, renewal is filed online with a valid ID, employer attestation or contract, and civil-
status evidence if any details changed. Geneva also states, in its FAQ, that where the person will work in
a different canton, the Geneva G permit should be cancelled and a new frontier application made in
the new work canton, even if the employer is unchanged. For non-EU employer changes, Geneva
requires the new employer to file 6–8 weeks in advance and makes clear that cases under five years, or
conditioned permits, remain subject to restrictive legal criteria.
Vaud publishes particularly useful forms. Form G2 is expressly for renewal or modification of a frontier
permit for Europeans and includes renewal, employer change, foreign-address change, change of
address within Vaud, cessation of work, ancillary activity and duplicate requests. The attachment list
shows current practice: ID copy, copy of the frontier permit, signed employment or mission contract,
and a copy of the latest payslip for renewal; civil-status documents are requested where relevant.
Vaud’s form also records the foreign address and any Swiss secondary address, which is important for
continuity analysis.
Ticino is unusually explicit on timing. It requires renewal 14 days before expiry; changes to data shown
on the permit, including personal data, foreign address and civil status, must be reported within 14
days of the change; and any new employment activity or change of job must be notified before the new
job begins. Ticino also states that if the frontier activity ended more than six months ago and/or the
permit has expired, the person must submit a new permit application. Its online forms confirm that a
modification filing is available only where the new occupation starts within six months of the prior one
and the permit has not expired.
Basel-Stadt shows how an EU/EFTA mobility right still translates into cantonal document control. Its
page says EU/EFTA frontier workers may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA area and work anywhere in
Switzerland, but still requires a mutation filing where the person changes job, leaves the job, changes
foreign address, civil status or name. For third-country cases Basel’s online form expressly covers new
issue, reissue, renewal, job change, conversion and an additional part-time post, showing that
canton practice treats many changes as application-driven events rather than mere notes to the file.
St Gallen and Zurich are especially useful for third-country cases. St Gallen’s official third-country
frontier-worker sheet says employer-change applications require the original permit and the new
contract; renewals must be filed two weeks before expiry; work at the new job may begin only after the
relevant work authorisation is obtained; and the permission ends when the employment ends, after
which the employer must report the exit date and return the original permit. Zurich states that third-
country frontier workers require a labour-market pre-decision for first employment, job changes and
change to self-employment.

Practical legal analysis
G. Practical legal analysis
What must remain true for frontier-worker status to continue
For EU/EFTA holders, the continuing status test is conceptually simple but operationally important:
residence must stay outside Switzerland and inside the EU/EFTA area; the work must remain in
Switzerland; and the return pattern to the foreign main residence must be preserved. SEM’s public
materials repeatedly frame the foreign residence as the main residence and the return obligation as
daily “as a rule”, or at least weekly. If the person stays in Switzerland during the week, that is compatible
with frontier-worker status, but the weekly stay must be handled through the local communal
registration rules rather than by treating the person as resident in Switzerland for immigration
purposes.
For non-EU holders, those same elements remain necessary, but they are not sufficient. The foreign
residence must remain in a recognised foreign border zone; the Swiss worksite must remain within the
Swiss border zone; the person must continue to hold a durable residence right in the neighbouring
state; and the labour-market conditions that justified admission remain in play for renewals and change
cases. In other words, third-country frontier-worker continuity is not just about commuting behaviour; it
is also about remaining within the narrow legal geography and labour-market channel that allowed the
permit in the first place.
Evidence likely to be required at renewal
The official materials do not support a single Swiss-wide checklist. Instead, they support a two-level
statement.
At federal level, renewal evidence for EU/EFTA frontier workers is chiefly proof that the contract and
identity remain current. SEM’s factsheet says renewal is possible where employment continues; the
OLCP directives speak of renewal/prolongation before expiry; and Geneva and Vaud practice show what
this looks like in real files: current passport or ID, employer attestation or current contract, current
permit, and any civil-status evidence if the record has changed. Vaud also asks for a recent payslip in
renewal/modification practice.
For non-EU frontier workers, the evidence base is broader because the eligibility conditions are broader.
Geneva requires the current passport, portrait photo, civil-status extract, proof of domicile in
neighbouring France showing more than six months’ stay, proof of durable foreign residence status, the
signed contract and the previous employer’s end-of-employment certificate. St Gallen requires, for
employer changes, the original permit and a copy of the new contract; Basel’s online process for third-
country cases also expects residence and labour-market evidence, including the existing permit and
current residence certificate.
As to the return pattern itself, the gathered official sources are strikingly practical. They impose the
rule very clearly, but the forms generally verify continuity indirectly through foreign-address
information, foreign residence certificates, and registration of any weekly stay in Switzerland, rather
than by asking for a commuting diary. That is an inference from the materials, not an express federal
statement, but it is the safest reading of the evidence gathered.

Employer changes, occupation changes and workplace changes
For EU/EFTA frontier workers, employer change is best understood as a notification-plus-update
problem, not as a labour-market-admission problem. Federal guidance gives EU/EFTA frontier workers
occupational and geographical mobility, but the same federal guidance also says that changes of
employer and certain other registered details must be announced to the competent canton before the
new activity begins. Geneva therefore requires a short employer-change filing with the old and new
employer evidence. This means content should avoid both extremes: it is wrong to say that EU/EFTA
employer changes are freely ignored, but it is equally wrong to say they always require a fresh
substantive work authorisation.
A change of job role or occupation for EU/EFTA frontier workers should generally be analysed in the
same way. The federal rule is professional mobility, and no surveyed official source reintroduces a prior
labour-market test for a simple change of role within the EU/EFTA frontier-worker regime. That said,
some cantonal practice records the employer name on the permit and requires notification where the
employment setting materially changes. The cleanest drafting line is therefore that EU/EFTA frontier
workers normally benefit from occupational mobility, subject to any required cantonal notification and
to profession-specific regulatory rules outside immigration law.
For non-EU frontier workers, the answer is much stricter. A change of employer or occupation is
expressly authorisation-required in official SEM material. Zurich’s page adds that job changes and
moves to self-employment need a labour-market pre-decision. Geneva then shows how that works
administratively: where the frontier worker has fewer than five uninterrupted years, or where the
permit is specially conditioned, the new employer-change filing remains subject to restrictive legal
criteria including economic interest and priority. This strongly supports a cautious drafting position that
a fresh labour-market review may apply again in non-EU change cases, especially within the first five
years and where the authorised job profile changes materially.
The work-canton point should also be separated by regime. For EU/EFTA frontier workers, federal law
removes substantive border-zone restrictions, but the permit remains administered by the canton of
work. Geneva expressly requires cancellation of the Geneva G permit and a new application in the new
work canton if the person will work in another canton, even with the same employer. That should be
treated as an administrative work-canton rule rather than as a substantive loss of EU/EFTA mobility. For
non-EU frontier workers, by contrast, the federal rule itself is restrictive: a permanent move of the
centre of activity to another canton’s border zone requires a new G permit, and work outside the border
zone requires another permit category altogether.
Changes in workload, salary and employment conditions
For EU/EFTA cases, the official sources gathered do not support a blanket statement that every change
in percentage workload, salary or ordinary contractual conditions must be separately authorised. The
better reading is narrower. The duration of the contract affects the length and even the type of permit,
so a change that moves the employment relationship across the legal thresholds of under three
months, over three months, or one year and above may affect the immigration basis. Renewal and
change forms also inspect the current contract and, in some cantons, recent payslips, which means
these matters may be examined evidentially even if they are not standalone filing triggers under federal
law.
For non-EU cases, the position is more sensitive because labour-market law is built into admission.
Official SEM and cantonal materials repeatedly refer to labour-market rules, domestic-priority rules and
compliance with wage and working conditions. Basel frames this expressly for third-country G permits;

Interruptions in employment and continuity of status
This is the area where drafting needs the most discipline.
The official materials gathered do not show a general Swiss rule that frontier-worker G status simply
survives an extended interruption in employment. Instead, they show a system that expects either
continuing qualifying work or a timely new filing. Ticino is the clearest: if the person stopped working as
a frontier worker more than six months ago and/or the permit expired, a new application is needed. St
Gallen, for third-country cases, says the permit ends when employment ends. Geneva’s renewal route is
framed only for persons still working for the same employer, while its employer-change route assumes
a timely filing before the new job begins.
For EU/EFTA unemployment, the legal picture may in broader AFMP terms be more nuanced than the
cantonal G-permit pages suggest, because worker-status doctrine exists elsewhere in the free
movement framework. But the gathered official frontier-worker materials themselves do not provide a
clear, dedicated retention pathway specifically for G permits during longer breaks. Internal immigration
content should therefore avoid saying that an EU/EFTA frontier worker can always keep or renew a G
permit during unemployment. The safer statement is that renewal and continuity will usually depend on
showing current qualifying employment, unless a more specific authority on retained worker status is
being separately relied on.
Drafting cautions
H. Drafting cautions
Do not write as if all G permits are part of one legal regime. The difference between EU/EFTA treaty-
based frontier-worker rights and non-EU labour-market-controlled frontier-worker admission
should appear early and stay visible throughout.
Do not write that renewal is automatic. Even where the legal conditions remain satisfied, official
sources still describe a renewal filing, renewal notice, or renewal application supported by current
evidence.
Do not write that an employer change is always unrestricted. For EU/EFTA cases, employer changes
normally require notification before the new activity begins. For non-EU cases, employer changes
plainly sit inside the authorisation system and may trigger renewed labour-market scrutiny.
Keep federal legal rules separate from cantonal document practice. For example, weekly return is a
federal continuity requirement; the precise evidence used to show continuity varies by canton. Vaud
asks for particular renewal documents, Geneva uses an expiry-notice workflow, Ticino imposes 14-day
deadlines, and Basel uses mutation forms.

Treat worksite changes carefully. In EU/EFTA cases, the core federal rule is mobility, but work-canton
administration may still require cancellation and re-filing when the centre of work moves to another
canton. In non-EU cases, the federal rule itself can force a new G permit or a different permit category if
the work moves outside the relevant border zone.
Do not conflate the immigration-law concept of “frontier worker” with the tax concept. SEM’s OLCP
directives expressly note that the tax-law and immigration-law concepts do not coincide.
Open questions and limitations
This pack is strongly grounded in official SEM and cantonal materials, but some areas remain thinner
than others.
The surveyed official materials are strong on status conditions, permit duration, employer change,
work-canton handling and documentary practice. They are weaker on a single, consolidated federal
statement covering every form of workplace move within the same canton, and they do not provide
a complete official federal rulebook on retention of G-permit status during unemployment or long
employment gaps, especially for EU/EFTA cases. The safest internal drafting position is therefore to
present continuity through interruption as fact-sensitive and to avoid broad retention claims unless a
