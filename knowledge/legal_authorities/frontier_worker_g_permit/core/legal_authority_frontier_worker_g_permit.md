

Swiss frontier worker G permit legal authority
pack
Executive legal summary
Swiss frontier-worker status is a work authorisation category for a person who lives abroad and works
in Switzerland; it is not the same thing as Swiss residence. For third-country nationals under the
Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, admission as a frontier worker requires a durable right of
residence in a neighbouring state, residence for at least six months in the neighbouring border
zone, and work within the Swiss border zone. The frontier-worker permit is limited in duration,
requires the holder to return to the foreign residence at least once a week, and may be renewed; after
five years of uninterrupted gainful activity there is a statutory right to renewal unless a revocation
ground exists. EU/EFTA frontier workers are governed primarily by the Agreement on the Free
Movement of Persons and its implementing regime; SEM’s official guidance states that they may live
anywhere in the EU/EFTA area, work anywhere in Switzerland, and must return to their main
residence abroad as a rule daily, or at least once a week.
The EU/EFTA and non-EU routes are materially different. EU/EFTA frontier workers benefit from
professional and geographical mobility, no longer being confined to border zones, and the permit is
valid for five years if the contract is indefinite or lasts more than one year; for contracts of more than
three months but less than one year, validity follows the contract term. By contrast, third-country
frontier workers remain bound to the neighbouring-state/border-zone model, must satisfy Swiss
labour-market rules, are usually granted a permit valid for one year, and remain subject to employer/
job-change constraints, especially during the first five years.
The correct legal characterisation of the arrangement matters. A G permit is for a person who is
genuinely a frontier worker. It is not the right route where the person has in substance moved to
Switzerland and should hold a Swiss residence permit; where the person is on local Swiss residence
and local hire; or where the person remains employed abroad and is merely posted or providing cross-
border services. SEM’s free-movement guidance expressly states that a frontier-worker permit cannot
be issued to service providers sent by a foreign undertaking, and SEM’s notification guidance
distinguishes frontier-work permits from the notification procedure used for EU/EFTA short-term
employment up to three months and for cross-border services up to 90 workdays per calendar year.
For drafting and content-system purposes, the safest operational rule is this: the G route requires a
credible foreign main residence, real Swiss work activity, and a factually coherent commuting
pattern. Evidence that tends to matter includes foreign residence records, proof of durable residence
rights abroad where relevant, the employment contract or self-employment evidence, the Swiss work
location, and records showing that changes of employer, address, or activity have been notified where
official practice requires it. Official materials are comparatively strong on eligibility, validity, changes,
and border-zone issues, but they are thin on a developed migration-law doctrine for hybrid or
remote frontier-working models.

Structural overview of the G permit route
The Swiss G permit route has two distinct legal architectures. For EU/EFTA nationals, the route sits
within the free-movement regime. SEM states that EU/EFTA frontier workers are nationals of EU/EFTA
states who reside in an EU/EFTA state and work in Switzerland in an employed or self-employed
capacity; they must return to their main residence abroad as a rule every day, or at least weekly. No
border zones apply to them any longer, and they may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA area and work
anywhere in Switzerland. For employment of less than three months, the usual route is not a permit at
all but the online notification procedure; if the work exceeds three months, a permit or frontier-worker
permit is required if the relevant conditions are met.
For third-country nationals, the legal route is much narrower. FNIA article 25 permits admission as a
frontier worker only where the person has a durable right of residence in a neighbouring state, has
lived for at least six months in the neighbouring border zone, and works in Switzerland within the
border zone. FNIA article 35 then requires weekly return abroad and makes the permit limited in time
but extendable. SEM’s official non-EU page summarises the same position in practice terms: third-
country nationals need a permanent or durable residence status in a neighbouring country, six months’
residence in the neighbouring border zone, and compliance with Swiss labour-market requirements;
the permit is usually valid for one year and limited to the border zone of the issuing canton.
A notable structural point is that not all admission criteria apply in the same way to third-country
frontier workers as to other non-EU workers. FNIA article 25 paragraph 2 expressly disapplies articles
20, 23 and 24 for frontier workers; in other words, the special frontier regime does not import every
ordinary admission requirement. However, SEM’s directives make clear that the labour-market rules in
articles 21 and 22 FNIA still matter and must be applied strictly, even for short assignments. In
practice this means that priority of the domestic/EU labour market and compliance with local wage and
working conditions remain central in third-country G cases.
The route also has a specific internal mobility logic. Under FNIA article 39, a third-country frontier-
worker permit holder may work temporarily outside the border zone, but moving the centre of activity
to the border zone of another canton requires a permit from the new canton; after five years of
uninterrupted activity there is a right to canton change and, after five years, a right to employer
change. The VZAE adds a practical overlay: after five continuous years, non-EU/EFTA frontier workers
must notify a change of job to the competent authority before starting. By contrast, SEM’s EU/EFTA
guidance treats frontier workers as enjoying professional and geographical mobility, while still requiring
administrative notification of changes to employer, address, and related details.
The route should therefore be understood as a status for cross-border labour, not a generic “working
near Switzerland” category. Its legal core is different from Swiss residence, from detached-worker/
service-provider regimes, and from purely foreign remote work. That distinction is central to correct
drafting.

Primary legal texts and official guidance
The primary federal texts for this route are the FNIA (SR/RS 142.20), especially articles 25, 35, 39, 40
and 62, and the VZAE/OASA (SR/RS 142.201), especially articles 1a, 3, 13a, 71a and 71b. FNIA article 25
supplies the third-country frontier-worker admission criteria; article 35 sets the weekly return rule and

the right to extension after five uninterrupted years absent revocation grounds; article 39 governs
activities outside the border zone, canton changes, employer changes, and moving into self-
employment; article 40 confirms that the cantons issue these permits; and article 62 supplies revocation
grounds. VZAE article 1a provides the immigration-law concept of employed activity, article 3 defines
cross-border service provision, and article 13a adds the post-five-year reporting rule for non-EU/EFTA
frontier workers.
For EU/EFTA frontier workers, the primary supranational source is the Agreement on the Free
Movement of Persons and its implementing ordinance/guidance. SEM’s January 2026 VFP directives
identify the frontier-worker rules in Annex I AFMP, in particular articles 7 and 13, and explain that EU/
EFTA frontier workers enjoy full professional and geographical mobility and are outside the old border-
zone logic. The same directives also make an important boundary point: a frontier-worker permit is not
for a service provider posted by a foreign undertaking.
The most useful official administrative materials are SEM’s route pages and directives. The EU/EFTA
G-permit page gives the current high-level rule set for weekly return, no border zones, and permit
validity; the non-EU G-permit page summarises the stricter neighbouring-state/border-zone model for
third-country nationals; the SEM factsheet on EU/EFTA frontier workers adds practical statements on
contract length and self-employment; the AIG directives and VFP directives provide the most detailed
operational statements on statutory interpretation, labour-market control, mobility, address
notifications, and the relationship between frontier-worker concepts in tax and migration law.
The official materials also support two particularly important route-boundary rules. First, short-term
employment by an EU/EFTA national with a Swiss employer, for up to three months in a calendar year,
ordinarily belongs under the notification procedure, not a G permit. Secondly, cross-border services
by posted workers or independent contractors belong under the services regime; up to 90 workdays
per calendar year the AFMP liberalises such services and uses notification, whereas service provision
over 90 days requires a work authorisation and there is no entitlement to receive one.
Cantonal practice notes
Official cantonal materials broadly track the federal distinction between EU/EFTA mobility and third-
country restriction, but they are useful because they show what evidence is actually requested and
how changes are handled in practice. Geneva’s English and French pages distinguish EU/EFTA and non-
EU cases; they confirm that the employer must apply in third-country cases, that UE/AELE frontier
workers working more than three months need a G permit, and that EU/EFTA change-of-employer cases
are treated by an announcement rather than as a new labour-market application. Geneva’s FAQ also
says the G permit is not available for simple job search, that end of employment must be notified,
and that a change of employer or foreign address must be announced within 14 days.
Geneva’s non-EU frontier-worker page is particularly valuable for evidence and compliance points. It
requires, among other things, a valid passport copy, photograph, civil-status document, proof of
residence in neighbouring France for more than six months, a copy of the durable foreign residence
title, the signed employment contract, labour-market search evidence, CV, diplomas and work
certificates, and an employer letter explaining the post and the economic interest. It also states, as
cantonal practice, that the employee may work only once the permit has been issued, and that labour-
market scrutiny includes priority of the Swiss/EU labour market, ordinary salary and working conditions,
and usually a full-time post. Those full-time and evidential requirements should be treated as Geneva
practice points, not universal federal rules.

Basel-Stadt’s official page confirms the federal EU/EFTA model in cantonal language: EU/EFTA frontier
workers may live anywhere in the EU/EFTA and work anywhere in Switzerland; the permit is issued for
five years where the assignment lasts at least one year, or for the shorter contract term where it does
not. Basel-Stadt also states that changes to self-employment, foreign residence, and any weekly stay in
Switzerland must be notified. Its online application materials for frontier-worker cases involving third-
country nationals list documents such as the valid passport, signed employment contract, current
frontier permit if any, proof of long-term residence status in France or Germany, and a current
certificate proving at least six months’ prior residence in the relevant border zone.
Ticino’s official G-permit pages add two useful administrative points. First, the canton expressly warns
that a frontier worker who does not return daily must notify the commune in which they stay overnight
in Switzerland. Secondly, for renewals and modifications, Ticino requires the foreign national to seek
renewal 14 days before expiry, notify changes to personal data and foreign address within 14 days,
and notify any new gainful activity or change of employment before starting the new work. If the
worker ended frontier activity more than six months earlier, or the permit has expired, Ticino treats the
matter as a new permit application.
Vaud’s official forms point in the same direction. Official snippets from Vaud materials show that
frontier-worker permits are issued through the commune of the workplace, and that practical
evidence on modification or renewal includes a valid identity document, a copy of the frontier permit,
and the employment contract or mission contract. Vaud’s G1 form materials also request extensive
evidence for self-employed or special categories. Those materials are useful as evidence checklists, but
they are cantonal forms, not an independent statement of federal law.
Practical legal analysis
A genuine G-permit case starts with foreign residence as the principal residence. For a third-country
national, that is a legal condition embedded in FNIA article 25 and reinforced by the weekly return rule
in article 35. For an EU/EFTA national, it is a free-movement condition expressed by SEM as return to the
main residence abroad daily as a rule or at least weekly. The legal consequence is that a person who
has in substance shifted the centre of life to Switzerland ordinarily does not fit the frontier-worker
route, even if they keep some foreign mailing address. Swiss weekly lodging is not impossible; SEM’s
VFP directives and Ticino’s practice both contemplate weekly stays in Switzerland, but only as an
incident of cross-border work, not as a substitute for genuine foreign residence.
The second pillar is real work in Switzerland. For third-country nationals, FNIA article 25 requires work
in Switzerland within the border zone, while FNIA article 39 allows only limited temporary activity
outside that zone. For EU/EFTA nationals, border-zone limits have been removed, but the logic is still
that the person is working in Switzerland. This is why a wholly foreign remote-working arrangement
does not naturally fit the G route, and why official materials are better read as presupposing an actual
Swiss worksite or a genuinely Swiss economic activity. The official migration materials reviewed do not
set out a developed federal quantitative test for hybrid or remote frontier working. That absence should
itself be treated conservatively in future drafting.
The third pillar is the nature of the working relationship. Swiss immigration law defines employed
activity broadly: it covers activity for an employer with a seat in Switzerland or abroad, and it is
irrelevant whether salary is paid in Switzerland or abroad. That means payroll mechanics and salary-
payment location are not decisive on their own. But the broader frontier-worker route still demands
that the activity legally belongs in the frontier-worker category rather than in the services/posting
category. VZAE article 3 defines cross-border services as a temporary service in Switzerland carried out

under a contractual relationship by a person or company domiciled abroad. SEM’s notification guidance
builds a separate regime for that category, and SEM’s VFP directives expressly say that frontier-worker
permits are not issued to service providers sent by foreign undertakings. In practice, the key question is
not “who pays the salary?” but rather “what is the real legal and economic arrangement: Swiss work by a
frontier worker, or foreign service provision into Switzerland?”
For third-country nationals, labour-market review remains central. SEM’s AIG directives say plainly that
articles 21 and 22 FNIA must be construed strictly for frontier-worker applications, even for short
assignments, and that the labour-market authorities must at least participate by way of prior decision.
Geneva’s official practice illustrates the expected evidence: proof of domestic/EU labour-market
searches, proof of the vacancy’s earlier notification to the employment authorities, and evidence that
ordinary salary and working conditions will be respected. These points matter both at first grant and
when the arrangement changes materially. They are also one reason why content should never imply
approval is automatic.
On changes and renewals, the safest distinction is again between EU/EFTA and third-country cases.
EU/EFTA frontier workers enjoy free-movement mobility, but SEM still requires notification of changes to
employer, business seat, work address, or foreign address before the new activity starts, and cantonal
practice uses change forms. Third-country frontier workers need permission for employer change
during the first five years, after which the law gives a right to employer change and the ordinance
imposes prior notification. Ticino’s practice adds strict local deadlines for renewal and modification,
while Geneva requires prompt notification of end of employment and change of employer.
The compliance-risk profile is therefore narrower than many public summaries suggest. High-risk fact
patterns include a sham or weakly documented foreign address; a person who in practice resides in
Switzerland; a person who no longer returns abroad weekly; a route that is in substance job-search
rather than frontier work; unnotified employer or address changes; third-country temporary-agency or
labour-lending structures that Swiss law generally excludes for first permits; and attempts to use a
frontier-worker permit for what is actually a posting or cross-border service. The FNIA’s revocation
grounds, Geneva’s warning about criminal exposure for unauthorised employment, and SEM’s strict
labour-market guidance support a conservative compliance stance.
One further drafting point is especially important for content systems: tax, social-security, and
migration labels are not interchangeable. SEM’s VFP directives explicitly state that the tax-law and
migration-law concepts of “frontier worker” are not the same. Social-security and health-insurance
materials may be relevant to evidence or practical consequences, but they should not be used to define
migration eligibility unless the migration source itself says so.
Drafting cautions
Future content on the Swiss G route should be written in deliberately narrow terms. It should say that
the route is for a person whose main residence remains abroad and who is genuinely carrying out
gainful activity in Switzerland, with different legal regimes for EU/EFTA and third-country nationals.
It should not suggest that all people who live near Switzerland and work for Swiss-related businesses fit
the route, and it should not imply that holding or renewing a G permit is automatic.
The most important drafting avoids five recurrent errors. First, do not collapse EU/EFTA and third-
country rules into a single generic statement; border zones and labour-market tests remain crucial for
third-country nationals, but not for EU/EFTA frontier workers. Secondly, do not describe the G permit as
a residence permit or as a route for moving to Switzerland. Thirdly, do not treat a posted worker,

Content should also be careful with mobility language. For EU/EFTA frontier workers, “no authorisation

cantonal approval/notification rules continue to matter.
A short limitations note is warranted. On the materials reviewed, official migration sources are strong
on definitions, border zones, permit validity, short-term notification, changes of employer, and the
distinction from services/posting. They are thinner on the immigration treatment of modern hybrid/
remote-working patterns and on a detailed federal evidential matrix for proving the practical reality of
commuting. Where future content addresses remote work, it should be framed conservatively as a risk
area rather than as a settled doctrine.
