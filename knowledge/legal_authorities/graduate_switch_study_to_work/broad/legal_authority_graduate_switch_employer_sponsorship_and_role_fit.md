# Swiss legal authority pack on employer sponsorship and role fit in graduate study-to-work transitions

This pack is written for internal immigration-law use. It addresses the Swiss legal framework that matters when a graduate moves from Swiss study residence to work-authorised residence, with a particular focus on employer sponsorship, role fit, qualification relevance, salary, contract evidence, and the employer-side justification expected by the authorities. The analysis is aimed principally at third-country nationals, because SEM treats EU/EFTA nationals under separate free-movement rules and places the graduate facilitation discussed here inside the non-EU/EFTA admission framework. 1

## Executive legal summary

### A.Executive legal summary

A switch from Swiss study residence to work-authorised residence is **not** **automatic.** Once studies end, further stay is governed by the general admission rules. For salaried work, Swiss law requires that the employment serve Switzerland's overall economic interest, that an employer file the application, and that the admission conditions in Articles 20 to 25 FNIA be met. Article 21 paragraph 3 FNIA creates a limited facilitation for foreign nationals with a Swiss university degree: they may be admitted without the ordinary resident-worker priority test if the proposed employment is of **high** **scientific** **or economic** **interest,** and they may be provisionally admitted for **six months after completion of Swiss education** **or** **training** to find such corresponding work. 2

**The** **employer** is **central** to the legal analysis. Swiss law does not create a free-standing "graduate sponsorship" category; instead, employer involvement is built into the ordinary admission rules. A salaried work case requires an employer's application, and SEM's official application-document checklist requires, at minimum, the employment documents, proof of qualifications, reasons the vacancy must be filled, a job description or duties list, and information on the company. The cantonal authority screens first, and in ordinary third-country work cases approved by the canton the matter is forwarded to SEM for approval; however, SEM's 2026 directives state that cantonal preliminary decisions for Swiss higher-education graduates under Article 21 paragraph 3 are **not** subject to the SEM approval procedure. 3

The decisive legal question is not simply whether the candidate graduated in Switzerland, but whether the **proposed role sufficiently matches the graduate's studies and serves a qualifying Swiss** **interest.** SEM's directives say that the relevant candidates are graduates of Swiss higher education who can use their acquired abilities at **a** **high** **level** and in areas where there is not already sufficient labour supply. The same directives state that general work areas with **no** **qualified connection** to the completed studies remain excluded, giving examples such as purely administrative or study-remote tasks, and they add that the completed education must match the job profile. This makes role mismatch, underemployment and generic entry-level placement a live legal risk in graduate-switch cases. 4

<!-- 1 -->

The employer must also prove that the **salary** **and** **employment conditions** meet local, professional and sector standards. Article 22 FNIA requires customary local/professional/sector conditions,and Article 22 OASA adds that the employer must submit a work contract or mandate confirmation showing the duration of the activity, the terms of employment and the salary. SEM's public guidance further says that the application should include a contract signed by both parties and marked so that it applies only if the work permit is granted; Geneva's official guidance likewise expects a signed contract and recommends salary benchmarking, including by the SECO wage calculator. 5

**On** **priority** **and** **quotas,** the law is facilitative but not exemptive. Article 21 paragraph 3 FNIA waives the ordinary resident-worker priority test for eligible Swiss-graduate cases, and SEM's directives expressly confirm that admission under that provision takes place **without** **examination** **of priority.** But the same directives also state that the other admission conditions in Articles 20 onwards remain applicable, so first-time B and L permits remain subject to quotas under current law. SEM's own legislative-project page confirms that a proposal to exempt Swiss higher-education graduates from annual quotas is only **a** **proposed** **amendment** and was remitted to the Federal Council in late 2023; it is not described as in force. 6

The publicly available official materials show a consistent pattern: the strongest graduate-switch files are those where the employer can clearly demonstrate the company context, business need, exact duties, degree-role fit, salary compliance, and why the proposed role is of high scientific or economic interest. Weak files tend to be those built around vague emmployer enthusiasm, generic junior work, undifferentiated "skills shortage" assertions, or missing commercial and documentary support. That conclusion is an inference from the recurring official document demands in SEM, Zurich and Geneva materials; the authorities do not publish a single codified "weak-points list." 7

## Structural overview and practical legal analysis

### B. Structural overview of employer sponsorship and role fitG. Practical legal analysis

The legal structure is sequential. A student permit does not convert of itself into a work permit. Article 27 paragraph 3 FNIA states that further stay after completion or abandonment of education is governed by the **general admission** **conditions,** and Article 54 OASA says that where a short-stay or residence permit was granted for a specific purpose, a **new** **permit** is **required** if **the** **purpose** **of stay** **changes.** For a graduate switching from studies to employment, that means a fresh work-authorisation analysis rather than a simple continuation of student status. 8

The employer-side logic begins with **Article** 18 **FNIA.** For salaried work, the activity must serve the overall economic interest, an employer application must exist, and the conditions in Articles 20 to 25must be satisfied. SEM's public guidance mirrors that structure: applicants must explain and document why admission is in Switzerland's overall economic interest, and employers must file the application with the competent cantonal labour-market or migration authority. Geneva's practice page is explicit that the Swiss company must lodge the application and recommends doing so roughly **six** **to** **eight** **weeks before entry.9**

For graduates of Swiss higher education, Article 21 paragraph 3 FNIA changes only one part of the test: **priority.** It does not remove the need for a legally adequate role, customary salary, quota availability,or a convincing employer case. SEM's directives are particularly clear on this point. They say that this facilitation is meant to enable Swiss companies,including **large** **companies, SMEs and** **start-ups,** and Swiss academic institutions to recruit well- and highly-qualified specialists who completed their studies in Switzerland; but they also say that only roles in which the graduate can use the acquired abilities at a high level, and where there is not already sufficient labour supply, qualify. The same directives exclude general work areas lacking a qualified connection to the completed course of study. 10

<!-- 2 -->

That makes the **degree-role** **mapping** a central evidential question. A sound application should show, in concrete terms, how the degree content leads into the proposed duties, why the duties are at an appropriately skilled level, and why the role is not a generic post-study foothold. The SEM directives' examples of qualifying work are typically in research and development, new technologies, or the application of acquired know-how infields of high economic interest. They say that high economic interest may exist where there is a proven labour need in the sector corresponding to the graduate's training, where the completed specialism is highly specialised and tailored to the job, or where filling the job will immediately create additional jobs or new mandates for the Swiss economy. 11

The **salary** element is not a secondary formality. It performs a protective and evidential function. At federal level, Article 22 FNIA requires compliance with local, professional annd sectoral norms, while Article 22 OASA lists the materials by which the authority will verify that point: a work contract or mandate confirmation stating duration, conditions and salary. SEM's public labour-market page adds that authorities use this control to protect foreign workers from abusive terms and the Swiss labour market from wage dumping, and that employers can help by usingstandard sector forms. In practice, Geneva expressly asks employers to demonstrate respect for usual wage and working conditions and says that attaching the extract from the **national** **SECO** **salary** **calculator** is useful. 5

The graduate's **qualifications** remain relevant even where the candidate has a Swiss degree. Article 23FNIA limits ordinary work authorisation to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, and requires the person's professional qualification, professional and social adaptability, language skills and age to support sustainable integration for residence-permit cases.SEM's general guidance says qualifications are checked by CV, diplôme evidence and work references. Zurich's official guidance likewise states that the professional qualifications must match the new activity, and for shortage occupations it notes that some roles not strictly requiring a university degree may still be approvable if there is at least a vocational qualification or at least five years' experience; but that is a broader third-country practice point, not a restatement of the graduate-switch rule itself. 12

**Resident-worker priority** is modified, but not every labour-market control disappears. SEM's directives say that admission of Swiss higher-education graduates under Article 21 paragraph 3 occurs without examination of priority. However, SEM's separate public guidance on vacancy notification under Article 21a FNIA says that, for professions with unemployment at or above the threshold, vacancies to be filled by third-country nationals must be registered with the regional employment service unless an exception applies, and employers must attach proof of that registration. The official materials reviewed do **not** clearly publish a general Swiss-graduate exemption from Article 21a vacancy-notification duties. Accordingly, it is prudent to treat Article 21 paragraph 3 as a waiver of **priority,** not necessarily a blanket waiver of **all labour-market notification obligations.** 13

On **timing,** the employer should file before employment begins and before the graduate's lawful basis for stay expires. SEM's procedure page says employees can only begin work after registration, and Geneva states that, depending on the category, work may begin only once permission has effectively been granted. Because Article 21 paragraph 3 FNIA gives Swiss graduates six months of provisional post-study admission to find corresponding work, that period should be used to secure and file the employer-led application rather than to begin speculative work. Article 29a FNIA also provides that persons staying only for job-search purposes have no entitlement to social assistance, underscoring the provisional and limited nature of this bridge period. 14

<!-- 3 -->

**Start-ups** **and** **small employers** can sponsor these cases, but they face a heavier evidential burden on business viability and economic interest. SEM's directives expressly say the graduate facilitation can benefit large companies, SMEs and start-ups. Yet SEM's application-document checklist requires start-ups to produce the deed of foundation, commercial-register entry, and a **three-year** **business plan** covering organisation, staffing development, financing, market and competition analysis. Geneva's official template similarly tells recent or newly created companies to explain their genesis, shareholders and financial means and to provide a three-year business plan; Zurich's guidance also points to a business plan, market prospects and financial statements as evidence of overall economic interest.In practical terms, smaller employers usually need more explanation, not less. 15

A final practical point is that **employer** **preference** **is** **not** **legal sufficiency.** It is not enough to say that the graduate is "well known to the company", "a good cultural fit", or "a strong junior hire". The official sources repeatedly ask for legally specific material: reasons the vacancy must be filled,a job description, proof of qualifications, evidence of the company situation, and a demonstration of overall economic or scientific interest. When the proposed role is under-levelled,generic or only weakly connected to the graduate's field, Vaud's 2024 official response indicates that refusals hnave often been linked to roles requiring a level of qualification **well below** that acquired by the graduates. 16

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts

The principal statutory texts are the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment.** For employer sponsorship and role-fit analysis, the most important FNIA provisions are Article 18 (overall economic interest; employer request; cross-reference to Articles 20-25), Article 20 (quota power), Article 21 paragraphs 1 to 3(priority and the Swiss-graduate exception, plus the six-month post-study bridge), Article 21a (vacancy-notification measures), Article 22 (salary and working conditions), Article 23 (personal requirements), Article 27 paragraph 3 (further stay after studies follows the general admission rules), Article 29a (jobseekers have no social-assistance entitlement), and Article 30(1)(g) (facilitating international economic, scientific and educational exchange). 33

On the OASA side, the key provisions are Article 20 and Annex 2 on quota numbers, Article 22 on how usual salary and working conditions are assessed and evidenced, Article 38 on ancillary student work during studies, Article 40 on post-graduate work in an academic specialisation context, and Article 54 on the requirement for a new permit where the purpose of stay changes. Article 22 OASA is particularly useful for evidence drafting because it says the employer must provide a work contract or mandate confirmation showing the duration of the activity, the conditions of engagement and the salary. 34

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

<!-- 8 -->

The most important administrative source is the **SEM** **FNIA** **directives,** chapter 4, point 4.4.6, in the 1January 2026 version. That section gives the clearest official explanation of the Swiss-graduate route: it identifies the target employers (including SMEs and start-ups), explains the meaning of high scientific and high economic interest, insists on a high-level and direct connection between degree and role, excludes unrelated/general wwork, confirms the priority waiver, and confirms that the other admissions conditions remain in force. It also states that cantonal preliminary decisions on these graduate cases are not subject to the SEM approval procedure. 4

SEM's public pages on the **basis for admission to the labour market, procedure,** **and application** **documents** are also important because they show the administrative expectations that recur in practice. Those pages explain the dual admission system; the overall economic-interest requirement; the ordinary priority and vacancy-notification rules; compliance with local/professional/sectoral salary standards; the need for an employer-filed application; and the minimum documentary package, including the contract, recruitment evidence where priority applies, proof of qualifications,reasons the vacancy must be filled, a role description, company information,and-for start-ups-a three-year business plan with staffing, financing and market analysis. 35

Finally, SEM's current legislative-project pages on the **proposed** **quota** **exemption** for Swiss higher-education graduates are useful not because they state current law, but because they show that the quota exemption remains **reform** **material,** not enacted lawv. For present drafting purposes, those pages are best used as a negative authority: they help explain why internal content should not yet say that Swiss-graduate cases are exempt from annual quotas. 36

## Cantonal practice notes and drafting cautions

### F. Cantonal practice notes

**In** **Zurich,** the official labour-market materials are strict about the employer-facing file. The canton states that third-country work authorisation can only be applied for by the employer, that first-time short-stay and residence permits are quota-limited, and that applications are individually assessed. Its 2024 explanatory note lists the materials that may be used to show overall economic interest: a **specific** **application** **rationale** (mandatory), business plan, and financial statements. For priority cases it asks for RAV and EURES advertising, dated advertisements and a candidate list with nationality, qualifications, application date and reasons for rejection. For salary it expects a dated, mutually signed contract; for qualifications it states that professional qualifications must match the new activity. 37

In **Geneva,** the official practice materials are even more explicit about the structure of the employer letter. The canton says the Swiss company should lodge the third-country application around **six** **to** **eight** **weeks** before entry. Its OCPM page lists the main annexes: the candidate's ID, CV, diplomas and work certificates, the employer's motivation letter, proof of search efforts on Swiss and EU/AELE markets, information on the company and staffing situation, and the signed employment contract. Geneva's official employer-letter template then drills down into what the letter should say:company history, activities, staff composition and existing permit types; annual accounts and,if there are losses, the recovery plan; for new companies, genesis, shareholders, financial means and a **three-year** **business** **plan;** a role description and an explanation of how the role supports the company's development; and concrete evidence of the **economic** **interest** **for** **the** **canton.** 38

The same Geneva template is especially useful for graduate-switch caution. It states that if the selected person has a Swiss higher-education diploma and the employer did not conduct recruitment searches for that reason, the employer must still prove why the recruitment is of overriding economic or scientific interest for **Geneva,** and the canton **recommends** **still carrying** **out searches** even for that profile. It also asks the employer to explain why this candidate was chosen and to demonstrate compliance with customary salary and working conditions, noting that the SECO national wage-calculator extract is useful. 30

<!-- 9 -->

In **Vaud,** the most useful public material found was an official 2024 response of the Conseil d'État. It is not a binding directive, but it is an official cantonal exposition of practice. It confirms that Swiss higher-education graduates may obtain residence authorisation where the proposed work is of overriding scientific or economic interest, that the employer is not subject to the priority principle in such cases, and that the other admission conditions remain applicable, especially salary and conditions customary to the locality, profession and branch. It also states that, in Vaud's general experience, permits are granted when the role is **in** **line** **with** **the** **studies** and carries overriding scientific or economic interest, and that rare refusals often concerned a role requiring a qualification level well **below** the graduate's level. Vaud's official Form 1350 also shows the administrative granularity expected from employers, including company identity, job category, proposed activity, gross salary, start date and contract annex. 39

### H.Drafting cautions

The first drafting caution is to separate **academic qualification from work-authorisation eligibility. A** Swiss degree is legally helpful, but it does not by itself satisfy the work-permit test. Internal content should say that a Swiss university or higher-education degree can open the Article 21 paragraph 3route, but only where the role is of high scientific or economic interest and the remaining admission conditions are met. Anything stronger risks overstating the lawv. 20

The second is todraw a sharp line between **employer** **preference** **and** **legal sufficiency.** Official materials consistently demand more than a statement that the employer likes the candidate. They ask for a detailed vacancy rationale, a role description or duties list, company information, qualifications evidence, salary evidence, and an explanation of overall economic or scientific interest. Where the role is generic, poorly described or only loosely connected to the graduate's studies, the case is materially weaker. 40

The third is to avoid saying that a Swiss-graduate case is automatically **quota-exempt** or automatically outside labour-market controls. Current official materials support only the narrower proposition that ordinary resident-worker priority is waived in qualifying Article 21 paragraph 3 cases. They do not support a current-law statement that annual quotas are inapplicable; to the contrary, SEM's directives say Articles 20 ff. still apply, and SEM's own reform page shows that quota exemption remains proposed rather than enacted. The official sources reviewed also do not clearly publish a blanket exemption from Article 21a vacancy-notification duties. 41

The fourth is to treat **role** **mismatch** **and** **underemployment** as live refusal risks. The best official wording is in SEM's directives and Vaud's 2024 statement. SEM excludes administrative or study-remote tasks and requires that the completed education match the job profile. Vaud says refusals have often concerned roles whose qualification level sits well below that acquired by the graduate. Internal drafting should therefore avoid presenting a broad range of junior, rotational or unspecified "graduate roles" as presumptively approvable. 42

The fifth concerns **start-ups** **and** **small employers.** Avoid presenting them either as barred or as lightly scrutinised. The official position is that the Swiss-graduate rule can benefit start-ups, but new or small businesses are repeatedly asked to produce business plans, accounts, financing explanations, staff

composition and market-analysis materials. Content should therefore frame start-up cases as possible but documentation-heavy. 15

The sixth is geographic. When drafting for practice, it is safer to write that cantonal implementation is **not fully** **uniform** and that local labour-market authorities may seek additional evidence. Zurich and Geneva both say applications are individually assessed and that additional documents may be requested; Geneva in particular publishes a developed template for the motivation letter, while Vaud's public material is more expository than procedural. Internal content should therefore avoid promising a nationally identical document package beyond the federal minimum. 43

## Source list and limitations

### I. Source list

The principal sources used for this pack were the following official or near-official materials.

Federal legislation and ordinance materials used as primary authorities were: the **Federal Act** on **Foreign** **Nationals and** **Integration** provisions on admission to work, priority, salary/conditions, qualifications, post-study stay and exceptions; and the **OASA** provisions on quota numbers, salary/ conditions evidence, ancillary student work, post-graduate work in academic specialisation,and change of purpose of stay. 44

The key federal administrative materials were: SEM's public pages on the **basis for** **admission** to **the** **labour market, procedure, and application documents;** the **SEM FNIA directives,** chapter 4, point 4.4.6 and related passages, state 1 January 2026; and SEMI's pages on the ongoing legislative project to exempt Swiss higher-education graduates from annual quotas. 45

The cantonal materials used were: **Zurich** 2024 explanatory guidance on gainful employment of third-country nationals and the Zurich work-permit pages; **Geneva** OCPM/OCIRT pages on third-country salaried work and the official 20023 employer motivation-letter template; and **Vaud** Form 1350 plus the 2024 Conseil d'État response on Swiss higher-education graduates from third countries. 46

### Open questions and limitations

Official federal materials are strong on the statutory framework and better than average on administrative practice, but they are thinner on **published** **case** **law** and on a definitive nationwide position about whether **Article** **21a** **vacancy-notification** **duties** always remain applicable in Swiss-graduate Article 21 paragraph 3 cases. The official cantonal materials also vary in depth: Geneva publishes very detailed practical guidance; Zurich publishes a structured evidential note; Vaud's most useful public explanation found was an official political response rather than a dedicated procedural guide. For those reasons, propositions on these points have been labelled conservatively as practice points or uncertainties where appropriate. 47
