# Swiss legal authority pack on graduate switch and high scientific or economic interest

**Suggested filename:**

legalauthoritygraduateswitchhighscientificoreconomicinterestpack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

A. The current Swiss federal route for a non-EU/EFTA graduate to remain after study is narrow and two-stage. First, a person who has completed a Swiss university degree may obtain a **six-month** **short-stay** **status** to **look** **for** **a** **qualified** **job.** Secondly, if an employer offers a job, that employment may be approved **without applying resident-worker priority** if the proposed wvork is of **high scientific or** **economic** **interest.** Official SEM materials are explicit that this is an exception to priority only; it is **not** an automatic right to work merely because the person graduated in Swvitzerland. 1

Degree completion and work eligibility must be kept separate. The six-month post-study status is a job-**search** permission, not a full work permit. SEM's 2026 directives state that the graduate must have a successful Swiss university qualification, sufficient means and suitable accommodation; if those conditions are met, there is a legal entitlement to the six-month stay. The permit starts when completion is established, even if the diploma has not yet been issued, any time already elapsed after completion is counted against the six months, work during the search period is limited to **15** **hours** **per** **week,** and the six-month permit is **not extendable.** 2

The decisive substantive test for the eventual job is whether the proposed employment is of **high** **scientific** **or** **economic** **interest.** SEM's 2026 directives say that "scientific interest" is to be interpreted broadly and is not confined to STEM subjects; it can include natural sciences, social sciences and humanities, provided the statutory conditions are met. Typical examples given by SEM are roles in **research and development, the application of new technologies,** or the use of acquired know-how in activity fields of high economic interest. 3

SEM's 2026 directives also explain when a role may be of **high** **economic** **interest**: where there is a demonstrated labour-market need for work corresponding to the person's training, where the completed field is **highly specialised and tailored to the post,** or where filling the post would **directly** **create additional jobs or generate new orders for the Swiss economy.** SEM further states that general areas of work with **no qualified connection to the completed studies** are excluded, giving examples such as administrative or study-distant tasks. 4

Even where resident-worker priority is waived, the graduate route remains subject to the **other** **admission** **conditions** in Articles 20 ff. AIG. SEM's guidance and directives therefore preserve the requirements on **quotas, salary and local employment conditions, housing,** and **personal** **qualifications.** In practice, this means that an employer still needs a properly documented application and a role that can withstand scrutiny under the wider third-country admission framework. 5

On quotas, the present law remains restrictive. SEM's directives expressly say that graduates admitted under Article 21 paragraph 3 AIG are still subject to the other admission requirements in Articles 20 ff. AIG, and SEM's and Parliament's own materials show that a separate project to exempt holders of Swiss university degrees from annual quota limits has **not** entered into force and was **returned** **to** **the** **Federal** Council. The Federal Council nevertheless fixed 2026 third-country quotas at **4,500** **B** **permits** **and** **4,000** **L** **permits,** confirming that quotas continue to exist in current lawW. 6

Official sources remain conservative about who benefits from Article 21 paragraph 3 AIG. SEM's 2026directives describe the beneficiary group as persons with a **Swiss** **university** **qualification,** including Bachelor, Master and doctorate, and explain that "Swiss university" covers universities and universities of applied sciences. Public cantonal pages in Zurich and Geneva follow the same understanding. By contrast, a separate legislative project sought to expand the route further, but that proposal is not in force. Separately again, SEM's 2026 directives now treat certain holders of a **Swiss higher** **vocational** **qualification** as "qualified" for Article 23 AIG where there is a close professional fit, but that is not the same thing as saying they clearly fall within the published Article 21 paragraph 3 graduate-switch route. 7

## Structural overview of high scientific or economic interest

B. The most accurate way to describe the current structure is as follows.

A non-EU/EFTA student who completes a qualifying Swiss university course may move onto a **short, six-month** **post-study** **search** **status,** but only if there is evidence of completion, funds and accommodation. That status is still tied to the logic of the original study route and is not itself a final labour-market admission. 8

If a job is then identified, the application shifts into a **third-country** **work-permit** **analysis.** The distinguishing feature of the graduate route is that the authority may dispense with **resident-worker** **priority** under Article 21 paragraph 1 AIG. Everything else remains live unless some other rule says otherwise. SEM's directives say this expressly, and Zurich's public page mirrors it by stating that priority is not checked but other third-country rules still apply. 9

The central substantive enquiry is not whether the applicant is a graduate in the abstract, but whether **the** **role** **itself** is sufficiently connected to Swiss scientific or economic interests. SEM's examples show that the authority is looking for roles involving research, development, new technologies, advanced know-how deployment, clear shortage evidence, strong specialisation, or direct economic spillovers such as job creation or new orders. Zurich's public practice note is materially consistent with this and gives the same types of examples. 10

The degree must also relate **qualitatively** to the job. SEM's directives say the completed education mnust match the job profile and exclude general work areas with no qualified relationship to the course completed. This is the clearest official basis for rejecting ordinary entry-level office work, generic graduate schemes detached from the subject studied, or roles framed mainly as convenience hires. 4

The employer's evidence remains crucial. SEM's general guidance says total economic interest must be reasoned and evidenced by the applicant side, while Geneva's ordinary third-country page requires a signed contract, diplomas, work certificates, recruitment evidence where priority applies, and an employer motivation letter describing the company's situation, plans and reasons for the hire. Even in Article 21 paragraph 3 cases, where priority may drop away, employers should still expect to prove the business rationale, role specificity and degree fit in a similarly documentary way. The last point is an inference from the structure of the official materials rather than an express federal checklist for Article 21 paragraph 3. 11

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts

The federal architecture is stable. SEM's official labour-market page identifies the key statutory provisions as **Articles** **18** **and** **19 AIG** on overall economic interest, **Article** **20** **AIG** on quotas, **Article** **21AIG** on priority, **Article** **21a** **AIG** on job-notification duties, **Article** **22** **AIG** on local and sectoral wage and working conditions, **Article** **23** **AIG** on personal qualifications, and **Article** **24** **AIG** on suitable housing. The same page expressly places the graduate exception under **Article** **21** AIG, and SEM's directives then explain in detail how Article 21 paragraph 3 operates after a Swiss university degree. 23

For the student side of the transition, SEM's 2026 directives treat the post-study search period within **Article** **21** **paragraph** **3** **AIG,** while also using the study-permit logic of **Article** **27** **AIG** for funds and accommodation by analogy. On the regulatory side, SEM's FAQ and directives refer to **Article** **38** **VZAE** for student side-employment during studies and up to 15 hours per week during the search period,

<!-- 6 -->

**Article** **39** **VZAE** for study programmes with compulsory internships, and **Article** **40** **VZAE** for scientific specialist work during continuing education. 24

A useful present-day tension is this: SEM's labour-market pages and graduate directives continue to describe the Article 21paragraph 3 route around **Swiss universities and universities of applied** **sciences,** but SEM's 2026 directives elsewhere also recognise certain holders of a **Swiss** **higher** **vocational qualification** as "qualified" under **Article** 23 **AIG** where the role has a close professional connection. That does not, on the current published sources, amount to a clear statement that all such persons are covered by the same graduate-switch exception in Article 21 paragraph 3. 25

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important operational source is SEM's 2026 directive set. It provides the clearest federal interpretation of: the six-month search status; the broad reading of "scientific interest"; the indicators of "economic interest"; the exclusion of ordinary study-distant work; the continuing relevance of quotas and other Article 20 ff conditions; and the evidence that may be used to demonstratelabour demand, including the SECO indicator system and the list of occupations subject to job notification. 26

SEM's public FAQ and English/German labour-market pages are shorter but important because they are current, public-facingJ official summaries. They confirm the two-step structure, the continued relevance of third-country qualification rules, the salary and contract requirements, the dual admission system, and the fact that the graduate exception is a deviation from priority rather than a general work right. 27

On current legislative status, the official Parliament file for the reform project on easier admission for holders of Swiss university degrees shows that the proposal was **returned** to **the** **Federal** Council,and the SEM legislative-project page still describes it as a **running** **legislative** **project** intended to create a quota exemption. That is strongevidence that the quota exemption is **not yet** **current** law. 28

For 2026, the Federal Council's official notice confirms that third-country labour-market admission remains quota-bound, with **4,500** **B** **permits** **and** **4,000** L **permits** for third-country workers for 2026. 29

## Cantonal practice notes

F. Zurich publishes one of the clearest graduate-specific cantonal pages. It states that not every Swiss course automatically justifies work, that only areas without an already sufficient labour supply come into question, and that a role may qualify where it is of **high** **scientific** **interest,** for example in research and development or the application of new technologies, or of **high** **economic interest,** for example by contributing to the creation of new jobs or generating new orders for the Swiss economy. It also says that the employer must filethe work-permit application and that resident-worker priority is not examined for the graduate route, while other third-country rules still apply. Zurich also confirms a separate six-month job-search permission after successful completion, subject to funds, accommodation and proof of completion. 30

Geneva publishes useful procedural material from a different angle. Its post-study page says that non-EU/EFTA students who completed studies at a recognised Swiss university or university of applied sciences may seek a **short-stay** **L** **permit** for job search, valid for **six** **months,** and that the application should be filed **no later** **than** 15 **days** **before** **completion.** It asks for a passport copy, final marks record, diploma if available, evidence of means for six months and evidence of housing. Geneva also

<!-- 7 -->

expressly says that graduates of schools not appearing on the recognised/accredited Swiss-universities list, and **postdoctoral** **researchers,** are not eligible for this specific permit. That is a cantonal publication, not a general federal code, but it is important because it shows how at least one major canton opoerationalises the route. 31

Geneva's ordinary third-country employment page is also valuable for evidence expectations. It requires, among other things, a motivation letter from the employer describing the business situation, plans and reasons for hiring, the candidate's CV and diplomas, a signed employment contract, detailed recruitment evidence where priority applies, and salary compliance with local standards; Geneva additionally states on its public page that the job should be **full-time.** That full-time point is best treated as **cantonal practice,** not as a universal federal rule. 32

These cantonal materials support a cautious internal drafting approach: federal law sets the test, but **evidential packaging and procedural details remain canton-facing.** Zurich is especially useful for the substantive examples; Geneva is especially usefuI for post-study filing mechanics and employer-document expectations. 33

## Practical legal analysis

G. The immediate analytical point is that a Swiss degree does not itself establish entitlement to work. Official sources support only two linked propositions: a qualifying graduate may obtain a short post-study search status, and a subsequent job may be admitted without resident-worker priority if the job itself is of high scientific or economic interest. Any drafting that compresses those propositions into "Swiss graduates may stay and work" is legally loose.

The role assessment is therefore the true centre of gravity. On the federal materials, the strongest cases will usually show all or most of the following features at once: a close and substantive connection between the completed degree and the proposed duties; a role involving research, development, technical implementation, advanced know-how or demonstrable specialist input; a labour-market need supported by evidence; and a business explanation showing why the role matters to Swiss scientific capacity or the Swiss economy. Zurich's page and SEM's directives broadly converge on this view. 16

Field of study should be handled carefully. Official sources do **not** support an internal rule that only STEM subjects can qualify. SEM's 2026 directives, citing case law, expressly say that social-science and humanities graduates can also be admitted under the scientific-interest leg if the statutory conditions are met. Equally, official sources do not support the opposite overstatement that "any field can qualify equally easily". In practice, shortage evidence, specialisation, innovation content and demonstrable labour-market need will usually make the file materially stronger. 4

Degree level should also be expressed conservatively. SEM's 2026 directives list **Bachelor,Master** **and** **doctorate** as qualifying Swiss university degrees, and say the list is not exhaustive; they also clarify that a personn who completed only the Master or doctorate in Switzerland can still fall within the route. However, the currently published federal and cantonal route materials still focus on Swiss universities and universities of applied sciences, while the separate reform proposal to extend and liberalise the route has not been enacted. That makes it risky to write as if the public route clearly extends, today, to all tertiary qualifications or to postdoctoral status. 34

Article 23 AIG remains a live issue and deserves careful internal handling. The general federal rule continues to admit mainly managers, specialists and other qualified workers. That can appear awkward for very fresh graduates, but SEM's directives on shortage occupations make the picture less rigid: in

<!-- 8 -->

occupations with a pronounced shortage, special skills may suffice and admissions can be granted even where the typically expected post-study experience is stillmissing. For graduate-switch cases, the safest formulation is that Article 23 is **not switched** **off,** but its application may be more realistic where the role is genuinely specialised or shortage-driven. 35

Employers should therefore be expected to justify more than mere convenience. Even where resident-worker priority does not have to be proven under Article 21 paragraph 3, the employer should still show, in substance, why the role counts as high-interest, how the training matches the duties, what shortage or scientific value exists, and why the Swwiss labour market or Swiss economic ecosystem benefits from this hire. Geneva's public requirement for an employer motivation letter is formally framed for ordinary third-country applications, but it provides a useful model of the documentary seriousness cantons expect. 36

Salary and working conditions should be treated as threshold compliance, not as proof of high interest. Federal guidance says salary, social security contributions and working conditions must match local, professional and sectoral norms; SEM's directives add that authorities rely on collective agreements, current statistical salary tools and a written contract containing the core employment terms. Where remuneration includes start-up-style participation, SEM's directives say only benefits with a contractual basis can count, and only where sufficient direct financial means remain available for living costs. An attractive salary may support credibility, but it does not replace the need to prove scientific or economic interest. 37

Evidence of economic need can and should be made concrete. SEM's directives identify tools such as the **SECO indicator system,** **the job-notification occupation list,** and other shortage analyses as legitimate aids in establishing need. In a well-built file, those materials can be paired with the job description, the candidate's thesis/research/project work, any regulated-profession requirements, and a short explanation showing why the acquired Swiss training maps directly onto the post. 38

Ordinary entry-level jobs present the greatest weakness. SEM's directives expressly exclude administrative or study-distant positions and say the education must match the role profile. SEM's general labour-market materials also stress that unqualified workers and helpers do not satisfy the personal-qualification framework. That does not mean every junior role fails. It does mean that a generic "graduate analyst", "assistant", "trainee", or "operations support" title, without very strong specialist duties and degree-role evidence, is a weak Article 21 paragraph 3 case. 39

Internships and traineeships require especially careful route analysis. During study, side work and compulsory internships sit under separate VZAE rules. After study, SEM's **Young** **Professionals** programme is a distinct exchange route available only for nationals of specified countries and subject to its own limits. A graduate-switch application should therefore not casually rely on the vocabulary of "traineeship", "internship", or "young professional" unless the legal basis is genuinely that separate programme. A job branded as a trainee role can still, in theory, qualify under Article 21 paragraph 3, but only if the underlying duties independently satisfy the high-interest and qualification tests. 40

Start-ups are not excluded. SEM's directives expressly say that the graduate route can assist **large** **companies, SMEs, start-ups and academic institutions** in recruiting well and highly qualified professionals trained in Switzerland. At the same time, early-stage employers will usually need stronger evidence of economic value and business substance than established employers. SEM's separate start-up/self-employment guidance shows the kinds of materials Swiss authorities regard as probative in entrepreneurial contexts: business plans, financing, market opportunity, staffing projections, investment and revenue expectations, and programmne/incubator support where relevant. That

<!-- 9 -->

guidance is not a direct Article 21 paragraph 3 checklist for employed graduates, but it is a sensible documentary analogue. 41

A narrower but practically useful point is procedural. SEM's directives say that cantonal pre-decisions on Swiss university graduates under Article 21 paragraph 3 AIG are **not subject** **to** **the** **normal two-stage** **canton-federal consent** **procedure.** That does not mean a low-evidence case becomes easy; it does mean that the operational burden falls heavily on the canton-facing file. 42

## Drafting cautions and source list

### H. Drafting cautions

Do **not** write that a Swiss graduate "may remain and work in Switzerland" without immediately distinguishing between the **six-month job-search stay** and the later **employment authorisation.** The official sources support a two-step structure, not a one-step work right. 12

Do **not** say or imply that current law exempts Swiss graduates from **quotas.** Current official material says otherwise, and the quota-exemption reform remains a live legislative project rather than enacted law. 6

Do not imply that any "graduate role", any junior office role, or any position labelled "trainee" is enough. The official federal test focuses on **high** **scientific** or **economic** **interest,** close degree-role fit,and exclusion of general study-distant work. 39

**Do** **not** over-correct by writing that only STEM roles qualify. SEM's own directives say the scientific-interest concept is broader than that. A safer formulation is that non-STEM fields can qualify, but the file needs strong evidence of scientific or economic interest. 4

Do **not** treat salary as the decisive criterion. Salary compliance is mandatory, but it is one admissibility requirement among several. It does not by itself prove Swiss scientific or economic interest. 37

Do **not** write as if postdoctoral researchers or holders of all Swiss tertiary qualifications are clearly within the published Article 21 paragraph 3 route. Current public materials remain centred on Swiss universities and universities of applied sciences; Geneva expressly excludes postdocs fromn its job-search permit page; and the broader reform project is not yet in force. If higher vocational qualifications are discussed, the safest statement is that SEM's 2026 directives recognise them as potentially "qualified" under Article 23 where there is a close fit, which is a different proposition. 43

Do **not** present cantonal practice as uniform. Zurich and Geneva publish useful but not identical public material, and some operational points, such as filing deadlines or full-time expectations, are clearly canton-facing rather than stated as universal federal rules. 33
