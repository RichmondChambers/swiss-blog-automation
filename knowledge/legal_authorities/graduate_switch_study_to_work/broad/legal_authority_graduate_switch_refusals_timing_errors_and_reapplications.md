# Swiss legal authority pack on graduate switch refusals, timing errors and reapplications

## Scope and executive findings

### A. Executive legal summary

This pack is directed at the classic Swiss **study-to-work transition for third-country nationals** who complete a Swiss higher-education qualification and then seek either the post-study job-search permit or a work-authorisation decision under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act. EU/EFTA graduates are governed by a different free-movement framework and should not be conflated with the AIG/VZAE graduate-switch route. 1

The first operational point is that Swiss public guidance treats the graduate transition as **two** **separate** **legal stages.** The first is the **six-month short-stay arrangement to search for qualified work** after a Swiss university or university of applied sciences qualification; the second is the **actual** **work-authorisation** **stage,** where the proposed role must satisfy the substantive admission rules, including the special graduate rule in Art. 21(3) AIG where available. The six-month search period is not itself a general work permit, is non-renewable, and can be shortened by delay after completion because time already elapsed after the degree is counted against the six months. 2

Most refusals in this area fall into two analytically distinct groups. **Timing** **and** **status** **problems** include filing too late, allowing the study permit to expire, misunderstanding when the six-month period starts, or beginning work before permission exists. **Substantive refusal grounds** include a weak fit between the Swiss degree and the proposed role, failure to show a high scientific or economic interest,salary below local norms, inadequate employer evidence, and the re-emergence of ordinary third-country labour-market tests if the Art. 21(3) route is not made out. Those categories should not be merged in drafting. 3

Swiss public materials are much clearer on the **admission** **criteria** than on the detailed handling of **late** **filing, expired permits, and post-refusal reapplications.** On those issues, the usable operational material is a combination of general SEM procedure guidance, SEM sections on appeal and reconsideration, and specific cantonal practice pages, especially Geneva and Ticino. Internal drafting should therefore state expressly when a point is a clear federal rule and when it is only a documented cantonal practice point. 4

### B. Structural overview of refusals, timing errors and reapplications

On completion of studies, the decisive date is **the point at which successful completion can be** **established;** the SEM guidance says a school confirmation is enough and the diploma need not yet have been issued. The six-month job-search period starts from that point, and if completion occurs before the study permit expires, the time already elapsed after completion is deducted from the six-month period. This creates a common timing trap: a graduate who waits too long after final results may still obtain the permit, but with materially less time remaining. 5

During that search period, the SEM guidance says the cantonal authority may allow work of up to **15hours** **per** **week,** by analogy with Art. 38 VZAE, and a higher workload is incompatible with the purpose of searching for employment. Geneva's public guidance is stricter in administrative phrasing and says the request must be filed **no later than 15 days before the end of studies;** it also says the permit is valid for six months, is not renewable, and is available only to graduates of recognised Swiss higher-education institutions. 6

Once a job is identified, the case moves to the work-authorisation stage. Under SEM guidance, Art. 21(3) AIG removes the ordinary priority test only if theactivity is of **high** **scientific** **or** **economic** **interest;** it does **not** remove the need to satisfy the remaining admission conditions. SEM's 2026 guidance states that graduates underArt. 21(3) remain subject to the other requirements in Art. 20 ff. AIG,which means internal drafting should not assume that salary, local conditions, qualifications, or quota exposure disappear merely because the applicant holds a Swiss degree. 7

If refusal occurs, the correct procedural response depends on timing and what has changed. A still-open refusal is for **appeal** through the route stated in the decision. A final refusal can sometimes be revisited by **reconsideration,** but SEM's procedural guidance is explicit that reconsiderationis not there to replace missed appeal deadlines or to repair facts and evidence that should have been produced in the first case. A truly new filing is strongest where there is a genuine change: a new contract, corrected salary, stronger role specification,new shortage evidence, or a restored lawful-residence position. 8

## Federal legal texts and official materials

### D. Primary legal texts

The governing federal texts identified by the SEM legal-basis pages are the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration Act, AIG/FNIA, SR 142.20,** and the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and** **Employment, VZAE,** SR **142.201.** For graduate-switch refusals, the key AIG provisions are the rules on entry and permit necessity, the obligation to obtain work authorisation, the possibility of requiring the decision to be awaited abroad, the general labour-market admission criteria, the graduate exception in Art.21(3), the salary and local-conditions rule, the qualification rule, and the cooperation / truthfulness obligations. The key VZAE provisions are those on student ancillary work and integrated obligatory practical placements. 18

Operationally, the most important primary-text consequences are these. First,every **gainful activity** **requires permission;** SEM's public guidance emphasises that the rule applies to paid and unpaid activity alike where the activity is normally remunerated. Secondly, **student ancillary** **work** is **tightly** **limited,** and the SEM guidance uses the student-work rules as the benchmark for the limited work which may be authorised during the six-month search period. Thirdly, the graduate exception is not a free-standing right to work: it is an admission route that still sits inside the wider AIG framework. 19

The public federal material is also clear that the six-month post-study arrangement is tied to a **Swiss** **university-sector** **qualification.** SEM's own guidance treats Bachelor, Master and Doctorate as recognised examples, says the list is not exhaustive, and defines "Swiss university" by reference to the Higher Education Act framework. Geneva's public guidance is even more concrete: it excludes graduates from schools that are not on the recognised Swiss higher-education lists. 20

<!-- 5 -->

There is one area where internal drafting should remain cautious. Public legislative materials discuISS reform proposals that would **further ease admission for Swiss-trained graduates,** including quota relief. However, the current 2026 SEM directives still say that Art. 21(3) cases remain subject to the remaining admission requirements in Art. 20 ff. AIG. For present operational content, the safer position is therefore to treat quota and the other ordinary conditions as still relevant unless a current consolidated legal text is checked separately in the underlying fedlex record. 21

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's public page on **labour-market admission for third-country nationals** is highly useful for refusal analysis because it translates the statutory structure into evidential expectations. It states that admission must serve the Swiss economy, that quotas are limited, that priority must be evidenced by real and suitably broad recruitment efforts, and that salary and employment conditions must match local and sectoral standards. It also lists the forms of proof authorities expect, such as RAV and EURES postings, dated advertisements, candidate overviews, and a written employment contract signed by both sides. 22

SEM's **AIG** **directives** are the principal official source for graduate-switch content. Section 4.4.6 explains what counts as a high scientific or economic interest and gives the operational filters that matter in refusals: the person should be using the skills acquired in Switzerland at a high level; there should not already be an adequate labour supply; a high economic interest may arise from demonstrated market need,a highly specialised field tightly fitted to the job, or a role that generates jobs or orders; general or study-remote work is excluded. Section 5.1.2 then explains the six-month search arrangement, the start of the six-month clock, the need for finances and accommodation, the maximum 15-hour authorised work possibility, and the non-renewable nature of the permit. 23

The SEM procedure pages add two important structural points. First, many ordinary third-country work files are **canton-first, SEM-second.** Secondly, the SEM directives state specifically that **cantonal pre-**decisions **admitting Swiss university graduates under Art. 21(3) are not subject to the two-stage** **canton-federal approval process.** That distinction matters because the procedural path, applicable remedy, and likely strategy can differ depending on whether the file is treated as a true Art. 21(3) graduate case or has fallen back into the ordinary third-country regime. 24

SECO's indicator system is not itself the legal test, but it is an **official labour-market** **tooI** that SEM guidance and case law treat as a useful aid in assessing whether there is a structural shortage in the relevant occupation. Content drafting should therefore treat shortage data as **supporting** **evidence,** not as a substitute for the legal requirement that the specific proposed role and the graduate's Swiss-acquired skills justify a high economic interest finding. 25

## Cantonal practice and case law

### F. Cantonal practice notes

Public cantonal guidance is especially helpful on **timing** **discipline,** which federal sources discuss less explicitly. Geneva publishes that a third-country student seeking the six-month post-formation search permit must file no **later** **than** **15** **days** **before the** **end** **of** **studies.** Geneva also says the permit runs for six months from completion, is not renewvable, and requires the final marks record, passport, evidence

of funds for six months, and accommodation proof. That is unusually concrete administrative guidance and should be treated as a genuine canton-specific practice point, not yet as a universal national rule. 26

Geneva also publishes strict guidance on **work** **before** **permission.** For ancillary student work, third-country nationals must wait for the cantonal decision before starting. For ordinary third-country employment, the employer files the application,and-save where there is already an entry authorisation with employment or an assurance of residence authorisation-the employee may work only once the wvork permit has been received. Public Geneva guidance further spells out the expected material: CV, diplomas, work certificates, employer letter explaining the project and reasons for engagement, recruitment evidence, staffing information, and signed contract. 27

Ticino's public pages show the same general strictness but with slightly different administrative detail. For third-country students, ancillary work can begin **only** **after** **authorisation** is **received.** For B-permit holders more generally, Ticino says renewal should be sought **14** **days** **before** **expiry,** changes in purpose or address must be notified within 14 days, the start of work must be notified no later than the day before it begins, and late compliance can lead to a fine. Ticino therefore supplies useful public evidence that permit-expiry and late-notification errors are treated seriously at cantonal level even where the public federal material is less explicit. 28

Zurich's public material is especially useful on the **quality** **of** **the** **employer** **file.** Its canton guidance says third-country workers need permission from the first day, the employer must apply, and there is no entitlement to the permit or to renewal. The Zurich labour-market PDF then identifies the practical documents expected in ordinary cases: a specific application justification, business-plan material where relevant, RAV and EURES advertising, ddated adverts, a candidate list with rejection reasons, a current bilateral signed employment contract, diplomas, employment certificates, CV, and passport copy. Even though this is not a graduate-route note as such, it is highly relevant once an Art. 21(3) case is weak or collapses into ordinary third-country analysis. 29

### G. Case law notes

Published case law on graduate-switch refusals is **not** **abundant,** and much of the usable public material comes through SEM's own restatement of case law in the directives. The most directly relevant published decision located for this pack is **Zurich** **Administrative** **Court VB.2024.00276,** a 2024 case on a Swiss graduate proposed for a "Business Development Manager" role. The court accepted that the graduate held a Swiss Master's degree, but still found no high economic interest because the field was not highly specialised or tightly matched to the role, the profile was too broad, and some supposedly distinguishing traits were biographical or cultural rather than Swiss-acquired academic skills. The court also recorded that the employer had provided no proper resident-worker-priority evidence and that the originally agreed salary was below local norms. 30

That Zurich decision is especially valuable because it shows **how** **refusal grounds** **interact.The** employer later argued that the salary had been raised and that shortage evidence supported the case, but the court still rejected the graduate route because the underlying role was too general and the substantive high-interest test was not met. The practical lesson is that a stronger salary or better labour-market evidence may not rescue a case where the role itself is not sufficiently specialised or sufficiently tied to the Swiss-acquired qualification. 30

On the **scientific-interest** limb, SEM's directives summarise Federal Administrative Court case law, including C-3859/2014, as saying the concept must be interpreted in light of academic freedom and assessed case by case by reference to the scientific community. The same SEM section then explains that the concept should be interpreted broadly, but not without limit. That is useful authority for internal drafting: scientific interest is not confinedd to a tiny set of laboratory or patent-heavy roles, but it still requires a serious academic nexus. 31

<!-- 7 -->

**For reconsideration,** **a** useful general migration-law example is **Zurich Administrative Court VB.** **2024.00060.** It is not a graduate-switch case, but it illustrates the accepted procedural idea that a materially new circumstance canjustify a fresh merits assessment, whereas a final decision without such a novum normally remains final. That is consistent with SEM's own chapter on remedies, which says reconsideration requires materially changed circumstances or genuinely new facts or evidence not previously usable. 32

## Practical legal analysis

## H.Practical legal analysis

The most important analytical separation is between **timing/default** **problems** **and** **substantive** **fit** **problems.** A late or badly timed file may fail even if the role is otherwise strong, because the six-month clock may already have been running, the permit may no longer be renewable, or the applicant may have drifted out of a clean lawful-status position. Conversely, a perfectly timely file may still be refused because the roleis too generic, the employer evidence is thin, or the salary is too low. Internal refusal content should always ask first: is the defect status/timing, role/substance, or both? 33

On **late** **filing** **and** **permit expiry,** the safest public authority is not a single federal "grace period" rule, but a combination of SEM structure and cantonal deadlines. Geneva requires filing for the post-study search permit by no later than 15 days before the end of studies; Geneva and Ticino require certain renewals before expiry; and SEM says that where a person seeks a new permit after a merely temporary stay and the requirements are not obviously met, the decision must be awaited abroad. In operational terms, once the study permit has expired and no protective filing has been made in time, the graduate is exposed not merely to administrative inconvenience but to a change in the entire legal posture of the case. 34

**On** **working** **before** **approval,** Swiss public guidance is severe. SEM states that every gainful activity requires permission, including unpaid activity where the work is normally remunerated. Geneva and Ticino both tell third-country students to wait for authorisation before starting, and Geneva's general third-country work page says that unless the person already holds the correct entry assurance,work is lawful only once the permit has been received. This means that a graduate who starts "just a little early" is not merely making a formal error; they are creating an independent compliance issue which can contaminate both status and credibility. 35

**On** **weak** **role** fit, the safest drafting formula is not that Swiss law demands a mathematically exact degree-job match, because the statute itself is phrased in terms of a role of high scientific or economic interest. But current official practice plainly asks whether the graduate will use **Swiss-acquired** **abilities** **at** a **high** **level,** whether the field is truly specialised, and whether the job profile actually corresponds to the completed training. SEM's directives exclude general administrative or study-remote functions, and the Zurich case shows that broad business-development roles may fail even where the graduate has a good Swiss degree.36

**On** **economic** **interest,** SEM's own material gives three main routes for evidencing it: a demonstrated labour-market need; a highly specialised field tightly fitted to the role; or a position whose filling will create jobs or generate work for the Swiss economy. Official shortage indicators can help, but they do not displace the need to show that this role and this graduate meet the statutory threshold. A weakly specified role in a broad field remains vulnerable even if there is some sectoral demand data in the background. 37

<!-- 8 -->

**On** **salary** **and** **local** **conditions,** internal content should treat these as fully load-bearing refusal issues. SEM's public guidance says the employer should file a signed employment contract and that the contract should contain the formula that it takes effect only if the authorities grant the work permit. Zurich's public PDF says the current and complete contract is mandatory. Geneva says the authorities will check usual salary levels and points employers to the cantonal salary calculator. The Zurich 2024case confirms that an under-market salary can boe a standalone refusal reason. 38

**On** **employer** **evidence** **weakness,** the public sources are more concrete than many practitioners assume. Zurich and Geneva expect not just a contract and CV, but also areasoned employer letter, recruitment evidence where required, staffing informnation, work certificates, diplomas, and-in ordinary cases-proof of serious searches in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area. For the six-month search permit, the graduate side must add proof of funds and accommodation. It follows, as an operational inference, that a refused file usually needs to be rebuilt around three evidential clusters: **status/timing, role fit/high interest,** and **salary/ordinary admission compliance.**39

**A** **rebuilt** **file** **after** **refusal** should therefore usually contain at least: proof of when completion was established; proof of lawful residence and filing chronology; the current permit and expiry history; a corrected and conditional employment contract; a precise job description tied to the graduate's Swiss-acquired modules, research, placements or thesis work; a detailed employer memorandum explaining why the role is specialised and why the Swiss training matters; salary benchmarking; and, if reliance on Art. 21(3) is doubtful, full ordinary labour-market evidence including recruitment records and staffing data. That list is an inference from the official evidential demands and the failure points found in the Zurich case. 40

**On** **appeal, reconsideration** **and** **reapplication,** the distinctions should be kept sharp. The written decision must state the ordinary remedies, deadline and appeal body. A cantonal refusal is appealed under cantonal procedural law to the higher cantonal instance or instances identified in the decision. Reconsideration is an extraordinary request back to the issuing authority, without a fixed form or deadline, but it is not a second chance to patch a case that was badly prepared the first time. Revision is different again: it concerns new facts that already existed but could not previously be used, rather than facts arising only afterwards. A "new application" after refusal is strongest only where there is a genuine new factual basis; otherwise it is vulnerable to being treated, in substance, as an inadmissible reconsideration attempt. 41

Finally, internal content should remember that **not** **every** **graduate-switch** **decision** **is** **equally** **appealable** **at** **federal-court** **level.** SEM's remedy guidance notes that on the field of foreign-national permits, ordinary public-lawappeal to the Federal Supreme Court is excluded where neither federal law nor international law gives an entitlement. That matters because the six-month search permit is described by SEM as carrying a legal entitlement when its conditions are met, whereas the later work-permit stage often remains heavily discretionary. Procedural strategy and how content describes "rights" should therefore differ between those two stages. 42

## Drafting cautions and source list

### I. Drafting cautions

<!-- 9 -->

Do **not** write as though every foreign student in Switzerland may remain for six months after graduation. The public federal and cantonal materials are tied to **recognised** **Swiss** **higher-education** **institutions,** and Geneva expressly excludes graduates from non-recognised schools. The six-month route should therefore be described as a **recognised-institutiongraduate** **route,** not as a generic post-study privilege. 20

Do **not** collapse the six-month search permit into the later work-authorisation decision. The first is a limited, non-renewable status to look for qualified work; the second is the actual emnployment-permit stage. Content that writes "graduates can work for six months after graduation" is inaccurate unless it immediately explains the limited authorised-work rule and the need for separate employment permission. 43

**Do** **not** overstate Art. 21(3) as a blanket exemption. The graduate route waives the resident-worker priority test only where the high scientific or economic interest threshold is actually met. Salary, local conditions, qualifications, and-on the safer current reading-other Art. 20 ff. conditions still matter. If the role is weak or too general, the case reverts to ordinary third-country analysis. 12

**Do** **not** imply that omitted evidence can always be repaired later by reconsideration. SEM's own remedy chapter says the opposite: reconsideration is not there to healparticipation failures from the original procedure. Where the original file was thin, a carefully rebuilt **new** **application** **based** **on** **a** **real change** **in** **facts** or **evidence** may be the sounder framing, subject always to lawful-residence issues. 44

Do not say that the graduate may file the work case personally unless a specific canton expressly allows that. The public guidance located for Zurich and Geneva puts the work application on the **employer.** Internal content should therefore distinguish clearly between documents the graduate supplies and documents the employer must supply. 15

Where the public sources are thin, say so. The clearest examples are the **precise** **nationwide** **consequences of late filing after study-permit expiry** and the exact boundary between a "fresh application" and a disguised reconsideration request. Onthose issues, public federal material gives principles, while the practical timing detail largely comes from cantonal pages and general Swiss administrative-law doctrine. Content should therefore avoid categorical national claims unless a federal text says them expressly. 45

The principal **federal legal and** **guidance** **sources** used for this pack are the SEM legal-basis pages identifying **AIG** **SR** **142.20** and **VZAE** **SR** **142.201;** the SEM public overview on labour-market admission for third-country nationals; the SEM public procedure page; and the 2026 **AIG** **directives,** especially chapter 4 section 4.4.6 and chapter 5 section 5.1.2, plus chapter 10 on remedies. 46
