# Swiss Graduate Switch Post-Study Job Search and Timing Authority Pack

## A. Executive legal summary

Switzerland does **not** have a general "post-study work visa" or an automatic right for international graduates to remain and work after finishing studies. For **third-country** **nationals** who graduate from a **Swiss** **higher** **education** **institution,** the key federal rule is the special regime reflected in **AIG/LEI** **art. 21(3):** they may remain **for six months after completion of studies** to look for a job that matches their qualifications, and they may be admitted without labour-market-priority testing **only** if the proposed employment is of **high** **scientific** **or** **economic interest.** Official SEM guidance presents this as a narrow, qualifications-linked route rather than a broad graduate work entitlement.1

For that six-month post-study search period, SEM's officiaI directives treat the route as a **cantonal** **short-stay** **authorisation,** typically an **L** **permit,** and require at least: a successful Swiss higher-education completion, **sufficient funds,** and **appropriate** **accommodation.** The directives further state that, if those conditions are met, there is a **legal** **entitlement** to the corresponding residence arrangement. The keytiming point is that the six-month clock begins **when** **successful completion** is **established,** not when the diploma is physically printed and not when the student permit later expires. The directives expressly state that a university confirmation is enough and that time already elapsed after completion is counted against the six months. The six-month search permit is **not extendable.** 2

During that post-study search period, official SEM guidance does not support unrestricted work. The directives say that **employment of up to 15 hours per week may be authorised by analogy to** **student ancillary** **work;** a higher workload would be incompatible with the purpose of job search. Cantonal guidance is consistent in principle, though public phrasing varies: Geneva says students who were already carrying out ancillary work may maintain it during the search period, while ETH Zurich says the samne employment restrictions apply as during a normal semester. Content should therefore describe this as **limited, ancillary work only, and only where authorised,** not as **a** full right to work after graduation. 3

Once a third-country graduate has a qualifying job offer, **employer** **sponsorship** **is still required** for a move into full employment. The labour-market-priority test is eased for eligible Swiss graduates under art. 21(3), but **other** **admission** **requirements remain**: overall economic interest, quotas,salary and working conditions, personal qualifications, and a role genuinely connected to the degree. SEM's directives also make clear that **generic administrative or degree-remote roles do not qualify.** Zurich's official cantonal guidance says the same: there is no automatic right to work after any Swiss degree, and the employer must file the application. 4

Timing mistakes are legally significant. Official sources consistently show that a **job** **offer** **alone** is **insufficient;** the employer must apply; standard public guidance says third-country work cases often take around **six** **to** **eight** **weeks** to process; SEM approval is **not** the same as entry permission where a visa is required; and, in the ordinary federal guidance, the worker may begin employment **only** **after** **registration** following entry. Separate SEM directives add an important practice point for this graduate route: cantonal preliminary decisions under **art. 21(3)** are generally **not subject** **to** **the** **ordinary** **two-stage** **canton-SEM** **approval procedure,** which helps explain why real-world handling is often canton-led and why public cantonal timing instructions matter. 5

For **EU/EFTA** **graduates,** the framework is different. They are not relying on the third-country graduate regime in art. 21(3), but on the **free** **movement** framework and the EU/EFTA residence-permit system. Federal SEM guidance confirms that **L permits without gainful employment are issued to EU/EFTA** **job** **seekers,** while cantonal practice pages show different administrative formulations for the job-seeking period**:** Basel-Stadt describes **three months permit-free plus a further nine months oon an L** **permit,** whereas Geneva describes a **364-day** **L** **permit** for post-study job search. Once an EU/EFTA graduate has a job, federal guidance says they register within **14** **days** **of arrival and** **before starting** **work;** jobs under three months operate under the notification procedure. 6

Quotas remain relevant for third-country graduates moving into work. SEM's official labour-market guidance states that third-country admissions are quota-limited under **art.** 20 **AIG/LEI** and that the Federal Council sets annual maximum numbers. The 2021 consultation report on the reform project expressly stated that admissions under art. 21(3) were subject to quotas; the SEM project page shows that the later proposal to exempt Swiss-university graduates from quotas was **sent** **back** and is **not** in **force** as of the page status reviewed; and the Federal Council's 2026 quota announcement confirms that third-country quotas remain in place for 2026. Content should therefore not say that Swiss graduates from third countries are "quota-free".7

## B. Structural overview of post-study job search and timing

The safest way to structure this route for **third-country** **graduates** is as a sequence of distinct legal phases**: student residence** under the study rules; **post-study job-search residence** for up to six months; and, if a suitable job is found, **work-authorised** **residence/employment** under the labour-market rules. Those phases should not be collapsed into one another. SEM's directives and FAQ describe them as different legal states with different conditions, especially on work rights and admissibility. 8

While on **student** **residence,** ancillary work is a narrow exception. Under the official SEM guidance on **art. 38 VZAE/OASA,** student work may generally be authorised only **after six months from the start of** **studies,** only if the school confirms compatibility with the course, and only up to **15** **hours per** **week** during term. Mandatory internships are separate and are governed by distinct rules. This matters because public-facing content often wrongly treats any work done during studies as if it were part of a broader post-study work right. It is not. 9

At the point of **completion,** the decisive event is not the ceremonial graduation date. The SEM directives say the job-search permit starts when it becomes clear that the degree has been successfully completed, and a school confirmation is enough; Geneva's official page says the date on the **final** **record** **of** **marks** is decisive; ETH Zurich's guidance says a programme letter confirming successful completion with a binding date can substitute for the diploma. In practice, the legally relevant completion date is therefore the point at which successful completion is officially evidenced,not the later issue date of the parchment. 2

During the **job-search** **residence** phase, the graduate may stay in Switzerland to seek a suitably qualified role, but that phase is still not the same as a work permit for full employment. The directives say only ancillary work up to 15 hours weekly may be authorised; Geneva frames this as the continuation of prior authorised ancillary work. The same directives state that the six-month permit is issued in **cantonal competence,** which is why canton-specific filing windows, document lists, and operational rules are important. 10

<!-- 2 -->

If the graduate secures a job, the case then moves into the **employment-authorisation** phase. For third-country nationals, the employer files the application. For graduates within art. 21(3), the labour-market-priority rule is relaxed, but the job must still be qualification-appropriate and of high scientific or economic interest, and quota exposure remains. Public SEM guidance on the ordinary route still warns that a job offer is not enough, that the employer must apply, and that work cannot begin until the legal process is comnplete. 11

**For EU/EFTA graduates,** the structure is different from the beginning. The graduate is not "switching" through art. 21(3). Instead, federal guidance places them in the free-movement framework, with work based on a valid employment contract and municipal registration, and with L-permit job-seeker arrangements available even without gainful employment. Cantonal practice still matters on duration and filing. 6

## D to F. Authorities and practice materials

**Primary** **legal texts.** The controlling federal instruments are the **AIG/LEI** **(SR** **142.20)** and the **V/ZAE/** **OASA** **(SR** **142.201),** identified on SEM's official legal-bases page. For this topic, thne core article set is: **AIG** **arts. 18-21 and** **21a** for labour-market admission, economic interest, quotas and labour-market priority; **AIG** **art.** 27 for study residence; and **VZAE** **arts. 38-40** for ancillary work, compulsory internships and work during higher education, with art. 55 **VZAE** relevant to job changes for L-permit holders. For EU/EFTA graduates, the primary legalframework is the **free** **movement** regime rather than the third-country graduate rule. 22

**Official federal guidance and administrative materials.** The most useful public SEM materials for this pack are: the **SEM** **FAQ-** **Working,** which states the six-month post-study search period for third-country graduates and the basic employer-sponsored work-permit rules; the **SEM** **labour-market** **admission** **page,** which explains the dual system, quotas and priority rules; the **SEM procedure** **page,** which sets out the ordinary employer filing process and the "no work before lawful completion of the process" message; and the **SEM AIG directives,** especially **chapter 4, section 4.4.6** and **chapter 5,**

<!-- 6 -->

**section** 5.1.2, which provide the most precise official treatment of timing, evidence, work limits during job search, and the cantonal nature of the search permit. 23

**Legislative-history materials.** The official **2021 consultation report** and the SEM legislative project page are important because they confirm two points that affect drafting: first, the old art. 30(1)(i) mechanism was rolled into art. 21(3); second, the later project to remove **quota** **counting** for this category is **not** **currently** **in** **force.** As a result, current content should continue to present quota exposure as part of the legal landscape for third-country graduate hires. 24

**Cantonal practice** **notes.** Geneva's official pages are especially useful for timing:for **third-country** **graduates,** the application should be filed **no later than 15 days before the end of studies,** the permit validity begins at completion, the permit is a six-month L **permit,** and the **final** **marks** **record** is treated as the operative completioon evidence; Geneva also publicly says that graduates from institutions not on the recognised Swiss list, and **post-docs,** are nnot eligible for that specific canton's post-study search permit page. 25

Zurich's official page is useful on the federal rule's practical limits. It says that not every Swiss degree automatically allows employment; only roles of **high** **scientific** **or** **economic** **interest** qualify; the **employer** must apply; and, for the six-month job-search permit, the graduate must show **funds,** **accommodation,** and the **diploma or completion confirmation.** Zurich also clearly distinguishes student ancillary work, internships, and the post-study switch into employment. 26

Basel-Stadt and Geneva are useful for **EU/EFTA** practice. Basel says EU/EFTA job seekers may remain **three months without a permit,** then receive an L **permnit for nine additional months,** and must file extension/conversion requests **three** **weeks** **before** **expiry;** Geneva instead frames the post-study job-search permit for EU/EFTA graduates as a **364-day** L **permit.** These differences show why internal content should treat EU/EFTA job-seeker permit timing as **cantonal** **administration** **under** a **federal** **free-movement** **framework,** not as one uniform nationwide duration formula. 21

Luzern's official material reinforces the orthodox third-country work point: work authorisation is needed **from** **the** **first** day, the **employer** files, and there is **no** **entitlement** to a particular work permit or to extension.That is not graduate-specific, but it is a useful cantonal reminder against language implying that a graduate can simply "switch" into full employment on their own initiative. 27

Relevant official university guidance should be used sparingly. ETH Zurich's page is useful only because it confirms the same timing logic as the SEM directives: apply **before** **permit expiry,** the six months run from **completion,** not from later permit expiry, and a **programme** **letter** can be used before the diploma arrives. 28

## G. Practical legal analysis

**For** **third-country** **graduates,** the legally safest sequence is this. First, remain on the student permit only for as long as that permit remains valid and for the original study purpose. Secondly,once successful completion is established, treat that date as the start of the graduate-switch timetable. Thirdly, file promptly for the six-month post-study search permit, because the directives say any time already elapsed after completion is counted against the six months. Fourthly, while on that search permit, describe work rights conservatively: only ancillary work up to 15 hours weekly may be authorised; unrestricted work is not available merely because studies are finished. Fifthly, once a suitable job is secured, the employer must file for work authorisation before full employment begins. The most important **timing** **trigger** is the completion date. Public content that tells readers to "wvait for the diploma" is unsafe. The directives say the diploma need not yet be issued; Geneva says the final marks record controls; ETH accepots a completion letter. The practical legal consequence is that the graduate should collect completion evidence **immediately** and should not assume that a later diploma ceremony preserves the full six-mnonth search window. 2

The six-month job-search status should be analysed as a **residence** **bridge,** not a work right. That bridge is narrow. The SEM directives say the purpose is to find a **qualified** job; the SEM FAQ says a job matching the person's qualifications; and Zurich says the eventual role must be in a field where there is not already a sufficient labour supply and where the activity reaches the required level of scientific or economic interest. This is why a graduate can lawfully remain to search, but cannot assume that any post-graduation job offer will convert the case into work-authorised residence. 30

When the graduate moves from search to employment, **employer** **sponsorship** **remainns** **central.** For the internal content system, the correct legal frame is: art. 21(3) gives an **eased admission** **test,** not a self-sponsored graduate settlement route. The employer still submits the work-permit application;the job must still satisfy the remaining admission criteria; and quota exposure still exists. SEM's labour-market page, FAQ and Zurich's public page are all aligned on that point. 31

**On** **work-start** **timing,** the conservative rule should be explicit. Official SEM guidance says a job offer is nnot enough; the ordinary third-country process involves filing, cantonal examination, and often federal handling; SEM approval is not itself entry permission where a visa is required; and, in the ordinary route, employment may begin only after registration. Although SEM's directives indicate that graduate cases under art. 21(3) are generally not in the oordinary approval category, the public sources do not justify a blanket statement that a graduate may start work as soon as the canton sayys "yes" or as soon as a contract is signed. Internal content should therefore warn that **work** **start** **must** **wait until the** **legjally required authorisation and registration steps have actually been completed.** 5

**On** **quota** **interaction,** the correct current statement is modest but important. Third-country admissions remain quota-limited in principle, and the Federal Council still fixes annual numbers.The specific reform to exempt Swiss-university graduates fromquota counting is not yet operative on the sources reviewed, and 2026 quotas remain in force. Internal content should therefore present quota risk as a live legal and practical issue, while recognising that quotas are set annually and should not be hard-coded in evergreen copy without a date. 7

**For EU/EFTA** **graduates,** the analysis is materially different. They should not be described as using the third-country "graduate switch" route. Federal SEM guidance instead ties work to the free-movement framework: an employment contract, commune registration within 14 days, and registration before starting work. For job-seeking without employment, SEM confirms the existence of L **permits** **without** **gainful** **employment** for EU/EFTA job seekers, but the reviewed cantonal sources describe the permit period differently. That difference does not change the fundamental point for content drafting: EU/EFTA graduates are under a **different** **legal regime,** with broader labour-market access and different residence mechanics. 6

## H. Drafting cautions

Do not describe Switzerland as offering a general **post-study** **work** **visa.** For third-country graduates, the official rule is a **six-month residence arrangement to seek a suitable job,** followed by a separate work-authorisation step if a qualifying role is found. 32

<!-- 8 -->

Do not say that graduates may work freely during that six-month search period. The safest formulation is that **limited ancillary work, up to 15 hours weekly, may be authorised,** and some cantonal/public sources frame this as the continuation of pre-existing authorised ancillary work. 2

Do **not** say that the six months run from **permit expiry** or from the date the diploma is handed over. The reviewed official sources point instead to **successful completion** as the trigger, evidenced by a school confirmation or final marks record. 2

Do not imply that any Swiss graduate can move into any job. The eventual role must be linked to the completed course and must meet the **high** **scientific** **or** **economic** **interest** threshold; general administrative or study-remote roles are expressly excluded in the SEM directives. 17

**Do** **not** omit the employer's role. For third-country graduates moving into employment, the empoloyer-led work-permit process remains central even though labour-market priority is eased for eligible Swiss graduates. 15

Do **not** say that art. 21(3) cases are **quota-free.** The official reform project to create that exemption is not yet in force on the sources reviewed, and 2026 quota arrangements for third-country workers remain in place. 33

**Do not** merge **student work, mandatory internship, post-study job search, young-professional/** **stagiaire** **exchange,** and **full employment** into one narrative. They are distinct routes with distinct conditions and timing. Zurich's official page is particularly useful for keeping those categories separate. 26

Do **not** speak about EU/EFTA graduates and third-country graduates as if they are in the same legal position. EU/EFTA graduates fall under the free-movement framework and canton-specific EU/EFTA job-seeker permit practice, not the third-country graduate rule in art. 21(3). 6
