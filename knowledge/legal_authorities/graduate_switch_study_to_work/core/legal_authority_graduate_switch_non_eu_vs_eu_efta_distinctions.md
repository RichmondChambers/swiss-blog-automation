# Swiss graduate study-to-work distinctions pack

**Jurisdiction**: Switzerland

**Purpose:** Internal legal authority pack for route-specific immigration content on graduate transitions from Swiss study status to work status.

**Scope**: Comparison of **EU/EFTA** and **non-EU/EFTA** graduates.

**Date** **basis**: Current to **23** **May** **2026,** using official materials and consolidated legal texts available through official sources or stable legal-text mirrors where necessary.

## Executive findings and framework

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss labour migration law operates through a **dual** **system.** For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** access to the Swiss labour market is principally governed by the **Agreement** on **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** and the implementing **Ordinance** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons.** Official SEM guidance describes this as an "easier access" framework for workers of all qualification levels. For **non-EU/EFTA** **nationals,** admission remains restricted to a limited number of **highly** **qualified** workers under the **FNIA/AIG** and **ASEO/VZAE,** with employer-driven authorisation procedures, labour-market checks, salary-condition checks, and annual quotas. 1

**For EU/EFTA graduates,** there is **no special federal graduate-admission exemption equivalent to** **Article** 21 **paragraph** **3** **FNIA.** Their post-study options are instead the ordinary **AFMP** **free-movement** **routes**: worker status, jobseeker status, or non-economically-active status if the legal conditions are met. In practical terms, an EU/EFTA graduate who finds work does not need to satisfy resident-worker priority, overall economic interest, or third-country quota rules; but they still need to comply with **notification,registration, and residence-permit formalities,** depending on duration and canton. **2**

For **non-EU/EFTA graduates of Swiss higher education institutions,** Swiss law contains a **targeted** **facilitation,** but it is narrower than many summaries imply. **Article** **21 paragraph** **3** **FNIA/AIG** removes the ordinary **precedence** test and allows admission where the graduate's employment is of **high** **scientific or economic interest;** it also grants a **six-month post-study period** to find corresponding employment. However, the official materials do not state that these graduates are generally exempt from the rest of the third-country admissions regime. SEM's own guidance still frames the graduate rule **as** a **derogation** **from** **precedence,** while Zurich's official practice note says that, apart from the non-application of resident-worker priority, the ordinary third-country rules continue to apply. That means content should not say that Swiss-educated non-EU graduates are broadly exempt from quotas, employer sponsorship, or labour-condition review. 3

The most important automation risk istherefore twofold. First, content must **not import third-country** **admission criteria into EU/EFTA graduate cases.** Secondly, content must **not overstate EU/EFTA** **simplicity** by implying that no formal step is needed after study: over-three-month work usually still requires **registration and a residence permit,** short employment can require **notification,** and cantonal handling can matter. 4

### B. Structural overview of non-EU versus EU/EFTA graduate distinctions

The cleanest legal architecture is to separate the issue into three questions.

The first question is **which legal framework applies.** For an **EU/EFTA graduate,** the operative frame is the AFMP/VFP free-movement regime. For a **non-EU/EFTA** **graduate,** the operative frame is the FNIA/ AIG and VZAE third-country regime, with a specific Swiss-graduate facilitation in Article 21 paragraph 3AIG. 5

The second question is **what the** **graduate** **must** do **after** **study** **ends.** An EU/EFTA graduate can remain as a **jobseeker** without a permit for up to three months, and for longer with an **L** **EU/EFTA** **jobseeker** **permit** if they have sufficient means; if work is obtained, the route shifts to the normal worker permit/registration framework. A non-EU graduate of a Swiss higher education institution can obtain a **six-month** **post-study** **search** **period** and, if an eligible job is found, may be admitted without the usual precedence test, but still through the ordinary third-country work-authorisation pipeline. 6

The third question is **who** **bears** **the** **administrative** **burden.** In EU/EFTA cases, the burden is usually light and largely documentary: proof of employment, filing/registration,and if necessary notification. In non-EU cases, the burden sits mainly on the employer: filing the application, supporting labour-market and salary-condition evidence, obtaining cantonal and SEM approvals, and dealing with visa steps where applicable. 7

## Authorities

### E. Primary legal texts

The main federal texts for this topic are the following.

The **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (AIG/FNIA,** SR 142.20) is the core text for third-country graduate switching. The most relevant provisions are **Articles 18-23** on admission for gainful activity, **Article** **21** **paragraph** **3** on Swiss higher-education graduates, **Article** **27** **paragraph** **3** on post-study stay being subject to the general admission rules, **Article** **29a** on no social-assistance claim for persons staying only to seek work, **Article** **34** on settlement permits, and **Articles** **44-46** on family reunification and work rights of spouses and children. 40

The **Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment (VZAE/ASEO,** SR 142.201) supplies the implementing machinery for third-country admissions. The relevant provisions include **Articles** **20-22**on quotas and salary/working-condition control, **Articles** **23-24** on study permits, **Article** **26** on work by family members of L-permit holders, **Articles** **38-40** on work during higher education, **Articles** **58-60**on permit duration and settlement, and **Annexes** **1-2** on the 2026 quota numbers. 41

The **Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons (VFP/OLCP,** SR 142.203) is the main implementing text for EU/EFTA nationals. The key provisions here are **Article** 4 on EU/EFTA short-stay, residence, and cross-border permits, **Article** **9** on notification and permit procedure, **Articles 16-18** on residence without gainful activity and job search, and **Article** 22 on the right to remain. 42

The treaty basis is the **Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP/FZA/ALCP,** SR 0.142.112.681), together with the EFTA Convention arrangements for EFTA nationals. Current SEM factsheets and overview pages treat these as the legal basis for EU/EFTA worker, student, non-working, and family-reunification rights. 43

### F. Official guidance and administrative materials

**The** **SEM non-EU** **labour-market guidance** is the most important official administrative explanation of current practice. It summarises overall economic interest, quotas, precedence,job-registration obligations, salary and working conditions, and the need for complete employer-supported applications. It also links the rules directly to the AIG and VZAE. 44

The **SEM** **"FAQ-Working"** page is the clearest official English statement of the non-EU Swiss-graduate facilitation. It expressly says that third-country nationals graduating from a Swiss higher-education institution have **six** **months** to find a job matching their qualifications and may be admitted in **derogation** **from** **precedence** if their work is of overriding scientific or economic interest. 45

For EU/EFTA nationals, the key official materials are the **SEM** **"FAQ-** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons",** the **SEM worker factsheet,** the **SEM non-working persons factsheet,** the **SEM family-reunification** **factsheet,** and the **SEM** **residence-permit pages.** Together they cover worker permits, registration deadlines, short-term notification, jobseeker permits, derived family rights, and settlement-permit planning. 46

The federal portals **admin.ch** and **ch.ch** add useful official clarification. The Federal Council's 2025 press release confirms that **third-country quotas remain unchanged for 2026,** while ch.ch clearly explains that family-reunification rights vary by status and that family members generally obtain permits conferring work rights, with a specific caveat for **L-permit holders,** who need a work permit first. 47

### G. Cantonal practice notes

Official cantonal practice matters because the federal framework is administered through cantonal filing channels.

**Geneva** is especially useful because it publishes separate pages for post-study job-search permits for **EU/EFTA** and **non-EU/EFTA** students. Geneva states that **the EU/EFTA post-study L permit** is valid for **364 days** and non-renewable, while the **non-EU/EFTA post-study L permit** is valid for **six months** and must be requested no later than 15 **days** **before** **the** **end** **of studies.** Both pages also state that ancillary work performed during studies may continue during the search period. 48

**Zurich** publishes a detailed page on third-country students and graduates. It states that Swiss-educated third-country graduates may be admitted where the job is of **high** **scientific** **or** **economic** **interest,that** **the** **resident-worker priority** is **not examined,** and that otherwise the third-country rules remain applicable. Zurich also states that a **six-mmonth** **short-stay** **permit** **for job** **searching** can be requested after successful completion of study, subject to funds, accommodation, and proof of completion. 49

**Ticino** illustrates an important EU/EFTA operational point. Its official page for EU/EFTA B permits says that workers must submit the request **before** **starting** **work,** and late filing can lead to a fine. That reinforces the drafting point that EU/EFTA transitions are not substantive third-country authorisations, but they are still not purely automatic from an administrative standpoint. 50

There is also a visible **cantonal tension** **on** **EU/EFTA** **work-start** **timing.** Geneva says that, for some employed EU/EFTA cases, work may begin once the complete file has been submitted, whereas SEM's general FAQ says that registration and application are required within 14 ddays of arrival **and** **before** **actually taking up work.** This should be handled as a **cantonal practice point,** not flattened into a single nationwide operational sentence. 51

## Analysis and drafting controls

### H. Practical legal analysis

The most robust comparative conclusion is that **EU/EFTA** **graduates** **and** **non-EU** **graduates** **do** **not** **use** **the** **same** **legal switch** **logic** at **all.** The EU/EFTA graduate moves from study status into a free-movement worker or jobseeker status, and the legal questions are mainly **duration, registration,** **permit category, and proof of means or employment.** The non-EU graduate moves into a **third-country admission** system and only benefits from a **narrow statutory relaxation** on precedence and post-study search time. 52

That means the content system should treat **resident-worker priority, overall economic interest,** **high qualification, annual quotas,** and the **SEM approval chain** as default issues only for **non-EU/** **EFTA** cases. Within that category, it should then apply the graduate carve-out carefully: precedence is relaxed, but the substantive requirement of a job of **high** **scientific** **or** **economic** **interest** remains,and current official materials still place these cases within the broader third-country admissions apparatus. 53

At the same time, EU/EFTA cases should not be reduced to "no permit needed". For ordinary work lasting more than three months, the person still needs to **register and** **obtain** **the** **appropriate** **residence** **permit,** and for work of up to three months there is generally still **anotification** step. The residence permit doubles as the work permit, but that is not the samme thing as saying that no residence filing is needed. 54

**The job-search** **comparison** is especially important for automated drafting. EU/EFTA job search is framed through the free-movement rules: three months without a permit, then a short-staypermit if means are shown, extendable up to one year with ongoing search efforts and reasonable prospects. Non-EU Swiss-graduate job search is framed as a **six-month** **post-study** **stay** to find corresponding employment. These are different legal constructs and should not be presented as interchangeable. 55

The **family-rights** **comparison** also matters. AFMP family members of EU/EFTA workers enjoy strong derivative rights, including labour-market access for spouses and eligible children irrespective of nationality. By contrast, non-EU family rights depend on the sponsor's status. If the sponsor has a **B** **permit,** Article 46 AIG supports work by spouses and children. If the sponsor oonly has an L **permit,** the family's work position is weaker and subject to authorisation under VZAE Article 26. That distinction is highly relevant in graduate-switch content because the first non-EU work permit after study may well be an **L** **permit** rather than a B permit. 56

Finally, long-term planning should not be written in a nationality-blind way. The **C** **permit** is **not** an **AFMP** **right,** and EU/EFTA nationals are not all on the same clock. Many EU-15/EFTA nationals benefit from five-year settlement treaties,whereas other EU nationals generally do not. For non-EU graduates, the safer planning baseline remains the AIG settlement framework rather than any "graduate fast track".57

### I. Drafting cautions

Use the following drafting rules in internal content.

**·Do not write that EU/EFTA graduates use the same admission framework as non-EU** **graduates.** They do not. EU/EFTA cases are AFMP/VFP cases; non-EU cases are AIG/VZAE cases. 58

**·Do not describe the EU/EFTA route as permit-free in general.** Over-three-month employment still requires registration and a residence permit; under-three-month work still usually requires notification. 59

**·Do not say that Swiss-educated non-EU graduates are exempt from all third-country** **admission** **criteria.** The gathered primary and official materials support only a **precedence** **derogation** plus a **six-month job-search window.** 60

**· Do not say that non-EU Swiss graduates are quota-free.** Current official materials still maintain third-country quotas for 2026 and do not carve them out in the sources gathered. 19

**·Do not equate residence registration with work authorisation.** In EU/EFTA cases the residence permit functions as work authorisation, but that is a different structure from the employer-led non-EU work-permit process. 61

**·Do not over-generalise work-start timing for EU/EFTA cases.** SEM's general wording is stricter than some cantonal pages. If operational timing matters, note the cantonal dependency. 62

**· Do not present spouse work rights as identical across both frameworks.** AFMP derivative rights are broader and more automatic than the position of family members of a non-EU graduate holding only an L permit. 63

**·Do not promise a five-year C permit outcome simply because the person is EU/EFTA.** Settlement is outside AFMP and remains nationality-and treaty-sensitive. 27

**·Do not include UK nationals within EU/EFTA logic.** The AFMP no longer applies to the UK. 64

**·Flag Liechtenstein as a special EFTA case where necessary.** SEM materials routinely note special rules for Liechtenstein. 65

#### Open questions and limitations

There is a genuine **federal-cantonal operational tension on when an EU/EFTA graduate may start** **work** after filing for over-three-month employment. SEM's general FAQ states that registration and application must occur within 14 days of arrival **and** **before** **taking** **up** **work,** whereas Geneva's operational page says that, in some cases, activity may begin once the complete file has been submitted. The safer drafting stance is to present Geneva as a **cantonal practice** **point,** not as a universal federal rule. 62

Official federal materials are also **thin on a bespoke "EU/EFTA graduate switch" concept.** The better legal description is that EU/EFTA graduates use the **ordinary AFMP** **worker** **or** **jobseeker** **framework,** while some cantons package this operationally as a post-study search permit. That packaging should not be mistaken for a separate federal graduate-admission regime. 80

Finally, this pack is focused on **study-to-work** **transition.** It does **not** fully analyse self-employment, posted-worker/service-provider scenarios, or profession-specific licensing for regulated occupations, all of which can alter procedure even within the EU/EFTA framework. 81
