## Executive legal summary

Swiss "student residence" is not, in legal terms, astandalone visa category. For stays over three months, the core issue is admission and maintenance of a residence status for a defined study purpose; the entry visa, where required, is only the entry instrument and does not itself confer the longer residence right. Under Swiss federal law, third-country nationals studying in Switzerland are generally assessed under Article 27 FNIA, read with Article 23 and Article 24 OASA, while EU/EFTA nationals are primarily governed by the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the EU/EFTA residence framework for persons not pursuing an economic activity. The FNIA itself expressly provides that, for EU and EFTA nationals, Swiss domestic foreign-nationals law applies only subsidiarily where free-movement law does not provide otherwise or where the FNIA is more favourable. 1

For non-EU/EFTA students, Article 27 FNIA sets cumulative threshold conditions: the institution must confirm eligibility, suitable accommodation must be available, sufficient financial means must exist, personal and educational requirements for the planned course must be met, and minors must have guaranteed supervision. The statute also makes clear that any continued stay after completion or discontinuation is governed by the general admission rules, not by a continuing student entitlement. Article 27 is phrased as a discretionary "may be admitted" provision rather than a right-conferring rule, and SEM guidance and cited case law make clear that even where baseline conditions are met, authorities remain attentive to restrictive admission policy, route abuse, weak educational progression, second-education patterns, and lack of credible temporary purpose. 2

The decisive evidential question is therefore not merely whether there is an offer letter from a school. SEM guidance requires a more complete picture: a personal study plan, the educational goal sought, proof of prior academic level, accommodation, funding, a motivation letter, curriculum vitae, language material, and-where visa is required-personal attendance at the Swiss representation abroad, usually with an interview. The school's role is important but not absolute: under OASA and the SEM directives, the school must confirm the applicant's educational level and language ability for the course,while the authorities remain responsible for assessing whether the application is genuine, temporary, and coherent. For private schools, a listing in the Swiss Private School Register creates an administrative presumption that the school can satisfy Article 24 OASA, but it does not remove scrutiny of the student's personal qualifications, nor does it displace cantonal recognition rules. 3

For EU/EFTA students and other economically inactive persons, the logic is materially different. SEM's EU/EFTA guidance states that they are entitled to a B EU/EFTA permit if they show sufficient financial means and adequate health and accident insurance. This is a rights-based free-movement framework, not the same discretionary Article 27 FNIA analysis used for third-country nationals. That said, the route remains purpose-bound: insufficient resources can lead to revocation or non-renewal, and the permit is still issued and administered by cantonal authorities. 4

Student work is ancillary, not the legal purpose of stay. Under Article 38 OASA, third-country nationals studying at a Swiss university or university of applied sciences may be authorised to work part-time only from six months after studies begin, with written compatibility confirmation from the institutionn,a 15-hour weekly cap outside vacations, an employer application, and compliant pay and working conditions. Mandatory internships are separately regulated under Article 39 OASA, and post-graduate scientific work under Article 40 OASA. These categories should not be collapsed into ordinary labour-market admission. 5

Renewal is not automatic. SEM guidance expects cantonal authorities to monitor whether study milestones and examinations are being passed in due time. Insufficient progress, repeated failures, interruption, unjustified course changes, implausible second education, or a change in the real purpose of stay can all lead to non-renewal. The directives also state, as a general rule, that education or further education is admitted for a maximum of eight years, with more-than-eight-year cases requiring sufficient justification and SEM approval. 6

Post-study transition is a separate legal question. Third-country nationals with a Swiss university degree may, under Article 21(3) FNJIA, benefit from facilitated admission to qualified work where the job is of high academic or economic interest, and they may be admitted for six months after graduation to find such work. SEM guidance states that this six-month stay is a cantonal short-stay authorisation,requires adequate funds and accommodation by analogy with Article 27 FNIA, allows at most 15 hours' weekly work by analogy with Article 38 OASA, and cannot be extended. Long-term planning therefore requires care: student residence is temporary; it does not itself create a work entitlement, a C permit entitlement, or a naturalisation entitlement. Ordinary naturalisation still requires ten years' residence and a C permit, and temporary education stays do not count towards the uninterrupted five-year period immediately preceding a C permit grant. 7

## Primary legal texts

The following are the principal texts that matter most to Swiss student and education residence analysis.

**FNIA** **Article** 2 - **scope** **and** **EU/EFTA** **priority.** The FNIA applies to foreign nationals unless other federal or international provisions apply, andd for EU/EFTA nationals it applies only subsidiarily wvhere free-movement law is silent or the FNIA is more favourable. This matters because it prevents later drafting from collapsing EU/EFTA study residence into the third-country Article 27 model. 43

**FNIA Article 5 and Article 10 - entry conditions and permit requirement.** Entry requires, among other things, sufficient means and-where only a temporary stay is planned-a guarantee oof departure. For non-gainful stays over three months, a permit is required, and the permit must generally be applied for before entry. This matters because many student cases involve both visa and permit sequencing, and because "temporary purpose" is already emboedded at entry level. 44

**FNIA** **Article** 27 **-** **education** **and** **training.** A foreign national may be admitted for education or training if the institution confirms eliggibility, suitable accommodation exists, the necessary financial means are available, and the person meets the personal and educational requirements of the programme; minors also need guaranteed supervision. Paragraph 3 provides that any stay after completion or discontinuation is governed by the general admission rules. This is the central legislative provision for non-EU/EFTA study residence. 20

**FNIA** **Articles** **32** **and** **33** **-** **L** **permit and** **B** **permit.** The short-stay permit is for limited stays up to one year; the residence permit is for stays exceeding one year. SEM guidance applies this distinction directly to study cases: programmes under one year generally lead to an L permit, and those over one year to a B permit. 45

**FNIA** **Article** **21(3)** - **Swiss-degree** **graduates.** Third-country nationals with a Swiss university degree may be admitted without the ordinary precedence test if their work is of high academic or economic interest, and they are to be admitted for six months after completion of studies in Switzerland in order to find suitable work. This is the core statutory basis for post-study transition after Swiss higher education. 46

**FNIA** **Article** **61-** **expiry** of **permits.** Permits expire on departure abroad, on the grant of a permit in another canton, on expiry of validity, and in specified removal/expulsion circumstances. Together with the registration rules, this matters for absence, change of canton, and loss of status. 47

**FNIA** **Article 34(5)** **-** **settlement** **permit planning.** Temporary periods of stay, especially for education or training under Article 27, do not count towards the uninterrupted five-year period immediately preceding the grant of a settlement permit. This is central for long-term planning. 48

**OASA Article 23-conditions for education and training.** The ordinance gives illustrative forms of proof for sufficient financial means, defines personal qualifications in anti-abuse terms, states that education/further education is generally admitted only up to eight years, and points student work questions to Articles 38 to 40. This provision operationalises Article 27 FNIA. 29

**OASA** **Article** **24** **-** **requirements** **for** **schools.** Schools must guarantee an appropriate course offer and comply with the teaching programme; the programme and duration must be fixed; the school management must confirm the candidate's academic and linguistic level; and the authorities may require a language test in duly motivated cases. This provision explains why school status and programme structure are legally central rather than merely evidentially helpful. 29

**OASA Articles 38 to 40- ancillary work, compulsory internships, post-graduate work.** Article 38governs part-time student work at Swiss higher-education institutions after six months and under a 15-hour weekly cap outside vacations. Article 39 governs compulsory internships, including the rule that the activity must not exceed half of the total training duration. Article 40 separately governs work during post-graduate scientific training. These provisions are the main legal basis for work during studies. 15

**OASA** **Article** **54** - **change** **of purpose.** Where a permit was granted for a stay with a determined purpose, a new permit is required if the purpose changes. This matters for course changes, post-study transitions, and attempts to treat a student permit as if it were a bridge to another route without formal re-authorisation. 39

**AFMP/free-movement framework for EU/EFTA persons without gainful activity.** **The** AFMP secures residence rights for persons without an economic activity in the host state, and SEM's official EU/EFTA guidance states that economically inactive EU/EFTA nationals, including students, have a right to stay if they have sufficient resources and adequate health/accident insurance. This is the core legal logic for EU/EFTA students. 49

**Higher-education recognition and accreditation materials.** Swissuniversities maintains the official overview of accredited Swiss higher-education institutions under HEdA, and the Swiss Accreditation Council is the competent accreditation authority under that framework. These materials matter when later drafting needs to distinguish recognised higher-education institutions from other providers. 50

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

SEM's consolidated foreign-nationals directives are the most important official administrative guidance on study residence. They expressly state that Article 27 FNIA is the governing admissions provision for education and training, that Articles 23 and 24 OASA govern personal qualifications and school standards, and that authorities must prevent study-based stays from being used to circumvent stricter admission paths. The directives also explain that the route remains temporary even though Swiss graduates may later receive a six-month job-search stay and, in some cases, facilitated labour-market access. 51

On evidence, the directives require a personal study plan, a statement of the objective qualification sought, school confirmation of the candidate's level and language ability, a motivation letter, proof regarding tuition, financial means, accommodation, CV and language assessment. For visa-required applicants, the directives also provide for personal appearance at the Swiss representation abroad, a personal interview, document-authenticity checks, and-where needed-language testing. The interview is not a mere formality: it is expressly used to assess intentions, background, and return prospects. 52

<!-- 9 -->

On language evidence, the official material is deliberately flexible rather than certificate-exclusive. OASA requires school confirmation of the candidate's language level and allows the authorities to require a language test in duly motivated cases. SEM guidance adds an evaluation form, recognised certificates where available, and interview-based assessment by the mission abroad, with sample proficiency levels that vary by type of institution and course. 53

On educational institutions and private schools, the SEM directives and circular of 16 May 2024 are particularly important. The circular states that private schools listed in the Swiss Private School Register are presumed to provide an appropriate course offer within the meaning of Article 24(1) OASA,so in normal cases no in-depth curricular re-check is needed. But that presumptionis rebuttable, and if concrete indications suggest the institution no longer satisfies the Register conditions, contact should be made with SEM and the Register and, in principle, no residence permits should be issued until the situation is clarified. Just as importantly, the circular states that even where the school is registered, the migration authority must still examine the student's poersonal qualifications under Article 27 FNIA and Article 23 OASA. Cantonal and cantonal-school recognition rules remnain fully applicable. 42

On schools that are private, new, unknown or promoted aggressively abroad, SEM guidance is stricter still. The directives require these schools to demonstrate solid long-term financing and stable organisational structures, and they envisage active information-sharing between cantonal migration offices, education departments, municipalities, Swiss missions abroad, SEM and the Private School Register. That guidance is directly relevant to risk assessment for lesser-known providers and to later drafting on school credibility. 54

On renewals and progress, the directives instruct cantonal authorities to verify that students pass interim and final examinations in due time, and they say that where students do not comply with their obligations, the purpose of stay is treated as achieved and the permit is not extended. The same section states that change of orientation or additional education is admissible only exceptionally and with sufficient reasons. The directives cite case law involving maximum-duration overruns, insufficient results, interruptions, repeated changes of study, second education and lack of guaranteed departure. 38

On work during and after studies, SEM guidance closely follows OASA. It reiterates the six-month waiting period and 15-hour weekly ceiling for ancillary work, the distinct legal treatment of compulsory internships, and the six-month non-extendable job-search stay for graduates of Swiss higher-education institutions, with funds and accommodation assessed by analogy with Article 27. The SEM FAQ on work for non-EU/EFTA nationals confirms the same six-month post-study search period. 55

The most useful official cantonal material retrieved for internal practice notes came from Zurich, Vaud and Basel-Stadt. Zurich's current official page states that third-country students at universities and universities of applied sciences may only take part-time work from six months after studies begin, subject to institutional confirmation, no study delay, a 15-hour cap, and an employer-filed work permit application; it also separates internships while studying abroad, while studying in Switzerland, before commencement, and after completion. This is procedural/explanatory cantonal guidance rather than federal black-letter law, but it is well aligned with OASA Articles 38 to 40. 56

<!-- 10 -->

Vaud's official student memorandum is older, but still useful as acantonal evidence-and-procedure note. It states that the cantonal population service generally pre-authorises the visa but that the Swiss consular representation abroad is the authority that actually issues it; the student must wait abroad for the final visa decision. It alsosays any change in the study plan must be reported immediately, gives an indicative cantonal financial benchmark of about CHF 2,000 per month plus accommodation for students not living in residence, and confirms the need for prior authorisation of ancillary work and internships. Because this is cantonal guidance and dated, it should be used as a practice point only, not as a federal rule. 57

Basel-Stadt's official visa page provides a helpful clean statement of principle: a visa is not a residence permit, but prooof that entry conditions were met at the time of issuance; the representation may issue a visa for up to three months itself, but for longer stays the representation needs the authorisation of the competent canton and there must be a legally recognised purpose of stay. This is useful for keeping visa and permit questions analytically separate in later drafting. 58

## Practical legal analysis

Swiss student residence differs structurally from work, family and retirement residence because the law treats it as a temporary, purpose-specific, non-gainful route with closely monitored educational credibility. The legislative design is not "admission to a school equals right to stay"; instead, Article 27FNIA and Articles 23 to 24 OASA make educational residence a composite assessment of institutional status, accommodation, finances, personal suitability and genuine course logic. That is why Article 27uses discretionary language and why SEM guidance devotes so much attention to anti-abuse analysis. 59

This matters especially for non-EU/EFTA cases. The route is not a weakly regulated stepping-stone into the labour mmarket or a fallback residence category. SEM guidance states expressly that study residence must not be used to evade stricter admission paths, and it requires authorities to examine prior stays, prior applications, family circumstances, prior schooling and country-of-origin factors when judging whether the application genuinely seeks temporary study or primarily durable residence. The importance of the study plan and motivation letter is therefore substantive, not cosmetic. 60

Finances mnust also be analysed practically rather than as a bare checklist exercise. The statute requires sufficient means; OASA gives possible proof categories; SEM guidance and cantonal practice illustrate document-authenticity checks by missions abroad, scrutiny of the true source of funds, and the possibility of verifying Swiss bank guarantees and scholarships. This makes "one bank letter" a starting point, not necessarily the end of analysis. The same is true for accommodation: it is a statutory condition, not an afterthought. 61

School status and programme structure are equally central. Article 24 OASA requires an appropriate course offer and a fixed programme, while SEM's guidance adds specific concern about low-quality, under-structured, or newly created private schools. The Swiss Private School Register is legally useful because it creates an administrative presumption for Article 24 OASA, but it is not a universal legal substitute for individual scrutiny and it does not displace cantonal education law. For recognised higher education, the safer reference points are HEdA accreditation and the official swissuniversities list. 62

Student employment must be framed as ancillary. Article 38 OASA is narrow: higher-education institution only, six-month waiting period, institutional compatibility confirmation, 15 hours a week outside vacations, employer application, and compliant wage/conditions. Internships must also be disaggregated. A mandatory internship under Article 39 OASA has different logic from ordinary labour-market admission, and post-graduate scientific work under Article 40 OASA is narrower still. Later drafting should therefore never say that "students may freely work part-time" or that "internships are always allowed if educational". 5

<!-- 11 -->

Renewal depends on progress and continuing study purpose, not merely on the previous permit still being valid. SEM guidance makes this explicit by directing cantonal authorities to monitor examinations and timely progress, treating non-compliance as meaning the stay purpose has been reached and refusing further extension. The same material shows why second education, repeated changes,and course interruption are recurrent refusal themes. This also explains why the ordinary eight-year framework is not a mere duration guideline but part of a broader proportionality and credibility assessment. 63

Post-study stay should be handled in later drafting as a transition question, not asthe last phase of the student permit itself. Swiss-degree graduates may have a six-month job-search bridge and, if they secure qualifying work, may benefit from a derogation from the precedence rule where the job is of high academic or economic interest. But the six-month period is short, purpose-bound, non-extendable in SEM guidance, and still requires accommodation and funds. Nothing in the reviewed material supports presenting it as automatic long-term residence. 17

Cantonal and consular handling materially affect preparation. The federal framework is national, but applications are operationally shaped by cantonal authorities, Swiss missions abroad, education departments and, in some private-school cases, by information-sharing with SEM and the Register. Official cantonal material shows that timelines, checklists, the treatment of study-plan changes and the sequencing between visa and permit are not purely formal. Internal content generation should therefore keep federal law separate from cantonal procedure, but should not ignore procedure as outcome-relevant. 64

Long-term planning should remain explicit and conservative. Student residence is temporary, temporary education periods do not count towards the uninterrupted last five years for a C permit, and ordinary naturalisation requires both ten years' residence and a C permit. It follows that student residence may be part of a wider migration history, but it is not, without more, a direct settlement or citizenship route. 19

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe the route simply as a "student visa" without explaining that the visa is an entry tool and the residence permission is the substantive status. 8

Do not imply that admission to a school alone is sufficient; accommodation, funding, personal/ educational suitability and, for minors, supervision are statutory conditions. 10

Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA cases into one framework; EU/EFTA students primarily sit within AFMP/free-movement logic. 65

Do not present student residence as a work route; student work is ancillary and tightly conditioned. 15

Do not suggest that part-time work is automnatic or unrestricted; the six-month waiting period, institutional confirmation, 15-hour cap and employer application matter. 66

<!-- 12 -->

Do not treat all internships as automatically permitted because they are educational; Article 39 OASA has its own separate test. 35

Do not omnit temporary-purpose and return-intention analysis in thirdd-country cases; OASA and SEM guidance make anti-abuse scrutiny central. 25

Do not overstate repayment of the study investment or labour-market prospects in Switzerland as a positive factor; SEM interview guidance is concerned with future plans mainly to test credibility and departure. 67

Do not treat a listing in the Swiss Private School Register as equivalent to a guaranteed immigration outcome or universal "state recognition". It creates an administrative presumption under Article 24OASA, but personal qualifications and cantonal school rules remain separate. 42

Do not present cantonal finance figures or processing times as federal law. They are procedural or evidential practice points only. 57

Do not imply that post-study work rights are automatic or long-term; the reviewed official materials support only a limited six-month job-search bridge and then a separate work-authorisation analysis. 68

Do not imply that student residence automatically leads to C permit or citizenship. 19
