# Swiss legal authority pack for student residence involving language schools, preparatory courses, foundation years and pathway programmes

Suggested filename:

legalauthoritystudentpermitslanguageschoolspreparatorycoursespack.pdf

# Executive legal summary

Swiss law does **not** operate as a single, unified "student visa" framework. For third-country nationals, the core residence basis for study is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and its implementing ordinance, especially Article 27 FNIA and Articles 23-24 OASA; that route is framed as a **discretionary** **admnission** to a temporary stay without gainful activity. For EU/EFTA nationals, the controlling source is instead the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, especially Annex I Article 24, read with the OLCP andSEM free-movement guidance; that route is materially different because it is treaty-based where the conditions are met. A national visa may be needed as an **entry** instrument for some third-country applicants, but the residence analysis itself is carried by residence-permit law, not by a single "student visa" category. 1

For third-country nationals, a language-school or preparatory-course case is strongest where the programme is a genuine part of a defined educational or professional pathway, is delivered by an eligible school, is full-time, has a fixed curriculum and duration, and the file shows objective reasons for undertaking that training in Switzerland. Current SEM guidance expressly accepts language-school cases only where language acquisition is necessary for the planned education or professional path and where there are objective reasons to undertake the language training in Switzerland. SEM guidance also requires a full-time programme of at least 20 hours per week and treats evening or otherwise limited programmes as non-qualifying. 2

For EU/EFTA nationals, the legal posture is more favourable but still not unlimited. Annex I Article 24(4) AFMP/ALCP gives a right to a residence title to a student who is enrolled in a recognised institution in Switzerland for a principal course of study or vocational training, has sufficient means so as not to become a burden on social assistance, and holds comprehensive sickness insurance. SEM's OLCP guidance further states that, unlike other economically inactive persons, persons in education need only make it plausible that they have sufficient means, and that the OASA restrictions in Articles 23-25do not apply to this treaty-based category. 3

As a practical matter, "foundation year" and "pathway programme" are not independent statutory categories. They are analysed functionally. If the programme is a recognised, time-limited bridge into an identified later course and the student can already satisfy the school-eligibility,language,finance and temporary-stay requirements, it may fit. If instead it looks like a loose sequence of language classes and contingent future study, the risk of refusal rises sharply. Zurich's current public practice is notably stricter than the federal baseline: it rejects the chaining of different training programmes,especially

where a language course is placed in front of a later academic or vocational course, subject to a narrow exception for foreign-language students preparing for the German examination required by ETH Zurich or the University of Zurich. 4

# Structural overview of the legal framework

The third-country framework begins with the general rule that a foreign national planning a stay of more than three months without gainful activity requires a permit and should apply before entering Switzerland. Where only a temporary stay is planned, the applicant must be able to guarantee departure. Article 27 FNIA then provides that third-country nationals **may** be admitted for education or training if the school confirms eligibility, suitable accommodation exists, the necessary financial means are available, and the applicant fulfils the personal and educational requirements for the planned course. Article 23 OASA adds anti-abuse controls, including the requirement that prior stays or surrounding circumstances must not indicate that the training is being used merely to circumvent ordinary admission rules; it also sets an ordinary maximum training duration of eight years. Article 24OASA deals with school quality, fixed curriculum and duration, and confirmation of language and educational readiness. 5

SEM's current 2026 directives are the most operationally important federal administrative source for third-country cases. They emphasise that student stays must not be used to evade stricter admission rules; require a personal study plan and a stated goal; say that only a full-time programme with at least 20 classroom hours per week qualifies under Article 27 FNIA; define full-time schools as establishments teaching every weekday; require proof that the student can follow the teaching without difficulty; and state that a stay permit for studies is issued as a B permit if the course exceeds one year, but as an L permit if it is shorter. Accessory work rights are also differentiated: ordinary accessory work after six months under Article 38 OASA is for students at universities and universities of applied sciences, while mandatory internships are tied to full-time training and capped at half of the total training duration. 6

The EU/EFTA framework is different in both structure and tone. Article 2 FNIA itself makes Swiss foreign-national law only subsidiarily applicable where the AFMP/ALCP contains different or more favourable rules. Annex I Article 24 AFMP/ALCP then supplies the core right for economically inactive persons and, specifically in paragraph 4, for students. SEM's OLCP guidance and factsheet present this as aright of residence for EU/EFTA retirees, students and other economically inactivepersons who have sufficient means and sickness/accident insurance, with special annual renewal rules for students until the end of the course. The same SEM guidance also states expressly that the OASA restrictions in Articles 23-25 do not apply to these treaty-based non-active stays. 7

Cantons remain central in practice. They examine the file, issue or renew permits, and in some cases their positive decision may still be subject to SEM approval. Vaud's study-permit form states that the cantonal authority may accept a file but the federal authority may still take it under control and refuse entry or the study permit notwithstanding cantonal acceptance. Zurich's guidance also notes that the office may submit cases to SEM, including because of higher migration-risk nationalities or evidential concerns. Internal drafting therefore needs to separate **federal** **legal basis, SEM** **administrative** **guidance,** and **canton-specific** **practice** **points** rather than collapsing them into one undifferentiated rule set. 8

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

SEM's 2026 directives are unusually important here because they do much of the real doctrinal work for language-school and preparatory-course cases. They say the applicant must submit a personal study plan and identify the intended goal; the school must confirm that the candidate has the educational level and the language knowledge needed for the proposed course; Swiss representations may interview the applicant about why Switzerland was chosen and what the future plan is on return home; and the authorities must do everythinng possible to prevent training permits from being used to evade stricter admission rules. The directives also record that a blanket practice of refusing study permits to applicants over 30 is unlawful under the constitutional ban on discrimination, while also stating that the temporary nature of study residence means Article 8 ECHR private-life arguments are weak in principle. 17

On school quality, SEM's guidance is equally practical. It says schools entered in the Swiss Private School Register are presumed to satisfy Article 24(1) OASA; new or unknown private schools must show lasting financial resources and stable organisation; and cantonal education authorities may be asked for an opinion or recommendation. That matters directly for pathway and foundation-year providers, because a sizeable part of the litigation risk often sits not in the student's profile but in the provider's status, structure, and programme design. 18

Zurich's current public guidance is the clearest cantonal practice note located in this research. It states that registered private lannguage schools are presumed to meet the foreign-national law requirements for proper instruction, but it also narrows the substantive scope: permits may be granted for language schools only for the learning of a Swiss national language or English, and no immigratioon permits are issued for learning other languages. Zurich also requires a detailed personal study programme explaining the exact duration and the educational objective, insists on a written return undertaking in non-university cases, monitors compliance with the study programme and examinations, refuses the chaining of programmes, and recognises only a narrow exception for foreign-language students preparing for the German examination required before study at ETH Zurich or the University of Zurich, with an initial German intensive course permit of up to six months and a possible extension to twelve months if attendance is serious and regular: 19

Vaud's study materials are more documentary than doctrinal, but they are highly useful for internal drafting. The canton's student form requires the school to confirm that the student has the required educational level and language knowledge for the proposed studies; requires a letter of intent explaining the reasons for choosing Switzerland and the student's future plans after the studies; requires the complete study plan in Switzerland to be declared, including all intended courses; requires immediate notification of any change of orientation, school or extension; and treats financial means as sufficient at roughly CHF 2,000 per month plus accommodation for students not in boarding arrangements. It also asks for a written commitment to leave Switzerland at the end of the intended studies. 20

Geneva's current official portals also help as cross-checks. The Geneva student-permit pages distinguish conditions by nationality, say that student residence is needed where the foreign national wishes to live and study in Geneva, and state for third-country nationals that the permit is ggranted for the duration of the course ifit lasts less than a year, or as a one-year permit renewable until the end of the studies if the course lasts longer; the English page adds expressly that student permits are strictly temporary. Ticino's SPOP student page separately identifies study permits, student work, notification of accessory work and training internships, which is useful confirmation that study residence and study-related work are administered as distinct legal questions. 21

## Practical legal analysis

**When** **language** **study** **may** **support residence.** Under the third-country framework, a pure wish to improve language skills in Switzerland is too weak on its own. The current federal guidance accepts language-school cases only where the language acquisition is necessary for the envisaged education or career path and where there are objective reasons to undertake the training in Switzerland. In practice, the file should therefore identify the downstream course,exam or professional objective and explain why Switzerland, and this school, are necessary rather than merely desirable. Zurich's published practice is stricter still and shouId be treated as a live risk signal in any canton likely to scrutinise similar facts closely. 22

**Preparatory courses for university or a vocational route.** Swiss law is capable of accommodating genuine preparatory study, but the safer files are those in which the later route is concrete and evidenced. SEM's own guidance recognises study-preparatory language courses, including language preparation in a university context, and requires only Level 1/A2 in such cases. At the same time, Zurich rejects programme chaining where a language course is inserted before a later general or vocational course or where matriculation remmains conditional and further preparatory courses must first be completed, save for the narrow ETH/UZH German-exam exception. The practical lesson is that a preparatory course should be presented as an integral step in a clearly identified course of study, not as an open-ended attempt to remain in Switzerland until something else becomes available. 23

**Foundation-year** **and** **pathway** **programmes.** These labels are not determinative in Swviss residence law. What matters is how the programme functions against Article 27 FNIA and Articles 23-24 OASA or, for EU/EFTA nationals, Annex I Article 24 AFMP/ALCP. A pathway programme is more defensible where the provider is recognised or otherwise clearly meets Article 24 OASA standards, the curriculum and duration are fixed, the course is full-time, the next step is defined in the file, and the school can certify both educational readiness and the language level needed to follow the teaching. It is less defensible where the "pathway" is really a sequence of contingent stages, particularly if the student's later admission is uncertain or the provider is new, unstable or weakly documented. 24

**Language** **level** at **entry.** The statute itself requires the school to confirm that the applicant meets the linguistic and educational prerequisites for the planned training, and it allows the authority to demand an additional language test in justified cases. SEM's current administrative guidance tightens this operationally by requiring a language-assessment sheet in all cases and by expecting that the applicant can follow instruction without difficulty. Imnportantly for language-school and preparatory-language cases, the SEM guidance places these at Level 1, equated with CEFR A2, rather than at true beginner level. That does not create a freestanding statutory rule,but it is a significant current administrative benchmark and should be treated as such in an internal content system. 25

**Duration, progression** **and** **anti-abuse.** The ordinary eight-year cap in Article 23(3) OASA is only part of the picture. SEM's directives make academic progression a renewal issue: students are expected to pass interim and final examinations within the expected period, subject to modest delay allowances, and repeated changes of course or additional training are only exceptionally acceptable. The same directives cite cases where refusal was upheld because the eight-year cap had already been exceeded, results were poor, the case involved a second training course, or departure after the training was not sufficiently guaranteed. Conversely, the directives also note a case showing that the absence of a diploma or vocational certificate at the end of the Swiss training is not, by itself, fatal under Article 27FNIA / Article 24 OASA. The correct internal synthesis is therefore not "diploma required", but rather "genuine, coherent, time-bounded progression required". 26

**EU/EFTA** **cases.** The treaty route removes several third-country obstacles but not the need for careful classification. The student must still be enrolled in a recognised institution in Switzerland, must pursue the training as a principal activity, and must show sufficient means and comprehensive health insurance. SEM's current OLCP guidance makes clear that this is a residence-right framework for persons in education and that the OASA restrictions in Articles 23-25 do not apply. That said, a language or pathway programme still needs to fit the treaty language of a recognised institution and a principal course of study or vocational training; internal drafting should therefore avoid assuming that every private language-school product automatically falls within the treaty guarantee. 3

# Drafting cautions

·Do not describe the subject as a single Swiss "student visa" regime. Distinguish entry visas from residence permits, and distinguish third-country residence under FNIA/OASA from EU/EFTA residence under the AFMP/ALCP and OLCP. 27

·For third-country nationals, use discretionary language: "may be admitted", "permit may be granted", "renewal may be refused". Do not write as though Article27 FNIA creates an entitlement. 9

·Do not say that "a language course is enough". The conservative proposition is narrower: language study may support residence only where it is necessary for the planned education or professional path and objectively justified in Switzerland. 22

·Do not omit the **full-time** issue. Current SEM guidance requires a full-time programme with at least 20 hours per week, and it excludes evening schools and other limited-course formats from the full-time category. 11

·Do not imply that applicants can arrive as true beginners for language-school or study-preparatory language-course cases. The present SEM guidance places those cases at Level 1/A2and still requires the school to confirm that the applicant can follow the teaching. 28

<!-- 6 -->

·Do not normalise programme chaining. Repeated study changes, weak progress, or sequential "language first, real course later" structures are classic refusal risks. Zurich's published practice is particularly adverse on this point. 22

·Do not imply post-studly job-search rights for language-school, pathway or ordinary private-school attendees. The six-month post-study job-search route identified in federal guidance is tied to graduates of Swiss higher-education institutions, not to preparatory or language-school attendance as such. 29

·Do not use age 30 as a hard refusal rule. SEM's current directives expressly record that the earlier blanket practice against applicants over30 was held contrary to the constitutional ban on discrimination. At the same time, age may still be one factual element among others in the overall temporary-stay and return assessment; it is simply not lawful as an automatic bar. 30
