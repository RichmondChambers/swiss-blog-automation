# Swiss student residence, settlement counting and naturalisation planning

## Executive summary and framework

A. **Executive** **legal summary.** Swiss law does not operate a single, unitary "student visa" regime. For third-country nationals, study-based admission sits under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and is discretionary: foreign nationals may be admitted for education if the statutory conditions are met, and any stay after completion or abandonment of studies is governed by the general admission rules. SEM's foreigners' directives treat study residence as a temporary stay, emphasise the need to prevent misuse of the education route, and state that, once the study purpose has ended, any further stay requires a new permit; as a rule, the person must leave Switzerland and await the new decision abroad unless the new admission conditions are obviously fulfilled. By contrast, for EU/EFTA nationals, residence as a student or other non-economically active person can arise under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, provided there are sufficient means, insurance and admission to a recognised institution; but the AFMP does **not** itself regulate settlement permits, which remain governed by Swiss domestic law and settlement treaties. 1

The crucial planning distinction is that **settlement counting** and **ordinary naturalisation counting are** not identical. For settlement under Article 34 AIG, the final five-year continuous residence window is stricter: temporary stays do not count there, and study periods count only on the conditional basis in Article 34(5) AIG, namely where, after the end of studies, the person has held a residence permit for a durable stay for two uninterrupted years. SEM further explains that, in the **first** five years of the ordinary ten-year C-permit route, all L and B periods may count regardless of purpose, including study; but in the **final** five-year B-permit period, temporaryresidence is excluded unless the Article 34(5) cure is satisfied. For ordinary naturalisation, by contrast, the federal residence count turns primarily on permit **category**: B and C count in full, F counts half, and L does not count. A person can therefore accumulate countable naturalisation residence through student-B years but still be unable to apply for ordinary naturalisation until a C permit is actually obtained. 2

A second distinction is between **continuity of permit status and citizenship readiness. A** C permit can lapse through deregistration or long absence, whereas ordinary naturalisation applies its own residence-abandonment rules and additionally requires a valid C permit at filing, during the procedure, at federal authorisation stage and at the final ordinary naturalisation decision. Short absences and certain educational or employer-directed stays abroad may still preserve naturalisation residence, but they do not erase the need to track permit validity, current status and cantonal/communal residence conditions separately. 3

B. **Structural overview** **of** **the** **legal** **framework.** The correct hierarchy is: treaty law first for EU/EFTA admission and residence where the AFMP applies; federal migration law and ordinances for third-country admission, permit changes, integration and settlement; federal citizenship law and ordinance for ordinary naturalisation; SEM directives and handbooks as the central administrative interpretation layer; and then cantonal rules and practice, especially for ordinary naturalisation procedure, local residence periods, evidence, language proof and timing. Cantons cannot undercut federal minima for ordinary naturalisation, but they can add local residence rules, local knowledge tests and, in practice, stricter compliance screening. 4

## Primary authorities and official materials

D. **Primary** **legal texts.** The core federal provisions for this topic are: Article 27AIG on admission for education; Article 17 AIG on staying pending a decision when a person moves from a temporary to a durable residence request; Article 21(3) AIG as operationalised by SEM for the Swiss-graduate six-month job-search arrangement; Article 34 AIG on settlement; Article 58a AIG on integration criteria; and Article 61 AIG on lapse of permits. At ordinance level, Article 54 VZAE requires a new permit when the purpose of stay changes, Article 60 VZAE governs ordinary settlement integration, and Article 62 VZAE governs early settlement. For citizenship, Article 9 BüG sets the federal formal requirements for ordinary naturalisation, Article 12 BüG sets the federal integration criteria, Article 33 BüG governs countable residence, and Article 16 BüV addresses educational and employer-directed absences abroad. 16

E. **Official guidance** **and** **administrative** **materials.** The most important federal administrative sources are the SEM **Weisungen und Erläuterungen I. Ausländerbereich** (status **1** January 2026),especially the chapters on education stays, integration and settlement, and SEM's **Handbuch** **Bürgerrecht,** especially chapter 3 on ordinary naturalisation. For EU/EFTA students, the SEM factsheet on non-gainful residence in Switzerland is the key operational source; it states the AFMP conditions, permit duration and renewal logic for students. SEM's public pages on ordinary naturalisation and on EU/EFTA B and C permits are also useful for high-level current practice statements. 17

F. **Cantonal practice** **notes.** Zurich's migration pages are particularly useful for third-country student practice and naturalisation administration. Zurich expressly requires third-country students over 30 to justify the course in Zurich, requires secured re-departure, discourages chaining of courses and generally caps study residence at eight years; it also notes the Swiss-graduate job-search short-stay

route. On naturalisation, Zurich states that B and C count fully, F counts half, and N and L do not count; it additionally requires, in general, two years in the same municipality before filing, screens debt enforcement history over five years, and treats moves dduring the naturalisation process differently depending on procedural stage. Geneva's official permit-C page distinguishes officeexamination from formal application, confirms that the AFMP itself does not govern C, lists treaty-state five-year categories and notes that N and F do not count for the C clock. Ticino's naturalisation page is a strong example of cantonal layering: it requires a valid C, 10 Swiss years with B/C/F (F half), 5 years in Ticino, 3years in the commune including the last 2, Italian at B1 oral / A2 written, and no social assistance in the 10 years before filing, at filing or during the procedure. Vaud's official publication usefully confirms the three-level communal, cantonal and federal ordinary naturalisation sequence and indicates an overall procedure length of roughly 24 to 36 months. 18

## Practical legal analysis

G. **Practical** **legal analysis.** For internal drafting, the planning sequence should always be separated into three questions: first, what is the **current** **legal basis** of residence; secondly, which past periods are **countable** **for** **settlement;** and thirdly, when the person will both have sufficient **naturalisation** **residence** and actually **hold** **a** **C** **permit.** Most public-facing summaries collapse those three questions, but Swiss law does not. 19

On the **student** **residence** **side,** third-country study stays are temporary by design. SEM's 2026directives say the educational route is intended to prevent circumvention of stricter admission rules; Zurich's practice shows howthis is operationalised through re-departure evidence, scrutiny of age and study plan, resistance to chained education periods, and a general eight-year ceiling. The same SEM directives also rely on Federal Supreme Court case law to state that a person holding a study permit cannot ordinarily invoke the private-life limb of Article 8 ECHR on the basis of that stay precisely because the temporary character was known from the outset. 20

**For EU/EFTA students,** the logic is materially different. The AFMP framework grants a residence right to non-economically active persons, including students, where the person has sufficient resources and comprehensive insurance; SEM's EU/EFTA factsheet also requires proof of admission to a recognised institution and states that the permit is issued for the duration of the training or for one year, renewing until the ordinary completion of the course if the conditions remnain met. But that treaty-based residence should not be mistaken for a treaty-based settlement entitlement: SEM's own C-EU/EFTA page says settlement remains governed by the AIG and settlemnent treaties because the AFMP contains no settlement-permit rules. 21

**On** **transition** **from** **study** **to** **work,** the main third-country bridge is the six-month job-search short-stay permit after a Swiss university degree. SEM states that there is a statutory entitlement to that six-month arrangement if the person has the qualifying Swiss degree, sufficient means and adequate accommodation. That bridge is useful, but only in a narrow sense. It does not itself generate a durable residence basis. It is also expressly excluded from countable residence under Article 34's five-year settlement logic, even though SEM says it does not interrupt that clock. For ordinary naturalisation it is worse still: because it is an L permit, it does not count at all under the federal residence-counting rule. 22

**On** **transition** **from** **studly** **to** **family** **status,** the system again changes legal basis rather than merely extending the student route. Where a person becomes the spouse of a Swiss citizen or of a C-permit holder, or otherwise falls within a family track, settlement may become available on a shortened timetable. Geneva's official page is especially useful here because it states that, for spouses of Swiss

citizens or of C-permit holders, the relevant five-year settlement period is calculated from the marriage or from the spouse's acquisition of Swiss nationality or a C permit. That means earlier student residence should not automatically be treated as satisfying the family-route shortcut, even if the broader history of lawful residence still matters for other purposes. 23

**The** **settlement count** itself has to be modelled with precision. Article 34(2) AIG requires at least ten years in Switzerland with L or B, plus the final five years uninterrupted with B. SEM then adds an important interpretative distinction. In the **first** **five** **years** of the ordinary ten-year route, all L and B periods are counted regardless of purpose, including educational residence. In the **last five** **years,by** contrast, temporary stays are not counted. Educational residence may nevertheless be counted retrospectively if, after studies end, the person then holds a B permit for a durable stay for two uninterrupted years. In practical terms, a study-B period can therefore "ripen" into settlement-countable residence only after a later conversion into durable B status. 10

That settlement logic produces a divergence which internal systems should capture explicitly. A **student** B can help with both the later ordinary naturalisation total and the settlement total, but not on the same terms and not necessarily at the same time. A **student** **L** can contribute to the total ten-year settlement residence under Article 34(2)(a), because that paragraph counts L or B, but the same L period does not count for ordinary naturalisation because Article 33 BüG uses a closed list that excludes L. This is one of the most important hidden mismatches in long-term planning. 24

SEM's 2026 guidance also contains a highly relevant **EU/EFTA** **doctoral/postdoctoral** nuance. It says that doctoral and postdoctoral residence of EUJ/EFTA nationals ordinarily does **not** count towards settlement because it is considered temporary. Hlowever, where the doctoral or postdoctoral period is tied to an employment contract of more than 15 hours per week and the person then moves into the private sector or public administration, SEM states that the period is counted retroactively. That is a specific administrative exception and should not be generalised to all academic study residence. 25

**For** **ordinary** **naturalisation,** the federal rules are structurally simpler but procedurally stricter. Article 9BüG requires a C permit and ten years' residence in Switzerland, of which three must falI within the last five years before filing. SEM's citizenship handbook then clarifies that the overall ten-year total may be interrupted, but the three-in-five requirement needs continuity subject to the statutory absence rules. It also states that the applicant must hold the C permit not only when filing but throughout the procedure, at federal authorisation stage and at the final cantonal ordinary naturalisation decision. A person whose student-B years are fully countable may therefore still be blocked until Cstatus is obtained and preserved. 26

Federal **naturalisation** **counting** turns on permit category, not on whether the B was issued for work, family or study. SEM states that the list in Article 33 BüG is exhaustive: B and C count, F counts at half weight, and certain EDA cards count; L, G, N and S do not. Zurich's ordinary naturalisation page repeats the same logic in operational form. For student planning, that means a person with several years on a student-B can build ordinary naturalisation residence from the first day of that B, whereas a person spending the same years on L cannot. The ages-8-to-18 double-counting rule still applies, but the actual residence must total at least six years. 27

The **integration** **layer** also becomes stricter as the residence status strengthens. Ordinary settlement requires the Article 58a AIG integration criteria and, under Article 60 VZAE, language at A2 oral / A1written. Early settlement under Article 34(4) AIG and Article 62 VZAE requires higher language at B1oral / A1 written and remains discretionary; SEM expressly says authorities need not examine early settlement ex officio without a specific request. Ordinary naturalisation requires broader integration

<!-- 5 -->

under Articles 11 and 12 BüG, with language at B1 oral / A2 written, participation in economic life or education, and compliance with public-order and constitutional values. 28

Financial and compliance checks are also not symmetrical. For settlement, SEM looks at public-order compliance, debts and taxes within the Article 58a framework and at the absence of revocation grounds. For ordinary naturalisation, SEM's handbook states that anyone who has received social assistance in the three years before filing or during the procedure fails the federal requirement unless the amounts are fully repaid, subject to the personal-circumstances safety valve in Article 12(2) BüG and Article 9 BüV. Cantons may go further in practice: Zurich focuses on debt-enforcement history over five years and current self-support or education; Ticino states that ordinary naturalisation is excluded if social assistance has been received during the ten years before filing, at filing or during the procedure unless fully repaid. 29

Finally, **interruptions, absences** **and** **deregistration** must be separated into two ledgers. Under Article 61 AIG, a B or C permmit lapses on deregistration abroad and, absent deregistration, after six months abroad; for C, continuity can be preserved for up to four years on application. For ordinary naturalisation residence, SEM states that short departures with intent to return do not break residence and that employer-directed or education absences abroad of up to one year can still be treated as temporary; but deregistration or living abroad for more than six months is treated as abandonment of Swiss residence. The correct internal model is therefore not a single "continuous residence" flag, but at least: permit-validity continuity, Article 34 settlement continuity, Article 33/16 BüG-BüV naturalisation continuity, and cantonal/communal residence continuity. 30

## Drafting cautions

### H. Drafting cautions.

·Do not describe the Swiss route as a single "student visa" framework. Internal content should distinguish, at minimum, third-country education residence under Article 27 AIG, EU/EFTA treaty-based student/non-economically-active residence under the AFMP, and the operational difference between B and L permits. 34

·Do not state that "study years count for the C permit" without the Article 34(5) qualification. Study residence is treated as temporary unless and until the post-study conversion condition is satisfied; the settlement effect is therefore conditional and sometimes retrospective. 10

·Do **not** state that "student residence does not count" without specifying the context. Student B years can count fully for ordinary naturalisation and can count in parts of settlement analysis; student L years do not count for ordinary naturalisation. 35

·Do not present post-study work as automatic for third-country nationals. A new legal basis is required after studies, and the six-month Swiss-graduate job-search permit is narrow, non-extendable and not itself a durable residence basis. 36

·Do not conflate "enough residence years for ordinary naturalisation" with "citizenship-ready". Ordinary naturalisation also requires a current C permit throughout the process, federal integration compliance, and whatever cantonal/communal residence, knowledge and evidential layers apply in the filing canton. 37

·Do **not** assume EU/EFTA nationality alone creates a five-year settlement entitlement. Settlement depends on the AIG plus settlement treaties or long-standing practice; the AFMP itself does not regulate the C permit. 38

·Internal datasets should store **permit form, permit basis, date of study completion, date of** **first durable B after study, deregistration events, actual absences abroad,** and **canton/** **commune** **residence** **history** as separate fields. Without those fields, Article 34 AIG counting and ordinary naturalisation counting cannot be modelled conservatively. 39
