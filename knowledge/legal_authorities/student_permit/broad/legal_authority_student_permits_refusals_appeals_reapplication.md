# Swiss legal authority pack on refusals, appeals, reconsideration and reapplication in student permit cases

Suggested internal filename:

legalauthoritystudentpermitsrefusalsappealsreapplicationpack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

Swiss study-related residence cases do not operate under one single "student visa"framework.For third-country nationals, the core residence basis is Article 27 AIG, read with Articles 23 and 24 VZAE and related SEM guidance; this is a **discretionary** admission ground, not a statutory entitlement. SEM's 2026AIG directives expressly state that Article 27 AIG governs study admissions, that Articles 23 and 24 VZAE supply the requirements on personal qualifications and schools, and that Article 27 AIG does not create a claim to the permit; they also note Federal Administrative Court authority confirming that even where Article 27 conditions are met, a request may still be refused within a restrictive admissions policy. 1

For EU/EFTA nationals, the position is materially different. Their residence forstudy is primarily governed by the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing regime, not by the restrictive third-country study framework. Under Annex I Article 24 AFMP, students who are enrolled at a recognised institution, have sickness insurance and can make financial self-sufficiency plausible obtain a treaty-based residence position; SEMI's 2026 VFP guidance states expressly that the restrictive admission requirements in Articles 23-25 VZAE do **not** apply to this non-economic residence category. 2

The most important refusal themes in third-country cases are stable across federal guidance, cantonal practice notes and cited case law: inadequate or poorly evidenced finances; incoherent or weak study plans; unsuitable educational progression, including second training, repeated changes of direction and poor results; doubts about the genuinely temporary purpose of the stay and the applicant's willingness to leave; doubtful or unsuitable schools; insufficient language ability; and adverse prior immigration history suggesting that study is being used to circumvent ordinary admission rules. The federal materials are unuusually direct on these points. 3

In practice, the strategic fork is between **appealing** **the** **refusal** and **rebuilding** **the** **case.** An appeal is strongest where the authority misapplied the law, ignored evidence already on file, wrongly treated a school as unsuitable, or failed to respect a treaty-based EU/EFTA position. Rebuilding is usually better where the file was objectively thin and can now be cured by new funding evidence, a better institution, stronger language proof, or a materially improved study narrative. That said, one must be careful not to assume that Swiss reconsideration practice is a second chance to remedy neglected cooperation: SEM's guidance states expressly that reconsideration may not be used to heal missed participation duties or to introduce facts that could and should have been produced the first time. 4

## Structural overview of the legal framework

The framework is structurally split between **entry** **clearance** **and** **residence** **authorisation.** For visa-required third-country nationals, a national visa process may run through the Swiss representation abroad,while the substantive residence authorisation is decided at cantonal level and, in some cases, is additionally subject to SEM approval. Cantonal materials from Geneva, Vaud and Ticino all reflect this separation: applicants are told to apply through the representation abroad where relevant, and Vaud expressly notes that the cantonal service may accept or refuse the request while the federal migration authority may still take over an accepted case and refuse entry or authorisation despite cantonal acceptance. 5

For third-country study residence, the legal architecture is cumulative. SEM's 2026 AIG directives identify Article 27 AIG as the substantive basis; VZAE Article 23 governs proof of means, circumvention indicators and the general eight-year limit; VZAE Article 24 governs school quality, programme structure, language and educational fit; VZAE Article 54 requires a **new** **permit** if the residence purpose changes; and SEM approval may still be required in categories fixed by the EJPD approvalregulation and Articles 85-86 VZAE. 6

For EU/EFTA students, Annex I Article 24 AFMP is the key norm. The official treaty text provides residence for non-economically active persons with sufficient resources and insurance, and specifically provides a student residence document limited to the duration of study, or to one year where study lasts longer than a year, provided the student is enrolled in a recognised institution chiefly for vocational education and has insurance and sufficient means established by declaration or equivalent proof. SEM's 2026 VFP guidance confirms that for students the financial means need only be made plausible and that student permits are renewed annually while the conditions continue to be met. 7

The remedies structure is equally split. Federal guidance states that the Federal Administrative Court hears appeals against SEM decisions, while the Federal Supreme Court is generally available against final cantonal or Federal Administrative Court decisions, **except** where Article 83(c) BGG excludes appeals in alien-law matters, including refusals of permits to which neither federal law nor international law gives a right. That exclusion is central for third-country student permits, but not for genuinely treaty-based EU/EFTA student rights. 8

## Primary legal texts, official guidance and cantonal practice notes

**Primary** **legal texts.** The controlling federal texts are: Article 27 AIG for study admission; Article 5(2) AIG for secure departure in temporary stays as reflected in the current SEM directives; Articles 23 and 24 VZAE on finances, circumvention, school quality and the eight-year rule; Article 54 VZAE on change of residence purpose; Articles 85 and 86 VZAE on SEM approval; Annex I Article 24 AFMP for EU/EFTA students; and Article 83(c) BGG on the exclusion of public-law appeals to the Federal Supreme Court where there is no domestic or international right to the permit. 16

**Official guidance** **/ administrative** **materials.** The most operationally important federal source is the SEM 2026 AIG directives, especially chapter 5.1 on education and chapter 10.8 on remedies. Those directives require a personal study plan, exact identification of the targeted diploma, confirmation by the institution that the applicant has the educational and language level needed, adequate financial proof, a suitable dwelling, and a genuinely temporary purpose. They also collate refusal case law, set out interview themes used to test return intentions, give a structured language framework,and summarise the law on appeal, revision and reconsideration. The SEM 2026 VFP guidance plays the same role for EU/EFTA cases and expressly distinguishes students from other non-economically active persons. 17

<!-- 3 -->

**CantonaI practice** **notes.** Zurich's published internal guidance is the clearest cantonal operational note available in the public domain: it requires a detailed study programme, reasons for choosing Zurich, a full CV and translated diplomas, fixes a practice figure of CHF 21,000 for 12 months, treats repeated programme changes as suspect, permits only one justified change of direction, and says that chaining programmes is generally not approved. Geneva's public pages require, for third-country applicants, a motivation letter, CV, detailed study plan, written undertaking to leave at the end of studies except for Swiss higher-education students, and evidence of means; the FAQ gives a practice benchmark of CHF 20,000 per year or CHF 2,500 per month and states that any school or study-plan change is subject to a new authorisation. Vaud's student form similarly requires a letter of intentions, CV, study plan, written departure undertaking, housing proof and financing proof; it also states that SEM can still intervene notwithstanding cantonal acceptance. Ticino's pages are useful mainly for the L/B distinction and entry sequencing: under one year normally tracks an L permit, over one year a B permit, and third-country nationals abroad must file through the Swiss representation and wait abroad for the outcome. 18

## Practical legal analysis

**Insufficient** **finances.** For third-country nationals, VZAE Article23 and the SEM directives treat financing as a core admission requirement, with acceptable proof including a Swiss-resident guarantor with means, a Swiss bank confirmation, scholarships or study loans, and in some cases other guarantees. Cantons then operationalise this with their own practice figures: Zurich uses CHF 21,000 for twelve months; Geneva uses CHF 20,000 per year or CHF 2,500 monthly; Vaud indicates around CHF 2,000 monthly plus housing costs. These cantonal figures are evidence of practice, not a single national statutory threshold, and content should not flatten them into one legally fixed amount. 19

**Weak study** **plan** **and** **educational** fit. SEM requires a personal study plan and exact identification of the degree objective; the application is then checked against the institution's official programme. Zurich's guidance is even more documentary, requiring reasons for choosing Zurich, programme length, target qualification, the applicant's educational history and future plans. A vague "I want to study in Switzerland" narrative is therefore not merely weak advocacy; it fails a published administrative criterion. 20

**Poor** **educational progression.** This is one of the most litigation-tested refusal grounds. SEM states that students must sit intermediate and finnal examinations within a useful timeframe, and that where they do not, the residence purpose is treated as exhausted and renewalI is refused. The same section notes that a change of direction or an additional training course is allowed only in sufficientlyjustified exceptional cases. The directive's own case-law table lists refusal scenarios including repeated failures, interruptions, study changes, second training and having already exceeded the normal eight-year duration. Zurich's public practice note aligns closely with that approach and adds that repeated shifts in study objective may show an attempt to achieve a de facto permanent stay. 21

**Doubtful** **return** **and** **temporary** **purpose.** This is a legally central theme in third-country cases. SEM's 2026 directives say expressly that study residence is a **temporary** stay and refer to Article 5(2) AIG, requiring the person to show a willingness to leave Switzerland once the study purpose has been fulfilled. The official interview questions used by Swiss representations probe precisely this point: future prospects in the home country, expected salary after return, whether Swiss tuition costs can realistically be recouped back home, where the family lives, and whether return is genuinely secured. Zurich's

guidance reflects the same logic and stresses that where forced return to the region of origin would be difficult, the evidential demands are higher. 22

**Unrecognised** **or** **unsuitable school.** VZAE Article 24 requires schools to guarantee a proper education, compliance with the programme, fixed programme duration, and confirmation of educational and language prerequisites. SEM's directives go further for private schools and new institutions, requiring proof of durable financing, stable organisation and, in practice, strong cooperation with cantonal education authorities; institutions in the Swiss private school register benefit from a presumption of compliance. Zurich similarly relies on the private school register and asks detailed questions about courses, examinations, diplomas and teaching staff. Evening schools or schools with only a limited offering are explicitly treated as problematic in SEM guidance. 23

**Insufficient language evidence.** Both the federal and cantonal materials make language competence a substantive suitability issue, not a formal add-on. SEM requires the institution to confirm that the applicant has the necessary language skills to follow the course and states that the applicant must prove the language level needed for the programme. The directives then map indicative levels by type of institution, with universities and technical universities generally at the highest level unless an approved preparatory language course is built into the programme. They also allow an interview-based assessment and, in doubtful cases, language testing. Geneva and Vaud both require the schooI to attest the applicant's linguistic fit, which means a bare self-declaration is weak. 24

**Previous failed applications or immigration history.** VZAE Article 23(2) expressly treats prior stays, prior applications and other circumstances as relevant where they suggest that study is being used to evade general admission rules. SEM repeats that proposition in its current directives, and Zurich's public note mirrors it almost verbatim. On an internal-content basis, prior refusals should therefore never be handled as "background only"; they must be analysed as a live legal factor going to circumvention, credibility and temporary intent. 10

**EU/EFTA** **contrast.** None of the above should be automatically transplanted into EU/EFTA student cases. Under Annex I Article 24 AFMP and SEM's 2026 VFP guidance, the key issues are enrolment at a recognised institution, insurance and sufficient financial means so that no social assistance is needed; the student's financial sufficiency may be shown by declaration or equivalent means, and the annual renewal logic is treaty-specific. SEM also states expressly that the restrictive third-country admission filters in Articles 23-25 VZAE do not apply to this category. 2

## Appeals, reconsideration and reapplication

The first procedural question is **who** **refused** **and** **at what** **stage.** Where a canton refuses the permit in its own competence, the immediate remedy is the remedy route stated in the cantonal decision; Swiss federal law requires upper cantonal courts as immediate predecessors to the Federal Supreme Court in ordinary public-law matters, but Article 83(c) BGG then blocks a Federal Supreme Court public-law appeal where there is no domestic or treaty right to the permit. Where the case is one of SEM approval, SEM can still refuse approval after a favourable cantonal stance, and appeals against SEM decisionns go to the Federal Administrative Court. 25

This matters strategically. In a third-country study case, an appeal is strongest where the refusal is legally wrong on the existing record: for example, a refusal based solely on age despite BGE 147 I 89; mischaracterising a recognised school; failing to apply the correct standard to language evidence already provided; or confusing a Swiss higher-education graduate's post-study options with the general temporary-purpose rule. In contrast, if the refusal is factually well-founded because the file lacked

coherent financing, contained a weak study narrative, or showed pooor progression, an appeal is often a poor vehicle for what is really a rebuilt case. 26

A complication is that SEM's directives state that, in Federal Administrative Court proceedings, the Court generally decides according to the facts and law existing at the time of its own judgment. That helps where circumstances have genuinely evolved during the appeal. But it does **not** convert the appeal into a free opportunity to reconstruct a neglected file, and internal drafting should avoid telling users that "all deficiencies can simply be fixed on appeal". 27

**Reconsideration** is narrower still. SEM's 2026 guidance treats the simple reconsideration request as a non-appeal procedural remedy derived from Article 29 BV. It is not subject to a formal deadline, but the authority need only entertain it where circumstances have changed materially since the original decision, or where the applicant can now adduce important facts or evidence that were previously unknown or previously unavailable without fault. The guidance is explicit that reconsideration cannot be used to cure omitted cooperation or to present facts the party had reason to submit in the original proceedings. If those threshold conditions are absent, the authority can issue an appealable refusal to entertain the request.15

**Revision** is different again. SEM's guidance summarises Article 66 VwVG and states that revision is an extraordinary remedy against a final appellate decision, available where genuinely new and material facts or means of proof are produced that pre-date the decision but could not previously be invoked without fault. The request must be filed within ninety days of discovering the revision ground and in any event within ten years from notification of the appellate decision. Later-arising facts are not revision grounds; they belong, if anywhere, in reconsideration or a new application. 4

**Reapplication** becomes the cleaner route where there is a true change of circumstances or a true change of purpose. VZAE Article 54 requires a new permit where the residence purpose changes. Geneva's official material says that any school change or study-plan change is subject to a new authorisation and must be reported immediately; its FAQ also states that after sufficiently long absence abroad the person must file a new study application on return. Zurich's guidance likewise treats repeated changes of programme as a serious issue and generally allows only one justified switch. In practice, the better route is usually a fresh application where the new file can honestly show:a recognised and suitable institution; a linear, credible study objective; robust language evidence; stable financing; and a materially better answer on temporary purpose and return prospoects than was available before. That last sentence is an analytical inference from the official materials, not a verbatim rule. 28

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss study cases as a single "student visa" route. The legally precise approach is to distinguish the entry document from the residence authorisation, and then distinguish both from any SEM approval stage. Geneva, Vaud and Ticino all publicly reflect that split. 29

Do **not** imply entitlement for third-country nationals. Article 27 AIG study residence isdiscretionary, and SEM's own guidance cites case law confirming that refusal can still occur even where the listed conditions are satisfied. By contrast, EU/EFTA student cases arise under a treaty-based regime and should be analysed as such. 30

Do **not** state or imply that age above thirty is a lawful refusal rule. SEM's 2026 directives say that a practice of refusing study permits solely because the applicant is over thirty violates the constitutional prohibition of discrimination, following BGE 147 I 89. Age may still appear in the broader factual matrix of progression or temporary-purpose analysis, but not as an automatic bar. 31

<!-- 6 -->

Do not present cantonal finance figures as if they were one uniform federal minimum. Zurich, Geneva and Vaud publish different operational benchmarks. The federal legal point is"sufficient means"; the quantum used in practice is canton-sensitive and fact-sensitive. 32

Do not assume that a written undertaking to leave is always required. Geneva and SEM both carve out Swiss higher-education students from the standard written departure undertaking, because Swiss law separately provides a limited post-degree job-search regime for Swiss higher-education graduates; nevertheless, SEM also states that study residence remains temporary and any further stay requires a new authorisation once the study purpose ends. 33

**Do** **not** understate the significance of study-plan changes, school changes or repeated programme shifts. Federal and cantonal sources treat these as potential indicators of circumvention and, in some settings, as a reason why a new authorisation is required. 34
