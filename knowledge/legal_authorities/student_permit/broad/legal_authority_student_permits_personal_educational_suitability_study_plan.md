# Swiss Student Permits and Personal-Educational Suitability

## Executive legal summary

**A. Executive legal summary.** For **third-country nationals,** the core legal framework is **Art. 27 of the** **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration** and **Arts. 23-24 of the Ordinance on Admission,** **Stay** **and** **Employment.** Art. 27 sets cumulative gateway requirements, including that the applicant must satisfy the **personal and** **educational** **prerequisites** for the proposed studies. But the route remains **discretionary**:the Federal Administrative Court repeatedly states that Art. 27 is a **potestative** provision rather than an entitlement, and that the authorities retain a broad margin of appreciation, including under the general public-interest balancing reflected in the FNIA framework and Switzerland's restrictive admission policy. A school admission letter is therefore necessary, but not sufficient on its own. 1

By contrast, **EU/EFTA** **students** are not primarily assessed under the third-country Art. 27 discretion model. Their position is governed by the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons,** especially **Annex** I, Art. 24, which gives a treaty-based residence position to students who are enrolled in an approved establishment, have sufficient financial means, and hold comprehensive sickness insurance. SEM's own materials present this as a **right** of **residence** for non-active EU/EFTA persons, including students, subject to those conditions; cantonal materials reflect the same distinctionby requiring a far lighter evidential package for EU/EFTA students than for third-country students. 2

For third-country cases, the **study plan, motivation letter, CV, diplomas/transcripts, language** **evidence, and future-plans / return evidence** are best understood as **evidential tools** used to prove Art. 27(1)(d), Art. 24(2)-(4) OASA, and the temporary-stay requirement in **Art.** 5(2) FNIA.They are not separate statutory tests. SEM requires a **personal study** **plan** identifying the exact intended qualification and compares it against the official programme of the institution; the school must confirm that the candidate has the educational level and language skills needed to follow the course. Cantonal practice materials in Geneva, Zurich, Lucerne, Vaud and Ticino broadly align with that evidential structure. 3

**The** **practical** **legal centre** **of gravity** in refusal cases is usually one of four things: weak fit between prior education and the new course; repeated or poorly explained changes of direction; a second or duplicative education that looks convenience-driven rather than educationally coherent; or credibility concerns that the study route is being used to secure a longer-term stay. Swiss authorities are expressly directed to prevent the study route from being used to evade the stricter rules on ordinary admission. The more the record shows a **linear, time-limited, professionally explicable programme,** the stronger the case; the more it looks like an improvised or serial course strategy after failures or major reorientation, the weaker it is. 4

## Structural overview of the legal framework

B. **Structural overview** **of the legal framework.** The internal system should treat Swiss student-permit analysis as **two legally distinct tracks.**

For **third-country** **nationals,** the starting points are **Art.** 5 **FNIA** on entry conditions for temporary stays, Art. 27 **FNIA** on study/training admission without employment, and **Arts. 23-24** **OASA** on finances, personal qualifications, programme structure, school requirements, and language / educational readiness. SEM's AIG Directives then operationalise those norms into the documentary and interview practice actually used by representations and cantonal migration offices. In cases subject to SEM approval, the Federal Administrative Court can review the refusal, and its judgments show how discretion is exercised in practice. 5

**For** **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the governing structure is the **AFMP,** not Art. 27 FNIA as a primary source of discretion. Annex I Art. 24 grants a residence position to students if they are enrolled, insured,and financially self-sufficient, with annual extension where the training exceeds one year. SEM's EU/EFTA guidance mirrors that position and states that non-active EU/EFTA nationals have a right to the B permit when the treaty conditions are met. The practical consequence is important for internal drafting: do **not** import third-country concepts such as "motivation letter credibility" or "educational necessity" into EU/ EFTA analyses unless a separate treaty-compliant issue genuinely arises. 6

Within the **third-country** track, the internal system should separate three layers. First, the **black-letter** **conditions**: school confirmation, accommodation, finances, personal / educational prerequisites,and-because the stay is temporary-a credible intention to leave once the purpose is fulfilled.Secondly,the **evidential package**: study plan, motivation letter, CV, diplomas, transcripts, language proof, interview answers, and where applicable a writtendeparture undertaking. Thirdly, the **discretionary** **balancing** **layer**:even where Art. 27 conditions look satisfied, the authorities may still refuse on a careful overall assessment, especially in second-education, repeated-education, or course-change cases, or where the Swiss course appears driven by personal convenience rather than a coherent educational need. 7

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

D. **Primary** **legal texts.** The main statute is **Art.27** **FNIA (SR** **142.20).** It provides that foreign nationals **may** be admitted for education or further training if: the school confirms that the course can be followed; suitable accommodation exists; necessary financial means are available; and the applicant satisfies the **personal and** **educational prerequisites** for the proposed programme. It also requires supervision for minors and states that any further stay after completion or abandonment is governed by the ordinary admission rules. The temporary-stay logic is reinforced by **Art. 5(2) FNIA,** which requires a guarantee of departure where only a temporary stay is intended. 20

The central implementing rules are **Arts. 23-24** **OASA/VZAE** **(SR** **142.201).** Art. 23 provides the non-exhaustive modes of proving sufficient finances, states that personal qualifications are sufficient notably where nothing indicates that the invoked course is being used to evade the general admission and residence rules, and sets the **eight-year principle,** subject to exceptions for targeted training.Art. 24 requires schools to guarantee proper instruction and compliance with the teaching programme; it also requires that the programme and duration be fixed, that the school confirm the applicant's language and educational readiness, and that a language test may be required in justified cases. 21

**For** **EU/EFTA** **students,** the controlling treaty rule is **Annex** I, Art. 24 **AFMP.** A student who has no residence right under another part of the Agreement must be issued a residence permit for the period of the training, or for one year if the training lasts longer, provided the national authority is satisfied that the student has sufficient financial means, is enrolled in an approved establishment for vocational training as his or her principal activity, and has all-risks sickness insurance. The permit is then extended annually for the remaining training period while those conditions continue to be met. 22

**E. Official guidance and administrative materials. SEM's AIG Directives, ch. 5.1.1,** are the most important administrative source for how Art. 27 is actually applied. They state that applicants must provide a **personal study plan** and specify the exact educational goal; the application is then compared with the institution's official programme; and the institution's management must confirm both

<!-- 4 -->

educational level and language ability. They also state that the authorities must prevent study stays from being used to circumvent ordinary admission rules. 23

SEM further makes clear that the route has a **temporary** character. The personal-qualification analysis looks not only at education, but also at prior stays and applications, age, family situation, social environment, origin region, and the home-country labour market. Where forced return would be difficult or impossible, the requirements are said to be higher and there must be concrete indications that voluntary return after the studies is highly probable. 24

The Directives also specify the **documentary** **file**: admission confirmation, the diploma needed for admission, a **motivation letter,** proof of tuition payment (or waiver), proof of finances, a written declaration to leave Switzerland after the course **unless** the course leads to a Swiss tertiary qualification, a CV, language evidence, and housing proof. In suitable cases the authorities may require a formal language test. Importantly, SEM says that a diploma or vocational certificate at the end of studies is **not** **itself** a **legal condition,** but students must take intermediate and final exams within a useful time; otherwise the purpose of stay may be treated as achieved and the permit not renewed. 25

**On** **interview-based** **assessment,** SEM requires visa-subject students to appear personally before the Swiss representation, subject to narrow exceptions where language competence is obvious and migration risk is low. The interview is expressly said to be useful for decision-making because itgathers information about the applicant and his or her future intentions and allows the representation to assess language ability. SEM even gives sample questions, including who the applicant is, prior studies,why Switzerland was chosen, future intentions after return, and whether the investment in Swiss studies can realistically be recouped in the home country. 26

**For EU/EFTA** **non-active** **persons,** including students, SEM's published treaty guidance states that the AFMP gives a **right** **of** **residence** provided there are sufficient resources and full sickness / accident insurance; for persons in education, the guidance adds the need to show credible means and enrolment in an approved Swiss institution, and confirms that the permit is issued forthe training period or for one year if the training lasts longer, with annual extensions while the conditions continue to be met. 27

## Cantonal practice notes

F. **Cantonal practice** **notes.** Cantonal material does not alter the federal test, but it is highly useful for identifying the **evidential expectations** that caseworkers actually apply to student files. The highest-value cantonal materials in this set are Zurich's detailed practice note, Geneva's current web guidance, and Lucerne's current split treatment of EU/EFTA and third-country students. Vaud and Ticino provide corroborative material, but the publicly indexed Vaud and Ticino documents used here are older and should therefore be treated as persuasive administrative evidence rather than current hard-edged policy statements. 28

**Zurich** is the most detailed cantonal source in this set. It requires a **personal and** **detailed** **study** **programme,** with the exact duration and justification of the educational objective, and specifically asks why Zurich was chosen. It also expects a detailed CV covering schooling, studies, work, and copies of translated diplomas and certificates. Zurich requires future-intention evidence and, outside the Swiss-higher-education exception, a written confirmation that the applicant will leave Switzerland on time after the course. It also treats CHF 21,000 for twelve months as sufficient for subsistence and accommodation in its practice. 29

<!-- 5 -->

Zurich's note is also particularly useful on **progression** **and** **course** **changes.** It says successive "stacking" of programmes is generally not permitted; one change of study direction at university or university of applied sciences may be allowed in justified cases; multiple Master's programmes may be possible, but the overall study stay must still stay within the eight-year principle; and repeated changes of educational objective are a strong sign that a person may in truth be seeking permanent residence. Zurich also requires permit renewals to check that students are following the programme and passing assessments within the prescribed timeframe, allowing only limited delay. 30

**Geneva's** current page for third-country students is a strong contemporary source for the expected documentary packet. It requires **a CV and motivation letter indicating intentions at the end of** **studies,** **a** **study** **plan** showing weekly hours, timetables, subjects, detailed programme, total duration and target diploma, a written undertaking to leave Switzerland after the expected studies (except for students admitted to a Swiss higher-education institution within the Higher Education Act definition), proof of finances, and proof of accommodation. Geneva also states that any change of school or study plan requires a **new** **authorisation** and must be notified immediately withsupporting reasons. 31

**Lucerne** is useful because it draws the EU/EFTAversus third-country divide sharply. For EU/EFTA students, it asks essentially for admission, finances, and identity documents. For third-country students, it adds the Art. 27 package: CV, school diplomas and work certificates, motivation letter, future plans, recognised language proof, and a departure undertaking. That is a useful cuurrent cantonal illustration of why the internal system must not conflate the treaty route with Art. 27 practice. 32

**Vaud's** still-published study-permit form, although older, remains helpful on structure. It says that any modification of the study plan during the Swiss stay must be reported immediately with an explanation, including a change of direction, change of school, or prolongation. It also requires an **intention** **letter** explaining the reasons for choosing studies in Switzerland and the applicant's future projects, plus a CV with dates, schools attended andd copies of diplomas, and an undertaking to leave Switzerland at the end of the planned studies. It expressly warns that false declarations or concealment of essential facts may lead to revocation. 33

**Ticino's** published notice, also older but still useful, states that the study permit is valid only for the territory of Ticino and for the stated purpose of stay, that there is no **right** to **renewal** or **to** **obtaining** **another** **permit** **later,** and that the permit may be revoked for false information, silence about material facts, breach of conditions, or abusive conduct. It also requires notification of relevant changes and confirms that ancillary employment for students at higher schools depends on confirmation thnat study duration will not be prejudiced. 34

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

G. **Practical** **legal analysis.** On the authorities, the key doctrinal point is that **prior academic record** **and** **fit** **with** **the** **proposed** **course** are not evaluated in the abstract. The question is whether the file shows that the applicant genuinely has the level, preparation and coherence needed for the new programme, and whether the overall path still looks like a **bona** **fide** **educational trajectory** rather than a residence strategy. That is why the school's confirmation under Art. 24(3) OASA matters, but does not end the matter. The authorities also scrutinise CVs, diplomas, transcripts, prior work history, and the explanation for the chosen course and chosen institution. 35

The strongest current Federal| Administrative Court example on **duplicative** **or weakly** **justified** **progression** is **TAF** **F-725/2024.** The applicant already held an architecture degree from the DRC and wanted to enter the third year of a Swiss Bachelor in architecture, with the aim of proceeding to a

<!-- 6 -->

Master. The Court upheld refusal, emphasising that he already had solid knowledge in the field, had not shown that a new long cycle in the same area was indispensable to practise his profession, and had not shown that he could not deepen his skills in his home country. The Court treated the proposed Swiss studies as a matter of personal convenience rather than a specifically justified educational need, and confirmed that the authorities could refuse even without separately deciding every Art. 27 element because of the broad discretionary boalance. 36

The main Federal Administrative Court authority on **course** **changes** **and inconsistent** **progression** is **TAF** **F-5251/2020.** There the applicant switched from economics to law after already arriving to study in Switzerland. The Court accepted that the file did not necessarily prove **abuse** in the narrow Art. 23(2) sense-that is, it was not shown that the studies aimed **only** to circumvent the usual admission rules-and even indicated that the Art. 27 conditions could be viewed as cumulatively fulfilled. But the Court still upheld refusal after a broader Art. 96 balancing, relying on the applicant's vacillating study plans, the weak overall coherence of the path, the lack of completed first-year progress, and the fact that the Swiss law studies were not shown to be indispensable or unavailable in MMorocco. This is an important internal point**: absence of abuse does not equal success** under Art. **27.** 37

A similar logic appears in **TAF F-5981/2017,** where the applicant changed from economnics to geosciences/environment and then to informatics. The Court stressed that a study permit is normally **for** **one** **course** **of** **study,** that the authorities must preserve the ability to assess whether a person who has failed one programme should be allowed to start another in Switzerland, and that repeated changes, long overall duration, and weak explanations made the Swiss study path look driven more by **personal convenience** than by educational imperatives. This judgment is a useful bridge between the Zurich practice note and federal case law. 38

**The** **motivation** **letter** should therefore be analysed as a legal-evidential document with four distinct functions. First, it explains the substantive reason for the course and the intended qualification. Secondly, it explains **why this institution, why this canton, and why Switzerland.** Thirdly, it links the course to the applicant's prior education and professional trajectory. Fourthly, it addresses **post-study** **plans,** including return and career use of the Swiss qualification where relevant. Geneva states this expressly; Zurich asks the same questions in substance; Lucerne and Vaud require comparable material. A weak letter is usually not fatal because it is "formally defective"; it is fatal because it leaves Art. 27(1)(d) and Art. 5(2) insufficiently proved. 39

The **study** **plan** should likewise be treated as more than a timetable. SEM requires a personal study plan specifying the exact goal, and Geneva expects weekly hours, timetables, programme detail, total duration and target diploma. Zurich expects a detailed programme plus reasons for choosing Zurich and the intended qualification. In internal drafting, the study plan should therefore be read as the core document for assessing wvhether the proposed course is **full-time, sufficiently** **fixedd,educationally** **coherent, and** **time-bounded.** A vague or open-ended plan weakens both Art. 24(2) OASA and the return analysis. 40

**On** **return** **/** **future** **plans,** internal drafting should be careful not to overstate. A written departure undertaking is often required in practice, but SEM and cantonal guidance recognise an exception for studies leading to a **Swiss** **higher-education** **qualification,** because such graduates may later seek the special post-study job-search / labour-market route. Even so, the student stay remains temporary,and SEM expressly says that any further residence after completion requires a **new** **permit.** The file should therefore still present future intentions in a credible way; the higher-education exception removes one document, not the temporary character of the route. 41

<!-- 7 -->

**On** **interviews,** the internal system should treat them as a credibility and suitability instrument, not a mere procedural formality. SEM uses the interview to test personal background, prior study history, reasons for choosing Switzerland, future plans, and language competence. Where a file contains an unusually polished written motivation letter but a poor interview, that discrepancy can matter materially. Equally, SEM allows exceptions to personal appearance where language aboility is obvious and migration risk is low, so the absence of a personal interview is not itself suspicious. 26

### H. Drafting cautions. The following cautions should be built into internal content generation rules.

·Do not present Art. 27 FNIA as conferring an entitlement. For third-country nationals, it is a **discretionary** route even where the documentary conditions appear satisfied. 42

·Do **not** collapse EU/EFTA and third-country analysis into a single "student permit" framework. EU/EFTA students sit primarily under **AFMP** **Annex** **I** Art. 24, which is treaty-based and materially different. 43

·Do **not** describe the motivation letter or study plan as autonomous legal tests. They are evidential instruments used to prove the statutory and treaty conditions. 44

·Do not say that Swiss law requires the applicant to prove that the course is **impossible** **elsewhere** or strictly **necessary** as a formal Art. 27 condition. The Court has accepted that "necessity" is not textual in Art. 27, but it can still weigh heavily in the discretionary balance, especially in second-education or duplicative-education cases. 45

·Do **not** convert the eight-year principle into an inflexible rule. OASA and SEM both allow exceptions for targeted, coherent programmes. 46

**·Do** **not** treat any course change as automatically fatal. Federal and cantonal authorities accept that one justified reorientation may be possible; what becomes damaging is serial reorientation, extended duration, or poor explanation. 15

**·Do** **not** use age as a proxy criterion. SEM now expressly records that a general practice of refusing study permits to persons over 30 would violate the constitutional non-discrimination rule. 18

·Do **not** treat lack of a final diploma as automatically disqualifying either. SEM states that a diploma is not itself a legal condition, but failure to sit or pass exams within a useful time strongly undermines progression and can justify refusal of renewal. 47

·Do **not** equate inconsistency with route abuse too quickly. Art.23(2) OASA and TAF F-5251/2020show that the anti-abuse inquiry is framed relatively narrowly; however, even where abuse is not proven, the permit may still be refused under the broader discretionary balance. 48

·Do **not** ignore credibility risks arising from false or incomplete information. Cantonal materials from Vaud and Ticino expressly link false statements or concealed essential facts to revocation risk. 19
