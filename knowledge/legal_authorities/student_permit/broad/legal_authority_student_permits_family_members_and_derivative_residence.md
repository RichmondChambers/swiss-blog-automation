# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Family Members of Student Permit Holders

**Suggested internal filename:**

legalauthoritystudentpermitsfamilymembersandderivativeresidencepack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate a single, unified "student visa" family route. For this topic, the correct framework depends first on whether the principal student is an **EU/EFTA** **national exercising** **rights** **under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons** or a **non-EU/EFTA national admitted for** **education or training under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and its** **implementing** **ordinance.** Those are materially different legal regimes, with materially different consequences for family members.1

For an **EU/EFTA** **student,** Swiss law recognises a **derivative** **treaty** **position** for family members. The student may be joined by a **spouse** and **dependent children;** the treaty text does **not** extend the student-specific family category to ascendants. The student must show **sufficient** **financial** **means for** self and qualifying family members and **all-risks** **sickness** **insurance;** the family permit is tied to the principal student's status, and the permit issued to a family member has the **same** **period** **of validity** as the principal permit. The spouse and dependent children or children under 21 of a treaty beneficiary may work, and children resident in Switzerland have equal access to general education, apprenticeship and vocational training. 2

For a **non-EU/EFTA** **student,** admission for studies is governed first by **Article** **27** **FNIA** **and** **Article** 23**OASA,** which require, among other matters, school confirmation, suitable accommodation, sufficient means and appropriate personal qualifications. Family members do **not** derive an automatic student-specific right from those provisions. Instead, any accompanying or later-joining family member must usually be analysed through the separate family-reunification provisions for the principal's permit category**: Article 44 FNIA** if the student holds **a residence permit B, and Article 45 FNIA** if the student holds a **short-stay** **permit** L. Both routes are discretionary: the statute says the family members "may **be** **granted"** a permit. Internal drafting should therefore avoid the language of entitlement for third-country student families. 3

Under the domestic third-country framework, the qualifying family members are the **spouse** and **unmarried** **children** **under** **18.** Registered partnershnips are treated analogously to marriage under **Article** **52** **FNIA.** By contrast, **unmarried** **cohabiting** **partners** are not statutory family-reunification beneficiaries under Articles 44 or 45. For EU/EFTA cases, SEM guidance states that "other family members", including a cohabiting partner, may be **facilitated** under the final sentence of Annex I Article 3(2), but they do **not** have a claim to family reunification on that basis. For third-country cases,canton-level partner routes exist, but they are outside family reunification and remain discretionary. 4

The principal operational conditions for third-country family admission are **cohabitation, adequate** **housing, no social-assistance dependency, and no supplementary boenefits** risk for the sponsor; for a B-permit sponsor, the incoming spouse must also satisfy the **A1** **oral language** requirement or show registration for a language course, whereas children are exempt. For an L-permit sponsor, the language requirement does not apply, but the family authorisation is explicitly limnited to a **short-stay** **permit** aligned to the principal's validity. 5

Insurance must be separated carefully by regime. Under the AFMP, sufficient resources and all-risks sickness insurance are built into theresidence analysis. Under the third-country domestic regimne, Articles 44 and 45 do not themselves create an express health-insurance admission condition in the same way, but **any person taking up residence in Switzerland must ordinarily obtain Swiss health** **insurance** **within** **three** **months,** and each family member is insured individually. Foreign students may in some cases seek an exemption if they already have equivalent cover, but that is a separate health-insurance question and should not be collapsed into the migration analysis. 6

## Structural overview of the legal framework

### EU and EFTA students under the free movement regime

The treaty structure matters. Annex I to the AFMP creates a right of residence for persons not pursuing an economic activity who satisfy the treaty conditions, and it then regulates family members separately. In the student context, **Annex** **I Article** **24(4)** deals with the principal student's residence conditions, while **Annex** I **Article** **3** defines which family members may join and what derivative rights they receive. The derivative position lasts only while the principal still has the underlying treaty right. 7

### Non-EU and non-EFTA students under the domestic migration regime

For third-country nationals, the legal entry point is **Article** **27** **FNIA** **and** **Article** **23** **OASA.** Those provisions govern the student's own admission for education or continuing education. Family analysis starts only afterwards, by asking which permit category the student holds and whether family members can be admitted under the family provisions applicable to that category. That is why internal drafting should distinguish clearly between the **study** **authorisation** and the **family-reunification** **authorisation.** 8

### Family analysis by sponsor permit category

If the principal student holds a **B** **permit,** family members fall within **Article** **44** **FNIA.** If the principal holds an **L** **permit,** family members fall within **Article** **45** **FNIA.** The practical significance is considerable: Article 44 brings the spouse-language condition and the domestic work right in Article 46; Article 45 does not create the same automatic employment position and expressly ties family members to a short-stay permission of the same category. 9

### General public-law overlay

Alongside migration law, there is a separate public-law layer on **compulsory** **health** **insurance.** Anyone settling in Switzerland must generally insure within three months, each family member is insured individually, and some foreign students may seek exemption if they hold equivalent cover. This overlay is relevant because cantonal migration files often ask for insurance evidence or premium estimates when assessing self-sufficiency, even though the migration-law basis and the health-insurance basis are analytically distinct. 10

## Primary legal texts

The main primary texts are these. First, for the **third-country** **study** **route, Article** 27 **FNIA** sets the conditions for admission for education or continuing education, and **Article** **23** **OASA** explains how sufficient funds may be evidenced and confirms that study programmes are, in principle, admitted for a maximum of eight years unless an exception is justified by a specific objective. **Article** 38 **OASA** is relevant because it restricts ancillary employment for many third-country students, which matters when assessing whether a proposed household budget is realistic. 23

<!-- 4 -->

Secondly, for the **domestic family route,** the core provisions are **Articles 44 to 47 FNIA,** plus **Articles** **49,** 50 **and** **52** **FNIA.** Article 44 governs spouses and children of a B-permit holder; Article 45 governs spouses and children of an L-permit holder; Article 46 addresses work rights; Article 47 supplies the principal family-reunification time limits; Article 49 allows exceptions to cohabitation where there is good cause and the family household continues; Article 50 governs post-breakdown retention in certain settings; and Article 52 extends the spouse provisions to registered partnerships. 16

Thirdly, the implementing ordinance matters. **OASA** **Article** **26** provides the work-permit mechanism for spouses and children of short-stay permit holders, and **Articles** **75** **to** 77 **OASA** remain relevant to later child reunification, exceptions to cohabitation, and retention after family dissolution. 24

For the **EU/EFTA** **route,** the principal treaty provisions are **Annex** **I Article** **3** **AFMP** on family members and their derived status, and **Annex** **I** **Article** **24** **AFMP** on residence for persons not pursuing an economic activity, including students. Article 3 defines the student-specific family circle, makes family-member permits coterminous with the principal permit, gives qualifying spouses and children the right to work, and guarantees children equal access to education and training. Article 24 governs sufficient means, sickness insurance and annual extension for students. 25

Finally, health-insurance law sits alongside the migration texts. The Federal Office of Public Health states that **anyone settling in Switzerland must obtain health insurance within three months,** and that **each family member, adult or child, is insured individually.The** same office explains that some foreign students may obtain an exemption if they have equivalent cover, but that depends on status and origin and is handled separately from the migration-law decision. 26

**Source-quality** **caution:** the authentic consolidated federal texts are the official German, French and Italian versions in the Swiss compilation. The English mirroring used here for some statutory wording is expressly informational and has no legalforce; internal content should therefore cite **article** **numbers** **and** **SR** **references** as the legal anchor, rather than relying on English wording alone. 27

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

### Federal guidance and administrative materials

SEM's **VFP** **guidance** is the most useful official interpretative source for the AFMP side of the analysis. It states expressly that the EU/EFTA family right is derivative, that a student's qualifying family is limited to **spouse** **and** **dependent** **children,** that economically inactive persons must demonstrate sufficient means for themselves and their families, and that student permits are extended annually. It also clarifies that "other family members", including a cohabiting partnner, may at most benefit from facilitated admission without a claim. 28

SEM's AFMP **factsheets** are alsoo operationally useful. The factsheet on non-active persons states that EU/EFTA non-active persons, including students, have a residence right only if they have sufficient resources and sickness insurance for themselves and their family members, with sufficiency measured against Swviss social-assistance levels. The AFMP family-reunification factsheet confirms the student-specific limit to spouse and dependent children, the need for suitable housing, same-duration derivative permits, work rights for qualifying family members, and equal educational access for children. 29

For the domestic third-country side, SEM's **AIG** **guidance** is the key administrative source. It states plainly that admission of family members of B-permit holders under Article 44 is discretionary,

elaborates the meaning of adequate housing and self-sufficiency, explains the spouse-language condition, and confirms that family members of L-permit holders receive a short-stay permit with the same validity period. It also notes that future income is normally not counted, though exceptionally it may be if the employment is secure, real and practically exercisable. 30

## Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal materials are not substitutes for the federal legal basis, but they are valuable for **evidence** **expectations** **and** **practical** **thresholds.** Zurich's family-reunification page for B/C permit holders gives a concrete room-count heuristic for adequate housing, requires landlord consent, requests twelve months of payslips orother proof of household support, and asks for Swiss health-insurance premium estimates and debt-register extracts. This is a good example of how cantons operationalise the Article 44 budget and housing assessment. 31

Geneva's family pages are especially useful because they distinguish permit categories clearly. For non-EU/EFTA sponsors, Geneva states that **B/C family members have the right to work** but **L-permit** **family members who want to work must obtain work authorisation;** it also states that children have access to general education, apprenticeship and vocational training on the same conditions as Swiss children, and that adequate housing and non-reliance on social assistance are required. Geneva separately maintains a **cohabiting-partner** route, which expressly says there is no **right** to that permit. 32

Geneva's student-permit FAQ is also a useful cantonal practice note for **student** **evidence** **standards.** It indicates that the canton commonly expects proof equivalent to about **CHF** **20,000** **per** **study** **year** or **CHF** **2,500** **per** **month** for the principal student, accepts a Swiss guarantor or certain first-degree relatives abroad, and requires housing information in operational form. Those figures are **cantonal** **practice,** not a federal statutory threshold, and should be labelled as such in internal drafting. 33

Basel-Stadt's page is a further example of canton-level operational language: it summarises family reunification around relationship, cohabitation, adequate housing judged by standards applicable to Swiss citizens in the area, self-support without state aid, and language requirements for incoming adults. That is useful as a practice cross-check, but still remains subordinate to the federal legal framework. 34

## Practical legal analysis

### Whether family members may accompany or later join

The correct answer is **yes for some categories, but on very different legal bases.** For an EU/EFTA student satisfying the AFMP conditions, accompanying or later-joining family members are part of a **derivative** **treaty** **structure.** For a third-country student, accompanying or later-joining family members are not admitted by the study authorisation itself; they must be assessed separately under Articles 44 or 45 FNIA, and the result remains **discretionary** rather than entitlement-based. 35

### Spouses, registered partners and unmarried partners

For third-country students, the safe domestic formulation is **"spouse** **or** **registered** **partner",** because Article 52 FNIA applies the spouse provisions to registered partnerships. Internal drafting should not casually expand that statutory category to unmarried partners. Where the relationship is non-marital, the legally accurate description is that any route lies **outside** **ordinary** **family** **reunification** and is

<!-- 6 -->

discretionary. Geneva's partner page is a good practice illustration: it allows a cohabiting-partner application, grants work permission if approved, but says expressly that there is no **right** to such a permit. 36

For EU/EFTA students, the treaty text uses **"spouse".** SEM's VFP guidance adds that "other family members", including a cohabiting partner, may be considered under the facilitation clause,but they cannot derive a **claim** to family reunification from that clause. That is a critical drafting distinction: "facilitated" is not the same as "entitled". 12

### Children, age limits and schooling

The age structure differs sharply by regime. Under the AFMP generally, descendants under 21 or dependent descendants may qualify, but for a **student sponsor** the treaty narrows the family circle to **dependent** **children.** Under domestic third-country family reunification, the statutory class is **unmarried** **children** **under** **18** only. Internal content should therefore avoid unqualified statements such as "children can always accompany a student"; it depoends on both **regime** and **age/dependency** **status.** 37

On schooling, the treaty regime is straightforward: the children of a residence-right holder, whether active or inactive, are to be admitted to general education, apprenticeship and vocational training on the same basis as nationals. For third-country family members, the rule is operationally more cantonal, but Geneva expressly says that children of L/B/C family-reunification beneficiaries have access to schooling and vocational pathways on the same conditions as Swiss children, and Zurich states that newly arrived compulsory-school-age children are admitted immediately regardless of residence status. 38

Where only one parent in Switzerland seeks to bring in a child, the family unit does not have to remain the old-style "full family" assumed in earlier case law. The Federal Supreme Court's **BGE** **136** II 78 altered that position, and SEM applies that jurisprudence to Article 44 as well. But this does **not** remove the need for scrutiny: parental authority, the foreign parent's consent where required, child welfare and possible abuse concerns still matter. 39

### Financial self-sufficiency, housing and insurance

On finances, the AFMP applies a treaty sufficiency test: the student must satisfy the authorities that neither the student, nor the spouse, nor dependent children will need social assistance, and that all-risks sickness insurance exists. SEM aligns the financial threshold for economically inactive persons with Swiss social-assistance levels, using the SKOS approach administratively. 40

Under the domestic third-country route, the budget analysis is stricter in a different way. Article 44requires that the family **not depend** **on** **social assistance,** and SEM guidance says the available means should at least meet SKOS levels; it also warns that future earnings are **normally** not counted. Future income can be considered only exceptionally where a real job is secure and the family situation makes the work practically exercisable. This is particularly important in student-family files, because a planned post-arrival job for the spouse or a hoped-for side job by the principal student often cannot safely carry the self-sufficiency case unless the evidence isunusually strong. 41

Housing is a recurring gatekeeper. Under the AFMP, the family must have housing of a normal local standard. Under Article 44 and Article 45, the housing must be adequate for the whole family. Cantonal evidence practice can be quite exacting: Zurich uses a room-count rule of thumb and requires landlord confirmation; Basel-Stadt frames adequacy by local Swiss standards. These canton-specific evidential

<!-- 7 -->

rules are useful internally, but they should be presented as **practice** **indicators,** not statutory norms. 42

Insurance should be analysed separately from permit conditions. Under the treaty student route, all-risks sickness insurance is part of the residence test. Under the domestic route, residence in Switzerland usually triggers mandatory health insurance within three months for each family member individually, although foreign students may sometimes be exempted if they already hold equivalent cover. A student exemption should not be assumed to resolve the insurance position of accompanying family members automatically. 43

### Duration alignment and work rights

Duration alignment is strongest and most explicit in two settings. Under the AFMP, a family member's permit has the **same validity** as the principal's permit, while the principal student's permit is normally granted for **one** **year** **at** a **time** and extended annually for the remaining study period. Under Article 45FNIA, the family member receives an **L** **permit** with **the same validity** **period** as the principal L permit. 44

For Article 44 B-permit cases, the Act is less explicit on permit length but the residence remains derivative in practice: continuation depends on the sponsor's continued B-permit status and on the Article 414 conditions continuing to be met. Internal content should therefore say that the family member's status is **linked** **to** the principal student's continuing authorisation, rather than claiming an autonomous time period. 45

Work rights also differ sharply. Under the AFMP, the spouse and dependent children or children under 21 of a residence-right holder may work irrespective of nationality. Under domestic law, **Article** **46** **FNIA** gives family members admitted under Articles 42 to 44 the right to work anywhere in Switzerland on an employed or self-employed basis. But that automatic right does **not** apply to Article 45 short-stay family members; OASA Article 26 instead requires a work-authorisation process, and Geneva's practice page reflects exactly that distinction. 46

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe this area as a single Swiss "student visa" route. The legally precise drafting split is: **EU/** **EFTA student under the AFMP** versus **third-country student under FNIA/OASA,** with family analysed under a separate derivative route in each case. 11

Do not imply entitlement for a third-country student's spouse or children. Articles 44 and 45 say family members **may** be granted a permit. That is deliberate discretionary language and should be preserved in internal content. 47

Do **not** overstate the position of unmarried partners. Under the domestic regime they are outside ordinary family reunification; under the AFMP they may at most fall into a facilitated "other family members" category without a claim. 17

Do **not** write as though parents or other ascendants may accompany a student. Under the AFMP student rule they are excluded; under domestic student-family analysis there is no ordinary Article 44/45 route for ascendants. 37

<!-- 8 -->

Do **not** assume that children's school enrolment helps create an immigration right. OASA expressly says that steps such as children's schooling, renting a flat or taking similar preparatory measures do not, by themselves, create aright in the authorisation procedure. 22

**Do** **not** build a household self-sufficiency case on speculative future earnings. SEM guidance says future income is generally not counted and only exceptionally considered where the job is secure, real and practically workable. This matters especially in student households because third-country student employment is itself limited by Article 38 OASA. 41

Do not collapse migration-law insurance requirements and health-insurance-law obligations into one statement. For AFMP students, insurance is part of the treaty residence test. For domestic student families, compulsory insurance usually arises from residence as a separate legal obligation, subject to limited exemptions in some student situations. 48

**Do** **not** present cantonal financial figures or room-count formulas as federal law. Zurich's room rule and Geneva's CHF 20,000 / CHF 2,500 practice are useful internal heuristics, but they remain canton-specific operational practice rather than statutory thresholds. 49
