# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Higher-Education Student Permits in Switzerland

## Executive legal summary

This pack concerns residence authorisation for higher-education study in Switzerland, not a single undifferentiated "student visa" route. Swiss law draws a basic ddistinction between, on the one hand, EU/ EFTA nationals who can rely on the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the parallel EFTA regime and, on the other hand, third-country nationals who fall under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and its implementing ordinance. The practical route also separates the **entry** **visa** question from the **residence** **permit** question, and separates the **study** **basis** **of stay** from any **gainful-employment** **basis** **of stay.** That distinction is fundamental for system drafting.

For third-country nationals, higher-education study is governed principally by article 27 AIG and articles 23 to 24 VZAE. The statutory conditions are: confirmation from the educational institution, appropriate accommodation, necessary financial means, and the educational/personal qualifications required for the proposed course. The statutory wording is discretionary: a foreign national **may** be admitted for education or continuing education if the conditions are met. Federal guidance is explicit that article 27does not confer a right to issuance, that the stay remains temporary, and that the regime must not be used to circumvent the stricter rules for labour-market admission. 2

For EU/EFTA nationals, the legal position is materially different. Under Annex I article 24 AFMP, persons not pursuing an economic activity, including students, have a treaty-based residence right if they have sufficient resources, all-risks sickness insurance, and are enrolled in an approved institution for their principal course of vocational training. Federal SEM guidance further states that, for persons in education, the restrictive AIG/VZAE student-admission rules do not apply, and no quotas apply to non-economic residence under the AFMP framework. The AFMP does **not** govern access to Swiss higher-education institutions or the award of scholarships; it governs residence. 3

The recognised higher-education categories for this pack are those in article 2 HFKG: **universities** (including cantonal universities and the ETH institutions), **universities** **of applied** **sciences,** and **teacher-training** **universities.** That classification matters both for identifying recognised institutions and for internal drafting around post-study and work rules. Federal migration guidance expressly links the higher-education concept back to HFKG for student work, and cantonal practice routinely refers to recognised/accredited Swiss higher-education institutions. 4

Doctoral students require particular care. A doctorate can plainly sit within the education route:federal guidance expects the personal study plan to identify the precise target, expressly mentioning the doctorate, and recognises that the normal eight-year cap may exceptionally be exceeded where the training follows a logical structure extending, for example, to a doctorate. But where the doctoral/ postdoctoral position is in substance a research employment with a work contract, the case must be analysed under the work rules rather than simply under the student rules. SEM guidance for EU/EFTA nationals states this expressly for doctoral/postdoctoral researchers with employment contracts;the same functional distinction is the safest approach for third-country cases, read together with the

definition of gainful activity, the article 40 VZAE rule on work during university-level continuing education, and article 54 VZAE on change of purpose requiring a new permit. 5

As a matter of structure, there is no separate federal permit class labelled "exchange student" or "visiting student". The safer legal analysis is functional. If the person is coming to follow, as their principal activity, an approved course or exchange period at a recognised Swiss higher-education institution, the study/non-gainful regime applies according to nationality and duration. If the person is instead coming mainly for a mandatory internship, salaried research, or another form of gainful activity, the analysis shifts to the internship/employment rules. This is an inference from the federal texts and is consistent with cantonal practice pages wwhich route exchange-type study under the student-permit process and route internships under separate work authorisation channels. 6

## Structural overview of the legal framework

**B. Structural overview of the legal framework.** The legal framework has four layers. First,the nationality gateway: article 2 AIG makes clear that EU/EFTA nationals fall primarily under treaty law where that law provides differently, and the AIG applies only residually or where it is more favourable. Secondly, the higher-education classification layer: article 2 HFKG defines the relevant higher-education institutions. Thirdly, the residence basis layer: AFMP non-economic residence for EU/EFTA students, versus article 27 AIG and articles 23 to 24 VZAE for third-country educational stays. Fourthly, the work overlay: ancillary student work, mandatory internships, doctoral/research employment, and post-study transition each have their own rules and should not be collapsed into the admission-for-study analysis. 7

**D. Primary legal texts.** The core federal texts are**:** the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and** **Integration** (especially articles 2, 5, 17, 21 paragraph 3, 27 and 34 paragraph 5); the **Ordinance** **on** **Admission, Stay** **and** **Gainful Employment** (especially articles 1a, 23, 24, 38, 39,40,54,56 paragraph 3and 58); the **Higher** **Education** **Act** / **HFKG** (especially article 2 on higher-education categories); and, for EU/EFTA positions, the **AFMP,** especially Annex I article 24 on residence for persons not pursuing an economic activity, including students. For health insurance, the immigration texts need to be read alongside the general Swiss compulsory health insurance framework as explained by the Federal Office of Public Health.8

E. **Official guidance** **/** **administrative** **materials.** The most important administrative materials are SEM's **Weisungen** **AIG** as at 1 January 2026, SEM's **Weisungen** **VFP** as at January 2026, the SEM factsheets on **residing in Switzerland without gainful activity andd free movement of persons with** **the** **European** **Union,** and official cantonal materials where those illustrate concrete dossier requirements or practice points. For insurance, BAG's official page on **foreign** **students** in **Switzerland** is the key federal administrative source on insurance obligation and possible exemption. 9

The case-law points that matter most in practice are already built into SEM guidance. They include: the unlawfulness of a blanket refusal practice based on an age threshold over 30 for student permits; the proposition that a student permit is temporary from the outset and does not of itself create article 8ECHR private-life protection; and the proposition that article 27 AIG does not itself create a right to a permit even where its conditions are satisfied. SEM also records case examples in which serial studies, second educations, wweak progression, or insecure departure justified refusal or non-renewal. 10

## Cantonal practice notes

Geneva's official OCPM materials are particularly useful for dossier design. The canton explicitly separates EU/EFTA student permits, third-country student permits, ancillary work during studies, mandatory internships, and post-studly job-search. For third-country cases, Geneva requires an admissibility attestation from the school, a CV, a motivation letter explaining intentions after study, a detailed study plan showing weekly hours, timetable, branches, programme detail, total duration and target diploma(s), proof of sufficient means, and a lease or host attestation. Geneva also states that study permits are issued for the duration of the course if it is under one year, and otherwise for one year renewable until the end of studies; they remain strictly temporary, with a normal maximum study period of eight years, and are tied to the school(s) shown in the study plan. 26

Geneva's work pages also show the operational split between **study** **and** **work.** For ancillary work, Geneva states that EU/EFTA students may work up to 15 hours per week during the academic year and 40 hours during holidays. For third-country students, Geneva adds the federal-style constraints: six months must have elapsed since the start of studies, the student must be enrolled at a Swiss higher-education institution, an employer application is required, and the student must wait for authorisation before beginning work. For mandatory internships, Geneva requires that the internship form an integral part of the studies, that it remain below haIf the total training duration, and that authorisation be obtained before start, whether or not the internship is paid. 27

Geneva's post-study page is a useful practice marker for third-country graduates. It states that a six-month non-renewable L permit may be sought by students from outside the EU/EFTA who have completed studies in a recognised Swiss higher-education institution or university of applied sciences, that the permit starts when studies are completed, that the file must include proof of means for six months and proof of accommodation, and that postdoctoral researchers arenot eligible for this specific cantonally described route. That last point is important as a practice signal: postdoctoral status is often treated as research employment rather than as a simple continuation of student status. 28

Vaud's English student application form is useful because it operationalises federal requirements in a structured way. It says the student must notify any change of study orientation, school or extension of stay; it treats financial means as sufficient, for a student not resident in school housing,at roughly CHF 2,000 per month plus accommodation; it repeats the six-month waiting period for ancillary work, the 15-hour weekly cap during term time and full-time work during holidays; and it requires every course envisaged in Switzerland to be set out in the study plan, including school, course, expected diploma and duration. It also repeats the rule that mandatory internships are authorised only where the student attendds a full-time occupational training institution and the total internship duration does not exceed half of the training. 29

Zurich's official labour-market page is narrower, but still relevant. It confirms that students enrolled at Swiss universities or universities of applied sciences may obtain permission for mandatory internships or diploma work with Swiss companies if a training programme is submitted, the activity does not exceed half of the total training duration, and salary/work conditions are met. Zurich also explains the post-study employment route for third-country Swiss graduates in labour-market terms: the graduate route does not automatically authorise work, the employment must be of high scientific or economic interest, and the employer must file the application, although the domestic-priority test is waived. 30

These cantonal sources should be treated as **practice** **illustrations,** not as exhaustive nationwide law. They are nevertheless highly useful for internal drafting because they show how federal requiremnents are evidenced in real files, especially around study plans, accommodation letters, proof of means, ancillary work and post-study transitions. 31

## Practical legal analysis

The correct starting point for **higher-education** **institution** **categories** is HFKG article 2. For immigration purposes, that means the system should not speak only of "universities" in the narrow sense. It should recognise three higher-education types: university institutions, universities of applied sciences, and teacher-training universities. Federal student-work guidance also folds the HFKG concept into migration practice. The safer drafting approach is therefore to use "Swiss higher-education institution" as the ummbrella term, and then map the institution to its HFKG category in the background logic. 32

**For** **Bachelor's** **and** **Master's** **admission,** the legal analysis differs by nationality. Under the AFMP, the immigration authority is concerned with residence conditions, not with giving the student access to the university. The student must be enrolled in an approved institution for their principal vocational training, but the AFMP does not itself regulate entry standards to the institution or scholarship eligibility. For third-country students, by contrast, the school's role is central to the immigration decision: the institution must confirm that the person can follow the intended programme, and the SEM guidance expects a personal study plan identifying the degree objective precisely, such as Bachelor's, Master's or doctorate, so that it can be checked against the official programme.This is one reason why vague statements such as "further study in Switzerland" are poor drafting. 33

**For** **doctoral study** **and** **research** **status,** the key issue is the true legal character of the stay.A doctorate can plainly be a study target under article 27 AIG, and federal guidance recognises a doctorate as part of a logical educational sequence that may justify an exception to the ordinary eight-year ceiling. But Swiss law distinguishes study from gainful activity. VZAE article 40 allows work in a university-level continuing-education context in the person's scientific specialism, provided there is an employer application, the wage and working conditions are compliant, and the work does not obstruct the continuing education. Once the position is in substance ordinary research employment, however, the safer analysis is a work route rather than a student route; article 54 VZAE requires a new permit when the legal purpose of stay changes. SEM's EU/EFTA guidance makes the functional distinction explicit by treating doctoral and postdoctoral holders of emnployment contracts as workers within the treaty regime. 34

**For** **exchange** **and** **visiting** **students,** the best conservative reading is that federal law creates no standalone exchange-student permit class. The key questions are instead: what is the principal activity, for how long, in what institution, and with or without work? If the principal activity is an approved course at a recognised Swiss higher-education institution, the case belongs in the study/non-gainful branch of the framework. If the principal activity is a mandatory internship, the internship rules apply. If the person is mainly working as a researcher, assistant or employee, the work branch applies. This inference is consistent with the structure of article 27 AIG, articles 23 to 24 and 38 to 40 VZAE,and the canton pages which split ordinary student permits from ancillary work and mandatory internship applications. 6

**On school confirmation and programme structure,** SEM guidance is exacting for third-country cases. It expects a personal study plan, a precisely identified target qualification, school confirmation on academic background and language ability, and comparison against the official programme. Geneva goes further in a way that is useful for internal drafting: it asks for the number of weekly teaching hours, timetable, detailed programme, total duration and target diploma(s). Vaud similarly asks the

<!-- 6 -->

student to list all envisaged courses in Switzerland, expected diplomas and duration. The internal system should therefore require a structured "programme evidence" block rather than a single upload labelled "acceptance letter". 35

**On financial means, accommodation and insurance,** third-country and EU/EFTA cases again diverge sharply. For third-country students, VZAE article 23 gives an essentially documentary model: sponsor undertaking with proof of means, Swiss-bank confirmation of sufficient assets, a binding scholarship/ loan commitment, or in special cases other financial guarantees. Appropriate accommodation is an express statutory condition under article 27 AIG and is operationalised cantonally through lease copies or host attestations. For EU/EFTA students, Annex I article 24 AFMP uses a lighter threshold: the student may satisfy the authority, by a statement or equivalent means, that sufficient funds exist, and must have all-risks sickness insurance cover. SEM's VFP guidance expressly says students only have to make adequate means credible, whereas other economically inactive persons must prove means more strictly. Separately from the immigration route, BAG states that persons who settle in Switzerland generally must take out Swiss compulsory health insurance within three months, although foreign students may seek exemption where they have equivalent cover, with different exemption rules for EU/ EFTA and non-EU/EFTA students. 36

**On** **student** **work,** the strongest federal rules are for third-country students. VZAE article 38 allows ancillary work for students at a university or university of applied sciences only after six months from start of studies, only with school confirmation that the work is compatible with the studies, only where term-time working hours do not exceed 15 per week, only on an employer application, and only if local wage/work conditions are met. SEM guidance links the relevant "higher-education institution" concept back to HFKG, which supports inclusion of teacher-training universities. Mandatory internships are separately governed by article 39 VZAE and must remain under half the total training duration. Under the AFMP, EU/EFTA students can hold student residence while carrying on limited ancillary work, but if the economic activity becomes real employment satisfying the treaty worker concept, the case should be analysed as worker residence instead. Cantonal practice often uses the same 15-hour in-term benchmark for ancillary student work. 37

**On** **post-study** **transition,** the third-country route is legally distinct from the later work-permit route. AIG article 21 paragraph 3 relaxes the ordinary domestic-priority rule for persons with a Swiss higher-education diploma if the employment is of high scientific or economic interest. SEM guidance says that, after graduation, such persons are therefore admitted temporarily for six months to find qualified employment; sufficient means and appropriate accommodation are required; the six-month short-stay authorisation starts when completion is established and is not renewable; and work of up to 15 hours per week may be authorised during the job-search period. Zurich and Geneva practice fit this federal structure. EU/EFTA graduates do not need that six-month third-country route; instead, they rely on the treaty jobseeker framework, under which no permit is needed for up to three months, and a short-stay permit may then be issued and extended up to one year if the person continues genuinely to seek work and has reasonable prospects, while remaining self-supporting. 38

Finally, on the **temporary** **nature** **of** **study** **residence,** the system should be strict. SEM guidance states that the study stay remains temporary notwithstanding the possibility of a post-graduation search period and later labour-market facilitation. Once the educational purpose ends, further stay requires a new permit, usually from a fresh legal basis. Case-law points carried into SEM guidance reinforce that temporary student residence does not itself found article 8 ECHR private-life protection, and that student stay does not ordinarily count towards settlement until followed by two uninterrupted years of durable residence after completion. 39

## Drafting cautions

Do **not** describe the Swiss position as a single "student visa" framework. For third-country applicants there may be a national entry visa, but the residence permit itself is separately analysed under article 27AIG and VZAE, and work authorisation is yet another layer. For EU/EFTA students, the source of residence rights is treaty law, not article 27 AIG. 40

**Do** **not** imply entitlement for third-country students. The statutory language is discretionary, and SEM's own guidance cites case law confirming that article 27 AIG does not itself create a right to a permit. If the system needs a short label, use "discretionary residence permit for education/continuing education", not "right to a student permit". 13

Do **not** assume that every doctoral or postdoctoral person is a "student". The legally relevant question is whether the stay is primarily educational or primarily gainful/employment-based.Employment contracts, real research work, and institutional employment status can shift the analysis into the work category. Geneva's exclusion of postdoctoral researchers from its third-country post-study student job-search page is a practical warning sign. 41

Do **not** treat exchange or visiting status as a legal category that answers the immigration question by itself. The system should instead ask: recognised host institution; principal study activity; duration; mandatory internship or not; paid research/employment or not; and nationality regime. 42

Do not present age over 30 as a lawful standalone refusal ground. SEM guidance records that a blanket practice refusing student permits over 30 is incompatible with the constitutional prohibition of discrimination. Equally, do not overstate article 8 ECHR arguments for student-permit holders, because SEM guidancerecords the opposite. 43

Do **not** say that studies "lead automatically" to settlement or work rights. Study residence is temporary; later work requires its own legal basis; and settlement counting is limited by article 34 paragraph 5 AIG. The one major qualified entitlement area is the six-month post-study search permit for qualifying third-country Swiss higher-education graduates who meet the statutory conditions, but even that short-stay route is separate from the later work-authorisation decision. 44

**Do** **not** omit programme-change controls. Federal and cantonal materials require immediate notification and fresh permission where the school, programme or legal purpose of stay changes. 23

Do **not** forget two operational cautions from SEM guidance: first, visa-required applicants are ordinarily expected to appear personally at the Swiss representation and may be interviewed on motivation, post-study plans and language ability; secondly, certain listed nnationalities remain subject to SEM approval in student cases. These are workflow risks rather than substantive rights points, but theymatter in a content system intended to produce precise internal guidance. 45

### Open questions / limitations

Public cantonal materials are uneven and not fully harmonised in presentation. This pack therefore uses Geneva, Vaud and Zurich as official practice illustrations rather than claiming a single nationwide cantonal approach on dossier detail,proof thresholds or ancillary-work handling. One point that should be checked cantonally if outcome-determinative is the treatment of **teacher-training** **university** diplomas under the post-study employment facilitation in article 21 paragraph 3 AIG: the HFKG classification strongly supports inclusion, but some migration guidance and cantonal pages speak more generally of "Swiss higher-education institutions" or specifically of universities / universities of applied sciences, so the safest internal drafting is to state the point carefully rather than absolutely. 55
