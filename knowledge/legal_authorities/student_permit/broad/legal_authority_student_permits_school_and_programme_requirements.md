## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

**For third-country nationals,** the legal starting point is **Article 27 FNIA,** under which a person **may be** admitted for education or training if the school management confirms eligibility, suitable accommodation is available, sufficient financial means are available, and the applicant meets the personal and educational requirements for the planned course. The wording is discretionary, not mandatory. SEM's 2026 Instructions expressly state that Article 27 FNIA does **not** confer an entitlement and that, even where the statutory conditions are met, an application may still be refused in light of Switzerland's restrictive admissions policy.1

The key **institutional requirement** for third-country cases sits in **Article** **24 OASA.** The current consolidated wording available through a law-text mirror sourced from admin.ch states, in substance, that schools educating foreign nationals must guarantee **proper** **education/training** and compliance with the **teaching** **programme.** SEM's 2026 Instructions then operationalise that standard through administrative practice: the school must be able to provide a credible, structured, and quality-assured programme; the authorities may scrutinise new or unknown private schools; and schools entered in the **Swiss** **Private School Register** benefit from a rebuttable presumption that Article 24(1) OASA is met. 2

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the position is materially different. Their stay for study is governed primarily by **the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons** and its domestic implementation, not by the discretionary third-country framework. SEM's 2026 OLCP Directives explain that persons in training need only make their sufficient means plausible, must be enrolled at an **approved** **educational institution** **in** **Switzerland,** and must pursue the course **as their principal activity** under Article 24(4) Annex I AFMP. SEM's factsheet for economicallyinactive EU/EFTA persons says the student must be admitted to **a recognised education and training institution** and be able to attend a general education or VET programme. These permits are issued and renewed **year** **by** **year** during the course of study. The same OLCP Directives also state that the restrictions in **Articles 23-25** **OASA** **do** **not** apply to non-economically active persons under the AFMP. 3

There is **no** **universal federal** **minimum-hours** **rule** identifiable in the primary materials reviewed for all student-permit cases. The federal test is instead framed in terms of a **genuine, principal/full-time** **course** and a school capable of delivering it properly. That said, cantonal practice uses hours-based evidence where relevant. Zurich's public guidance states that, for stand-alone German language-study admissions, **it** recognises only **full-day schools offering intensive German courses with at least 20teaching hours per week.** Geneva requires the study plan to state the **number of weekly teaching**

**hours,** the timetable, subjects, programme detail, total duration, and intended diploma(s). Fribourg likewise requires the programme, duration, intended diploma, CV, and copies of diplomas. 4

For internal drafting, the safest formulation is therefore: **school quality, institutional status, and** **programme structure are nnot merely background facts; they are part of the legal assessment of** **whether the stay is genuinely and properly for study.** In third-country cases especially, **the** school's credibility, recognition, registration status, timetable, diploma pathway, and the applicant's fit for the course all go directly to the permissibility of the permit. 5

## Framework and table of legal propositions

### B. Structural overview of the legal framework

The framework divides into two tracks.

**For third-country nationals,** the structure is**: FNIA Article27→OASA Articles 23-24→SEM** **Instructions** **2026** → relevant cantonal practice. Under this track, the school and programme are tested for substanntive credibility. The school must be able to deliver proper training; the school management must confirm the candidate's suitability; and the applicant must present a concrete and convincing programme. This is a discretionary admission route. 6

**For EU/EFTA nationals,** the structure is**: AFMP Annex I Article 24 → OLCP Articles 16-20 → SEMM OLCP** **Directives** **2026** / **SEM** **factsheets** → cantonal registration practice. This track is treaty-based and therefore materially stronger. The student must show insurance, sufficient means, and enrolment at an approved institution for a principal course of study. OASA Articles 23-25 do not govern this treaty category.7

Recognised Swiss higher-education institutions sit on a separate but related axis. **swissuniversities** publishes the official overview of **HFKG-accredited** Swiss higher-education institutions. SEM's Instructions use the HFKG categories when defining "Swiss higher education institution" for post-study job-search and ancillary work, expressly including universities, the ETH domain, universities of applied sciences, and pedagogical universities. That does not mean that only HFKG institutions can support a student permit; rather, **it** means that **recognised higher-education institutions occupy the highest-confidence** **end** **of** **the credibility** **spectrum,** whereas private and newer institutions require closer immigration-law scrutiny under Article 24 OASA. 8

#### Primary legal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts

The core federal provision is **Article** **27** **FNIA.** Its structure matters. It requires school confirmation of eligibility, suitable accommodation, sufficient financial means, and fulfilment of personal and educational requirements; for minors, supervision must also be guaranteed. Because the provision says foreign nationals **may** be admitted, it creates no automatic right for third-country applicants. 1

The institutional rule is **Article** **24 OASA,** which requires schools training foreign nationals to guarantee **a proper course of education or training** and compliance with the **teaching programme.Although** direct article-level capture from Fedlex was technically inconsistent in the browser, the current article wording was identifiable via a law-text mirror that states it draws itsdata from admin.ch, and it matches SEM's 2026 administrative guidance. 19

**For EU/EFTA students,** the primary rule is **Article** **24** **Annex** **I AFMP,** as explained in the SEM OLCP Directives. A person in training must plausibly show sufficient means, must be enrolled at an **approved** **educational institution in Switzerland,** and must be pursuing the course **as the principal activity.** Student permits under the treaty framework are renewed annually until the end of the course so long as the conditions continue to be met. 20

Recognised Swiss higher-education institutions are anchored in the **HFKG** framework. swissuniversities publishes the official overview of accredited Swiss higher-education institutions entitled to use the designation "higher education institution". SEM's Instructions, when discussing ancillary work and post-study job-search, rely on those HFKG categories and expressly include universities, universities of applied sciences, and pedagogical universities. 8

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's **2026** **Instructions** are the most important operational source for third-country cases. They require the school director to confirm that the applicant has the educational level and language skills needed to follow the course. They also distinguish different language-skill expectations by type of institution, including universities and technical universities, and emphasise that passing a language test alone is not enough; the school must confirm that the applicant can actually follow the course. 21

The same SEM Instructions are the main source on **private schools** **and** **new** **institutions.** If a new or unknown school recruits abroad, Swiss representations are to inform the competent cantonal migration authorities and SEM. From the outset, the school must prove secure long-term financing and stable organisational structures capable of delivering a high-quality service. SEM lists a **business** **plan** and a **statement or recommendation from the competent cantonal education department** among the measures to be required for admission of new private schools. 15

<!-- 4 -->

The **SEMM Circular of 16 May 2024** on schools entered in the **Swiss Private School Register** is critical. **It** recommends that cantonal and city migration authorities treat register entry as a **rule-based** **presumption** that Article 24(1) OASA is satisfied, so that an in-depth review of the school and course is ordinarily unnecessary unless there are concrete and obvious indications that the school no longer meets the register criteria. The circular also states that this presumption does **not** displace scrutiny of the applicant's personal requirements under FNIA Article 27 and OASA Article 23. 22

The same circular is equally important for what it **does** **not** do. It states that register entry is **not** **required** for institutions in the cantonally or federally recognised higher vocational sector, especially **Höhere** **Fachschulen** **(HF).** It also says that, for hotel schools with federally recognised educational programmes, the cantonal/federal recognition of those programmes is regarded as sufficient satisfaction of the requirements under Article 24 OASA. 22

SEM's official page on stays **without** **gainful activity** confirms that the 2024 circular is an annex to the current Instructions on this topic. That page also confirms the current status date of the Instructions used here. 23

## Cantonal practice notes

### F. Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** is particularly useful because it publishes a detailed student-admissions instruction for third-country nationals. Zurich requires a **personal and** **detailed** **study** **programme** stating the exact duration of the course and the reasons for the training objective. It also requires a justification for choosing Zurich as theplace of study, an immatriculation confirmation, the diploma required for admission, and translated copies of prior diplomas and work certificates. Zurich's public website separately summarises the key admissibility points, including over-30 justification, secured departure, sufficient means, and the risk created by repeated course changes. Crucially, Zurich states that for stand-alone German-intensive language-study cases it recognises only **full-day** **schools** **with** **at least** **20** **teaching** **hours** **per** **week,** and it generally limits such admissions to a maximum of 12 months. 24

Zurich also takes a firm line against **stacking** **programmes.** It says that chaining multiple courses is generally not approved, particularly where a language course is planned before a general or vocational course,or where university matriculation remains conditional on prior classes. One justified change of study direction may be allowed, but further changes will generally be refused because of the risk of exceeding the overall eight-year study ceiling referred to in SEM practice. 25

**Geneva** publishes one of the clearest documentary checklists. For third-country student permits, it requires an **admissibility certificate from the school, a CV and motivation letter,** and a **study plan** stating the **number** **of** **weekly** **teaching** **hours,** the timetable, the detailed branches and programme, the total duration of study, and the diploma(s) sought. Geneva also requires a written undertaking to leave Switzerland at the end of the planned studies, except for students admitted to a Swiss higher-education institution within the meaning of the HFKG. Geneva further states that permits are granted only for the school(s) foreseen in the study plan, and **any** **change** **of school** or **study** **plan** **requires** a **new authorisation** and must be reported immediately. 26

**Fribourg** offers a useful cross-check. For third-country students it requires the application to include a final enrolment confirmation from the university or other higher-education institution, proof of sufficient funds, the **programme** **of studies** **year** **by** **year,** the intended diploma, the CV, copies of prior diplomas, and a written undertaking to leave Switzerland after the study stay,on failure, or if the study plan is not respected. Fribourg's separate ancillary-work form also reflects its practice that only students enrolled **full** **time** **at higher-level schools** may obtain work authorisation, and that the academic course must remain the principal purpose of stay. 27

These cantonal materials are not federal law, but they are highly probative of how migration authorities actually test **institutional seriousness** and **programme genuineness.** For internal drafting, Geneva and Zurich are especially useful because they reduce the federal standards into concrete documentary requirements that can be translated into system rules and content prompts. 28

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

### G. Practical legal analysis

The authorities do not examine only whether a person has a letter from a school. They examine whether the **school-programme-applicant combination** is migration-law credible. On the school side, that means proper educational delivery, observance of the official programme, stable operations, quality controls, and - for private schools - either register coverage or some other solid basis for trust. On the programme side, that means a coherent course with identifiable hours, timetable, branches, duration, and diploma outcome. On the applicant side, that means educational fit, language fit, and a plausible progression rather than a chain of opportunistic or repetitive courses. 29

The distinction between **recognised higher-education institutions and private schools** is therefore functional, not merely formal. HFKG-accredited institutions listed by swissuniversities occupy the least contentious end of the spectrum, especially where the acceptance is unconditional and the course is clearly structured. Private schools can still qualify, but they carry a heavier evidential burden unless they are in the Swiss Private School Register or another officially recognised segment such as HF or a federally recognised hotel-school programme. 30

The **full-time/principal-study** requirement is central but must be stated carefully. At federal level, the materials reviewed do not establish one universal numerical threshold applicable to all programmes and all cantons. The safer proposition is that the course must be the applicant's **principal activity** and must be sufficiently substantial and structured to qualify as genuine education or training. Where a system needs a more operational benchmark, it should make clear that any hours threshold is **practice-based,** not a universal statutory rule. Zurich's 20-hours-per-week rule is the strongest published example found, but it is clearly tied to Zurich's practice for stand-alone German-intensive courses,not all Swiss student permits. 31

Ancillary work rules also reinforce the institutional distinction. For third-country nationals, SEM's Instructions explain that ancillary work is tied to study at a **Hochschule** **or** **Fachhochschule,** requires a school confirmation that the job is compatible with the course, and is capped at 15 **hours** **per** **week** outside holidays. Fribourg's form similarly says the student must be enrolled full time at higher-level schools and that the study goal must remain primary. A school offering only limited or evening teaching is therefore a poor fit for immigration credibility where the applicant also seeks work. 32

Finally, the materials show that **school quality and immigration credibility are linked explicitly,** not merely by implication. SEM's 2026 Instructions provide that problematic schools should be visited; authorities should exchange information about dubious schools, agents and irregularities; schools should report problems with students; and good student support reduces misuse of thne residence purpose, including unauthorised work. SEM also says that the higher the quality of the training, the

### H. Drafting cautions

**Use** **two** **separate** **legal pathways** in internal content. For **EU/EFTA,** the correct frame is treaty-based residence under the AFMP/OLCP for persons in training at an approved institution, with sufficient means and insurance. For **third-country** **nationals,** the correct frame is discretionary admission under FNIA Article 27 and OASA Articles 23-24. Do not collapse these into a single "student permmit" rule-set. 34

Do **not** say that any school acceptance letter is enough. The safer internal formulation is that the authorities expect a **detailed** **and** **credible** **programme** **package,** often including admission confirmation, timetable, weekly hours, branches, total duration, intended diploma, prior qualifications, and an explanation of purpose and post-study plans. 35

Do **not** equate the **Swiss** **Private** **School Register** with general academic accreditation or automatic permit approval. The legally accurate statement is narrower: register entry creates a **presumption** that the immigration-law school-quality requirement in Article 24(1) OASA is met, unless concrete contrary indications arise, but the applicant's personal conditions and the overall credibility of the study stay still require separate review. 22

Do **not** gJeneralise Zurich's **20-hours-per-week** language-school rule into a federal rule. Internal content should state that published cantonal practice may use quantitative thresholds in certain categories, especially stand-alone language-school cases, but the federal legal test is broader and turns on whether the course is genuine, properly structured, and pursued as the main purpose of stay. 36

Be cautious with **age-based** **language.** Zurich's public guidance says applicants over 30 must justify their Zurich study plan. At the same time, the Federal Supreme Court's published case summary for **BGE** **147** I 89 frames refusal of a study permit through the lens of possible **age** **discrimination** and discusses the established administrative practice of generally refusing study permits to persons over 30. The safe drafting position is that age may inform an individual credibility assessment, but a blanket over-30 rule should not be presented as a self-standing legal requirement. 37

Avoid overly broad claims about course progression. It is defensible to say that repeated changes of course, poor results, course interruptions, or serial programme-stacking can undermine credibility and may support refusal or non-renewal. But it is not safe to say they automatically do so in every canton. SEM's own practice materials and Zurich guidance support the more conservative formulation. 18
