# Swiss Student and Education Permits for Non-EU/EFTA Nationals

Suggested filename: legalauthoritystudentpermitsnoneueftastudentspack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

**A. Executive legal summary. For non-EU/EFTA nationals,** the core legal basis for **a** Swiss student or education permit is **Article** **27** **FNIA.** It is a **discretionary** admission power, not a right: foreign nationals may be admitted for education or training if the school confirms eligibility, suitable accommodation is available, the required financial means are available, the applicant meets the personal and educational requirements for the proposed course, and, if the applicant is a minor, supervision is guaranteed. Continued stay after the course ends or is discontinued is not governed by Article 27 itself, but by the ordinary admission rules. 1

Article 27 is implemented principally by **Articles** 23 **and** **24 OASA.** Article 23 sets out how financial means may be evidenced and explains that the personal requirements are met, in particular, where there is no indication that the proposed studies are merely a device to circumvent the general admission and residence rules. It also states that education or training is **normally** **authorised** **for** no **more** **than** **eight years,** with exceptions for a targeted course of study. Article 24 requires the school to provide proper education, comply with its programme, fix the programme and its duration, and confirm that the applicant has the requisite educational background and language ability; the authorities may demand a further language test in justified cases. 2

The route is legally distinct from the **EU/EFTA** **treaty** **position.** Under Article 2 FNIA, the FNIA applies to EU/EFTA nationals only subsidiarily where the **Agreement** on **the** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** does not provide differently, or where the FNIA is more favourable. SEM's federal factsheet states that, under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, **EU/EFTA students and other non-economically active** **persons** **have** **a** **right** **of residence** if they have sufficient means and comprehensive health insurance; for persons in education, the federal treaty-based framework turns on means, insurance and admission to a recognised educational institution. That treaty-right position should never be conflated with the third-country Article 27 framework. 3

Visa sequencing is nationality-sensitive. For stays over 90 days, Switzerland distinguishes between a **Schengen** **visa** **(type** C) for short stays and a **national** visa **(type** **D)** for longer-term stays. The FDFA states that a type D visa is for longer stays and is issued **subject** **to** **authorisation** **from** **the** **competent** **cantonal migration** **authority.** At statutory level, visas are issued by Swiss representations abroad on behalf of the competent authority, and a longer stay without gainful employment is permit-subject. Where a person has entered lawfully for a temporary stay and later seeks a longer stay, **Article** **17** **FNIA** requires the person in principle to **wait** **abroad** for the decision unless the canton considers the admission requirements clearly fulfilled. 4

Renewal is also tightly controlled. Federal guidance states that if the course lasts more than one year, a **B** **permit** is generally issued; if it lasts less than one year, an **L** **permit** is used. Only full-time schools with a programme of at least 20 **weekly** **hours** qualify under the SEM directives. Students must make reasonable progress, including sitting intermediate and final examinations within a useful time; otherwise the authorities may treat the purpose of stay as achieved and decline renewal. A change of purpose requires a **new** **permit** under Article 54 OASA, and after completion of studies any further stay requires a fresh legal basis. 5

The most important drafting point is this: since the 2011 revision, a guaranteed departure from Switzerland is **no longer** **an** **express** **statutory** **condition** **of** Article 27. But the route remains legally **temporary,** and the authorities still scrutinise whether the proposed study plan is genuine and whether the application is being used to evade the broader admission regime. That distinction is central to accurate drafting. 6

## Structural overview of the legal framework

### B. Structural overview of the legal frammework. The framework is layered.

At the top sits the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons** for EU/EFTA nationals. Article 2 FNIA makes clear that the FNIA is only subsidiary where treaty rules apply, and SEM's federal materials confirm that EU/EFTA students fall under a treaty-right structure rather than the discretionary third-country framework. 3

**For non-EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the primary statutory provisions are in the **FNIA.** The main provisions for this topic are Article 6 on visa issuance, Article 10 on permit requirement for longer non-gainful stays, Article 17 on waiting abroad pending a permit decision, Article 21 paragraph 3 on the six-month post-study job-search rule for Swiss university graduates, Article 27 on study admission, and Article 34paragraph 5 on the limited way in which study residence can later count towards permanent residence. Article 3 paragraph 3 also matters because it anchors the broader admission policy context. 7

The next layer is the **OASA,** especially Articles 23 and 24. These are the core implementing provisions. Article 54 matters where the person changes the purpose of stay; Article 56 paragraph 3 is relevant for the one-time character of certain short stays; and Articles 38 to 40 govern ancillary work, which is a separate question and should not be folded into the core Article 27 analysis. 8

The live administrative framework is supplied by the **SEM** **AIG** **Directives** and the **FDFA** **visa** **and** **entry** **guidance.** Those materials are not legislation, but they are highly important for how the law is actually administered: they address study plans, school recognition, language checks, personal appearance at the Swiss representation, document packages, how the eight-year rule is handled, what counts as a full-time school, and how private schools are assessed. 9

Finally, there is **cantonal** **practice** **and** **case** **law.** Cantonal materials are not nationally binding, but they are important evidence of how federal norms are operationalised, particularly on financial proof, housing evidence, minors, annual renewals and visa sequencing. The key case-law themes are discretion, the temporary character of the route, the distinction between formal statutory requirements and discretionary or abuse-based assessment, and the rejection of blanket age rules. 10

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

D. **Primary** **legal texts.** Article 27 FNIA is the centrepiece. It sets the statutory **conditions** for study admission and makes clear that the route is for **education** **or** **training** and that continued stay after completion or discontinuation is governed by the ordinary rules, not by a built-in post-study right under Article 27 itself. Articles 10 and 17 FNIA frame the procedural position: longer non-gainful stays are permit-subject, and persons who lawfully entered for a temporary stay but later seek a longer stay must in principle await the decision abroad unless the canton finds the admission requirements clearly met. Article 6 regulates who issues visas, and Article 21 paragraph 3 creates the separate six-month job-search rule for persons with a Swiss university degree. Article 34 paragraph 5 is important because studyperiods generally do not count in the same way towards permanent residence. 17

Articles 23 and 24 OASA are the key implementing provisions. Article 23 is the main authority for financial proof, personal suitability and the eight-year rule. Article 24 is the main authority for school standards, fixed programme/duration, school confirmation of educational and language prerequisites and possible further language testing. Article 54 OASA confirms that if a permit was granted for a specific purpose of stay, a change ofpurpose requires a new permit. 18

**E. Official guidance / administrative materials.** The SEM AIG Directives, in force as updated on 1**January** **2026,** are the most useful federal administrative source for how Article 27 is applied in practice. They require a **personal study** **plan** with a precise goal, comparison of that goal against the official school programme, and school confirmation that the candidate has both the required educational level and the necessary language ability. The directives also say that a permit for study purposes may not be refused because of the applicant's age alone, citing **BGE 147** I **89,** and that the temporary character of the permit means the person cannot ordinarily rely on Article 8 ECHR private-life protection to stay on, citing **BGE 144 I 266** and **2C916/2021.** 19

The same SEM guidance is also the clearest federal source on **anti-abuse, documents,** **financial** proof, **permit type, full-time schooling, school recognition and renewal discipline.** It requires a personal appearance at the Swiss representation for visa-requiring applicants unless an exception is justified; lists theexpected application documents; treats a school programme of at least 20 **weekly** **hours** as the baseline for a qualifying full-time school; states that students must make reasonable exam progress; and explains that schools entered in the **Swiss** **Private** **School** **Register** are generally presumed to meet Article 24 paragraph 1 OASA, while new or unknown private schools face greater scrutiny. 20

**On** **entry** **and** visas, the FDFA's 2026 pages state that Switzerland issues type C visas for stays up to 90days and **type** **D** **national visas** for longer stays, and that a type D visa is issued only with the

## Cantonal practice notes

**F. Cantonal practice notes.** These sources are useful for **evidence and procedure,** but they should be treated as **cantonal practice,** not as nationwide federal law. 22

**Zurich.** Zurich's practice note on education for third-country nationals states that, for living costs and suitable accommodation, **CHF** **21,000** **for** **twelve** **months** is treated as sufficient for evidence purposes. It also stresses secured return, looks closely at the applicant's personal circumstances and motivation, and says that if the course is not at a Swiss higher education institution a written return undertaking is required. Zurich's public guidance also states that applicants over 30 must justify the chosen course of study in Zurich and that isolated German-intensive language-school permits are limited to full-time schools with at least 20 **hours** **per week,** generally for a maximum of 12 **months.** 23

**Geneva.** Geneva's official student-permit page for third-country nationals states that the permit is granted for the duration of the course if thecourse lasts less than one year, and otherwise is granted for **one** **year, renewable** until the end of the studies. It describes student permits as **strictly** **temporary,** says that in principle only studies or training up to **eight years** are admitted, and requires any change of school or study plan to be notified immediately because it is subject to a **new** **authorisation.** Geneva also states that ancillary work is only possible for students enrolledin a **Swiss** **higher** **education** **institution,** at the earliest six months after starting studies, and only with a separate work authorisation. 24

**Vaud.** Vaud's student form is unusually useful because it shows what the canton expects as evidence: a definitive adImission certificate; confirmation that the student has the required educational level and language knowledge; proof of first-semester tuition or ability to pay; a letter of intention; CV with supporting diplomas; specified forms of financial proof; housing evidence such as a lease, host confirmation or school room confirmation; and a written commitment to leave Switzerland at the end of the intended studies. It also states that for a non-boarding student, sufficient means are treated as about **CHF** **2,000** **per month, plus** **housing** **costs.** For minors, Vaud requires a signed document from the legal representative indicating to whom the child's custody is entrusted, except for boarding-school cases. Vaud's English form also states that the cantonal population service generally **pre-authorises** **the** **issuance** **of** a **visa,** but the Swiss representation abroad remains the authority that issues it, and the student must await the final consular visa decision abroad. 25

**Basel-Stadt.** Basel-Stadt's migration guidance states that once an application is approved, the cantonal migration office issues an **authorisation** **to** **grant** a **visa,** which the applicant uses to collect the visa at the Swiss representation abroad; after entry, the person registers locally in Basel-Stadt. Basel-Stadt also expressly notes that a visa is **not** a residence permit. 26

## Practical legal analysis

**G. Practical legal analysis.** The route should be analysed as a **temporary, purpose-bound, non-entitlement-based** **admission** **route** for third-country nationals. The core legal mistake to avoid is treating school admission as if it automatically produces residence permission. It does not. Article 27FNIA and Article 24 OASA separate the applicant-side conditions from the institution-side conditions, and the SEM directives add a further practical requirement for a clear personal study plan tied to a precise educational objective. A university or school acceptance letter is therefore necessary,but it is not sufficient on its own. 27

<!-- 5 -->

**On** **accommodation** **and** **finances,** federal law is deliberately framed in evidential terms rather than fixed national figures. Article 23 paragraph 1 OASA lists acceptable ways to prove means, but it does not itself set a flat federal amount. Cantons therefore often operationalise the federal rule with indicative thresholds or documentary expectations, as Zurich and Vaud do. For internal drafting, those cantonal figures should be described as **practice** **points** **or** **evidential** **benchmarks,** not as nationwide legal thresholds. 28

The **most** **legally** **sensitive** **area** is the relationship between the 2011 reform and the continuing anti-abuse analysis. FAC **C-7924/2010** held that the former express requirement of assured departure was removed by the 2011 revision and is no **longer** a **formal statutory** **condition** under Article 27. But the same judgment and the current SEM directives preserve a meaningful role for the temporary character of the stay and for the Article 23 paragraph 2 OASA anti-circumvention analysis. The correct synthesis is therefore**: absence of secured departure is not, by itself, an express Article 27 ground of refusal;** **however, unclear long-term intentions, prior procedures, implausible study planning or signs of** **abuse may still justify refusal in the overall discretionary assessment. 6**

That same distinction matters for **second** **qualifications** and older applicants. The SEM directives now explicitly reject the old practice of refusing study permits simply because the applicant is over 30, citing **BGE** **147** I **89.** But that does not mean age is irrelevant in practice. Together with FAC **C-7924/2010,** the materials show that age, prior qualifications, second education, family situation and future plans may all remain relevant to the exercise of discretion and to the credibility of the temporary educational purpose. Zurich's own practice note reflects exactly that kind of scrutiny. 29

Renewal is not automatic either. Federal guidance says that students must sit intermediate and final examinations within a useful time and make real progress; otherwise the authorities may take the view that the purpose of the stay has been exhausted and refuse further renewal. Importantly, the **award** **of** **a diploma is not itself a statutory condition,** and FAC **C-6783/2009,** as summarised in the SEM directives, is cited for that proposition. What matters is the proper continuation and completion of the authorised programme, not a free-ended right to remain until any desired educational milestone has been reached. The SEM directives also state that a change of field or an additional course is permitted only in sufficiently justified exceptional cases. 30

**For minors,** the federal statute is brief but strict: supervision must be guaranteed. Cantonal materials show how that is operationalised. Vaud, for example, requires a custody or care document signed by the legal representative, unless the minor is in a boarding-school setting. Internal drafting should therefore avoid generic wording such as "minors may apply through a school"; the betterformulation is that **appropriate supervision and legal-care arrangements must be evidenced,** and practice is likely to be more exacting where the school is not a boarding institutionา. 31

The end of studies is another high-risk drafting area. Article 27 paragraph 3 FNIA says that continued stay after completion or discontinuation is governed by the **general admission** **requirements.** Article 54 OASA separately provides that a change of purpose requires a new permit. The narrow statutory relaxation is Article 21 paraggraph 3 FNIA, which gives persons with a **Swiss** **university** **degree** a six-month period to seek suitable work. SEM guidance emphasises that this job-search authorisation is a separate competence pathway and is **not** **extendable.** This should be described as a **separate** **post-study** **route,** not as an automatic extension of the original study permit. 32

<!-- 6 -->

Lastly, study residence should not be marketed internally as a simple route intolong-term residence. Article 34 paragraph 5 FNIA makes clear that temporary stays, especially for education and training, do **not** count in the normal way towards the uninterrupted residence period for permanent residence, save for the later inclusion rule after two years of subsequent residence. The SEM directives also stress that the permit's temporary character weakens any later attempt to rely oon Article 8 ECHR private life as a reason to stay. 33

## Drafting cautions and source list

### H. Drafting cautions.

·Do **not** describe the Article 27 route for non-EU/EFTA nationals as a right or entitlement. It is a **discretionary** admission route. 34

·Do not collapse EU/EFTA students into the same framework. EU/EFTA students are treated under the **Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons** and the FNIA applies only subsidiarily.3

·Do not state that "guaranteed departure" is an express current statutory condition of Article 27. The correct formulation is that the permit remains legally **temporary,** and temporary-purpose/ anti-abuse concerns remain relevant through Article 23 OASA, Article 5 paragraph 2 FNIA as used in federal guidance, and the discretionary assessment. 6

·Do **not** treat cantonal financial figures such as **CHF 21,000/year** in Zurich or **CHF** **2,000/month** **plus** **housing** in Vaud as federal legal thresholds. They are canton-level evidential practice points. 35

·Do **not** say that any school admission letter is enough. The school must satisfy Article 24 OASA, the programme and duration must be fixed, and the institution must confirm the applicant's educational and language prerequisites. 36

·Do **not** imply automnatic renewal, automatic work rights, automatic post-study stay, or automatic counting towards settlement. Each of those points is separately regulated and limited. 37
