## A. Executive legal summary

Swiss post-study residence is not a single, unified "student visa" regime. The starting split is foundational: for **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing ordinance govern residence where they apply; for **non-EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration governs study residence and any later work admission. The Act itself makes the distinction explicit by providing that treaty rules prevail where applicable, while the ordinary foreign-nationals regime continues for others.1

**For non-EU/EFTAstudents,** residence for education or training is purpose-bound. Article 27(3) FNIA provides that any **further stay after completion or discontinuation** of education is governed by the **general admission** **conditions** of the Act, and SEM's current directives add that a **new stay** **after** **studies** **requires** **a** **new** **authorisation** under Article 54 OASA. In other words, there is no automatic continuation of the study permit once the educational purpose ends. 2

The principal Swiss post-study route for non-EU/EFTA nationals is narrow. Article 21(3) FNIA allows a foreign national with a **Swiss** **higher-education** **degree** to be admitted, by way of derogation from the ordinary priority rule, if the proposed gainful activity is of **high** **scientific** or **economic interest;** the same provision also allows a stay of **six** **months** **after** **completion** of Swiss education or training in order to find such activity. Current SEM guidance states that this six-month stay is handled cantonally, requires proof of a Swiss higher-education diploma (or an equivalent school attestation of completion), **sufficient** **funds** and **adequate** **accommodation,** begins to run from the date studies are actually completed, and **cannot** **be** **extended.** SEM further states that work of up to 15 **hours per** **week** may be authorised during the search period by analogy with Article 38 OASA; a higher workload would be incompatible with the purpoose of the permit. 3

That six-month graduate search stay is not a general post-study right for all Swiss-educated persons. Federal guidance defines the route around graduates of a **Swiss** **haute** **école;** SEM explains that this covers Swiss universities and universities of applied sciences, and includes persons who came to Switzerland only for a Swiss master's or doctorate. Cantonal practice pages reinforce the narrow scope: Geneva limits the route to graduates of recognised Swiss higher education instituutions and expressly states that **post-doctoral researchers** **are** **not eligible** for that permit; Zürich frames the route around a completed Swiss **Hochschule** degree and proof of successful completion. 4

Moving from the search stay to actual employment still requires a **work** **authorisation** **under the** **ordinary** **labour-admission** **structure,** save only for the waiver of the domestic/EU/EFTA priority rule. Article 18 FNIA still requires an **employer** **application** for salaried work and compliance with Articles 20to 25; SEM's 2026 directives are explicit that, after Swiss studies, the labour-priority rule is waived but

**the** **other admission** **conditions** remain applicable. Those remaining conditions include the overall economic interest test, the annual **quota** **system,** and compliance with local/sectoral wage and employment conditions. SEM also stresses that the job must correspond to the completed studies and that unrelated roles remain excluded. 5

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the legal picture is materially different and treaty-based. Students fall under the "persons not exercising an economic activity" rules in Annex I AFMP, which require sufficient means and sickness insurance; the treaty text gives students a residence title limited to the duration of studies,or one year if the studies exceed one year, with annual renewal for the remaining study period. After studies, there is **no** **special Swiss-graduate** **six-month** **route** comparable to Article 21(3) FNIA. Instead, an EU/EFTA graduate either moves directly into worker status on the basis of employment, or uses the general treaty-based **job-seeker** framework. Under Annex I AFMP, job-seekers may stay for a **reasonable** **period,** which SEM-following Antonissen-treats as up to **six** **months;** if job search continues beyondd three months, a short-stay EU/EFTA permit is issued for three months and may be extended, in federal practice, up to **one** **year** if there is a real prospect of engagement and the person has sufficient means. 6

Quota issues remain live only on the **non-EU/EFTA** side. As of 2026, the Federal Council has retained the ordinary annual contingents for third-country workers at **8,500** **permits** in total, split into **4,500** **B** **permits** **and** **4,0000** **L** **permits.** A federal reform project to exempt Swiss higher-education graduates from those quotas remains **pending** **and** **not** in **force**: SEM still lists the project as ongoing, and Parliament's public file continues to show it as **returned** to **the** **Federal Council.** Drafting should therefore proceed on the basis that the current law still waives the priority rule for qualifying graduates, but **does not yet waive the quota system.** 7

## B. B. Structural overview of the legal framework

The correct internal structure is a **three-track** framework. First, there is the **third-country** **education** **route** under Article 27 FNIA, which governs admission for education and whose purpose ends with completion or discontinuation of studies. Secondly, there is the **third-country** **graduate** **exception** in Article 21(3) FNIA, which does two things only: it permits a six-month post-completion search stay for a qualifying Swiss higher-education graduate, and it disapplies the labour-priority rule if that graduate later seeks admission to a qualifying job of high scientific or economic interest. Thirdly, there is the **EU/** **EFTA** **treaty** **route,** which does not depend on Article 27 FNIA at all, but on Annex I AFMP and OLCP. 8

**For** **third-country** **nationals,** the route is layered rather than automatic. Entry and stay for education depend on admission tostudy, adequate accommodation and sufficient means under Article 27 FNIA. Once the study purpose ends, Article 27(3) redirects the person to the ordinary admission architecture; SEM operationalises that redirection through Article 54 OASA, requiring a fresh authorisation basis. Only if the person has a qualifying Swiss higher-education degree does Article 21(3) create a specific post-completion search window. Employment after that is still processed through the labour-market system in which the employer applies and the general wage, quota and qualification structures remain. 9

For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the analysis starts with the treaty. Annex I AFMP gives students a residence right as non-active persons if they are enrolled, insured and have sufficient means. The same treaty annex separately gives job-seekers a right to stay for a reasonable period and allows exclusion from social assistance during that period. SEM's 2026 OLCP directives then translate that treaty framework into Swiss practice: annual student permits for the duration of study; direct movement into employment when there is a declaration of engagement or employment certificate; and, where the graduate is only

<!-- 2 -->

searching for work, a non-quota short-stay job-seeker permit beyond three months, with a possible extension where there remnains a real prospect of engagement. 10

Cantons sit at the implementation layer. Federal law and SEM guidance supply the controlling framework, but cantonal authorities process the permit, set filing mechanics and often publish practical conditions. Geneva's pages are particularly clear on filing deadlines, documentary proof, and the continued maintenance of ancillary work during the search period; Zürich's pages clearly distinguish the six-month third-country graduate search permit from the subsequent employer-led work-permit process. For internal drafting, cantonal points should therefore be treated as **practice** **notes,** not as independent sources of national law. 11

### D. D. Primary legal texts

The **Federal Act** on **Foreign** **Nationals and** **Integration** supplies the core statutory architecture. Article 2 states that the Act applies only insofar as no other federal rule or international treaty applies, and expressly subordinates the position of EU and EFTA nationals to the free-movement regime where that regime contains different rules. Article 5 requires the financial means necessary for temporary stay; Article 10 makes a permit mandatory for stays of more than three months without gainful activity; Article 18 makes salaried work admission dependent on an employer application and compliance with Articles 20 to 25; Article 20 authorises annual quantitative limits; Article 21 contains both the ordinary labour-priority rule and the Swiss higher-education graduate exception; Article 27 regulates study residence; and Article 29a excludes persons staying solely for job-seeking,and their family members, from any claim to social assistance. 26

Article 21(3) FNIA is the key statutory hinge. Its first sentence removes the **priority** requirement for a foreign national with a **Swiss** **higher-education** **degree** where the employment is of **high** **scientific** **or** **economic** **interest;** its second sentence creates the **six-month** **search** **period** after completion of education or training in Switzerland. That text is statutory; the detailed operational conditions-funds, housing, permit coding, start-date mechanics, non-renewability and limited ancillary work-come from current SEM guidance and cantonal implementation rather than from the Act itself. 3

Article 27 FNIA is equally important for what it does **not** give. It allows admission for education or continuing education where the school confirms admission, suitable accommodation is available, the necessary financial means are available, and the individual has the personal and educational prerequisites. But Article 27(3) then states plainly that any **further stay** in **Switzerland** **after** **completion** **or abandonment** of education is governed by the general admission conditions of the Act. That provision should be treated as the principal statutory reason why post-study residence and post-study work must be analysed as **separate** **legal bases,** not as a continuation of study residence. 27

**The Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons,** especially Annex **I,** is the primary text for EU/EFTA nationals. Annex I Article 2 gives workers and job-seekers a right to stay in the host state for a reasonable period to look for work; the treaty text itself says that this period may be six months and states that job-seekers can be excluded from social assistance during it. Annex I Article 24 then governs non-active persons, including students: students must show sufficient means and sickness insurance, and receive a residence title limited to the period of study, or one year if the studies exceed one year, with annual renewal for the remaining study period. 28

At ordinance level, the operative points needed for this topic are reflected in official SEM materials current to 2026. SEM's LEI directives apply **Article** **54** **OASA** to require a new authorisation after studies, **and** **Article** **38** **OASA** to set the 15-hour ancillary-work framework (used by analogy for the graduate job-search stay); SEM's OLCP directives apoply **Articles** 16 **to** **18** **OLCP** to the financial-means test and the EU/EFTA job-seeker permit structure. Where the content system needs a primary-law label for those points, the correct references are therefore Article 54 OASA, Article 38 OASA, and Articles 16 to 18 OLCP, but the safest cited operational elaboration is the current SEM guidance rather than bare ordinance drafting. 29

#### E-F. E. Official guidance / administrative materials and F.

## Cantonal practice notes

The principal **federal administrative** **authority** is the SEM's 2026 LEI guidance. Two passages are especially important. First, the SEM says that a new stay after training is subject to a **new** **authorisation** and cites Article 54 OASA for that proposition. Secondly, in its dedicated section on the Swiss graduate route, the SEM explains the six-month permit's cantonal handling, the requirement of a Swiss higher-education diploma, the housing and funds requirement, the running of the six months from the actual completion date, the deduction of any period already elapsed after completion, the 15-hour weekly work limit by analogy to Article 38 OASA, and the **non-prolongation** of the six-month permit. For internal drafting, these passages are the most operationally useful federal statements on the third-country post-study route. 30

The SEM's labour-market chapter then explains what happens after the search period when the graduate has found a job. It states that the concept of **high** **scientific** **interest** must be read broadly, in light of academic freedom, and is not confined to particular scientific branches; it also states that **economic** **interest** may be present where there is a demonstrable shortage in the relevant labour market, where the graduate's course is highly specialised and well matched to the position, or where the appointment will immediately generate jobs or mandates for the Swiss economy.The same guidance also states that the graduate route does not apply to sectors with no direct connection to the completed studies, and that all admission conditioons under Articles 20 et seq. FNIA continue to apply save for the labour-priority rule. 20

The SEM's **OLCP** **guidance** and free-movement factsheet perform the samne function for EU/EFTA nationals. They confirm that non-active EU/EFTA persons, including students, have a treaty-based residence right if they have sufficient funds and insurance; that students receive annual permits for the duration of studies; that a person admitted as a student may take up **principal** **employment** **by** producing a declaration of engagement or employment certificate; and that EU/EFTA job-seekers may stay without a permit for up to three months, then receive a short-stay permit for a further three months, with a possible extension up to a total of one year if genuine job-search efforts continue and there is a real prospect of engagement. The same federal materials also confirm the exclusion from ordinary social assistance during the job-seeker period and the non-application of quotas to that category. 31

**On** **cantonal practice,** Geneva is particularly explicit. Its third-country page requires filing **no** **later** **than** 15 **days** **before** **the** **end** **of** **studies,** states that the permit starts at the completion date shown by the final record of grades, confirms a validity of six **months** and non-renewability, and requires proof of financial means, housing, and the final academic record or diploma. It also states that ancillary work already held during studies may be maintained during the search period. Geneva further states that graduates of institutions not on the recognised Swiss higher-education list, and **post-doctoral** **researchers,** are not eligible for that permit. 17

Geneva's EU/EFTA page is also useful as a practical note, although it should not be elevated above federal law. It offers an EU/EFTA graduate a job-search **L** **permit valid** **for** **364 days and** **not renewable,** requires proof of identity, diploma and means, and says that ancillary work already exercised during studies may continue during the job-search period. That appears to be a cantonal way of operationalising the federal treaty-based one-year maximum job-seeker framework, rather than a free-standing national rule of its own. 32

<!-- 5 -->

Zürich's official pages are valuable as a second cantonal cross-check. Zürich states that third-country students may receive ancillary-work permission only from **six** **months** **after** the start of training and only where the school confirms compatibility with the studies and work stays below **15** **hours** **weekly** outside holidays. For post-study practice, Zürich states that third-country graduates with a Swiss higher-education degree can be admitted to work only where the activity is of high scientific or economic interest; the **employer** must apply; the domestic priority check is **not** performed; and a separate six-month search permit can be requested if sufficient means, suitable accommodation and the diploma or completion confirmation are present. 33

### G. G. Practical legal analysis

The most important practical distinction is between a **third-country** **graduate-search** **authorisation** and a **graduate** **work** **authorisation.** The first is a short-stay permit whose sole purpose is to allow a qualifying Swiss higher-education graduate to remain in Switzerland briefly to look for an appropriate role; the second is simply an ordinary work authorisation processed within the normal labour-admission framework,albeit with one facilitation: the **priority** **test** under Article 21(1) FNIA is waived. Any internal drafting that collapses these into one continuous right will misstate the law. 34

The nature of the third-country six-month search stay itself needs careful expression. The statutory wording of Article 21(3) is potestative: the graduate "may" be admitted. Yet the SEM's current guidance states that where the person shows a Swiss higher-education degree, sufficient means and adequate accommodation, the person has a **right to have the conditions of stay regulated** during that six-month period. The safest internal formulation is therefore: the route is founded on a discretionary statutory provision, but current federal administrative guidance treats the six-month short-stayregulation as available where the prescribed documentary conditions are met. That is more accurate than either extreme of describing it as a full statutory entitlement or as entirely unstructured discretion. 35

The expression **"high** **scientific** or **economic interest"** should also be handled conservatively but not narrowly. SEM's 2026 guidance, citing Federal Administrative Court case law, reads "scientific interest" broadly in light of the constitutional freedom of science and explicitly says that graduates in natural sciences, social sciences and humanities may all qualify. It then gives working economic-interest indicators: demonstrable labour shortage in the relevant field, highly specialised training closely matching the role, or immediate job creation / new mandates for the Swiss economy. At the same time, SEM excludes jobs wvith **no** **direct** link to the completed studies, such as unrelated administrative roles. An internal content system should therefore evaluate both the **substantive** **Swiss interest** in the role and the **study-to-role nexus.** 20

The labour-market overlay remains significant for third-country graduates. SEM's public labour-market guidance states that third-country admission remains limited to qualified workers, rests on the overall economic interest, is subject to annual quotas fixed by the Federal Council, and requires local/sectoral wage and working conditions. That remains true even for Article 21(3) graduates because SEM states expressly that only the **priority** **rule** falls away; the remainder of the Articles 20 et seq. framework stays in place. The current 2026 quota total remains 8,500, so internal drafting should not say that Swiss graduates are quota-exempt under current law. 36

On the **EU/EFTA** side, the correct analysis is simpler but should be kept distinct. An EU/EFTA student's residence is treaty-based and dependent on enrolment, means and insurance during studies. After graduation, that person does not need a Swiss-specific graduate privilege equivalent to Article 21(3) FNIA: the treaty already allows direct access to the labour market on proof of employment, and separately allows staying as a job-seeker for a reasonable period. Federal practice now treats that treaty right as a three-month unpermitted stay followed by a short-stay permit, extendable up to a total of one year if there is a real prospect of engagement. That is materially more generous, and structurally different, than the third-country six-month non-renewable arrangement. 37

<!-- 6 -->

Pending reform should be marked only as **pending.** The Federal Council's 2021 consultation project sought to exempt third-country graduates of Swiss higher education from the annual quota counts where their activity is of overriding scientific or economic interest. Parliament did not enact that proposal; instead, the public parliamentary file records that the project was **returned** **to** **the** **Federal** **Council,** and the parliamentary commission publicly explained that it wanted a constitutionally compliant alternative, for example by simplifying procedures or extending the period during which labour priority does not apply. Until a new enactment actually enters into force, internal drafting should assume that the only operative facilitation remains the current Article 21(3) structure. 38

### H-I. H. Drafting cautions and I. Source list

A few drafting points should be treated as hard rules. First, do **not** describe Swiss post-study residence as one "student visa" framework: EU/EFTA graduates are analysed under treaty law, whereas non-EU/ EFTA graduates are analysed under the FNIA/OASA system. Secondly, do not imply that a study permit continues automatically after the end of studies; it does not. Thirdly, do not present the third-country graduate search permit as a general right for all Swiss-educated persons or for aIl discontinuations of study; it is tied to a qualifying Swiss higher-education completion. Fourthly, do **not** describe the graduate work route as a stand-alone permit type divorced from ordinary labour admission: it is an Article 21(3) facilitation within the wider Articles 18 to 25 FNIA framework. Fifthly, treat the15-hour **work limit during the third-country search period** as an SEM-guidance rule applied by analogy, not as an express statutory sentence. Sixthly, keep clear that the six-month/non-renewable rule belongs to the **third-country Swiss higher-education graduate route,** while the EU/EFTA job-seeker framework is different and may run up to one year in federal practice. Finally, speak cautiously about reform: quota exemption for Swiss graduates is **not** **yet** **in** **force.** 39
