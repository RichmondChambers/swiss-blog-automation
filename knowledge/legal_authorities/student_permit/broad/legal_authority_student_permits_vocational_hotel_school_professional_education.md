# Swiss legal authority pack on vocational education, hotel schools, professional schools and practical education programmes in student-permit cases

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not operate a single, unitary "student visa" regime for all education cases. The legally correct framework depends first on whether the person is an **EU/EFTA** **national within** **the** **scope** **of** **the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons** or **a non-EU/EFTA national subject to the Federal** **Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. For non-EU/EFTA nationals,** residence for education or training rests primarily on **Article** 27 **FNIA** and the implementing rules in **Articles 23-24** **OASA,** while paid practical elements are dealt with through the **derogation** **mechanism** **in** **Article** **30(1)(g) FNIA** and **Articles 38-40 and 42 OASA.** For **EU/EFTA nationals,** the core residence basis is **Article 24 of Annex I** **AFMP,** implemented in Swiss practice through the FMPO/VFP and SEM guidance; where the treaty conditions are met, the position is treaty-based rather than purely discretionary.1

For non-EU/EFTA education cases, the statutory conditions are conservative and cumulative: the school must confirm eligibility, suitable accommodation and sufficient financial means must exist, and the applicant must meet the personal and educational requirements of the course. OASA then sharpens that framework by requiring credible proof of funding, anti-circumvention scrutiny, a fixed programme and duration, and a school that offers appropriate teaching and complies with the programme. SEM guidance adds that private or unfamiliar schools may be scrutinised closely, but schools entered in the Swiss private-school register can normally be treated as satisfying the immigration-law requirements, and **federally recognised programmes in higher professional education, especially HF hotel** **schools, are treated as "recognised schools" even without private-school-register entry.** 2

The most important operational distinction for practical elements is between **ancillary** **employment** and a **mandatory internship integrated intoa full-time programme.** For non-EU/EFTA nationals, **Article** **38** **OASA** allows ancillary work only for persons studying at a **university** **or** **university** **of** **applied** **sciences** and only after six months, with a 15-hour weekly cap outside vacations, school confirmation, an employer application, and compliant wage and working conditions. By contrast, **Article 39 OASA** governs **full-time training with a compulsory internship:** the internship may be authorised if it does not exceed **half** of **the** **total training** **duration,** an employer applies, local/ professional/sector pay conditions are met, and appropriate accommodation is available. This is a different legal route from ordinary labour-market admission. 3

That distinction matters acutely for **hotel-school and professional-school programmes.** SEM expressly treats **federally recognised HF hotel-school programmes** as recognised schools for Article 24 OASA purposes, but SEM's own explanation of **Article** **38** **OASA** confines the ancillary-employment route to **HFKG** **higher education** **institutions** such as universities, universities of applied sciences and teacher education universities. It follows that a hotel school marketed as "higher education" is not enough by itself: the legal analysis turns on the institution's actual Swiss status and the structure of the programme. If the programme is an **HF** **/** **higher** **professional education** course with integrated

practice, the safer immigration analysis is usually **Article** **27** **plus** **Article** **39** **OASA** for mandatory placements, not a generic "student work" entitlement. 4

For EU/EFTA nationals, the position is materially different. A student who has no residence right under another AFMP provision must receive a residence permit if they show sufficient means, all-risks sickness insurance, and registration at an approved establishment to follow, as their principal activity, a vocational training course. The AFMP does **not** regulate access to vocational training itself or maintenance assistance. SEM's VFP guidance further states that EU/EFTA persons in education have a legal entitlement if the treaty conditions are met; their permit is issued for one year at a time and renewed annually until the regular end of training if the conditions continue to be met. 5

## Structural overview of the legal framework

**For non-EU/EFTA nationals,** the starting point is residence **without gainful employment** under **Article** **27** **FNIA.** That provision is discretionary in form: foreign nationals **may** be admitted for education or training if the school confirms suitability, accommodation and financing are in place, and the person meets the personal and educational requirements. SEM guidance states expressly that no entitlement arises to the grant or renewal of an education-based residence permit under the general discretionary regime, and that such permits remain tied to their stated purpose. 6

OASA then provides the operative filters. **Article** 23 **OASA** sets out how financial means may be proved, treats personal qualifications as insufficient where the invoked course appears to be a device to evade the general admission rules, and states that education or continuing education is in principle admitted only for a **maximum** **of eight years,** with derogations only for a specific training objective. **Article** **24OASA** requires an appropriate course offer, compliance with the teaching programme, a fixed programme and duration, and confirmation by school management that the applicant has the necessary prior education and language skills. That is the core legal architecture for vocational, professional and other non-university study cases.7

Paid work during a third-country education stay sits on a separate track. Ordinary salaried employment is governed by **Articles** 18 **and** **20-25** **FNIA,** which require an employer application and import the usual labour-market admission tests, including quotas and precedence of domestic / free-movement labour. By contrast, **Article** **30(1)(g) FNIA** authorises derogations from Articles 18-29 in order to facilitate international exchange and professional education / training. **Articles 38-40** **OASA** operationalise that derogation for ancillary work, compulsory internships, and postgraduate work during Swiss university / UAS study. 8

For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the governing framework is treaty law. **Article** **24** **Annex** **I AFMP** gives residence rights to economically inactive persons, including students,subject to sufficient means and sickness insurance, and for students specifically requires enrolment in an approved establishment for a vocational training course pursued as the principal activity. SEM's VFP guidance stresses that, unlike third-country education admission, EU/EFTA cases within the AFMP are rights-based and that the restrictive OASA provisions for non-active third-country persons do not apply. 9

Two additional structural points matter in drafting. First, after completion or discontinuation of a third-country education course, continued residence is governed by the **general admission** **rules,not** by any automatic educational continuation right. Secondly, periods of stay for education or training do **not** **normally** **count** towards the uninterrupted residence period for settlement; they are only counted if, after completion, the person held a residence permit for an uninterrupted further period of two years. 10

## Primary legal texts

The core third-country statutory base is the **FNIA. Article** **27** setsthe admission conditions for education and training. **Article** **30(1)(g)** authorises derogations from the normal admission rules to facilitate international economic, scientific and cultural exchange and professional education / training. **Articles** **18** **and** **20-25** remain the baseline for ordinary gainful employment, including employer application, quotas, precedence, wage conditions, qualifications and accommodation. **Articles 32-33**confirm that short-stay and residence permits are granted for a **specific** **purpose** and may be made subject to conditions, while **Article** **34(5)** provides that education stays do not generally count towards the key settlement-permit qualifying period. 18

The main implementing ordinance for third-country education cases is the **OASA. Article** **23** deals with proof of funds, anti-circumvention and the principle that education stays should not exceed eight years unless justified by a specific objective. **Article** **24** regulates what counts as a sufficient school structure. **Article** **38** creates the limited ancillary-work route for university / UAS students; **Article** 39 governs compulsory internships in full-time programmes; **Article** **40** concerns postgraduate work during Swiss university / UAS study; **Article** **42** governs trainee / young-professional arrangements. 19

For EU/EFTA nationals, the governing primary text is Article 24 of **Annex I AFMP.** It provides a residence right for economically inactive persons who have sufficient means and sickness insurance, and a specific student rule requiring an approved establishment and vocational training as the principal activity. The same provision says the Agreement does not regulate access to vocational training or student maintenance assistance, and that student permits are renewed for the remaining training period so long as conditions remain met. 20

Swiss implementation of the AFMP is through the **FMPO/VFP** and the body of SEM VFP guidance. In the materials reviewed, SEM's 2026 VFP instructions are the clearest operational source on sufficient means, annual permit duration for persons in training, the inapplicability of OASA Articles 23-25 to AFMP non-active cases, and the handling of internships and ancillary work for EU/EFTA persons in education. 21

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes

SEM's **AIG** **instructions** are the most valuable administrative source for this topic. They explain that applicants should present a current and plausible personal study plan identifying the objective of the education; they also record that a rigid age-based refusal practice for study stays was curtailed by **BGE** **147** **I** 89, while still permitting more searching scrutiny of coherence as age increases. The same guidance stresses the temporary nature of education permits and, citing **BGE** **144** I **266** and **2C916/2021,** states that their predictably temporary character normally prevents reliance on Article 8ECHR private life to insist on continued stay. 22

The AIG instructions also contain the most useful material specifically on **hotel schools. SEM** distinguishes language expectations for different hotel-school settings, notes that recognised HF hotel-school programmes are to be treated as recognised schools for Article 24 OASA purposes, and sets out the handling of new or unknown private institutions. Where a private school is new or unfamiliar,the cantonal education department's recommendation and evidence of stable financing and organisation are expressly relevant. 13

SEM's **VFP** **instructions** perform the equivalent function for EU/EFTA cases. They confirm that persons within the AFMP have a legal claim to permit issuance if the treaty conditions are met, that economically inactive EU/EFTA cases are not subject to the restrictive third-country OASA provisions, and that persons in education need only make sufficient means plausible rather than satisfy the stricter funding proof models used for third-country cases. Thesame guidance also explains that marginal ancillary work may be undertaken by EU/EFTA students, but if the person is actually exercising worker status within the AFMP sense, the case should be handled under the worker rules rather than simply as a student case. 23

**On** **cantonal practice,** the clearest public material located in this research concerns **cantonal** **competence rather than canton-specific doctrinal divergence.** SEM states that cantons determine the competent authorities for AFMP permits and notifications; cantonal migration authorities examine the supporting evidence; and, in third-country education cases, cantonal migration and education authorities have a central role in assessing private schools and programme quality. For compulsory internships in another canton lasting more than three months, SEM states that a **letter-form consent** **in the** **work canton** is required if the school canton has already authorised the whole course including the internship and subsequent return to theory. 24

A separate official route also needs to be ring-fenced in internal drafting: **Young** **Professionals** / **trainees.** SEM's Young Professionals page explains that this route is for people who have **completed** their education, is designed for career development, lasts a maximum of 18 **months,** requires work in the learned profession or study field, and does **not** allow part-time work or self-employment. It is therefore not interchangeable with an education-based residence for a current hotel-school or vocational programme. 25

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

The first drafting rule is to classify the institution **before** classifying the permit. "Hotel school", "professional school" and "practical education programme" are not immigration-law categories. The decisive questions are whether the institution is a **HFKG** **university** **/** **UAS,** a **federally** **recognised** **HF** / **higher professional education provider,** or some other **private school;** whether the programme is genuinely educational; and whether any work element is **mandatory** **and curricular** or merely optional. SEMM's own materials show that these distinctions change the legal route. 4

The second rule is not to overstate work rights. For **non-EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the after-six-month ancillary-employment route in **Article** **38** **OASA** is textually confined to universities and universities of applied sciences. It should therefore not be presented as a general right for all hotel-school or professional-school students. If the course is an HF hotel-school programme with required practical semesters, the correct analysis will usually focus on **Article** **39** **OASA,** and only where the internship is genuinely compulsory, embedded in a **full-time** programme, and does not exceed **half** of total course duration. If the placement is optional, extra-curricular, or a disguised labour arrangement, the safer view is that the case falls outside Article 39 and may require ordinary labour-market admission analysis instead. 26

The third rule is that even where work is allowed within an education structure, **employer-side** **compliance** **does** **not** **disappear.** Article 39 OASA still requires an employer application and compliant Swiss wage and working conditions, and Article 38 OASA does likewise for ancillary employment during university / UAS study. That means an internal content system should avoid language suggesting that a "student permit" by itself authorises work. The legal permission depends on the specific work provision being engaged and the employer-side filing and labour-conditions checks being satisfied. 27

The fourth rule concerns **renewal and** **progress.** For third-country cases, the permit remains purpose-bound. SEM states that non-extension is justified where the condition linked to the permit's purpose is no longer met, and that the education purpose itself is such a condition. It is therefore safer to draft in terms of the applicant continuing to pursue the authorised programme, continuing to satisfy financial and accommodation conditions, and continuing to fit within the approved curriculum and duration, rather than asserting any freestanding right to renewal. The **eight-year** **principle** in Article 23 OASA is an additional warning against serial or loosely connected study extensions. 28

The fifth rule is to avoid careless post-completion promises. **Article** **27(3) FNIA** sends continued residence after completion or discontinuation back into the general admission rules. The special six-month job-search facilitation under **Article** **21(3)** **FNIA** is described by SEM as applying to **Swiss** **university** **degrees** within the HFKG universe. That should **not** be generalised to hotel-school, HF or other professional-school graduates unless the actual programme is a Swiss university / UAS qualification within that statutory meaning. 17

The sixth rule is to keep the **EU/EFTA** treaty position separate. Within AFMP scope, the residence right is treaty-based and the OASA restrictions for third-country education admission do not apply. EU/EFTA persons in training must still show sufficient means, sickness insurance and approved educational enrolment with vocational training as the principal activity, but the legal character is different. SEM also notes that internships and ancillary work for EU/EFTA students are possible, and that short remunerable internships generally require notification; however, once the person is in truth exercising worker status, the case should be analysed under the worker provisions, not left in a vague "student work" category.

The seventh rule is to be careful with **age** **and** **human-rights** **language.** SEM's guidance reflects that a rigid "over 30" refusal practice for study cases was curtailed by the Federal Supreme Court, so age should not be treated as an automatic bar. At the same time, SEM still treats age as a factor that can justify closer scrutiny of the plausibility and coherence of the study plan. Conversely, because study residence is temporary by design, SEM's cited case law indicates that students will not usually be able to invoke strong Article 8 ECHR private-life arguments merely from time spent in Switzerland under that permit category. 22

**Open** **questions** **/ limitations.** Publicly accessible cantonal materials located in this research were materially thinner than the federal guidance, so the cantonnal notes above are chiefly competence-based. For live case handling, the system should therefore force a **programme-specific verification** **step** on current school recognition, institutional status, and whether the internship is truly mandatory in the official curriculum before generating any confident proposition on work rights or renewability. 30
