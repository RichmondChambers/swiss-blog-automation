## A. Executive legal summary

For **third-country** **nationals,** stays for education or training in Switzerland are governed principally by **Article 27 of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act** and **Articles 23-24 of the Ordinance on** **Admission, Stay** **and** **Employment.** The legal structure is expressly **discretionary**: the statute says that foreign nationals may be admitted for education or training if the listed conditions are met, and recent cantonal and federal-level materials continue to state that **there** is **no** **legal entitlement** to a permit under Article 27. The route is also treated as **temporary,** not as a settlement rouute. 1

The **ordinary** **duration** **framework** for third-country study stays is **eight** **years.** That eight-year period is not framed in the ordinance as an absolute statutory prohibition, but as the **ordinary** **maximum**: education and training are "ingeneral" authorised for up to eight years, and exceptions are possible where they serve a **targeted** course of education or training. Current SEM guidance adds that such exceptions must be **sufficiently reasoned,** must reflect **a logical educational progression,** and must not be used to circumvent stricter admission rules. Where a third-country study stay is expected to run beyond eight years, or where there are substantive concerns about insufficient study commitment or repeated changes oof educational objective, the matter must be submitted to the **SEM** **for approval** under the EJPD approval ordinance. 2

**A change of field, a second education,** or an **additional programme** is therefore not prohibited in every case, but it is treated as **exceptional** and requires a convincing justification. SEM guidance states that a change of academic direction during the course of study, or an additional education, is only approved in **sufficiently** **justified** **exceptional** **cases.** Zurich's official cantonal instruction is even more explicit: one justified change of study direction may be approved; repeated changes, programme stacking, or a pattern suggesting an attempt to prolong residence may lead to refusal; and even where several Master's programmes are accepted in sequence, the overall study stay should still remain within the eight-year framework unless SEM-approved exception logic applies. 3

Progress matters. SEM guidance requires students to sit interim and final examinations **within** **a** **useful** time, and if they do not, the purpose of stay may be treated as **achieved,** so that the permit is not extended. At the same time, the guidance-relying on Federal Administrative Court case law-also makes clear that the law does **not** require the course to end with a diploma as such; the decisive issue is whether the training remains genuine, structured and pursued with sufficient seriousness.Recent Zurich case law illustrates both outcomes: an extension was refused where a student had been exmatriculated for repeated failures and then tried to restart in essentially the same field at another institution without a persuasive exceptional justification; but an over-eight-year extension was allowed where delays were closely documented, the course still formed part of a first education with a logical build-up, adverse events were evidenced, later progress was strong, and completion remained realistically imminent. 4

Once the original education or training is **completed** **or** **discontinued,** continued stay is not a simple "extension" of the prior study permit. Article 27 paragraph 3 AIG provides that continued stay after completion or discontinuation is governed by the **general admission** **rules,** and Article 54 VZAE provides that a **new** **permit** is **required** where the original permit was granted for a specific purpose and that purpose changes. That matters acutely for post-completion second degrees and "additional qualifications": the legal analysis should begin from **fresh** **admission,** not from any assumed continuation right. 5

For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the starting point is materially different. Their legal position is anchored first in the **Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons,** especially **Annex I, Article 24,** which gives economically inactive persons, including students, a **treaty-based** **right** of **residence** if they have sufficient financial means and comprehensive sickness insurance,and-specifically for students-if they are enrolled at an approved institution for vocational training. The treaty text and SEM's current factsheet state that students are issued a permit for the duration of the training or for one year if the training lasts longer, with renewal while the eligibility conditions continue to be met. This is therefore **not the** **same legal route** as the discretionary third-country Article 27 regime, and the third-country eight-year framework should **not** be presented as the source of the EU/EFTA student position. 6

A further drafting nuance is important. The express requirement of **"secured** **re-departure"** was removed from Article 27 AIG by the 2010 reform that entered into force on 1 January 2011. However, the temporary character of the stay still matters through **Article** **5** **paragraph** **2** **AIG** and through the anti-circumvention logic in **Article** **23** **paragraph** **2** **VZAE.** Current SEM guidance therefore still examines whether the case shows an intention to use study as a route around the ordinary admission rules, even though "secured return" is no longer a standalone text requirement of Article 27 itself. Older authorities that speak in those terms should accordingly be read with care. 7

## B. Structural overview of the legal framework

The law divides into two distinct tracks.

For **EU/EFTA nationals,** the hierarchy is**: AFMP treaty text → Annex I Article 24 on economically** **inactive persons and students → implementing ordinance and SEM free-movement guidance→cantonal registration and permit administration.** On that track, the core question is whether the person falls within the treaty conditions, not whether the canton should exercise broad admission discretion. 8

**For non-EU/EFTA nationals,** the hierarchy is**: AIG Article 27 → VZAE Articles 23-24 and 54→EJPD** **approval ordinance Article 4(b) → SEM AIG directives → cantonal practice.** On that track, Article 27 is **a temporary, purpose-specific and discretionary** admission ground. The ordinance supplies the normal eight-year framework and anti-circumvention test; theEJPD approval ordinance identifies the cases that must be sent to SEM; the SEM directives explain how the federal administration expects cantons to assess progression, course changes, additional studies and abuse indicators. 9

The practical decision structure for third-country cases is therefore normally this: first, is the course genuine, full-time and at a qualifying institution; **second,** are finances, accommodation, language and educational prerequisites proven; third, do previous stays, repeated changes or poor progress suggest circumvention; **fourth,** is the educationn still realistically targeted and completable in a normal period; and fifth, if the case approaches or exceeds the ordinary maximum, or otherwise falls wwithin Article 4(b) of the EJPD approval ordinance, must the extension be referred to SEM. 10

### D-F. Primary legal texts, official guidance and cantonal practice notes

D. **Primary** **legal texts.** For third-country nationals, the core statute is **Article** **27** **AIG.** It requires confirmation from the educational establishment, suitable accommodation, sufficient financial means, and fulfilment of the personal and educational requirements for the planned course; paragraph 3 then states that continued stay after completion or discontinuation falls back to the general admission framework. The general temporary-stay rule in **Article** **5** **paragraph** **2** **AIG** requires a guarantee of departure where only temporary stay is planned, and **Article** **17** **AIG** states that a person who entered for a temporary stay and later applies for a longer stay must normally await the decision abroad unless the admission requirements are clearly fulfilled. At ordinance level, **Article** **23** **VZAE** sets the anti-circumvention test and the ordinary eight-year framework, while **Article** **24** **VZAE** requires the course and its duration to be defined and the school to confirm that linguisticand educational prerequisites are met. **Article** **54** **VZAE** requires a new permit when the original permit was purpose-specific and the purpose changes. Finally, **Article** **4(b)** of **the** **EJPD** **approval ordinance** identifies the study extensions that must be sent to SEM. 21

For **EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the relevant primary text is the **AFMP,** especially **Annex** **I Article** **24.** That provision grants economically inactive persons, including students,a residence right if they have sufficient means and all-risks sickness insurance; and it states specifically that a student enrolled in an approved establishment for vocational training is to be issued a residence permit for the period of training or for one year if the training exceeds one year, with annual renewal for the remaining training period while the conditions continue to be met. 22

**E. Official guidance and administrative materials. SEM's AIG directives,** current as of **1 January** **2026,** are the key federal administrative source for third-country study cases. They require a personal study plan stating the exact qualification sought; they stress that the route must not be used to avoid stricter admission rules; they explain the ordinary eight-yearrule and the "logical educational progression" exception; they require timely examination progress; they state that a change of academic direction or additional education is exceptional; they note that post-completion stay requires a new permit; and they explicitly reject any automatic age-based refusal for applicants over 30. 23

<!-- 4 -->

SEM's **private-school circular** **of** 16 **May 2024** is not about duration as such, but it is practically important on school recognition and abuse monitoring. It says that schools entered in the **Privatschulregister** **Schweiz** may generally be treated as satisfying the Article 24 VZAE quality requirement, but it also insists that the apoplicant's personal requirements under Article 27 AIG and Article 23 VZAE must still be examined carefully. The circular further expects cooperation on irregularities such as abuse of the stay purpose, illegal work and disappearance from the place of study. 24

SEM's **2021 explanatory** **report** on the reform concerning graduates of Swiss higher education institutions is the clearest official source for the historical point that the express requirement of **"gesicherte Wiederausreise"** was removed from Article 27 AIG with effect from1 **January** 2011.That report is important because many older authorities still use that language; the safer modern formulation is that temporary intent remains relevant through Article 5 paragraph 2 AIG and Article 23paragraph 2 VZAE, rather than as an express text requirement of Article 27 itself. 25

F. **Cantonal practice** **notes.** Zurich's migration instruction is the most developed official cantonal source located on this issue. It states that **stacking** different educational programmes is not permitted as a rule; one justified change of study direction may be approved; repeated changes may evidence a durable-stay strategy; several Master's degrees may be undertaken consecutively but still within the overall eight-year period; and any justified extension beyond eight years must be sent to SEM. That instruction is materially useful for internal risk analysis, but it remains **cantonal** practice,not a nationwide federal rule. 26

Geneva's official pages are useful in both tracks. For **third-country** **students,** Geneva states that study permits are strictly temporary, that only education or further training lasting a maximum of **eight years** is in principle accepted, and that any change of school or study plan requires a **new** **authorisation** and must be notified immediately with reasons. For **EU/EFTA** **students,** Geneva states that they **benefit** **from** **a** **right** of **residence** if they have sufficient financial resources and health/accident insurance, but it still treats the permit as temporary and requires any change of school or study plan to be notified and authorised anew. 27

Vaud's form and notice are older, but still useful as a cantonal administrative signal. It requires **every** **modificationn** to the study plan during the Swiss stay to be reported immediately to the Service de la population, expressly including a **change of orientation, a change of school,** or an **extension** beyond the dates originally declared in the study plan. It also warns that federal approval may lengthen processing where the file must be referred upward. 28

Ticino's official pages are more procedural than analytical, but they remain useful as Italian-language administrative material confirming the permit categorisation: study stays of more than one year require **a** **B** **permit,** while shorter study stays require an **L** **permit,** and the canton's legal-bases page correctly points users to the AFMP,AIG, VZAE and VFP/OLCP as the governing sourceS. 29

### G. Practical legal analysis

**Duration** **limits and** **the** **eight-year** **framework.** Internally, the safest proposition is that the eight-year rule is a **third-country** **ordinary** **framework,** not a rigid universal prohibition and not the source of EU/ EFTA students' residence rights. The ordinance wording, the SEM directivesand the EJPD approval ordinance all point in the same direction: eight years is the normal ceiling for education/training under Article 27 AIG, but exceptions remain legally possible where the file shows a genuinely **targeted** educational path. The most persuasive positive factors are those expressly named in current guidance: **a** **logical build-up** between stages, a clearly identified final objective, realistic timing, and absence of circumvention indicators. 30

<!-- 5 -->

**Approaching or exceeding eight years. A** case close to eight years should be treated as an **exception** **dossier,** even before the duration is technically exceeded, because the SEM approval ordinance is triggered when the stay is expected to last beyond eight years. The file should therefore be built around a chronology of all Swiss study periods, exact reasons for delay, current credits/ECTS, remaining modules, expected completion date, and evidence showing why the porogression still makes educational sense. The Zurich 2025 case is important precisely because it shows that an over-eight-year extension can still succeed where the educational arc remains coherent and first-education based, earlier delays are evidenced, and completion is realistically imminent. But the same judgment also showshow narrow the window is: the court coupled the extension with a clear warning that any further delay or another faculty change would likely make continued stay indefensible. 31

**Second** **education.** At federal level, a purely optional second education is high risk. SEM guidance still cites Federal Administrative Court decisions in which permits were righntly refused in second-education settings, especially where there was no compelling need for the further course and where the overall route no longer looked temporary or purpose-limited. Those authorities still support the proposition that a second education is weaker than a first education and remains fully subject to discretionary refusal. That said, because the explicit "secured departure" requirement was removed from Article 27 in 2011, the better modern analysis is not to say that second education automatically fails for lack of "guaranteed return", but rather that it is vulnerable because it may cease to look like a targeted temporary training path and may instead suggest a route-around-admission case under Article 23paragraph 2 VZAE. 32

**Change** **of** **academic** **direction.** A mid-course change is not impossible, but it should be framed as a tightly evidenced exception. Federal guidance uuses restrictive language; Zurich's cantonal instruction goes further and states that one justified change may be approved, while repeated changes may point to an attempt to obtain a durable stay. Internally, the strongest change-of-directioon files are those where credits transfer cleanly, lost time is limited or objectively explained, the new course cures a demonstrated misfit in the prior programme, and the remaining study period remains realistically completable within a clearly defined period. The Zurich 2025 decision is the best current example:the court accepted the switch from Psychology to Business Administration only because the earlier low progress was substantially explained by an accident and later exam-technical problems, the student then performed well in the new programme, and completion by a specific near-end date remained realistic. 33

**Additional programme after** **completion.** Once the original programme is finished, the analysis changes materially. Article 27 paragraph 3 AIG and Article 54 VZAE mean that post-completion residence is governed by the general admission rules and requires a **new** **permit.** A second programme after completing the first should therefore be analysed as a fresh application for a new study purpose, not a simple extension of the old one. This makes "additional degree after completion" cases appreciably harder than a well-justified mid-course redirection. Nationally, the federal guidance supports a restrictive view; cantonally, Zurich's instruction shows some space for sequential Master's programmes, but that remains a cantonal administrative approach and still does not displace the general third-country limits on duration, coherence and abuse review. 34

**Failed** **or** **repeated** **courses.** The law does not require a diploma as a standalone condition, but it does require serious, timely pursuit. Repeated failures, exmatriculation, long periods with very few credits and inability to show a realistic completion trajectory are all red flags. The Zurich 2024 case is a strong adverse authority: after exclusion from one Bachelor's programme for failed examinations, the student enrolled in a substantially similar programme elsewhere, but the court held that the original educational purpose had effectively come to an end and that the new attempt was not sufficiently justified as an exceptional change. The opposite lessonfrom Zurich 2025 is that documented external causes matter: where poor results are tied to provable accident, procedural exam defects or similar non-routine circumstances, and the student later performs convincingly, the conclusion may change. 35

<!-- 6 -->

**Evidence** **of** **logical educational progression.** The decisive evidential items are already identified by SEM: a personal study plan; a clear statement of the qualification sought; the school's confirmation of admission and ability; the official programme; and, where relevant, evidence showing why the course sequence is a logical progression. For extension or over-age files, the "logic" should be demonstrated concretely rather than asserted. Good evidence includes continuity between stages, credit recognition, supervisory letters, academic timetables, remaining-credit calculations, medical evidence for interruptions, and proof that the newor additional programme genuinely completes or deepens the original educational objective. Bare preference, general employability claims or a desire for "better opportunities" will usually be too weak on their own. 36

**Abuse-of-route** **concerns.** This is the central negative theme in the federal materials. The statutory anti-circumvention test in Article 23 paragraph 2 VZAE, the SEM directives, and the private-school circular all show that study-route abuse is assessed broadly. Relevant indicators include earlier migration history, previous refusals, repeated school or field changes, implausible or opportunistic course choices, weak academic performance inconsistent with genuine study, poor alignment between education history and new course, and signs that the person's real objective is long-term stay rather than completion of a specific educational objective. The SEM interview questions reproduced in the directives-about family location, home-country prospects, future plans and return economics-show the practical lens. 37

**EU/EFTA** **distinction.** None of the above third-country discretionary logic should be transplanted wholesale into treaty cases. EU/EFTA students have a different legal foundation: a residence right under the AFMP, subject to funds, insurance and enrolment conditions. That does not mean cantonal administration disappears-Geneva, for example, still requires school and study-plan changes to be notified and authorised-but the legal analysis begins from treaty right, not from a broad discretionary admission power under Article 27 AIG. Internal drafting should keep the two tracks separate from the first line. 38

## H. Drafting cautions

**·Do not write as though Switzerland has one single "student visa" framework.** Internally, separate at least**: EU/EFTA treaty-based residence for students, third-country education** **permits under Article 27 AIG, and post-completion / changed-purpose applications** **requiring a new permit.** 39

**·For third-country cases, avoid entitlement language.** Use formulations such as "may be admitted", "may be extended", "requires SEM approval", or "can be granted in sufficiently justified exceptional cases". Avoid "is entitled to", "has a right to", or similar phrasing unless the authority is specifically AFMP-based. 40

**·Do not overstate the eight-year rule.** It is best described as the **ordinary framework** or **ordinary** **maximum** **duration** for Article 27 cases. It is not a hard nationwide statutory bar in every possible fact pattern, and it is not the legal source of EU/EFTA students' residence rights. 41

<!-- 7 -->

**·Do not describe "secured re-departure" as a current standalone Article 27 requirement.** The explicit requirement was abolished in 2011. If internal drafting needs the point, frame it as the ongoing relevance of the stay's temporary character under **Article** **5** **paragraph** **2** **AIG** and of anti-circumvention under **Article** 23 **paragraph** **2** **VZAE.** 25

**·Label cantonal materials** **as cantonal.** Zurich's rules on one permitted study change, sequential Master's programmes and programme-stacking are valuable risk markers, but they are not themselves federal legislation. Geneva and Vaud pages are likewise useful administrative practice sources, not nationwide binding doctrine. 42

**·Use** **older** **case** **law** **carefully.** The SEM directives still rely on older BVGer decisions on second education, insufficient results and studies used as a migration route. Those cases remain relevant, but any proposition built on "return secured" language should be harmonised with the post-2011 statutory wording and the present Article 5/Article 23 structure. 43
