# Swiss legal authority pack on residence of EU/ EFTA students in Switzerland

## A. Executive legal summary

Residence of EU/EFTA students in Switzerland is primarily a treaty-based free-movement matter, not an ordinary domestic immigration concession. The core framework is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU and its Member States, identified by SEM as SR 0.142.112.681, together with the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons, identified by SEM as SR 142.203. SEM's AIG guidance states expresslythat the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act applies to persons benefiting from free movement only subsidiarily, where the treaty and implementing rules do not provide otherwise or where the AIG is more favourable. The EFTA regime is described by SEM as largely equivalent for Iceland and Norway, with Liechtenstein subject to special arrangements. 1

For EU/EFTA students, the legally relevant frame is "economically inactive residence", but with student-specific rules. SEM's 2026 VFP instructions state that persons in education are part of the non-economically active categories under Annex I to the AFMP, but that, unlike other economically inactive persons, students need only make it credible that they have sufficient financial means; they must also show admission to a recognised educational institution in Switzerland in order principally to pursue a specific vocational education or training course. SEM also states that there are no quotas for non-gainful AFMP stays and that the restrictive admission conditions in Articles 23-25 OASA do not apply to EU/EFTA economically inactive stays. 2

The arrivalI and permit position is condition-based rather than pre-entry discretionary. SEM's free-movement FAQ states that pensioners, students and persons of private means must register with the local authorities at their place of residence and apply for a non-working residence permit no later than 14 days after arrival. The same FAQ states that students are granted a permit for the duration of their studies, or for one year with annual renewal until completion if the requirements remain satisfied. Geneva's official student page reflects the same structure: if study lasts less than a year, the authorisation is issued for that duration; if it lasts longer, it is issued for one year and renewed to the end of studies. 3

The permit label should be drafted cautiously. SEM's general non-working factsheet speaks of a five-year B EU/EFTA permit for economically inactive persons, but SEM's free-movement overview places students under the one-year, annually renewable L EU/EFTA category. Geneva's material describes the student authorisation functionally, by duration, rather than by hard-coding one permit type.For internal drafting, the safest national description is therefore "an EU/EFTA student residence authorisation, usually issued for one year and renewed annually until normal completion of studies, with shorter study periods granted for their actual duration", unless a canton-specific form is being described. 4

"Sufficient resources" is a real condition, but the evidential burden is lighter for students than for other economically inactive persons. SEM's VFP instructions say that, in principle, means are sufficient where a Swiss national in the same situation would not have to claim social assistance, andthat the Swiss Conference for Social Welfare guidelines are the relevant benchmark under Article 16 VFP. The same instructions then distinguish students, who need only make sufficient means credible. SEM's public non-working factsheet likewise says that means are sufficient where they exceed the level that would trigger Swiss social assistance, and that authorities may revalidate a permit after two years or revoke / refuse renewal if the means are no longer available. 5

Health insurance must be treated with particular care. FOPH's specialist guidance states that EU/EFTA students in Switzerland for education or training are not required to take out Swiss health insurance if they are not working in Switzerland and therefore remain insured under the social security system of their country of residence; treatment in Switzerland is then available on presentation of the EHIC. The same FOPH guidance says that if they start working in Switzerland, they must insure in Switzerland under the place-of-work principle, and that students with equivalent private insurance may seek cantonal exemption. FOPH's general insurance FAQ also repeats that specific EU/EFTA student exception. A general SEM FAQ reminder that persons residing more than three months in Switzerland must take Swiss insurance should therefore be read subject to this more specific, student-focused FOPH coordination guidance. 6

Accommodation is legally important, but not always in the same way. The AFMP student conditions highlighted by SEM focus on resources, insurance and admission to a recognised institution;they do not present "adequate accommodation" as a standalone treaty condition for the student's own residence right. In practice, howvever, cantons regularly require evidence of an actual Swiss residence address when registering and issuing the permit. Geneva requires either a copy of the lease or a host's attestation with supporting identity and main-lease documents, and its FAQ explains how temporary or later-confirmed addresses are handled. By contrast, for family reunification under the AFMP, adequate housing is expressly stated by SEM to be a legal condition. 7

Family members matter, but student sponsors have narrower family rights than many other AFMP sponsors. SEM's family reunification factsheet states that, for students, family reunification is limited to the spouse and dependent children. SEM's VFP instructions state the same point expressly and add that ascendants are excluded where the principal AFMP right in Switzerland is as a student. For non-working EU/EFTA sponsors more generally, sufficient means remain necessary, and family reunification also requires adequate housing. 8

Student employ/ment is possible, but must be described carefully. SEM's VFP instructions say that EU/ EFTA students with a study permit may engage in marginal ancillary employment, which must be notified to the competent permit authorities. The same instructions add that taking up employment after admission as a student is permissible if an employment declaration or work certificate is produced and there is no public-order or security problem. They also state that where doctoral or post-doctoral candidates perform genuine and effective work meeting the AFMP worker test, they must instead hold a worker permit under the employment provisions. Cantonal practice reviewed for Geneva and Ticino uses a 15-hour weekly term-time limit, with fuller holiday work, but that cap appears in the cantonal materials sampled and should not be elevated into a universal federal proposition unless the canton in question is being specifically described. 9

Loss of conditions can affect both renewal and continued validity. SEM's public material states that economically inactive EU/EFTA permits may be revoked or not renewed if the person no longer has sufficient financial means. SEM's VFP instructions add the same for loss of sufficient means or adequate health insurance. Separately, AIG guidance states that residence permits lapse upon deregistration abroad and, for residence-permit holders, when the person remains abroad for more than six months; Basel-Stadt and Ticino publish the same practical rule in their permit information. Because AIG is only subsidiary in AFMP cases, this is best understood as a subsidiary lapse rule rather than a free-standing treaty condition. 10

The non-EU/EFTA student route is materially different. SEM's AIG guidance states that admission for education and training outside the EU/EFTA sphere is governed by Article 27 AIG and Articles 23-24OASA, is structured to prevent circumvention of stricter admission rules, and requires the foreign national to satisfy personal and educational conditions. The same guidance and the official Lucerne page show that, for third-country students, suitable accommodation, proof of sufficient funds, and a broader domestic-law compliance package are required. SEM also records case law that Article 27 AIG does not confer a right to the permit and that a restrictive admissions policy may justify refusal even if the Article 27 conditions are met. That is the key contrast: AFMP student residence is condition-based and treaty-grounded; Article 27 AIG study residence is distinct, domestic and discretionary. 11

## B. Structural overview of the legal framework

At the top of the hierarchy sit the AFMP and the parallel EFTA free-movement regime. SEM's VFP instructions identify the AFMP as SR 0.142.112.681 and the implementing free-movement ordinance as SR 142.203. SEM's AIGinstructions explain that the EFTA Convention framework contains substantially the same free-movement rules for the relevant EFTA States and that Liechtenstein is dealt with under special arrangements. 1

The second layer is the federal implementing regime under the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons and SEM's VFP instructions. For economically inactive EU/EFTA residence, SEM's VFP instructions point specifically to Annex I AFMP Articles 2 and 24 and to VFP Articles 16-20. Those provisions and instructions are the main operational source for student residence, sufficiennt resources, insurance, permit duration, post-arrival procedure, and family reunion under the treaty regime. 12

The AIG sits behind that framework as a subsidiary source. SEM states clearly that AIG applies to free-movement beneficiaries only where the treaty and its implementing rules do not contain a different rule or where AIG is more favourable. That matters here for issues such as general lapse of permits on deregistration or prolonged absence abroad, and for keeping EU/EFTA student residence conceptually distinct from the domestic Article 27 AIG student route for non-EU/EFTA nationals. 13

A separate but necessary layer is Swiss health-insurance law, as administered by FOPH. FOPH's student-insurance guidance links the relevant legislation to Article 2 KVV and explains the EU/EFTA student coordination exception, while its general FAQ states the ordinary three-month insurance deadline for those who are in fact subject to Swiss compulsory insurance. In practice, residence-law drafting for students should therefore read migration rules and social-insurance coordination rules together rather than assuming a single immigration rule answers the insurance question. 14

The final layer is cantonal and communal implementation. Registration happens with the local or cantonal authorities after arrival, address evidence is checked cantonally, and extension practice is administered locally. Geneva, Ticino, Lucerne, Zurich and Basel-Stadt all publish operational material illustrating this implementation layer. These cantonal materials are significant for evidence, forms, deadlines and practice, but they do not displace the treaty-based federal conditions. 15

## D and E. Primary legal texts and official guidance / administrative materials

**Primary** **legal texts.** The main primary instruments identifiable from the federal materials arethe AFMP of 21 June 1999, identified by SEM as SR 0.142.112.681; the VFP, identified by SEM as SR 142.203; and the AIG, identified by SEM as SR 142.20. SEM further identifies OASA / VZAE as SR 142.201 for the domestic non-free-movement student regime. For EU/EFTA student residence specifically, SEM's VFP instructions place the operative rule set in Annex I AFMP Articles 2 and 24, read with VFP Articles 16-20. For insurance, FOPH's student guidance links the operative exemption framework to KVV Article 2. 26

**Official** **federal** **migration** **guidance.** The most useful federal administrative sources for internal drafting are SEM's 2026 VFP instructions, SEM's free-movement FAQ, SEM's public factsheet on residence without gainful activity, SEM's free-movement overview factsheet, and SEM's family reunification factsheet. Together, those materials cover the treaty basis, registration, permit duration, sufficient resources, insurance, family scope, family housing, post-study position, and loss of conditions. 27

**Official** **federal** **health-insurance** **guidance.** FOPH's "Foreign students in Switzerland" page and its insurance FAQ are indispensable because they supply the student-specific social-insurance coordination answer that is not stated with the same precision in the general migration pages. Those pages confirm the home-system / EHIC exception for non-working EU/EFTA students and the switch to Swiss insurance when the student works in Switzerland. 14

**Official domestic-law** **guidance** **for** **contrast.** SEM's AIG instructions provide the clearest official federal explanation of the non-EU/EFTA Article 27 AIG framework. They emphasise that third-country student admission is governed by Article 27 AIG and Articles 23-24 OASA, is designed to prevent avoidance of stricter admission rules, and does not create an entitlement even where the domestic conditions are fulfilled. 28

## F and G. Cantonal practice notes and practical legal analysis

**Cantonal practice** **notes.** Geneva's official student pages are the most operationally detailed of the cantonal materials reviewed. For EU/EFTA students, Geneva requires a valid identity document, a school enrolment attestation, proof of financial means or guarantee, and a copy of the lease or a host's attestation with supporting lease and identity documents. Geneva also states that student authorisations are strictly temporary, are issued for the planned educational establishment(s), and that any change of school oor study plan must be reported immediately because a new authorisation is required. Geneva's FAQ adds that a temporary address may be used before permanent accommodation is found and that a frontier student residing in France and merely commuting to study in Geneva does not need a residence permit, although separate frontier-work issues arise if the person wants to work or undertake a placement. 29

<!-- 5 -->

Ticino's official student notice adds useful practice points on status maintenance. It requires notification within 14 days of address changes, changes of nationality, civil-status changes, transfers to another commune or canton, and departure abroad. It also states that renewal must be requested at least 14days before expiry and that the student permit loses validity at expiry, at the end of the study period when residence in fact ceases, or following revocation. For ancillary work, Ticino states that school or higher-education students may request permission for side work up to 15 hours per week, provided the school certifies that the studies will not be prejudiced. 30

Lucerne's page is especially helpful for drawing the EU/EFTA versus third-country line. It says that EU/ EFTA students receive a permit when they can credibly show sufficient financial means and demonstrate admission to a public or private teaching institution in Switzerland. For third-country students, the same page then lists cumulative Article 27 AIG-style conditions, including suitable accommodation, necessary financial means, and personal/educational prerequisites. That contrast is highly useful for internal drafting because it shows, in an official cantonal explanation, that "suitable accommodation" sits naturally inside the third-country Article 27 framework and is not framed in the same way for EU/EFTA students. 31

Zurich and Basel-Stadt are useful for generaI AFMP implementation points. Zurich's page states that non-working EU/EFTA persons seeking family reunification must show sufficient financial means and provide a copy of the lease for adequate housing. SEM's FAQ adds that AFMP permits are valid throughout Switzerland and that, when moving canton, the permit holder must register with the new canton and have the new address entered on the permit. Basel-Stadt states that the B permit is sent to the residential address after registration and that it lapses oon deregistration or after six months' actual stay abroad, subject to a suspension request. 32

**Practical** **legal analysis.** For internal content-system purposes, the safest characterisation is that EU/ EFTA student residence is a specialised AFMP residence basis nested within the economically inactive framework, but not identical to the broader "economically inactive" category. Students have their own evidential standard on resources, their own permit-duration logic, and narrower family-reunification rights. It is therefore inaccurate to treat "student", "retired" and "private means" as a single undifferentiated residence route, even though they sit in the same general treaty chapter. 33

On financial means, the best conservative drafting is that the legal benchmark remains freedom from Swiss social-assistance dependence, with Swiss social-assistance standards used for orientation,but that for students the proof threshold is expressly lower: they need only make sufficiency credible. SEM's VFP instructions also cite Federal Supreme Court case law on third-party support and on the proposition that an EU/EFTA national cannot simply derive an AFMP residence right from the employment of a third-country spouse admitted through family reunification. The operational lesson is that support from others may matter, but the resources analysis should remain focused on whether the student's own AFMP conditions are genuinely met and stably evidenced. 34

On health insurance, internal drafting should privilege the specialist FOPH pages over generic one-line reminders. The specific rule for EU/EFTA students is not "Swiss insurance is always mandatory after three months". The more precise rule is that a non-working EU/EFTA student who remains insured in the home-state social-security system is not required to take Swiss compulsory insurance, and may rely on EHIC treatment rights in Switzerland; if the student starts working in Switzerland, Swiss insurance becomes mandatory on the place-of-work principle. Where private insurance is relied on, the exemption question is administered cantonally and depends on equivalence. 6

<!-- 6 -->

On accommodation, it is important not to overstate the law. The materials reviewed do not show "adequate accommodation" as a standalone AFMP student-residence condition equivalent to the Article 27 AIG third-country condition. What they do show, consistently, is that cantons require a real Swiss residence address and documentary proof of occupancy in order to process registration and issue the permit. The only clear AFMP housing condition evidenced in the reviewed federal material is the adequate-housing requirementfor family reunification. For a content system, the correct wvording is therefore that EU/EFTA students normally need a real Swiss address and documentary proof of where they live, while adequate housing is expressly a family-reunification condition. 35

On work during studies, the safest national proposition is that ancillary employment is possible but not wholly deregulated. SEM's VFP instructions say side work is possible and must be notified, and that post-admission employment is permissible on presentation of employment documents. They further distinguish cases where the person is in truth exercising genuine and effective employment under the AFMP worker ruules. The reviewed cantonal material from Geneva and Ticino uses a 15-hour weekly term-time ceiling and fuller holiday work, but because the federal materials reviewed do not formulate that ceiling as a general national rule for all EU/EFTA student cases, the better drafting is to describe it as reviewed cantonal practice and flag canton-checking as necessary. 36

On the end of studies and loss of status, student residence should be treated as purpose-bound and temporary. Geneva explicitly describes study permits as strictly temporary. SEM says student permits last only while the study-purpose conditions remain met. If the student completes studies and wants to remain, the route must change: worker status if employment begins, job-seeker status if applicable, or another AFMP-compliant non-working basis. Geneva expressly offers EU/EFTA graduates a 364-day L permit to seek work after studies, and SEM's VFP instructions also recognise AFMP job-seeker residence beyond the initial three-month period where the person has the necessary resources. 37

**Open** **questions** **and** **limitations.** Two areas remain practice-sensitive rather than fully harmonised on the reviewed sources. First, the exact permit label for student cases is not presented uniformly across the official summaries reviewed, so national drafting should avoid absolute statements that all EU/EFTA students always receive either L or B permits. Secondly, the 15-hour term-time work ceiling is clearly evidenced in the cantonal materials reviewed, but not yet in the federal treaty guidance collected here as a universally stated AFMP rule. Those points should therefore be treated as canton-specific verification issues rather than hard-coded national propositions. 38

## H. Drafting cautions

Do not describe this route as a single "retirement visa" or undifferentiated "economically inactive" framework. Students fall within the non-gainful AFMP chapter, but SEM's materials treat them distinctly on resources, permit duration and family scope. Internal drafting should therefore refer to an AFMP / EFTA student residence basis, not to a generic retirement or private-means route. 33

Do not imply that all residents staying more than three months must always take Swiss basic health insurance. The specialist FOPH rule for EU/EFTA students is narrower and more precise: a non-working student who remains insured in the home-state system does not have to enter Swiss compulsory insurance, whereas working in Switzerland usually triggers Swiss insurance at the place of work. 14

Do not describe adequate accommodation as a universal AFMP condition for the student's own permit unless yoou are discussing a particular canton's evidential practice. The safer formulation is that a real Swiss address and documentary proof of residence are usually needed in practice, and that adequate housing is expressly required for AFMP family reunification. 35

<!-- 7 -->

Do not universalise a 15-hour term-time work ceiling as if it were a single federal treaty rule, unless the system is explicitly describing Geneva, Ticino, or another canton whose published practice has been verified. Nationally, the higher-confidence proposition is that ancillary work is possible, must be notified / documented, and may shift the case into worker-status analysis if it becomes genuine and effective employment. 36

Do not conflate EU/EFTA student residence with Article 27 AIG third-country student residence. The latter is a separate domestic framework with stricter conditions, including suitable accommodation, and SEM records that it does not confer an entitlement even where the statutory criteria are met. 39

Do not imply a continuing right to remain in Switzerland on student status after studies end. The permit is temporary and study-purpose specific. Continued residence requires another legal basis, such as worker status, job-seeker status, or another compliant AFMP category. 40
