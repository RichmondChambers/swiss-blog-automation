# Swiss legal authority pack for minor student residence and boarding schools

## Executive legal summary

Swiss student residence for minors does not operate under a single, unitary "student visa" regime. The correct framework depends first on nationality. For EU/EFTA nationals, the main legal basis is treaty-based residence under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing ordinance, with a right to residence where the treaty conditions are met. For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the relevant basis is the domestic education-and-training admission route under Article 27 AIG and Articles 23-24 VZAE, which is discretionary and temporary, not an entitlement merely because the listed conditions appear to be met. Entry clearance, where required, is a separate visa step that follows the residence-authorisation process and must not be collapsed into the residence analysis. 1

For minors specifically, the safest conservative reading of the current materials is that Swiss practice focuses on documented supervision, accommodation and welfare rather than on a universal requirement for a formal Swiss court-appointed guardian in every case. Current cantonal implementation materials operationalise the minor-specific requirement through evidence of boarding-school residence, or, where the minor is not in residence, a signed document from the legal representative identifying the adult or arrangement to whom custody/care is entrusted. Cantonal pages also insist that suitable accommodation and care for minor children be ensured. 2

Boarding schools matter because SEM guidance expressly treats internats as full-time schools, and Canton Vaud's current study-permit checklist materially differentiates between students "in residence" and students not in residence. In Vaud, separate evidence on financial means, accommodation and minor custody is expressly waived for students in residence, whereas a non-residential minor must provide a document from the legal representative stating to whom custody is entrusted. This makes the boarding-school structure legally important, not merely factual background. 3

Across both regimes, renewal is not purely administrative. Residence remains tied to the educational purpose, the continued fulfilment of conditions, and actual attendance/progress. SEM guidance states that intermediate and final examinations must be taken within a useful period and that failure may justify refusal to renew. Geneva and Valais likewise present study permits as temporary and,where studies last more than one year, issued or renewed yearby year.4

## Structural overview of the legal framework

The legal framework is best read in four layers. First, the treaty layer: EU/EFTA nationals who are pupils or students fall under the AFMP/ALCP regime for persons not pursuing an economic activity, especially Annex I, Article 24, together with the Ordinance on Free Movement of Persons and SEM's OLCP directives. That route is rights-based if the treaty criteria are satisfied: sufficient means, all-risks insurance, and enrolment in an approved institution for a principal course of education or vocational training. Students' permits under that treaty route are limited to the duration of the training or one year where the training exceeds one year, and then extended annually while conditions remain satisfied. 5

Secondly, the domestic admission layer for third-country nationals: SEM's 2026 AIG directives state that the admission requirements for education and training are governed by Article 27 AIG, with the requirements on personal qualifications and schools governed by Articles 23 and 24 VZAE. Those directives emphasise that study residdence must not be used to circumvent stricter admission rules, that the stay is temporary, that the applicant must present a personal study plan and school confirmation, and that a residence permit under Article 27 AIG does not create a legal entitlement. Published SEM guidance also records Federal Administrative Court authority confirming that a permit may still be refused on restrictive-admission grounds even where Article 27 conditions are met. 6

Thirdly, the administrative-practice layer: SEM guidance, canton pages and canton forms supply the operational detail that matters most for minors. These materials flesh out what "appropriate accommodation", "sufficient means", "recognised school", "full-time programme", "temporary purpose", and-in the minor context-effective supervision and care actually mean in day-to-day case handling. That is especially visible in Vaud, Geneva, Valais and Zurich practice materials. 7

Fourthly, the child-welfare overlay: the migration materials reviewed do not set out a single mandatory immigration-law template for guardianship in all minor study cases, but they do point clearly towards child-welfare scrutiny. Vaud's child-hosting materials state that where a family receives a child and assumes day-and-night care, the arrangement is subject to authorisation and state supervision. SEM guidance also expects schools to supervise students closely, maintain accurate residence information, and notify authorities of serious or repeated problems. For an internal content system, that means welfare and safeguarding are not peripheral; they are built into the evidence assessment.(8

## Primary legal texts

The core treaty text is the AFMP/ALCP, especially Annex I, Article 24 on residence for persons not pursuing an economic activity. For students, Article 24(4) requires proof-by statement or equivalent means-of sufficient financial resources so that neither the student, spouse nor dependent children will claim host-state social assistance during the stay, enrolment in an approved establishment for a principal vocational training course, and all-risks sickness insurance. Article 24(5) then provides for annual extension of student residence where training exceeds one year. 10

On the domestic side, the current SEM AIG directives identify Article 27 AIG as the governing federal provision for admission for education and training, with Articles 23 and 24 VZAE supplying the detailed criteria on personal qualifications and on schools. Those directives further state that Article 27residence is limited to persons attending a full-time school with a programme of at least 20 hours per week and that internats qualify as full-time schools. They also rely on Article 54 VZAE for the proposition that, once the educational purpose ends, any further stay requires a new permit basis. 17

For EU/EFTA nationals, the implementing ordinance is the OLCP/VFP. SEM's current OLCP directives cite Articles 16-20 OLCP alongside Annex I, Articles 2 and 24 AFMP foor stays without gainful activity. They explain that sufficient means are assessed by reference to Swiss welfare thresholds and that the initial permit for non-active persons is generally five years, but for persons in training the validity is only one year and renewed year by year until the courseends while conditions remain fulfilled. 18

It is important, however, not to over-import AFMP logic into third-country cases. SEM's current AIG directives expressly say that AIG study residence is there to regulate temporary educational stays outside the EU/EFTA framework and to prevent their use as an oбxoд of stricter admission rules. The directives also record published case law to the effect that Article 27 AIG confers no legal entitlement. 19

## Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's 2026 AIG directives are the most important official federal administrative source for third-country study residence. They require a personal study plan identifying the desired qualification, confirmation from the school that the applicant has the educational level and language ability to follow the course, proof of sufficient means, and proof of an appropriate dwelling. The directives stress the temporary nature of the stay, insist that the person must intend to leave after the educational purpose is completed, and caution against using the study route to secure de facto long-term settlement. 6

Those directives are especially useful for boarding-school matters. They treat internats as full-time schools; set a 20-hour weekly threshold; recognise organised in-house accommodation as relevant to the institutional structure; and state that schools, private-school registers, migration authorities and education departments should maintain active information-sharing. Critically, SEM says schools must supervise students closely during their stay and inform authorities where there are serious or repeated problems. It also says communes need the students' exact actual addresses and schools must report moves and longer absences. 15

For EU/EFTA students, the OLCP directives and SEM's factsheet on residing in Switzerland without gainfuI activity are the clearest federal administrative sources. They confirm the treaty-based right for economically inactive persons including students, require sufficient means and comprehensive insurance, and explain that students need only make it plausible that they have sufficient means, provided they are enrolled in an approved institution for their principal course of training. 20

The federal entry and visa materials are equally important to keep the route analytically clean. FDFA's visa guidance explains that a national visa D is for stays over 90 days and is issued subject to the authorisation of the compoetent cantonal migration authority. SEM's nationality-based entry guidance adds that, in principle, even visa-exempt nationals require the residence-permit process to be initiated before entry for stays exceeding 90 days. 21

## Cantonal practice notes

**Vaud.** Vaud's official study-permit checklist is the strongest minor-specific cantonal source located. It asks for theschool details, the minor student's parents' names, and a reference person in Switzerland. It states that, before entry, the student applies via the Swiss consular post; that Vaud usually pre-authorises the visa whereas the consular post remains formally competent to issue it; and that the student must wait abroad for the visa outcome. Most importantly for minors, Vaud's documentary list distinguishes students in residence from others. For students not in residence, the file should include proof of financial means, accommodation evidence, and a minor-custody document signed by the legal representative stating to whom custody is entrusted. For students in residence, those items are marked as exceptions. 13

**Geneva.** Geneva's current OCPM student-permit pages frame the study permit as strictly temporary, normally issued for one year where studies last longer than a year, and tied to the educational establishment(s) set out in the study plan. They require, among other things, a school eligibility certificate, detailed study plan, wvritten undertaking to leave Switzerland at the end of the course,proof of financial means or sponsorship, and proof of accommodation through a lease or landlord attestation. Geneva's FAQ gives concrete practical thresholds for study cases handled there-CHF 20,000 per study year or CHF 2,500 per month-and accepts a provisional address pending biometrics, but still requires formal accommodation evidence once the definitive address is known. 22

**Valais.** Valais's current canton page is useful because it states the points in plain terms and does so in French and German. For EU/EFTA students, it confirms the one-year renewable permit structure, sufficient means, insurance, recognised school status, full-time study and a programme of at least 20weekly hours. For third-country students, Valais requires a personal study plan, CV, school confirmation of academic and language suitability, proof of paid school fees, evidence of sufficient means, appropriate accommodation, and assurance that care of minor children is guaranteed. It also repeats the one-formation/maximum-eight-year logic as a general rule for third-country cases. 23

**Zurich.** Zurich's official migration guidance is useful on sequencing. Its published practice note states that visa-exempt pupils and students can obtain an assurance of residence authorisation from the Migrationsamt while still abroad; and that, where a visa-exempt student has already entered Switzerland, an in-country grant is only exceptionally possible if all necessary documents are already present. This supports a conservative internal rule that pre-entry filing remains the default even for visa-exempt third-country minors coming for long study stays. 24

### Practical legal analysis

**Article** **27** **supervision** **requirement for** **minors.** The authorities reviewed do not present minor supervision as a free-standing label detached from evidence. In practice, it is folded into the assessment of whether the stay is genuinely educational, safely structured and credibly organised. The strongest documentary pattern is: if the child lives in a boarding school, the institutional residence and school oversight may itself meet much of the supervision concern; if the child lives outside school residence, the file should identify the responsible adult in Switzerland and contain a legal-representative document entrusting custody/care accordingly. That is the operational meaning to build into an internal content system. 25

**Boarding-school structure.** Boarding schools are not merely "accommodation providers". SEM's guidance treats internats as full-time schools, and Vaud's checklist treats residence status as decisive for the evidence package. A boarding school should therefore be documented as an integrated educational-and-pastoral structure: school recognition, full-time timetable, residence confirmation, pastoral/supervision arrangements, named contact point, and confirmation that the child will in fact live on campus. That package is stronger than a simple school admission letter plus separate rental arrangement. 3

**Host** **family** **or** **guardian** **evidence.** The reviewed materials do not justify a blanket statement that every minor student must have a formal Swiss guardian. What they do justify is a conservative requirement for a specific, documentary adult-care arrangement where the child is not fully resident in an internat. Vaud demands a custody-entrustment document signed by the legal representative for non-residential minor students. Geneva requires formal host/landlord documentation where the student is housed by another person. And Vaud's child-hosting brochurewarns that a family taking a child into day-and-night care is subject to authorisation and state supervision. For internal drafting, the better proposition is therefore: a host-family arrangement may be viable, but only if the host arrangement is formally documented and checked for any child-placement authorisation issues under the relevant canton's child-protection regime. 26

**Parental authority, parental consent and civil-status evidence.** No single federal migration document located publishes an exhaustive "minor student" list of family-law documents. The public materials do, however, point unmistakably towards the need to identify the legal representative and to substantiate custody/care arrangements. Vaud requires the legal representative's custody-entrustment document; Zurich's passport-office form states that minors need the consent of both holders of

parental authority for identity-document applications when both are not present. The conservative internal filing position should therefore be to collect, where applicable, the child's birth certificate, both parents' identification, notarised or otherwise formal consents from each holder of parental authority, and any custody judgment, divorce/separation order, death certificate or guardianship/curatorship instrument needed to explain why one adult can decide alone. That is a drafting recommendation grounded in the evidence pattern, not a claim that everycanton publishes the same mandatory checklist. 27

**Welfare** **and** **safeguarding** **concerns.** Swiss migration practice, as visible from the sources reviewed, is sensitive to welfare even where the file is formally a study-residence application. SEM tells schools to supervise students closely and report serious, repeated problems; communes need the actual residence address; and cantonal child-placement materials treat prolonged day-and-night hosting of children as a supervised activity. For minors, especially younger boarders, a thin file that proves admission and funding but says almost nothing about daily care, emergency contacts, pastoral arrangements, medical decision-making and holiday arrangements is therefore legally fragile. 28

**Financial support, accommodation and insurance.** Here the routes diverge sharply. Under AFMP/ ALCP,sufficient means and all-risks sickness insurance are express treaty conditions; Swiss guidance assesses means by reference to Swiss welfare thresholds and extends student permits annually while those conditions continue. Under Article 27 AIG for third-country nationals, the current federal and cantonal materials focus more on proof of means, school fees, accommodation and temporary purpose than on insurance as a separately foregrounded admission criterion, although health-insurance compliance remains a practical Swiss residence issue after arrival. Geneva's published thresholds are useful as canton-specific benchmarks but should not be universalised into federal law. 29

**Travel and** **visa** **sequencing.** The safest internal formulation is: first obtain or seek the cantonal residence authorisation for studies; then, if the child's nationality requires it, obtain the national visa D for entry; and in ordinary cases await that outcome abroad before travel. Vaud says exactly that the student must wait abroad for the visa decision. FDFA explains that the national visa D is issued subject to cantonal migration authorisation. SEM's nationality pages add that even visa-exempt nationals ordinarily need the residence process started before entry for stays over 90 days, and Zurich's guidance treats in-country filing after visa-free arrival as exceptional rather than routine. 30

**Renewal and** **school attendance.** For minors, renewal should be treated as a live supervisory checkpoint rather than a mere extension request. SEM requires actual examination progress within a useful period and allows non-renewal where that does not happen. Geneva says changes of school or study orientation require a new application and immediate notification with reasons. AFMP students renew annually while conditions persist; third-country permits are likewise presented by the cantons as annual, temporary documents linked to the approved plan. Boarding-school files should therefore preserve attendance records, residence confirmations, reports of conduct where needed, next-year enrolment letters and updated funding/accommodation evidence. 31

### Drafting cautions and source list

The first caution is conceptual: do not describe Swiss minor study residence as a single "student visa" regime. Internally, the system should distingJuish clearly between the **substantive** **residence** **basis** and the **entry-clearance** **step.** For EU/EFTA, the residence basis is treaty-based and rights-oriented if the conditions are met. For third-country nationals, the residence basis is Article 27 AIG/VZAE and remains discretionary even where the evidence is strong. 32

<!-- 7 -->

The second caution is evidential: do not assume that "boarding school" automatically answers the supervision issue unless the documents show that the child will actually reside there and that the school provides organised supervision. Conversely, do not assume that an off-campus host family can simply be described as "guardian" without checking the legal basis, the scope of the host's authority, and whether any canton-level child-placement authorisation or supervision questions arise. 33

The third caution is drafting language around entitlement. For third-country minors, avoid formulations such as "is entitled to a study permit once admitted by the school". That is too strong. A safer formulation is that the canton **may** **grant** residence under Article 27 AIG where the education,means, accommodation, temporary-purpose and minor-supervision elements are convincingly established. SEM's own guidance records that Article 27 does not confer a legal entitlement. 6

The fourth caution concerns family-law documents. Because no single federal public source located sets out a complete minor-student family-law checklist, the internal system should frame civil-status and custody evidence as a **conservative** **best-practice** **bundle** rather than an exhaustively codified federal list. It should prompt for the documents needed to prove who may validly consent and who will exercise care in Switzerland, especially where one parent is absent, custody is shared, or the child will live outside a boarding school. 27
