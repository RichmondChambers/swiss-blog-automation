# Swiss Client-Site Work Route Classification

## Authority Pack

Prepared for internal use by an immigration law content system. Jurisdiction: Switzerland.The analysis below is intentionally conservative and focused on route selection, evidential risk, and official authority rather than commercial drafting or general labour-leasing doctrine.

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not treat all client-site work alike. The starting point is whether the activity in Switzerland is a form of **gainful activity** at all; Swiss migration guidance interprets that concept broadly and focuses on whether the activity would normally be remunerated on the Swiss labour market, not simply on whether the individual is paid in Switzerland or by whom. Business meetings without productive work can fall outside gainful activity, but once the individual is carrying out substantive work in Switzerland for a Swiss-facing project, the case normally moves into a work-authorisation analysis. 1

For route classification, the most important dividing line is usually between **genuine** **service** **delivery** / **posting** and **labour** **leasing.** Official Swiss guidance defines posting as a foreign employer sending its own worker to Switzerland to perform services **in** **the** **employer's** **name** **and** **for** **its account,** while the employment contract remains with the foreign employer. By contrast, labour leasing exists where the worker is placed at another undertaking's disposal and the Swiss user enterprise receives the material right to direct the work; Swiss authorities stress that contract labels such as "work contract", "service agreement", or "secondment" are not determinative if the facts show subordination to the Swiss client. 2

**For EU/EFTA** providers, genuine cross-border service provision can often use the **notification** route for up to **90** **effective** **working** **days** **per** **calendar** **year,** but only for specific categories: posted workers from an EU/EFTA-based undertaking, self-employed EU/EFTA service providers, and certain short Swiss employments by Swiss employers. The notification route is therefore **not** a general permission for any client-site work. It does not legalise labour leasing from abroad, and some sectors require notification from the first day. If the work exceeds the 90-day / three-month threshold, a permit is required; outside certain special sectoral agreements, there is then no AFMP-based right to admission and national-law discretion applies. 3

**For third-country** **nationals,** client-site work is generally analysed under national law. Where there is genuine cross-border service delivery by a foreign employer, SEM guidance places the case under **AIG** **Article** 26 and requires a permit application supported by a project contract, project description, and a detailed secondment confirmation. Zurich's official materials distinguish between **firmeninterne** **Entsendung** and **externe** **Entsendung** and treat third-party client work as the latter. Where the Swiss entity is the real or factual employer, the analysis should move to **local Swiss** **employment** rather than service provision. 4

The highest-risk misclassification is **staff** **augmentation** **dressed** **up** **as** a **project.** SECO guidance treats labour leasing as present where the Swiss client directs the work, integrates the worker into its

organisation, provides the means of work, and the supplier is essentially billing labour time rather than taking responsibility for a defined result. Cross-border labour leasing from abroad into Switzerland is prohibited, including indirect variants in which a supposed service provider sends workers it has itself borrowed from a leasing firm. That prohibition is especially important because it means some client-site structures are not merely "wrong route" cases; the route may be legally unavailable in the first polace. 5

Swiss official materials are strong on the extremes-clear postings, clear labour leasing, and clear local hires-but thinner on mixed modern delivery models such as agile implementation teams, embedded engineers, and managed-service consultants working day to day from a Swiss customer site. In those mixed cases, the safest approach is to treat route selection as **fact-sensitive** and to document, with precision, who instructs the worker, who bears performance risk, what deliverable is owed, whose tools and systems are used, and whether the Swiss client is receiving a project result or simply additional labour capacity. 6

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

## Primary legal texts

The core primary instruments for this topic are the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons** with the EU, especially **Article 5 AFMP and Articles 17-23 of Annex I,** together with the **Verordnung** **über** **den** **freien** **Personenverkehr** / **VFP** (FMIO), which governs the EU/EFTA notification and permit framework for service providers. Official federal pages on posted workers and SEM's AFMP materials identify those provisions as the controlling treaty layer for EU/EFTA cross-border service provision. 38For national law, the key immigration instruments are the **AIG** and the **VZAE.** In route-selection terms, the crucial provisions are those on **gainful activity, local employment, cross-border service** **provision, self-employment, short stays, salary and working conditions,** and **employer / recipient** **obligations.** Canton Basel-Stadt's official posting poage and SEM's legal-bases materials both list AIG and VZAE as central sources, while SEM's AIG guidance operationalises Articles 11, 18, 19, 22, 26, 91 and related ordinance provisions for employment-related cases. 39

<!-- 10 -->

The labour-market and posted-worker layer is made up of the **Posted** **Workers** **Act** **(EntsG)** and the **Posted** **Workers** **Ordinance** **(EntsV).** These do not answer every immigration-route question, but they are highly relevant because the short-term notification system for EU/EFTA postings sits in this framework, and because EntsG supplies the minimum-conditions, inspection, chain-liability, and sanctions rules that become relevant once a case is properly classified as a posting. Official posted-worker pages expressly identify EntsG and EntsV as governing the working and pay conditions of posted workers in Switzerland. 40

Where labour leasing is in issue, the controlling legislation is the **Arbeitsvermittlungsgesetz** **(AVG)** and the **Arbeitsvermittlungsverordnung** **(AVV).** These are the source of the Swiss prohibition on direct labour leasing from abroad, the permitting regime for Swiss-based labour-leasing businesses, and the doctrinal framework for distinguishing leasing from a genuine work or service contract. SECO identifies AVG and AWV as the legal basis for private employment services and labour leasing, and SEM's AIG guidance cross-refers directly to that regime when discussing foreign labour supplied to Swiss user enterprises. 41

## Official guidance and admninistrative materials

The most important federal administrative source is **SEM's** **AIG** **guidance, Chapter** 4 **"Aufenthalt** **mit** **Erwerbstätigkeit",** updated to **1** **January** **2026.** It is the strongest official source on the broad definition of gainful activity, third-country service providers, evidence required for secondment cases, labour-leasing cross-references, and the factual-employer concept under Article 91 AIG. 42

For EU/EFTA cases, the key administrative source is **SEM's** **VFP** **guidance,also** current to **2026.** It covers the AFMP notification route, calculation of days, permit treatment above 90 days, anti-circumvention concerns such as Swiss letterbox entities, and the position of EU/EFTA workers in the labour-leasing context. 43

SEM's public-facing materials are also important because they state the route rules in operational terms. The most useful are the **notification procedure page, the AFMP FAQ,** and **the fact sheet on cross-border** **service** **provision.** Together they confirm the eligible categories for notification, the counting of the 90-day limit, the exclusions from service liberalisation, and the absence of an AFMP right for non-special-agreement services above 90 days. 44

On the labour and posting side, the official **posting.admin.ch** **/ entsendung.admin.ch** portal is central. It provides the ordinary-language definition of posting, the legal-source map, work-permit screening concepts, and the sanctions overview. Even where its pages are concise, they are useful because they express the federal administration's own distinction between posting and labour leasing and link route classification to the applicable labour-market obligations. 45

Finally, **SECO's 2024** **instructions** on **AVG/AVV** and its labour-leasing leaflet are essential for route selection whenever a client-site deployment could be staff augmentation. Those materials are the clearest official statement that contract nomenclature is not decisive, that subordination and

operational integration matter, and that cross-border labour leasing from abroad is prohibited, including indirect forms. 46

# Cantonal practice notes and practical legal analysis

## Cantonal practice notes

**Canton** **Zurich** is the most useful canton for route-selection evidence because its official materials are unusually explicit. Zurich's employer flow-chart distinguishes **Lokalanstellung** from **Entsendung,** and its supporting PDFs then distinguish between **external secondment** **to** **a** **third** **company** and **firm-internal secondment.** For external client-site cases, Zurich requires the Swiss client / assignment contract, the project description, and a detailed secondment confirmation or secondment contract. For internal group assignments, Zurich removes the third-party contract element and focuses on the internal host-company relationship. 47

Zurich's practice notes are also useful because they show what an authority wants to see when the route is being tested. The required evidence is not merely proof of identity and qualifications; it is proof of the **commercial** **and** **operational structure** of the assignment: what the project is, who the Swiss counterparty is, what the role is, where the work is done, how long it lasts, how the worker is paid, and who bears posting expenses and insurance obligations. That evidential design strongly suggests that Zurich is testing for genuine service delivery rather than bare labour supply. 48

**Canton** **Geneva** provides a clear operational rule-set for EU/EFTA and third-country service providers. For EU/EFTA undertakings or self-emmployed providers, Geneva splits the process at 90 **days** **/** **three** **months**: notification below that threshold, cantonal authorisation above it. Geneva also states expressly that this route does **not** extend to **direct** or **indirect** **labour** **leasing** **from** **abroad,** which is a valuable cantonal confirmation of the federal position. For third-country undertakings or self-employed providers, Geneva requires a canton-level authorisation from the outset and does not permit the work to start before authorisation is granted, save for limited entry-authorisation cases. 49

**Canton** **Basel-Stadt** is less detailed on classification, but its official posting page is still useful because it defines posting as temporary work in Switzerland **on** **behalf** **of** **a** **company** **based** **abroad,** while the worker's residence remains abroad, and it lists the principal federal legal instruments that govern route and compliance analysis. Basel therefore works well as a concise canton-level confirmation of the federal legal architecture. 50

Taken together, the cantonal materials support three conservative propositions. First, cantons expect route selection to be backed by **project-level evidence,** not broad assertions. Secondly, cantons do distinguish between **internal group assignments and external customer projects.** Thirdly,where cantons mention labour leasing expressly, they treat it as a separate and potentially prohibitive issue, not as a mere variation of posting. 51

## Practical legal analysis

The most reliable practical test is to ask: **what has the foreign supplier promised the Swiss side? If** the foreign undertaking has promised a defined project, deliverable, installation, implementation, specialist task, or other concrete service result, and it remains responsible for delivering that result with its own staff, the case points towards **service** **provision** **/** **posting.** If, instead, the Swiss client is effectively buying access to named individuals whose day-to-day tasks it assigns and supervises, the case points towards **labour** **leasing** **or** **local employment.** SECO's official indicators-subordination,

<!-- 12 -->

client tools, time-based charging, and absence of result responsibility-support this way of framing the question. 52

A second practical question is**: who exercises the material right of instruction?** Official Swiss materials do not insist that the foreign employer must control every physical aspect of the worker's day; they do, however, treat the transfer of essential instruction rights to the Swiss user enterprise as a hallmark of labour leasing. That means ordinary site coordination, access rules, safety rules,and deliverable acceptance by the Swiss client are not the decisive issue. The decisive issue is whether the Swiss client is functionally managing the worker as part of its own operations. This is an inference from the official indicators, and it should be presented as such in internal content. 26

A third question is**:** is **the worker integrated into the Swiss client's organisation?** SECO's guidance says leased workers are embedded with the user enterprise, subject to its instruction and control, and usually work with the user enterprise's means of production, while the user enterprise bears the performance risk for the work done. By implication, extensive use of the Swiss client's premises, systems, tools, and management routines is not legally neutral. It is a route-selection risk factor when combined with client-led tasking and weak deliverable responsibility on the supplier side. 26

**The** **intra-company** **versus** **customer-project** divide should be handled separately. Zurich's official approach is especially instructive: internal secondment means temporary work in the Swiss branch, subsidiary, or office of the same business, whereas external secondment means the worker is temporarily carrying out a project for the foreign employer at **the Swiss** **customer's** site, under a contract between the Swiss customer and the foreign employer. A case should therefore not be described as "intra-company" merely because the foreign employer belongs to a multinational group. The Swiss host must itself be the internal group entity receiving the work. 15

**For** **self-employed** cases, the official materials are deliberately sceptical. SEM warns about sham self-employment, and Zurich requires proof of genuine independent business activity, including evidence of invoices, own equipment, liability insurance, customer relationships, and who bears materials, accommodation, and damage risk. The practical imnplication is that "self-employed" should be treated as an evidence-heavy route, particularly where the person will work at a Swiss client site for a single Swiss counterparty. 21

**On** **Swiss** **client** / **recipient** **obligations,** the AIG guidance makes an important distinction. If a Swiss person or company directly receives a cross-border service from abroad, it must verify that the foreign workers are authorised to work in Switzerland. If, however, the Swiss customer contracts with a **Swiss** provider on a work or service contract, there is no statutory permit-check duty under Article 91(2) AIG as described in the SEM guidance, though the guidance still recommends checking in practice. This is a narrower immigration-law point and should not be confused with wider due-diligence or subcontractor-liability rules under labour law. 53

**The** **misclassification** **consequences** are material. At one level, the wrong route may simply mean that the worker has no right to start work until a permit is granted, or that the case falls outside any AFMP-based entitlement above 90 days. At a higher level, if the facts amount to laboour leasing from abroad, the structure may be prohibited altogether. In parallel, route errors can create illegal-employment exposure under the AIG framework and EntsG sanctions risk in posted-worker cases, including fines and service bans. 54

### Drafting cautions

Internal content should **never** say or imply that "client-site work up to 90 days is permitted by simple notification". The official rule is narrower: the notification system is available only to certain EU/EFTA route categories, and the same online system is also used for some short **Swiss** **employments** by Swiss employers. Notification therefore does not, by itself, tell you whether the case is cross-border service provision, local employment, or something impermissible. 55

Internal content should also **never** suggest that calling the contract a "service agreement", "statement of work", "project contract", or "secondment" is enough to settle the route question. Swiss official guidance is explicit that labels do not decide classification. Safer drafting should say that authorities look at the contract, the project description, the role, the real reporting line, the day-to-day working conditions, and the actual allocation of instruction and performance risk. 25

Where client-site work is described as **intra-company,** the content system should require the drafter to state **which** **Swiss** **group** **entity** is the host and whether any **third-party** **Swiss** **client** is also receiving deliverables. If a third-party Swiss client is the real recipient, Zurich's official framework points away from an internal-transfer description and towards external service delivery or another route. 15

Content should distinguish carefully between **foreign** **employer** **control** and **Swiss** **client** **coordination.** A cautious formulation is: client-site work may fit a service-delivery route where the foreign employer remains responsible for a defined service result, keeps the employmnent relationship, and documents the assignment through a project contract and secondment confirmation; if the Swiss client directs the worker as part of its own operations, labour-leasing or local-employment issues arise. That formulation is conservative because it tracks the official indicators rather than overstating bright-line rules. 56

Where official materials specify evidence, internal templates should capture the same facts. At minimum, the system should prompt for the **Swiss contract counterparty, project description, wvork** **location, assignment duration, worker's role, baseline salary, Swiss allowances, expense handling,** **social-insurance position, insurance cover,** and **reporting line. A host letter or client confirmation** is not expressly listed in the federal sources as a standalone mandatory document, but it is a sensible internal supporting document because it can record preciselythe facts that the authorities repeatedly ask to see. That last point is an internal drafting recommendation drawn from the evidence demands in the official materials. 13

Finally,mixed cases should be drafted in **probabilistic** **and** **escalatory** terms, not as absolutes. Official Swiss materials themselves say that the distinction between work / service contracts and labour leasing can be difficult and that the classification is often case-specific. Internal content should therefore avoid categorical statements such as "embedded project staff remain posted workers as long as the foreign employer pays them". That is too strong for the official materials and creates avoidable compliance risk. 36
