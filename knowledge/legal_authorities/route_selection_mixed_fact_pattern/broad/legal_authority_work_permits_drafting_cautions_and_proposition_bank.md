# Swiss work-permit drafting cautions and legal proposition bank

## Executive legal summary

This pack is an internal Swiss authority note for route selection, drafting control, and automated content guardrails. The safest starting point is that Swiss work-authorisation analysis does **not** begin with the label attached to the trip or assignment. It begins with six variables: nationality, employer or contracting entity, nature of the activity, duration, place of work, and whether the arrangement is a local hire,a posting, self-employed service provision, or a staffing/labour-leasing model. Swiss official guidance treats the concept of gainful activity broadly and objectively: an activity can count as work even if it is unpaid, short, or described as a business, volunteer, or project visit. Borderline cases are decided case by case by the competent cantonal labour-market authority, with SEM deciding doubt cases under the ordinance framework. 1

For EU/EFTA nationals and qualifying EU/EFTA-based service providers, Switzerland appliesa more liberal regime under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, but that regime is still route-specific and procedural. It distinguishes sharply between local employment in Switzerland,posted workers, and self-employed service providers. A notification route may exist for certain short assignments, but notification is not the same as permit approval, and the liberalisation does not cover all sectors or all models. Official materials state expressly that staffing/job placement and staff leasing remain outside the relevant liberalisation, and services beyond the 90-day threshold generally move back into permit analysis without a general legal entitlement. 2

For third-country nationals, the baseline regime is much stricter. Switzerland follows a dual system: EU/ EFTA workers benefit from easier access under free movement rules, whereas other nationals are admitted only on a complementary basis, usually as highly qualified workers and subject to overall economic interest, annual quotas, precedence for workers already integrated into the Swiss/EU/EFTA labour market, and scrutiny of salary and working conditions. SEM's public guidance also makes clear that a Swiss job offer alone does not authorise a start date. 3

The strongest drafting risk areas are these: conflating business visits with work; stating or implying that "under 90 days" means no work-authorisation risk; assuming that unpaid activity, foreign payroll, or a foreign employment contract removes Swiss work-permit issues; treating intra-group assignments as automatically exempt; treating EOR/payrolling as a neutral immigration solution; and presenting cantonal practice as if it were uniform federal law. On remote and hybrid work, official materials exist but remain highly fact-sensitive; they must not be generalised beyond the precise scenarios discussed by SEM. 4

## Structural overview of drafting-control principles

For internal drafting purposes, the most useful structural divide is between **work** **classification** and **route** **consequences.** First ask whether the activity is work at all under Swiss immigration law. Only then ask which route, if any, may apply: notification, local-hire permit, posted-worker permit, self-employed service route, frontier-worker route, or a non-work residence category. Swiss official guidance is explicit that gainful activity is interpreted broadly, that duration is not determinative of whether work exists, and that short duration affects route mechanics only after classification. 5

A second essential divide is between **employment with taking up a post in Switzerland** and **work** **without taking** **up** **a** **post.** SEM's directives treat work for an employer seated in Switzerland, or in a Swiss establishment of a foreign company, as work "with taking up employment"; by contrast, self-employment and work for an employer seated abroad are treated as work "without taking up employment", including cross-border service providers such as salespersons, installers, and exhibitors. This distinction is a major drafting control, because it helps separate local hire from cross-border service provision. 6

A third divide is between **immigration** **status** **and** **other** **legal frameworks.** Notification can coexist with posted-worker wage rules, social-security issues, tax effects, labour-leasing rules, and professional-qualification checks. SEM's home-office circular expressly states that immigration-law classification does not control tax or social-security classification, and the SEM notification page adds that certain regulated professions require a separate professional-qualification notification to the competent authority. Automated content should therefore avoid treating "Swiss work permit" as the only relevant legal layer. 7

The recommended internal label set is as follows. A **clear** **rule** is a proposition stated in legislation or repeated consistently in current SEM/SECO official guidance. A **qualified** **rule** is a proposition that is broadly true but dependent on route conditions, exclusions, or thresholds. A **practice** **point** is an administrative or cantonal operational point that is useful in drafting control but not safe to universalise as nationwide hard law. An **uncertain** point is one wvhere public official materials are thin, highly factual, or expressly case-by-case. That final category is particularly important for business-visit boundaries, hybrid work, self-employment genuineness, and intra-group labour leasing.

When in doubt, Swiss official materials point back to the canton. SEM's directives say that the distinction in concrete cases must be made by the cantonal authority competent for labour-markket admission, and doubt cases shouldI be submitted to SEM. That means automated content should default to restrained phrasing and escalation, not confident route promises, where the fact pattern contains mixed Swiss and foreign elements.8

### Primary legal texts and official administrative materials

The core federal legal framework is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Gainful Employment. Those texts underpin the public SEM guidance on work with and without taking up employment, labour-market admission, and short-duration rules. English translations exist for information, but Refworld reproduces the official warning that the English FNIA text does not have legal force and that legally operative versions are the German, French, and Italian texts on Fedlex. The same practical caution applies to the OASA translation. In addition, official publication records show further 2026 amendment activity, so current drafting should always be checked against the latest official-language consolidated text on Fedlex rather than relying only on an English convenience copy. 29

<!-- 11 -->

The international-law backbone for EU/EFTA nationals is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, together with its annexes and implementing rules. Official SEM and SECO materials emphasise that the AFMP does **not** give a general right to come to Switzerland to provide any kind of service. Its service liberalisation is partial, limited to specific categories and durations, and explicitly leaves staffing/ job placement and staff leasing outside the liberalised field. Swiss drafting should therefore avoid simplifying AFMP service rights into a generic "90-day business mobility"rule. 30

Two further federal regimes matter repeatedly in mixed work-permit fact patterns. First, the Posted Workers Act and its ordinance govern Swiss minimum wage and working-condition protections for posted workers and interact with the immigration notification/permit system. Secondly, the Employment Services Act and its ordinance regulate private employment agencies and staff leasing; in practice they are central whenever a service model starts to resemble labour hire, payrolling, EOR, or a client-directed secondment. For drafting control, this means that "immigration route" analysis is incomplete if the fact pattern suggests posting or labour leasing. 31

The most useful public SEM materials for route drafting are the pages on working in Switzerland, non-EU admission criteria, the notification procedure, the EU/EFTA service-provider factsheet, and chapter 4of the AIG directives. Those materials provide the best official public explanations of the broad work concept, the difference between local employment and cross-border service work, 8-day and 90-day thresholds, posted-worker conditions, and the non-EU evidential burden. They should be treated as primary administrative guidance for automated content controls. 32

Particularly important specialised SEM materials include the project staff/service-provider appendix,the circular on religious activities, and the circular on home office. These are not broad statutory restatements; they are targeted administrative clarifications. For that reason they are highly valuable in internal drafting, but the propositions drawn from them should usually be labelled as **qualified** **rule** or **practice** **point,** not as universal black-letter law. 33

### Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal practice should be treated as operationally important but not universal federal law. Zurich's official guidance for EU/EFTA posted workers says that work of up to 90 days per calendar year can be permit-free but still notified, while work beyond 90 days requires a permit before the activity starts; Zurich also states that extensions beyond the 90-day service period are approved only in exceptional cases. For self-employed EU/EFTA nationals without Swiss residence who want to work beyond 90 days, Zurich says a permit is issued only if the activity serves Switzerland's overall economic interests, the person is a specialist, and foreign self-employment can be proven. 34

Geneva's guidance for EU/EFTA companies and independents offering services or posting staff into the canton draws the same broad federal split between notification up to 90 days and permit applications beyond that. It adds operational detail: work over 90 days should be filed 6 to 8 weeks in advance, work cannot begin before the permit is granted, and Geneva expressly excludes direct or indirect staff leasing from abroad from this route. Geneva also says the detached worker or independent must reside in

Switzerland during the mission; this should be treated as a canton-specific practice point, not a proposition to universalise nationally without further confirmation. 35

Basel-Stadt's guidance is operationally useful for notification mechanics. Its notification page states that each place of work must be notified separately, changes to existing notifications must be requested through the canton, and entirely new assignments require new notifications. Basel-Stadt's third-state posting guidance also restates that some sectors require authorisation from day one, while other third-state postings generally become permit-relevant once the eight-day threshold is exceeded. 36

Ticino's labour-market monitoring office provides two helpful practice points. Ticino also restates that posted third-country nationals of EU/EFTA companies must previously have been admitted to the regular labour market of that EU/ EFTA state for at least 12 months. 37

### Practical legal analysis

#### Business visit versus work

Swiss official materials support a narrow and conservative business-visit category. The AIG directives mention business meetings without gainful activity, exploratory contacts for possible contracts, and certain non-market activities as examples outside gainful activity. But the same directives stress that gainful activity is interpreted broadly, that the test is objective, and that the decisive question is whether the activity is one that is normally remunerated on the Swiss labour market. SEM's religious-activities circular reinforces the same approach: even activities that may appear temporary, ceremonial,or unpaid can still count as gainful activity if they go beyond mere attendance and resemble functions usually carried out for remuneration. The drafting consequence is simple: do not publish generic "business visitor" claims unless the fact pattern is genuinely limited to meetings or exploratory contacts. 38

#### Local hire, posting, intra-company transfer, and labour leasing

The safest federal distinction is between work for a Swiss employer or Swiss establishment, on the one hand, and work for an employer seated abroad, on the other. A genuine posting keeps the foreign employment contract in place. Once there is a new direct hire by a Swiss branch or establishment, SEM's project-staff guidance says the case should generally be treated as a new admission procedure. That same appendix says permits can be project-specific and that a project change can amount to a job change. In other words, route classification can change even where the worker, client, or canton appears unchanged. 39

Intra-company transfer status should also be handled with discipline. SEM's labour-market criteria page notes a limited exception to precedence for operational transfers of senior managers and essential specialists in international companies, but SECO's staffing guidance separately makes clear that Swiss law does not grant a general blanket exemption for intra-group staff leasing. That means "ICT" and "group secondment" are not safe drafting shortcuts. They may help explain a route, but they do not eliminate the need to analyse whether the case involves a new Swiss hire, a genuine posting, or staff leasing. 40

<!-- 13 -->

Labour leasing and disguised staffing are a major Swiss drafting hazard. SECO's guidance treats subordination to the Swiss client, client-side instructions, client tools, integration into the client's organisation, and remuneration by hours rather than a delivered result as classic signs that a supposed services arrangement may in reality be staff leasing. This matters because staff leasing from abroad into Switzerland is prohibited, including indirect forms, and because EOR/payrolling structures can themselves be classed as labour leasing. Any automated content that sees terms such as "managed service", "consultant", "vendor resource", "payrolling", "EOR", "assigned to client team", or "reports to Swiss manager" should default to manual review. 41

#### Remote work, home office, and mixed fact patterns

Remote work is one of the least suitable areas for broad automation. SEM's 2022 home-office circular says that, under the AFMP, EU/EFTA nationals working from Switzerland in home office for an employer seated abroad may in some cases be treated as non-economically active rather thanas workers in Switzerland, **provided** there is no direct link to the Swiss labour market and no Swiss customer support or acquisition activity. SEM even states that the mere physical presence of the EU/EFTA worker in Switzerland with a laptop does not by itself create Swiss worker status for AFMP purposes. 27

The circular then draws an important contrast for third-country nationals. Under the broader statutory employer concept used in Swiss third-country immigration law, work for an employer seated in Switzerland **or** **abroad** can fall within the definition. SEM states that any home-office activity with an effect on the Swiss labour market is always permit-relevant, and that new admission for third-country nationals who want to work in Switzerland only in home office for a foreign employer without Swiss labour-market impact should occur, if at all, under a residence category without gainful employment rather than through a work route. The same circular adds that immigration-law treatment does not settle tax or social-security treatment. This is why remote-work content should be drafted as fact-sensitive and heavily qualified. 27

#### Open questions and limitations

Public official materials remain thin, fragmented, or expressly fact-sensitive in at least four areas. First, there is no fully public, exhaustive, safe list of business activities that always fall outside gainful activity; the examples are illustrative, not comprehensive. Secondly, hybrid and remote arrangements with mixed Swiss and foreign elements are addressed mainly through circular practice, not through simple public hard rules. Thirdly, intra-group labour leasing remains a practice-heavy area in which federal staffing guidance warns against blanket exemptions. Fourthly, many operational points are cantonal and can differ in process, evidence, or informal expectations. In all four areas, automated content should be allowed to say only that individual legal analysis is recommended. 42

### Drafting cautions and prohibited wording

### Common overstatements to avoid

Do not say or imply that EU/EFTA nationals "do not need work authorisation" in Switzerland. The official position is more precise: some EU/EFTA work can proceed under notification or residence-registration formalities, but those formalities still matter, and they differ between local hires, posted workers, and self-employed providers. Equally, do not write that "under 90 days" or "under 8 days" means no Swiss work-permit issue. Those thresholds operate only within specific legal routes after the activity has already been classified. 43

<!-- 14 -->

Do not say that unpaid work, volunteering, religious activity, or family support is outside the rules.Swiss official guidance repeatedly takes the opposite approach and treats the concept of gainful activity objectively and broadly. Likewise, do not say that foreign payroll or a foreign employment contract avoids Swiss work-authorisation issues. SEM's rules for third-country nationals expressly use a broad employer concept that includes foreign employers, and the project-staff appendix makes clear that the real structure of the assignment matters more than who pays salary on paper. 44

Do not say that intra-company transfers, secondments, or EOR structures are automatically compliant. Swiss guidance distinguishes very carefully between limited immigration exceptions, genuine postings, and prohibited or regulated labour-leasing structures. Any wording that suggests that group membership or an outsourced payroll vehicle "solves" Swiss immigration should be blocked. 45

### Route-conflation risks

The most common route-conflation error is to mix up **notification, registration,** visa, and **permit** **approval.** These are not synonyms. A visa may still be needed in addition to a permit. A notification route is not a residence permit. Registration with the commune is not the same thing as labour-market authorisation. A third-country national may also need both work authorisation and visa issuance before travel, and a person holding a residence card from an EU/EFTA state still does not automatically gain Swiss work rights. 46

A second frequent error is to conflate local hire with posting. Local hire usually points to work for a Swiss employer or Swiss establishment and can require a different authorisation path from a genuine posting under a foreign contract. A third is to treat any client-site consulting arrangement as a service contract; Swiss labour-leasing guidance makes plain that client control, client tools, and integration into the Swiss client's business can change the legal character of the arrangement. 47

### Unsupported assumptions to avoid

Avoid assuming that a Swiss branch, host company, or group company may receive a foreign worker without changing route analysis. Avoid assuming that a seconded worker can move freely from one Swiss project to another under an existing approval. Avoid assuming that "independent contractor" status is accepted merely because the contract says so; SECO guidance expects proof of genuine self-employment and uses control-based criteria to distinguish independent work from staff leasing.Avoid assuming that remote work is neutral simply because it is performed from home. 48

#### Fact patterns requiring manual review

Manual review should be mandatory where any of the following appears in the brief or draft: third-country nationality; UK nationality or UK-based service mobility references; foreign employer plus Swiss client site; Swiss branch plus foreign payroll; project-based work with evolving scopes; client supervision or use of client systems; EOR, payrolling, staffing agency, or labour-hire language; "business trip" used to describe productive implementation work; unpaid, volunteer, religious, family-care, or internship activity; remote or hybrid work from Switzerland; regulated professions; construction, hospitality, cleaning, security, itinerant trade, sex industry, or landscaping; or work spread across more than one canton.These are all points where Swiss official materials either impose special rules or indicate that the classification is highly factual. 49

The following forms are conservative and reusable."Whether Swiss work authorisation is needed depends on nationality, employer location, activity, duration, and whether the arrangement is a local hire, a posting, or another cross-border service model." #### Internal warnings for automated blog generation

The automation layer should hard-block the following formulations unless a human reviewer expressly approves them after source checking: "permit-free", "no work permit needed because under 90 days", "business visitor", "no permit because unpaid", "foreign poayroll means no Swiss approval", "intra-company transfer exemption", "remote work is allowed", "EOR solution", "notification approval", "Swiss work rights from EU residence card", and "automatic approval". ### Federal legislation and treaty framework

The primary statutory backbone is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Gainful Employment. Refworld's official pages reproduce the warning that the English translations are informational only and identify the most recent translated statuses available there, while official publication records show further 2026 amendment activity, reinforcing the need to check current Fedlex consolidations in German, French, or Italian. 29

The international-law framework for EU/EFTA nationals is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. SEM's EU/EFTA service-provider factsheet is the most accessible official English summary of its limited service-liberalisation effects and exclusions. For practical drafting, it should be read together with the Swiss notification guidance and the implementing free-movement materials referenced by SEM. 52

The Posted Workers Act and Posted Workers Ordinance matter whenever foreign employers post staff into Switzerland. SECO's posting page explains the continuing employment relationship in a posting and the application of Swiss minimum labour standards to posted workers. 53

The Employment Services Act and its ordinance matter whenever a services model may in reality involve staff leasing, agency activity, payrolling, or an EOR structure. SECO's AVG/AWV manual is the leading official administrative source for those questions. 54
