# Swiss legal authority pack on unpaid work, trial work, shadowing and training in Swiss work-authorisation analysis

## A. Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law does not treat "unpaid" as a safe harbour. The baseline rule is that any foreign national who wishes to carry out gainful activity in Switzerland requires authorisation,regardless of how long the stay lasts. Swiss federal law then defines gainful activity broadly: it includes any employed or self-employed activity that is **normally carried out for remuneration, even if it is performed free** **of charge.** The implementing ordinance goes further and says that any activity for an employer in Switzerland or abroad counts as employed activity regardless of whether salary is paid in Switzerland or abroad, and regardless of whether the work is only hourly, daily, or temporary. It expressly lists apprentices, interns, volunteers, sportspeople, social workers, missionaries, religious workers, artists, and au pairs as forms of employed activity. 1

The decisive immigration question is therefore usually **not** whether the person is paid, or poaid adequately, or only receives expenses. The decisive question is whether the activity is of a kind that is normally remunerated on the Swiss labour market and whether it affects that labour market. SEM's work-authorisation guidance states this expressly, says the distinction is basedd on **objective** **rather** **than** **subjective** criteria, and explains that even work done wholly free of charge, or for only food and accommodation, may still be gainful activity for immigration purposes. Doubtful cases are for the competent cantonal labour-market authority, with SEM decidling if the case remains doubtful. 2

That said, not every visit involving a workplace is gainful activity. SEM's official annex on the concept of gainful activity distinguishes between, on the one hand, **purely** **theoretical** or **technical training** **without practical integration into the work process,** short business meetings, and passive attendance at conferences or workshops, and, on the other hand, **on-the-job** **training, productive** **familiarisation, trainee placements, project work, managerial supervision, maintenance work,** **and** **similar** **operational activity,** which it treats as gainful activity. Passive observation may therefore fall outside work authorisation rules, but only if it remains genuinely passive. The moment the visitor is integrated into a workflow, uses systems in a productive way, substitutes for staff, or delivers services to a Swiss recipient, the safer classification is work. 3

Trial workis treated narrowly. SEM states that probationary work can be permit- and notification-free only where it is genuinely part of a recruitment assessment, there is still no employment contract, it is not directed at producing output, and it does not exceed **half** a **day,** save in justified exceptional cases where it may extend to **one** **full working** **day.** Longer assignments are subject to permit or notification rules. SEM also states that the short-stay "120-day / 4-month" route may not be used for a probationary period. 4

Internships and traineeships usually fall on the "work" side of the line, even if unpaid. Federal law expressly names the intern and the volunteer as employed activity, SEM treats trainee placements and on-the-job training as gainful activity, and Geneva's official practice states that even an **unpaid**

**internship** requires work authorisation, with short EU/EFTA frontier internships under three months handled through the notification procedure and longer cases through the permit route. For internships tied to study, SEM and cantonal guidance remain strict: mandatory internships may fit theeducation route, but they still require prior immigration handling, and pre-admission internships are, as a rule, to be completed abroad unless a narrow exception is made. 5

EU/EFTA nationality changes the **route,** but not the need to classify the activity correctly. Under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the Swiss notification system, qualifying EU/EFTA workers and service providers may work in some short-term cases through **notification** **rather than** **a** **permit.** But that liberalisation only applies if the activity is correctly classified and the applicable notification conditions are met. Swiss-employer hires of up to three months require notification;posted workers and self-employed service providers are subject to the eight-day rule and, in certain sectors, to notification from day one; services beyond 90 working days require a permit. 6

The practical internal drafting position should therefore be conservative. Do not tell users that no salary means no work. Do not tell users that a short trial day, a "shadowing" visit, or an "onboarding" week is safe merely because it is short. Instead, analysisshould turn on the actual facts: whether the person is only observing, whether there is productive contribution, whether the activity is normally remunerated, whether the Swiss recipient derives operational benefit, whether the person is being trained or is training others, whether there is integration into normal operations, and which immigration route applies to the person's nationality and work pattern. 7

## B. Structural overview of unpaid work, trial work, shadowing, and training

The most reliable Swiss structure is to sort fact patterns into four buckets.

**Activities that are usually outside gainful activity** are those that remain genuinely non-productive and non-market in character: short business meetings, contract negotiations, preparatory visits, purely theoretical or technical courses without practical work, and passive attendance at conferences or workshops. SEM also treats certain personal or non-market activities outside gainful activity, and SECO notes that friendship services, neighbourly help, and usually mutual family help are normally outside notification and permit duties. 8

**Activities that are usually work for immigration purposes** are those that form part of an employer's or recipient's operations, or are of a type ordinarily remunerated on the labour market: internships, trainee programmes, volunteering in the ordinary sense, mission or religious service, on-the-job training, productive induction, project implementation, maintenance work, managerial feedback or supervision, and service delivery to Swiss clients or group entities. Federal law and SEM1 guidance deliberately cast this net broadly. 9

**Activities** **that** **are** **fact-sensitive** are trial work, shadowing, observation, onboarding, and practical training. These sit on the boundary because the same label may cover either a passive familiarisation visit or a productive placement. SEM's federal guidance allows only a very narrow permit-free trial-work exception. Cantonal labour-market materials from Zurich and Ticino are useful practical indicators: they both focus on whether the person is mostly watching and learning, whether there is independent work, whether training objectives are documented, and whether any employer benefit is only marginal. But those cantonal materials are not themselves universal federal immigration rules. 10

<!-- 2 -->

**Route consequences depend on nationality and work pattern once the activity is classified.** For third-country nationals, the basic permit rule applies from the outset, subject only to express exceptions or special routes. For EU/EFTA nationals and qualifying service situations, the AFMP and the Swiss notification regime liberalise some short-term work, but only within the specific notification framework. In all cases, work may begin only after the required notification or authorisation step has been completed. 11

## D.Primary legal texts

The key federal statute is the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** **Act.** Article 11 does the basic work: it imposes authorisation for gainful activity regardless of duration, and it expressly says gainful activity includes activity normally performed for remuneration even when carried out free of charge. Articles 18to 26 then provide the main admission architecture for third-country work cases: overall economic interest, employer application, labour-market priority, customary wage and working conditions, qualifications, accommodation, cross-border services, and special rules for frontier workers and certain teaching or religious functions. Article 27 governs admission for education, andArticle 30 allows certain exceptions or facilitations, including for international exchange and vocational education and training. 26

<!-- 7 -->

The main implementing ordinance is the **Ordinance** **on** **Admission, Stay** **and** **Employment.** Articles 1a to 4 are especially important for mixed fact patterns. Article 1a defines employed activity broadly, names internship and volunteering expressly, and removes any relevance from temporary form or foreign payroll. Article 3 defines cross-border services as a time-limited service in Switzerland under a contract performed by a person or undertaking based abroadd. Article 4 gives the cantons the first-instance power to classify whether conduct is gainful activity, with SEM deciding doubtful cases. Article 7 confirms that sectoral or professional practice permits do not replace immigration permission to work. Articles 12 and 14 then structure short-term and cross-border work, including the eight-day rule and the sectors where authorisation or notification is required from day one. 27

For education and training routes, the ordinance and SEM's guidance matter together. Article 38 and the related SEM guidance regulate ancillary work during studies and cap semester-time ancillary employment at 15 hours per week. Article 39 addresses full-time school programmnes with a **mandatory** internship and requires the internship to remain part of the course and not exceed half of the total training. Article 42 underpins the stagiaire route under Switzerland's trainee-exchange agreements. None of these provisions create a general rule that practical training is outside work authorisation; rather, they create specific, structured routes for certain training-related work. 28

For EU/EFTA nationals, the relevant treaty is the **Agreement** on **the** **Free Movement** **of Persons. Its** service-provider rule grants a right to provide a service for up to **90** **effective** **working** **days** **per** **calendar** **year,** and Switzerland implements that liberalisation through the notification system and the free-movement implementing ordinance. The route is therefore more liberal than the third-country system, but it remains a route for **work** rather than proof that the activity is not work. 29

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important administrative source is **SEM's** **AIG** **guidance, chapter** 4, which states that the concept of gainful activity is intentionally broad in the interest of a controlled admissions policy for labour from third countries. It explains that the relevant inquiry is whether the activity is one generally performed for remuneration on the Swiss labour market, not whether the individual personally needs the income, and says expressly that complete lack of pay or only a subsistence allowance does not prevent the activity from being gainful activity. It also explains that certain non-market activities remmain outside the concept and that the canton must classify borderline cases, referring doubtful matters to SEM. 15

SEM's annex on the **concept** **of gainful activity** is especially valuable for route selection. It draws the cleanest official line available between non-work and work in mixed fact patterns. On the non-work side, it places purely theoretical or technical training without practical work, seminars,school-like classroom teaching, participation in workshops, short business meetings, negotiations, information-exchange meetings, preparatory employment visits, and passive conference attendance. On the work side, it places on-the-job training and familiarisation integrated into the work process, trainee placements, repair and maintenance work, project deployments, artistic activity, and even recurring managerial performance or feedback meetings with staff in Switzerland. 3

SEM's same chapter and annex also provide the best federal guidance on **trial** **work** and certain forms of volunteering. Trial work is only exempt if it is truly evaluative and very short. Volunteering, by contrast, is generally treated as gainful activity in immigration law; the special exemption that SEM

<!-- 8 -->

recognises is narrow and limited to certain already-admitted refugees, temporarily admitted persons, protected persons, and stateless persons, under strict conditions of altruistic purpose, unpaid performance, and low average hours. That special carve-out should not be generalised to new entrants or ordinary "volunteer internships". 30

SECO's official materials are most helpful on **notification** **mechanics** and on identifying cases that are plainly outside the work sphere. SECO's black-work portal says that permit or notification issues only arise if there is gainful activity, and it expressly places friendship services, neighbourly help, and usually mutual family help outside the regime. Its EU/EFTA notification materials then explain who may use notification, the relevant sectors, the eight-day rule for posted workers and self-employed service providers, and the rule that the work may begin only after the required notification or permit step. 31

The SEM and SECO notification materials should be read together. SEM explains the categories eligible for notification and the deadline rules: Swiss-employer hires of up to three months require notification no later than one day before the first day of work, while posted workers and self-employed service providers generally require notification eight days in advance once the eight-day threshold is exceeded, and always from day one in the listed sensitive sectors. Servicesbeyond 90 working days require a permit. 32

## F. Cantonal practice notes

Official cantonal materials confirm the federal line that **unpaid** **does not** **mean** non-work.Geneva's OCPM states, for frontier EU/EFTA employment, that all **gainful activity, including** an **unpaid** **internship,** **requires** **work** **authorisation.** Its FAQ then adds that an unpaid internship in a Geneva undertaking still needs immigration handling: under three months it is handled by the short-duration notification procedure, and above three months through the work-authorisation route. Geneva also states, for student internships, that the application must be **filedwhether** **the** **internship** is **paid** **or** **unpaid,** and before the internship starts. 25

Zurich's Tripartite Commission has published a short labour-market note on **Schnupopern, Probearbeit** **and** **Praktika.** It is not a federal immigration text, but it is a useful practical indicator. It defines "Schnuppern" as getting to know the profession or business rather than providing labour, limits it to one to five days, and says the activity should mainly consist of watching, trying things out and getting to know the team, with **no** **independent performance** **of** **work.** It defines trial work as work done in a recruitment process before the employment contract is concluded, limits it to up to two days, and again says that the activity should mainly be observation and familiarisation rather than independent work. 33

Ticino's Tripartite Commission has issued practical criteria for determining whether a **stage** is a genuine internship. Again, this is primarily a labour-market/ wage-classification tool, not a federal immigration rule, but it is highly instructive for mixed fact patterns. It looks for documented training goals, identifiable supervisors, scheduled review discussions, a final certificate recording learning outcomes, non-repetitive work, hours aligned with training needs, no guaranteed recruitment, and-most importantly for immigration analysis-that the intern's work **must** **not** **have** **a** **profit-making** **purpose** **for** **the** **employer,** with any business benefit being only marginal. 34

These cantonal materials should be used carefully. Under VZAE article 4, the canton does decide borderline classification questions in the first instance, so cantonal practice matters. But Zurich's and Ticino's documents are not universal immigration safe-harbours. They are better treated as **supporting** **indicators** for the same underlying distinction found in federal law and SEM guidance: passive

observation and structured learning are easier to defend than productive contribution or operational integration. 35

## G. Practical legal analysis

**No salary does not remove work-authorisation risk.** The safest internal statement is that lack of salary is legally **non-decisive** in Swiss immigration law. If the activity is one normally performed for remuneration, or it feeds into a Swiss business's or recipient's operations, Swiss law can still classify it as gainful activity. This is especially important for "unpaid internship", "voluntary placement", "training week" and "shadowing" labels, which may sound non-commercial but often describe activities that the ordinance and SEM treat as work. 36

**Trial** **work** **and** **trial days.** There is a federal safe space for genuinely short, non-productive recruitment testing, but it is very narrow. SEM allows half a day, exceptionally one full day, where the purpose is only to test suitability for a specific post, there is still no employment contract, and the work is not directed at generating output. Once the trial extends beyond that, or becomes operationally useful, permit or notification issues arise. The 4-month/120-day short-stay route cannot be used to legitimise a probationary period. Zurich's labour-market note points in the same direction by insisting on a genuine recruitment process and on observation and trying-out rather than independent work. 37

**Shadowing** **and** **observation.** Swiss federal immigration materials do not create a general "shadowing exemption" by that name. The closest safe category is genuine observation or familiarisation with no productive contribution, no independent work, and no meaningful integration into the host's workflow. SEM's annex supports that in practice by exempting short business meetings, preparatory visits, and theory-only learning; Zurich's note similarly says "Schnuppern" should mainly be watching and getting to know the team. But the federal sources are thin on ordinary workplace "shadowing" beyond those analogies, so any hands-on tasks, substitution of staff, productive use of tools or systems, or regular hours should push the analysis toward work. 38

**Onboarding** **and** **induction.** "Onboarding" is not a legal category. The correct analysis is functional. If the visitor is in classroom-style training, attending information sessions, or receiving theory-only manufacturer or group training with no practical work, SEM's annex supports a non-work reading. If, however, the person is being familiarised "on the job", working on the company's systems, sitting in the operational team, or otherwise integrated into the work process, SEM explicitly classifies that as gainful activity. Many internal "induction weeks" will therefore be work for immigration purposes even if they are called training, and even if no separate salary is paid for that week. 3

**Training** **received** **versus** **training** **delivered.** This is one of the most important drafting distinctions.A person who comes to Switzerland only to **receive** theory-based or technical training may fall outside gainful activity if there is no practical integration into production or services. A person who comes to Switzerland to receive **on-the-job** training at Swiss workstations or systems is much more likely to be treated as working. A person who comes to Switzerland to **deliver** training, conduct workshops, instruct Swiss staff, or provide demonstrations to a Swiss recipient will usually be closer to cross-border service provision under VZAE article 3 and the AFMP notification framework. That last point is, in many concrete cases, a **reasoned** **inference** from the service-provider rules and SEM's operational examples rather than a single express federal "training delivery" rule; internal drafting should say so plainly. 39

**Workshops, demonstrations and knowledge transfer.** The federal materials clearly exempt passive attendance at workshops or conferences, especially where the participant does not present. They do **not** clearly exempt the active delivery of content. Internal content should therefore avoid saying that workshops or demonstrations are visitor-safe. Where a foreign national is engaged to present, instruct, audit, train, or transfer know-how in Switzerland for the benefit of a Swiss employer, client, affiliate,or project, the safer assumption is that the case raises at least notification issues and often permit issues. The same is true for senior managers who come repeatedly to conduct feedback meetings or fix objectives for staff in Switzerland: SEM treats even that activity as gainful activity in its annex. 16

<!-- 10 -->

**Internships and traineeships.** Internships usually require the strictest analysis because they are expressly named as employed activity. If the intern is a third-country national and the case is a normal Swiss-market placement, the standard third-country conditions may become difficult: employer application, economic interest, labour-market priority, customary wages and conditions, and qualifications. That is one reason an "unpaid internship" is often not easier but harder. Specific educational or exchange routes may exist, but they are tightly framed. Under the study route, the internship must genuinely be part of a recognised full-time programme and must not exceed half of the training. SEMM says pre-admission internships usually belong abroad. For individuals with completed training, SEM states that interns should be paid according to customary rates appropriate to their training level. Geneva's cantonal practice reinforces that both paid and unpaid study internships require prior handling. 40

**Volunteer-like** **activity.** Internal content should ddistinguish carefully between ordinary "volunteer work" and the narrow federal carve-out for certain already-admitted humanitarian statuses. Federal law and the ordinance expressly include volunteers within employed activity, and SEM says volunteering is in principle subject to permit or notification rules. The narrow exemption in SEM guidance applies only to certain recognised refugJees, temporarily admitted persons, protected persons and stateless persons already in Switzerland, and only where the work is unpaid, altruistic, and averages no more than six hours per week. New entrants from abroad for volunteer work, or "volunteer internships" failing those limits, remain within the work-authorisation frame. 41

**EU/EFTA** **route** **selection.** Once an activity is classedd as work, EU/EFTA nationality may simplify the route but does not eliminate the regulatory question. Swiss-employer hires of up to three months can generally proceed by notification; posted workers and self-employed service providers can work under notification up to 90 working days, subject to the eight-day rule and the day-one sectors; longer service deployments require a permit. This means that short unpaid internships, short internal secondments, or training delivery visits by EU/EFTA nationals may still require notification even if no permit is needed. Internal drafting should therefore separate the **classification** question from the **route** question. 42

**Evidence** **needed** **to** **classify** **the** **activity.** In future content, the following evidence should be requested or described as classification-critical: the written invitation or assignment letter;any employment contract, internship agreement or secondment letter; the exact duration and frequency of attendance; whether the person will perform tasks or only observe; who supervises them; whether they will access systems, tools, clients or production; whether there is a training plan with learning objectives; whether the activity is mandatory for a course; school confirmation if it is an educational internship; whether there is any salary, stipend, room-and-board or expense reimbursement; and whether the Swiss host expects an operational output. SEM's study and short-stay guidance repeatedly looks to written contracts, secondment confirmations and training documents for this purpose. 43

**Open** **questions and** **limitations.** Official federal materials are strongest on unpaid work generally, trial work, volunteering, study internships, business meetings and on-the-job training. They are materially thinner on ordinary corporate "shadowing", on active participation in workshops where the visitor is speaking or demonstrating, and on one-off internal observation visits by foreign staff. Those cases are therefore best described internally as **fact-sensitive** **and** **canton-sensitive,** not as settled safe harbours. 44

## H. Drafting cautions

Future internal content should avoid fiverecurring errors. First, it should never say or imply that **unpaid** **means** **no** **work.** That is contradicted by federal statute and ordinance. Secondly, it should never say that a short activity is safe merely because it is short. Duration affects the route, but it does not erase the need to classify the activity, and some short activities still require notification or permnit handling from the first day. Thirdly, it should not use employment-law labels such as "shadowing", "probation", "internship" or "volunteering" as if they decide immigration classification. Swiss immigration law looks to function, labour-market impact and operational integration. 45

Fourthly, content should distinguish **passive** **observation** from any **productive** **contribution.** A safe formulation is that observation-only visits may in some cases fall outside work authorisation rules, but only if the visitor is mainly watching, learning and attending, with no independent work and no significant operational output. The moment the person works on systems, supports projects, trains staff, carries out maintenance, or fills a labour need, immigration work rules are engaged. Zurich and Ticino materials can support those distinctions as practical indicators, but they should be described as cantonal practice points rather than universal federal rules. 46

Fifthly, content should distinguish **receiving** **training** from **delivering** **training.** Pure classroom-style receipt of theory-based training may be non-work; on-the-job training may itself be work; and delivering instruction or demonstrations in Switzerland will often be service provision or gainful activity.The source material is not equally explicit in all these variants, so the prudent drafting line is to mark active teaching, training delivery, demonstrations and operational knowledge transfer as requiring route checking rather than visitor-safe assumptions. 47
