# Swiss Work Authorisation Authority Pack forDirectors Board Members Shareholders

## Founders and Signatories

## A Executive legal summary

Swiss work-authorisation analysis is driven by **what the person actually does in or for Switzerland,** not by the corporate label they hold. Swiss immigration law requires a permit or other compliant route for gainful activity, and the concept of gainful activity is deliberately broad: for third-country nationals it covers any activity for an employer whether the pay is made in Switzerland or abroad and even if the work is only hourly, daily, temporary, or unpaid in the specific case; self-employment is defined by operating within one's own organisation, under one's own authority, with one's own entrepreneurial risk. The competent cantonal labour-market authority decides in the individual case whether a specific activity counts as gainful activity, and doubtful cases must be referred to SEM.1

The most important internal classification rule is therefore this: **passive** **ownership** is **not** **work;** **corporate status is not immigration permission; active management is usually work.** SEM's own guidance expressly states that no immigration claim can be derived merely from business formation or participation, and that even a title such as president or CEO, an employment contract, a commercial-register entry, or even sole signatory authority does not by itself prove either employment or self-employment. The analysis remains fact-sensitive and functional. 2

**For third-country** **nationals,** the default routes are strict. If the person is employed by a Swiss employer, the case is analysed as employment under Article 18 AIG, with the usual requirements of economic interest, employer sponsorship, labour-market priority, salary and conditions, and qualification level, subject to limited entrepreneur/investor and other exceptions in Article 23(3) AIG. If the person is genuinely self-employed, Article 19 AIG applies, and SEM guidance adds that the planned activity must have sustainably positive effects on the Swiss labour market; business plans,financing, operating setup, and an independent livelihood must be evidenced.3

**For EU/EFTA** **nationals,** the framework is more liberal but still activity-based. A person who actually takes up self-employment in Switzerland can obtain a residence permit under the free movement regime if they can prove a real and sustainable independent activity with a regular livelihood. CroSS-border service provision is also liberalised, but only **up** to **90** **effective** **wvorkdays** **per** **calendar** **year** and normally through the notification procedure; beyond that threshold, there is no AFMP-based right and Swiss domestic permit rules apply. 4

The safest conservative internal position for **directors, board members, founders, shareholders,** **beneficial owners, and persons with signing authority** is as follows. Merely holding shares, being the beneficial owner, receiving dividends, voting at shareholder level, or carrying out ordinary management of one's own assets is not, without more, gainful activity. By contrast, regularly directing staff in Switzerland, running the Swiss operation, negotiating and implementing business on the ground, signing and executing revenue-generating contracts in Swvitzerland, serving clients, or otherwise performing ongoing operational functions will normally require work-authorisation analysis as

employment, self-employment, or short-term service provision depending the facts. Official Swiss materials are comparatively thin on board-only attendance, so the classification of board meetings and strategic oversight should be stated with care and as fact-sensitive rather than as a blanket rule. 5

## B Structural overview of director board member and shareholder work-authorisation analysis

The structure of the analysis should start with a threshold question: is **the** **activity** in **or for** **Switzerland** **"gainful activity"** **at** all? Swiss law and SEM guidance answer that by looking to labour-market impact and the objective nature of the task, not to the person's own description of it. Activities that are ordinarily remunerated on the market are treated broadly as gainful activity even if the person receives no salary in the particular case. By contrast, some limited activities are expressly treated as non-gainful, including certain purely theoretical familiarisation tasks, punctual contacts to explore contractual opportunities, and business meetings without gainful activity. 1

The second question is **what** **legal role** **the** **Swiss** **activity** **occupies.** If the person works for a Swiss employer, Swiss authorities treat that as a Swiss employment case. If the person truly operates on their own account and risk, the case is analysed as self-employment. If the person remains employed abroad and performs a time-limited service in Switzerland, the service-provider rules may apply. SEM's directives stress that mixed indicators are common in founder and shareholder cases, so the authorities must weigh which features predominate in the specific file. 6

The third question is **whether a corporate-law title is merely evidential or actually reflects** **operational activity.** Swiss company law distinguishes between the shareholder/general meeting sphere and the management/representation sphere: the general meeting is the supreme body of a company limited by shares, while GmbH members are jointly responsible for management unless the articles provide otherwise, and each GmbH managing director may represent the company. Immigration law, however, does not equate those civil-law capacities with permission to work. SEM expressly warns that title, employment contract, register entry, and signatory authority do not settle the immigration classification. 7

The fourth question is **which** **nationality** **regime** **applies.** Third-country nationals generally need advance labour-market approval for Swiss employment or self-employment. EU/EFTA nationals may have AFMP rights for genuine employment or self-employment in Switzerland, and a separate notification route for some short cross-border services, but even under the AFMP the person must fit the correct category and the activity must be real rather than nominnal or sham. 8

## D to F Authorities and practiceD Primary legal texts

The core statutory framework is the **AIG** and the **VZAE.** Under AIG Article 11, work needs a permit unless an exemption applies; Article 14 allows the Federal Council to create lighter rules for temporary cross-border services; Article 18 regulates Swiss employment; Article 19 regulates self-employment; Articles 20 to 24 govern quotas, labour-market priority, salary and conditions, qualification level, and accommodation. Under VZAE Articles 1a to 4, employment, self-employment, and cross-border services are defined, and the canton decides whether the activity counts as gainful activity. VZAE Articles 6 and 7add two particularly important negative rules: business formation/participation creates no permit claim, and professionnal authorisation does not replace migration authorisation. 19

<!-- 5 -->

For **EU/EFTA nationals,** the governing framework is the **Agreement on the Free Movement of** **Persons** and the implementing **VFP/VEP** **practice** **materials.** SEM's VFP directives, applying Article 12Annex I AFMP, state that EU/EFTA nationals who enter Switzerland to take up genuine self-employment receive a five-year permit if they can already prove that self-employed activity. The same directives also explain the cross-border service regime: up to 90 workdays per calenddar year the activity is generally notification-based; beyond that there is no treaty-based right and domestic Swiss permit rules govern. 20

Swiss company law matters only as background evidence of **corporate** **capacity,** not as immigration permission. The general meeting is the supreme body of the Swiss company limited by shares, while GmbH management and representation areallocated to the managing directors, with at least one authorised representative resident in Switzerland. These powers can help identify what the person is actually doing, but they do not themselves answer whether Swiss work authorisation is required. 7

## E Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's **AIG** **directives** are the most useful official source for this topic. They expressly say the concept of gainful activity is broad and depends on whether the activity is ordinarily remunerated on the Swiss labour market. They also preserve a narrow non-gainful zone for ordinary asset management, punctual contract-exploratory contacts, and business meetings without gainful activity. Most importantly for founder/shareholder files, SEM says that self-employment is a case-by-case assessment, that mixed indicators may exist, that the relevant factors also apply to co-founders and shareholders, and that title, employment contract, commercial-register entry, and even sole signatory authority do not by themselves establish either employment or self-employment. 21

SEM's founder/self-employment guidance for **third-country nationals** is also especially important. It requires proof not only of the person's own qualifications but also of the new business's financial and operational viability and of **a sustainably positive impact on the Swiss labour market.** SEM lists diversification of the regional economy, job creation or preservation, significant investment, and generation of orders for the Swiss economy as relevant indicators, and expects a business plan with activity,financing, market, personnel, investment, turnover, and profit projections. SEM further indicates that initial permits for new businesses are usually limited to a first phase of up to two years and that renewal can be refused where the business-plan objectives are not met. 22

SEM's **VFP** **directives** **and** **notification** **page** are the key official materials for EU/EFTA route selection. They identify three categories that may use the notification route for up to three months or 90workdays in the calendar year: EU/EFTA nationals taking up a short Swiss employment, self-employed EU/EFTA service providers based in the EU/EFTA, and employees posted by an EU/EFTA company.They also specify the eight-day prior notification rule for self-employed service providers and posted workers, the sectoral first-day notification list, and the fact that over-90-day services require a permit with no treaty-based right. 23

SECO's materials matter mainly for **evidence** **and** **sham** **self-employment** **controls.** SECO requires self-employed EU/EFTA service providers, on request, to produce the notification or permit, A1 social-

## F Cantonal practice notes

Official cantonal materials broadly track the federal logic, but they are useful because they show what **evidence** authorities actually request. Zurich states that third-country nationals need a permit from day one for Swiss employment, and its work-permit guidance for third-country self-employment asks for material such as a business plan, planned balance sheet, information on loans, and lease documents for business premises; it also restates that self-employment must generate enough income to cover both business costs and living costs. 25

Geneva's official pages frame self-employment the same way: any foreign national who wishes to live in Geneva and pursue self-employed gainful activity needs the appropriate residence/work authorisation, with different tracks for EU/EFTA and third-country nationals. Geneva expressly notes that third-country nationals do not enjoy facilitated labour-market access and must demonstrate the economic interest of the application, with review by the cantonal labour-market authority. 26

Vaud and Basel-Stadt provide especially concrete filing clues. Vaud's form for EU/EFTA frontier self-employed persons asks for a lease of business premises, detailed administrative steps for launching the independent activity, a business plan, and proof of financial resources; Basel-Stadt provides separate pages and forms for third-country and EU/EFTA self-employed persons, points applicants to a business-plan checklist, and for EU/EFTA short services requires the provider to carry the notification or permit, A1, and contract documentation. These materials are practice notes, not legislation,and should be described as canton-specific implementation aids. 27

## G Practical legal analysis

### Passive ownership and beneficial ownership

**A** **shareholder,** **beneficial** **owner,** or investor who merely supplies capital, receives dividends, approves matters as owner, and undertakes no Swiss operational activity should normally be analysed as a **passive** **owner** **rather** **than** **a** **worker.** The best official support is SEM's statement that ordinary management of one's own assets is not gainful activity, together with VZAE Article 6(2), which denies any immigration claim from company participation alone. The official texts do not use the phrase "beneficial owner" as a standalone migration category, so this proposition is best presented as a conservative inference from the activity-based structure of Swiss work-authorisation law. 28

### Board members directors and strategic oversight

Where the person's Swiss presence is genuinely limited to **isolated** **board** **meetings, shareholder** **meetings, receipt of information, exercise of voting rights, high-level strategy discussions, or** **punctual contract-exploration** **meetings,** the best official analogy is SEM's category of business meetings without gainful activity and punctual contacts to clarify contractual possibilities. That supports a cautious position that **pure** **governance** **attendance** may sit outside gainful activity. However,the official materials found do **not** contain a bright-line rule stating that all board activity is permit-free,and they do not specifically exempt directors' or board members' functions as such. For internal drafting, this must therefore be described as a **qualified, fact-sensitive** **practice** **point,** not as a guarantee. 11

<!-- 7 -->

The position changes quickly once the board or director role becomes **operational.** If the director or board member is effectively managing local staff, taking day-to-day decisions, implementing projects, negotiating and executing client contracts in Switzerland, supervising delivery, or otherwise participating in Swiss operations, the case is much harder to characterise as a non-gainful meeting.At that point the broad Swiss concept of gainful activity is usually engaged, and the file must be classified under the correct substantive route: Swiss employment, self-employment, or short-term service provision. 29

### Persons with signing authority

**Signing** **authority** is **not** a **safe harbour.** SEM is explicit that title, CEO/president designation, employment contract, and entry in the commercial register-even with sole signatory powers-are not enough on their own to classify the person as employed or self-employed. In practice, signatory authority is therefore an **important evidential factor,** because it may indicate control and external authority, but it does not answer the migration question without examining the underlying Swiss activity. Internal content should never suggest that "because the person has sole signatory authority,no work permit is needed". 12

### Founders entrepreneurs and founder-employees

Founder cases commonly present mixed indicators. SEM's directives say explicitly that authorities must weigh the indicators case by case and that both employment-like and self-employment-like features may coexist. A founder who genuinely operates through their own organisation, under their own authority, absorbs entrepreneurial risk, and is not subordinate to another organ may be classed as self-employed. But a founder who is, in substance, hired by a Swiss company or parent-controlled structure, subject to instructions and organisational integration, may still be classed as an employee even if they hold shares or are registered as a director. 30

For **third-country** **founders,** Article 19 AIG is the key route if the activity is truly self-employment. SEM's official stance is that the business must show a sustainable positive impact on the Swiss labour market. Zurich, Basel-Stadt, and Geneva all reflect the same operational logic by requiring or pointing to business plans, financing evidence, business premises, and qualification material. This is why incorporation on paper is insufficient: Swiss authorities expect evidence of a real business capable of supporting itself and benefiting the Swiss economy. 31

**For** **EU/EFTA** **founders,** the route is easier, but still not automatic. SEM requires proofof actual and sustainable self-employed business activity in Switzerland, supported by business books and other documents. The authorities may revisit the file if there are serious doubts about whether the activity is genuine or sufficient to provide a livelihood. That makes superficial founder structures, dormant Swiss entities, or "paper" board positions unsafe from a work-authorisation perspective even for EU/EFTA nationals. 16

### Employment by the Swiss company

If the individual is **employed** **by** **the** **Swiss** **company,** the case should be analysed as employment even where the person is also a shareholder, director, or founder. Zurich's official guidance defines Swiss employment by reference to an employment relationship with an employer seated in Switzerland. For third-country nationals, that means a permit is needed from day one and the employer must apply; for EU/EFTA nationals, a short Swiss employment of up to three months may proceed via notification, while longer employment requires the appropriate residence or frontier permit. 32

<!-- 8 -->

#### Client-facing service delivery and revenue-generating activity

In internal route selection, **client-facing or revenue-generating activity performed in Switzerland** should normally be treated as work unless a clear non-gainful exception is available. This includes delivering services to Swiss clients, carrying out projects, installing systems, or performing operational tasks which affect the Swiss labour market. For EU/EFTA nationals, such activity may fall under the service-provider regime up to 90 workdays; after that, the AFMP route drops away. For third-country nationals, it generally falls under AIG employment or service-provider rules depending who employs the person and where the employer is based. 33

### Remuneration salary dividends and director fees

The **form of remuneration is relevant but not decisive.** Salary strongly supports an employment analysis, but Swiss law expressly does not make classification depend on where the salary is paid, and SEM treats ordinarily remunerated work as gainful activity even where the work is unpaid in the individual case. Conversely, dividends are consistent with passive investment, not active work, where the person is merely managing their own assets. The hardest area is director fees or board honoraria: the official materials located do not contain a dedicated black-letter rule, so the prudent internal position is that remuneration for the exercise of a board or organ function in Switzerland should trigger work-authorisation analysis rather than being assumed exempt. 1

#### Time spent in Switzerland and place of effective activity

Time in Switzerland matters in **two** **different** **ways.** First, as a matter of classification, even short or day-wise activity can still be gainful activity; Swiss law does not restrict the concept to full-time or long-term work. Secondly, time matters for route selection because EU/EFTA notification rules and 90-day service thresholds are time-based. This means that repeated brief visits by a foreign director can still amount to work, even if the immigration route, where available, is simplified. 34

**The Swiss place of management or operational activity** alsomatters as evidence. SEM's VFP directives warn that a Swiss-incorporated entity cannot be used merely to create the appearance of Swiss employment while the person remains effectively dependent on aforeign employer; the canton may examine whether the Swiss company has real infrastructure, management, office functions, and decision-making power. Likewise, proof of self-employment requires a genuine business establishment and effective activity in Switzerland, not only registration formalities. 35

### H and I Drafting cautions and source list

## H Drafting cautions

Internal content shouId state, plainly and repeatedly, that **ownership** **alone** **does** **not** **create** **a** **right** to **work in Switzerland** and that **company incorporation alone does not create immigration status.** That follows directly from the activity-based structure of AIG/VZAE and from VZAE Article 6(2). 9

Do not write that **board membership, shareholder status, founder status, or signing authority** automatically removes the need for work-authorisation analysis. SEM's official guidance says the opposite: the title and register position are not determinative. 36

<!-- 9 -->

**Treat board meetings and shareholder meetings** cautiously. State only that **some limited** **governance or business-meeting activity may fall outside gainful activity,** but that Swiss official materials are thin and fact-sensitive on this point. Once the activity shades into ongoing management, operational execution, or client delivery in Switzerland, permnission analysis is required. 11

Avoid promotional wording around "entrepreneur permits". The correct legal analysis is route selection between Swiss employment, self-employment, EU/EFTA free movement, and temporary service provision. For third-country founders, stress the labour-market and economic-interest test, not a supposed immigration entitlement based on business creation. 15

Where evidence is incomplete, insist on a factual matrix covering at least: nationality; actual employer; corporate office; ownership percentage; beneficial ownership; place of work; duties in Switzerland; frequency and duration of presence; remuneration type; signatory powers; reporting line; business plan and financing; commercial-register status; client-facing tasks; and whether services are rendered to Swiss clients or through a Swiss establishment. This evidence list is grounded in the federal and cantonal materials reviewed. 37

### Open questions and limitations

The official materials reviewed are **strong on activity-based classification1, founder/self-employment,** and **EU/EFTA service provision,** but they are **comparatively thin on express, board-specific** **immigration** **treatment.** In particular, no official source located here states in terms that attendance at board meetings or shareholder meetings is always permit-free. The safest internal approach is therefore to treat purely episodic governance attendance as a **qualified,** **fact-sensitive** **business-visit** **point,** and to escalate any case involving repeated presence, operational authority, client work, signatory execution in Switzerland, or remuneration for real board functions. 44
