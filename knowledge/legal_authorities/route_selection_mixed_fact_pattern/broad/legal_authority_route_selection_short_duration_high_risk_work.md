# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Short Duration High Risk Work

## A Executive legJal summary

Swiss law starts from a restrictive premise: foreign nationals who wish to work in Switzerland require authorisation **irrespective** **of the** **period** **of stay,** and "gainful employment" is defined broadly as any salaried or self-employed activity normally carried out for payment, whether or not payment is actually made. The implementing ordinance reinforces that point by stating that dependent work remains gainful employment even where it is performed only hourly, day-by-day, or temporarily. In other words, short duration is not, by itself, a safe harbour. 1

For route-selection purposes, the decisive question is usually **what** **the** **person** **will actually** do **in** **Switzerland,** not how briefly they wvill do it. Official SEM materials distinguish sharply between non-productive activities such as short business meetings, negotiations, information exchange, and passive attendance at conferences, on the one hand, and productive or operational activities on the other. SEM's own examples treat on-thne-job training integrated into work processes, repair and maintenance work, project implementation, repeated on-site programme roll-outs, and regJular management visits to conduct staff appraisals or set objectives as gainful activity. 2

For EU/EFTA-based service situations, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and the notification regime create an important **exception,** but only a controlled one. Up to 90 effective workdays per calendar year may fall under notification rather than permit requirements for eligible categories, yetthe same federal material makes clear that activity in certain sectors is reportable from day one, that the eight "notification-free" days are cumulative, that day-counting applies both to the sending undertaking and to the worker, and that even one hour of work counts as one workday. Where notification is unavailable, insufficient, or exceeded, permit analysis remains necessary. 3

The high-risk fact patterns for internal analysis are thereforethose involving hands-on technical work, installation, commissioning supervision, maintenance, repairs, testing/troubleshooting by analogy, implementation at a Swiss client or operational site, productive training, local Swiss job starts, repeated short visits, regulated professions, financial services, sensitive first-day sectors, and any structure that looks like a fragmented or dressed-up attempt to avoid the 8-day or 90-day thresholds. Official Swiss guidance also shows that Swiss recipients and host sites can acquire compliance exposure: persons obtaining cross-border services must ensure the foreign service provider is authorised, and host operations are advised to verify authorisation for foreign staff performing assembly or maintenance work on site. 4

The conservative internal rule should therefore be this: do **not** **treat "one** **day",** "a **few** **days", "urgent",** **"training", "implementation", or "customer support" as low-risk labels.** First classify the activity. Then test whether it is gainful work, whether it is a Swiss job start or cross-border service, whether EU/ EFTA notification is legally available, whether a first-day sector or regulated profession rule applies, whether cumulative or repeat presence is building up, and whether the client-side facts make the arrangement look like local employment or disguised labour leasing. 5

## B Structural overview of short duration high risk work

Swiss route selection for short assignments works best if analysed through four gates.

The first gate is **whether the Swiss activity is "gainful employment" at all.** FNIA article 11 and OASA article 1a cast the concept broadly, and SEM guidance says the decisive issue is whether the activity is one that is in principle performed for remuneration on the Swiss labour market. SEM expressly says the duration of the work is irrelevant, and that the question is not whether the individual needs Swiss income to live, but whether the activity as such affects the Swiss labour market. 1

The second gate is **classification** **of the** **activity.** Official SEM examples place short business meetings, negotiations, contract signature, strategy sessions and information exchange outside gainful activity. By contrast, SEM classifies operational training integrated into work processes, practical on-the-job system work, repair or maintenance in Switzerland, project implementation, and repeated on-site programme delivery asgainful activity. Federal guidance also gives a narrow delivery-side exception:merely transporting clearly defined goods ordered abroad, which could equally have been delivered by post, is not treated as permit-requiring gainful activity; but adding Swiss-facing offers or marketing during that visit turns the situation back into a service activity.6

The third gate is **which** **legal route** is **theoretically** **available.** For EU/EFTA nationals taking Swiss jobs of up to three months, and for eligible EU/EFTA-based posted workers and self-employed service providers up to 90 workdays per calendar year, the notification procedure can replace a permit. Outside those categories, or once those thresholds are exceeded, permit analysis is required. SEM's own guidance further states that services in the financial sector remain subject to prior permit requirements and do not use the ordinary notification route. 7

The fourth gate is **whether special rules displace the ordinary short-duration logic.** They do so in at least five recurring ways: sectors with first-day notification or permit exposure; regulated professions with separate professional-qualification procedures; emergency cases where only the advance-notification period may be shortened; cumulative day counting across the calendar year; and anti-avoidance scrutiny where a structure appears designed to evade service limits, such as sham Swiss establishments or disguised labour leasing. 8

## D Primary legal texts and E Official guidance

The core statutory spine is compact but powerful. FNIA article 11 supplies thebaseline rule that work in Switzerland requires authorisation irrespective of the period of stay, and that gainful employment captures work normally performed for payment whether or not payment is actually made. OASA article 1a extends that breadth by stating that dependent work remains gainful work even where only hourly, daywise, or temporarily performed; article 3 defines cross-border service work as a temporary service in Switzerland under a contractual relationship by a person or undertaking based abroad; and article 4assigns initial classification questions to the cantonal labour-market authority, with doubtful cases referred to SEM. OASA article 7 then makes clear that sectoral or professional operating permissions do not replace immigration authorisation. 9

The basic short-duration federal rule outside the EU/EFTA service-liberalisation context is OASA article 14. It provides that foreign nationals performing cross-border services or temporarily working in Switzerland on behalf of a foreign employer need a permit once activity lasts more than eight days in a calendar year, and regardless of duration in construction, hospitality and cleaning, security, itinerant trade, the erotic sector, and landscaping. The provision is structurally important even where, for EU/ EFTA/FZA cases, it is displaced in practice by the more favourable notification route: it shows how Swiss law treats "short work" as a controlled exception, not as inherently authorisation-free. 27

The official SEM materials do most of the practical classificatory work. The AIG guidance says the concept of gainful activity is intentionally interpreted widely and that the real question is whether the activity affects the Swiss labour market. It also states expressly that the duration of the work is irrelevant and that any activity going beyond a mere favour and normally performed for remuneration is work. That same guidance instructs Swiss host operations to verify authorisation where foreign workers are used foroutside assembly or maintenance work, and notes that persons obtaining cross-border services must ensure the foreign service provider is entitled to work. 28

SEM's annex on the concept of gainful activity is the strongest practicalI authority for mixed fact patterns. It treats the following as **non-gainful** when genuinely limited to those facts: representative management visits, negotiations, contract signature, information-only coordination meetings, and

passive conference attendance. It treats the following as **gainful**: production-related or integrated practical training, maintenance on Swiss machinery, implementation of software at Swiss headquarters, reorganisation work on site over several mnonths, repeated management visits to conduct appraisals, and other operational project deployments. That annex does not use every modern label-such as "commissioning", "troubleshooting", or "testing"-but the official examples sit close enough to support conservative treatment of such activities by analogy where they involve productive, technical, or operational delivery. 29

For EU/EFTA/FZA cases, SEM's notification material and VFP guidance provide the key operational rules. The route is available for EU/EFTA nationals taking Swiss employment of up to three months, for posted workers from EU/EFTA undertakings, and for EU/EFTA self-employed service providers, each within the 90-workday or three-month framework. The mmaterials emphasise that each assignment must be notified separately, that the place of work canton processes the filing, that the 8-day and 90-day calculations are cumulative, and that one hour of work still uses one day. They also state that if the work obviously requires more than 90 workdays, a permit must be sought before work starts, and that extension beyond 90 days converts the case into a permit matter under national law with no treaty-based right outside special service agreements. 30

The same federal guidance identifies specific zones of heightened short-duration risk. First, financial services requiring prior authorisation remain permit-bound and do not use the ordinary notification route. Second, direct or indirect labour leasing from abroad is prohibited. Third, regulated professions remain subject to separate qualification controls. SEM's FAQ points expressly to diploma recognition for regulated professions such as doctors, nurses, and architects, while SBFI states that EU/EFTA service providers working for up to 90 days in a regulated profession must complete the SERI declaration procedure. 31

Finally, the posted-worker compliance layer matters even where immigration route selection appears straightforward. SECO explains that the Posted Workers Act requires foreign employers posting staff to Switzerland to respect Swiss minimum wage and working-condition rules, and it publicly lists service providers subject to final service bans. SECO's sanctions catalogue sets out concrete fine bands for late or missing notifications, progressive sanctions for wage underpayments, and service bans that may extend to years. The same catalogue also addresses false self-employment and documentary failures. These sources confirm that short assignments can carry material enforcement exposure even where the immigration route is "only notification". 32

## F Cantonal practice notes

Published cantonal material is mostly procedural rather than doctrinal, but it still gives reliable route-selection signals.

**Zurich** states that gainfully employed EU/EFTA persons may work up to 90 workdays per calendar year without a permit only if the assignment is duly notified, and that the day-one sectors mirror the federal list. Zurich also adds a useful compliance warning: the 8-day advance period runs strictly,work may not begin before it expires, and a "short-notice client request" or poor staffing plan does not qualify as a notifiable emergency. Its guidance for self-employed service providers requires on-site production of the notification confirmation or authorisation, A1, and the Swiss client contract or written order confirmation; if those documents are missing, fines and work stoppage can follow. Zurich also notes that a provider who is self-employed abroad may still be classified as employed under Swiss law. 33

<!-- 8 -->

**Basel-Stadt** emphasises that notifications are routed through the place of work and that **each** **worksite** **must** be **notified** **separately,** which is practically significant for technicians moving between sites in a single canton. Basel's canton website also publishes sector-specific pages showing how immigration analysis intersects with local regulatory control: for health professions, professional activity in one's own professional responsibility requires cantonal health authorisation; and for the erotic sector, Basel states -relying on Federal Supreme Court case law-that sex workers are generally treated as employees and that the operation's manager is the employer. Those pages are not general immigration doctrine, but they are useful illustrations of how cantonal authorities classify "sensitive" short-duration work conservatively. 34

**Geneva** frames route selection in a notably clear wvay. For EU/AELE undertakings or independents, a mission of up to 90 days per year or three consecutive months is handled by federal notification; beyond that, a cantonal authorisation is reqquired, with the application sent to the OCPM around 6-8weeks in advance. Geneva's labour-market directorate expressly states that it decides authorisations for EU/AELE businesses or independents wishing to post staff or provide services in Geneva for more than 90 days. For Swiss direct hires of EU/AELE nationals, Geneva aIso separates out short employment by online notification from longer stays requiring residence/work authorisation. 35

**Ticino** provides useful Italian-language confirmation of the same architecture. Itsnotification page states that service work above 90 workdays or three months per calendar year remains permit-bound, that permits are subject to labour-market review including quotas, local priority, wage and condition controls, and qualifications, and that first-day notification applies in the standard sensitive sectors. It also requires an employer declaration on Swviss minimum wage and working conditions for posted workers and refuses incomplete notifications. 36

The cantonal picture is therefore consistent with the federal one. There is no visible cantonal trend in the official material towards treating short technical assignments as immigration-light by default.On the contrary, the published cantonal pages reinforce strict advance filing, site-specific reporting, sensitivity around false self-employment, and separate licensing for regulated activities. The limits of the published cantonal record should, however, be stated candidly: beyond these procedural pages, there is relatively little public cantonal doctrine explaining how authorities classify modern technical tasks such as commissioning, diagnostics, or software go-live support in marginal cases. 37

## G Practical legal analysis

The conservative analytical sequence for internal route selection should be as follows.

Start by asking whether the person is doing **work** in the Swiss sense. If the visit consists only of negotiations, information exchange, contract signing, passive attendance, or other genuinely non-productive presence, the file may sit outside gainful activity. If, however, the individual will install, repair, maintain, configure, supervise commissioning, instruct operational staff after installation, implement software, test or troubleshoot in a way integrated into client delivery, or otherwise contribute operational value on site, the federal materials strongly point towards gainful activity. That remains true even if the assignment is brief, unpaid in the individual case, or described commercially as "support", "training", or "customer success".38

Next distinguish **Swiss job** **start** from **cross-border** **service provision.** SEM's immigration framework treats these as different channels. A Swiss employment relationship, even for a short period, is not analysed in the same way as a temporary service performed under an overseas contract. In mixed fact patterns, watch for facts suggesting that the worker is functionally integrated into the Swiss operation, uses the Swiss host's tools or premises, or is in reality taking instructions locally. SEM's guidance is explicit that actual working arrangements matter more than formal paperwork, and its sham-establishment warning shows that authorities will look past Swiss paper entities created only to bypass the 90-day service cap. 39

<!-- 9 -->

Then test **whether** **notification** is **genuinely** **available.** It may be available for EU/EFTA direct hires up to three months and for eligible EUJ/EFTA-based posted or self-employed services up to 90 workdays.It is not the correct answer for everyone. Non-EU/EFTA employers sending staff face a permit route rather than the FZA notification route; third-country nationals posted by EU/EFTA businesses must already have been lawfully integrated into the EU/EFTA labour market for the required prior period; services above 90 days move into permit territory with no treaty entitlement; and finance services may require prior authorisation rather than notification even below 90 days. 40

After that, test **special sector and** **profession** **rules.** Construction, hospitality, cleaning, security, itinerant trade, the erotic sector, and landscaping are high-risk because notification or permit exposure starts on day one. Regulated professions are high-risk because migration notification may be only one layer of the analysis: SBFI's SERI declaration route, diploma recognition, or cantonal professional licensing may still be needed. This means that phrases such as "only one day", "only a few sessions", or "only short cover" are legally weak where the work is regulated or sensitive. 41

The next check is **cumulative** **presence.** Official materials count effective workdays, not continuous stays, and they count them strictly. Work performed intermittently over months is still cumulative.A worker who has already used eight "free" days may trigger notification requirements for a new sending company, and a sending company that has already used its company-level allowance may trigger notification obligations for newly sent workers who themselves have not yet worked in Switzerland that year. This makes repeat troubleshooting visits, phased installations, rolling project sprints, monthly governance meetings with operational content, and multi-site support models particularly exposed. 42

The materials also support a conservative stance on **anti-fragmentation.** Switzerland's published rules do not use that label often, but the legal effect is close. If the assignment is known from the outset to exceed the eight-day free allowance, notification must start from day one. If the work is obviously going to exceed 90 workdays, a permit should be sought before commencement. If the project is split into fragments and thereality points to one longer services engagement, the cumulative counting rules, the sham-Swiss-establishment warning, and the host-site/recipient diligence obligations together make it unsafe to assume that fragmentation will succeed. That conclusion is partly inferential, but it is a strong inference from the official framework. 24

Emergency cases require speciaI discipline. Official sources describe only a **narrow exception** **to** **the** 8-**day** **advance-notification** **period,** not a general waiver of notification or permit requirements. The situation must involve repairs, accidents, natural disasters, or other unforeseeable events, and the claim must fit the timing conditions set boy the federal materials. Zurich expressly says that short-notice instructions or weak manpower planning do not count as emergencies. Internal drafting should therefore never state or impoly that urgency itself removes the need for formal pre-clearance. 17

Finally, consequences matter. Working without authorisation can expose the worker to penalties for unauthorised work; employers and Swiss recipients of unauthorised cross-border services can face criminal exposure; and repeated employer breaches can lead to future admission refusals and cost recovery. On top of that, the posted-worker system can impose administrative fines, back-pay consequences, and service bans. That enforcement mix explains why even apparently tiny assignments often require careful route selection before anyone travels. 43

## H Drafting cautions and I Source list

### HI Drafting cautions

Do **not** draft future short-duration content as though "up to 90 days" were a general business-travel exemption. The official Swiss materials do not support that. The 90-day concept belongs to specific notification pathwaysfor specific categories, and those pathways are still controlled immigration procedures. 44

**Do** **not** suggest that a one-day, two-day, or otherwise very short assignment is automatically outside work-authorisation rules. Swiss federal law says the opposite in principle, and the sector lists reinforce it with day-one exposure in sensitive branches. 45

Do **not** collapse together business visits, notification cases, short-term permits, posted work, self-employed services, and Swiss local employment. Each route has different legal predicates, different actors responsible for filing, and different consequences if misclassified. 46

Do not use commercial labels such as "training", "implementation support", "inspection", "go-live", "commissioning", "troubleshooting", "warranty support", or "client success" without analysing what actually happens on the ground. Official Swiss guidance treats comparable productive and operational activities as work. Where the exact label is not discussed in an official source, say so and reason by analogy from SEM's examples instead of claiming an express exemption that is not there. 29

Do **not** imply that urgency erases the need for notification or permission. The official materials support only a narrow emergency relaxation of the **advance-notification** **period,** and even that is tightly defined. 17

**Do** **not** ignore the Swiss client or host site. Where work is done at a Swiss operational site, the host's tools, premises, instruction power, or contractual role may change the classification risk, and the recipient may carry due-diligence exposure. 47

Where internal content must simplify, the safest summary sentence is this: **brief** **work** **in** **Switzerland** **may still require notification, permit analysis, or both if the activity is productive, technical, on-site, cumulative, regulated, or performed for a Swiss recipient in a way that affects the Swiss** **labour market. 48**

### Open questions and limitations

Official Swiss sources are strong on the general framework, on hands-on repair/maintenance,on implementation work, on operational training, and on cumulative day counting. They are less explicit, at least in the publicly accessible federal and cantonal material reviewed here, on some modern labels such as "troubleshooting", "testing", "commissioning", and "consulting delivery" when used as stand-alone commercial descriptors. The safest approach is therefore to classify those activities by analogy to the closest official examples rather than to claim a separate official category. Published cantonal materials are also more procedural than interpretive, so canton-specific handling in edge cases should be treated as potentially practice-sensitive even where the federal route-selection principles are clear.
