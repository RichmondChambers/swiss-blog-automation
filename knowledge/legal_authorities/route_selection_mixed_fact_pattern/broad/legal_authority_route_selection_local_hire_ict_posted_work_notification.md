## Summary and framework

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss route selection is not elective. The correct route follows the factual structure of the work arrangement: who the legal employer is, where that employer is established, whether there is a genuine Swiss employer or only a Swiss host/client, whether there is a real group relationship, whether the worker is sent to deliver a defined service or simply supplied as labour, who directs the day-to-day work, whether the worker is integrated into Swiss operations, and whether a permit entitlement arises from the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons with the EU/EFTA or, failing that, from the ordinary rules of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and its ordinances. The official materials are especially clear that notification is only a procedural channel for specified short-term categories; it is not a separate substantive category that can cure a mnisclassified arrangement. 1

At a high level, the classification questions run as follows. If the worker is employed by a Swiss employer, the case is a Swiss local hire or "taking up employment in Switzerland" rather than a posting; for EU/EFTA nationals this may use the short-term notification system for employment ofup to three months,while for third-country nationals a permit is required from day one. If the worker remains employed by a foreign employer and is temporarily sent to Switzerland, the next question is whether the case is a genuine posting/service provision, a genuine intra-group transfer, or an impermissible labour-leasing arrangement. If it is a genuine EU/EFTA cross-border service provision, notification may be available for up to 90 effective working days per calendar year; beyond that, or outside the liberalised sectors and persons, the case movesinto permit territory under Swiss immigration law. If the arrangement is really the supply of labour under Swiss-host control rather than the performance of a defined service under the foreign employer's responsibility, Swiss authorities treat that as labour leasing rather than posting, and cross-border labour leasing from abroad is not liberalised under the AFMP and is in principle prohibited under the Employment Service Act. 2

For genuine intra-company transfers, Swiss law does not provide a free-standing "ICT permit" in the EU-law sense. Instead, the relevant Swiss route is the operational transfer framework in the FNIA/OASA system, especially the exception for senior managers and essential specialists in international companies. Official SEM guidance states that Article 46 OASA is designed to facilitate recruitment by Swiss international companies and research institutes of senior managers, highly qualified experts and indispensable specialists, and that the precedence rule does not apply in that setting. Where the transfer relies on Switzerland's GATS commitments, SEM guidance also requires at least one year's prior employment abroad and a Swiss branch, subsidiary or establishment of the same business group. 3

The most useful conservative distinction between routes is therefore this. A **Swiss local** **hire** is characterised by a Swiss employer and integration into Swiss employment. A **posted** **worker** / **cross-border** **service** **provider** remains employed abroad and is sent temporarily to perform a defined

service in Switzerland, usually project-based. A **genuine** **ICT** remains an intra-group assignment into a Swiss branch, subsidiary or establishment of the same group, typically for management, specialist or knowledge-transfer purposes. **Notification** is only available where the category and nationality/ employer-establishment rules permit it. **Labour** **leasing** is present where the Swiss entity is effectively receiving manpower under its own instructions rather than a result-oriented service; that route cannot be regularised by calling it a posting or ICT. 4

### B. Structural overview of route-selection analysis

A conservative route-selection analysis should be done in this order. First, identify the **legal employer** and where it is established. Swiss official guidance and cantonal practice treat "taking up employment in Switzerland" as an employment relationship with an employer established in Switzerland, while "posting" assumes that the employment contract remains with the foreign employer. 5

Secondly, identify the **Swiss-side** **entity** and its role: Swiss employer, Swiss host within the same group, Swiss client/recipient under a service contract, or Swiss end-user receiving labour. This matters because Zurich's official document sets distinguish "firmeninterne Entsendung" with no third party involved from a posting "mit Einsatz in Drittfirma", where the authority expects a Swiss service contract/work contract with the Swiss recipient, a project description, and assignment documents. 6

Thirdly, ask whether the worker is sent to perform a **defined** **service/result** or whether the foreign undertaking is in reality only supplying labour. SECO's guidance on labour leasing says that labour leasing is indicated where the Swiss user undertaking has the essential right to instruct and supervise, where the worker is integrated into the Swiss undertaking's organisation and time structure, where billing is for working time rather than a defined result, and where the supplying undertaking does not bear the risk of poor performance in the way a contractor would under a work/service contract. Conversely, no labour leasing is present where the Swiss side lacks that substantive right of instruction and the contractor is responsible for the deliverable.7

Fourthly, test whether there is a **genuine corporate** **link.** SEM guidance defines an intra-company transfer by reference to a foreign company's employee working temporarily in a Swiss branch, subsidiary or establishment, and for GATS-based ICTs requires one year's prior emmployment abroad. Zurich's own forms reinforce this by requiring identification of both the home company and the host company, and by separating intra-group files from third-party service-provider files. If there is no real group relationship, the case should not be drafted as ICT. 8

Fifthly,check **nationality** **and** **employer** **establishment.** Notification is available only for the official categories: EU/EFTA nationals taking short Swiss employment, workers posted by undertakings established in the EU/EFTA, and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers; for third-country nationals posted by EU/EFTA undertakings, permanent admission to the EU/EFTA labour market for at least twelve months is required. For third-country nationals and third-country employers outside those channels, the ordinary FNIA route applies. 9

Sixthly, consider **duration** **and** **cumulation,** but with caution. Duration is legally important for whether the notification system is available and whether the 90-day AFMP service liberalisation has been exhausted. It is not decisive for the uunderlying characterisation of the arrangement. SECO's labour-leasing guidance expressly says that the duration of the assignment and the type of work do not determine whether labour leasing exists. A short assignment can still be a local hire, a posted service, or an impermissible labour-supply arrangement depending on the facts. 10

## Federal texts and official guidance

### D. Primary legal texts

The core federal texts are the **Foreign** **Nationals and** **Integration** **Act** (FNIA/AIG, SR 142.20),the **Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment** (OASA/VZAE, SR 142.201), the **Ordinance on** **Free Movement of Persons** (FMIO/VFP, SR 142.203), the **Agreement on the Free Movement of** **Persons** with the EU, including **Annex** I, the **Posted** **Workers Act** (EntsG, SR 823.20) and its ordinance (SR 823.201), and the **Employment** **Service** **Act** (AVG, SR 823.11) with the **Employmennt Service** **Ordinance** (AVV, SR 823.111). SEM and SECO official pages list these instruments as the governing legal framework for labour-market admission, notification, posting and labour leasing. 23

Within that framework, the key article clusters are these. FNIA Articles 18-24 govern ordinary admission of third-country workers, including overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, salary/working conditions, personal qualifications and accommodation; Article 30 contains exceptions; Article 11 is the statutory gateway for work authorisation and is read broadly with OASA Articles 1-4. OASA Article 46 is the main international-company transfer provision in Swiss practice. FMIO/VFP Article 9 governs the notification channel for short-term work, and FMIO/VFP Article 15 governs services exceeding 90 day/s in the EU/EFTA context. AFMP / Annex I provide the limited service liberalisation on which the 90-day service regime is built. EntsG and EntsV regulate Swiss minimum labour standards for posted workers, while AVG/AVV regulate labour leasing and placement. 24

A practically important point from the primary-lawv structure is that the AFMP does not create full EU-style services freedom in Switzerland. Official SEM materials say expressly that financial services requiring prior authorisation and labour placement/labour leasing are not liberalised, and that only certain service categories benefit from the liberalised 90-day regime. They also note that, in sectors covered by specific service agreements between Switzerland and the EU, broader rights may exist for the duration of the service; outside those specific agreements, services beyond 90 working days fall back into the FNIA regime. 25

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful federal administrative materials for route-selection work are the **SEM** **FNIA** **directives,** especially chapter 4 on gainful employment; the **SEM** **VFP/FMIO** **directives** on free movement, notification and EU/EFTA services; the **SEM notification-procedure pages** and **user guidance;** the **SEM** **factsheet on cross-border service provision (EU/EFTA);** SECO's official pages on **posting, self-employed service provision,** and **private employment services and labour leasing;** and SECO's detailed **guidance** **on** **the** **Emmployment** **Service** **Act.** Together, these materials are considerably richer on classification than the bare statutory texts alone. 26

For local hire versus posting, the official guidance is especially clear on formal structure. SEM and Zurich both define "taking up employment in Switzerland" by reference to a Swiss employer, whereas posted workers remain under the foreign employment contract. That distinction should therefore be the starting point in drafting. The difficulty arises where the paper structure and the operational reality diverge; in those cases, Swiss practice looks beyond labels to the actual organisation of work. 27

For ICT versus local employment, SEM's guidance makes a real doctrinal difference between ordinary third-country admission and the special operational-transfer framework. Managers and indispensable specialists transferred within international companies can benefit from relaxed precedence treatment, but only where the facts fit an actual intra-group transfer into a Swiss branch, subsidiary or

<!-- 7 -->

establishment. Zurichn's separate evidence lists for "firmeninterne Entsendung" and for assignments into a third-party Swiss business are very useful confirmation that cantonal practice also treats this as a distinct classification question. 28

For posted work versus labour leasing, the official materials are stronger on the labour-leasing side than on the posting side. SECO's AVG guidance gives a detailed functional test: who instructs, who supervises, whose timetable and organisation govern the worker, whose tools and materials are used, whether the contract is priced by hours or by result, and who carries the risk of poor performance. That guidance is the safest official basis for distinguishing a true service contract from prohibited cross-border labour supply. The official posting portal summarises the same distinction in plain terms: labour leasing exists where instruction rights are ceded to the Swiss user undertaking, and unlike posting the leased workers do not act in the name and for the account of their employer. 29

For notification versus permmit, the official rule set is straightforward. The notification channel is available only for the recognised short-term EU/EFTA categories, with eight-day advance notice for posted workers and self-employed providers, and day-before notice for short-term Swiss hires. Services above 90 effective working days per calendar year require a permit, and outside special service agreements there is no AFMP-based entitlement to such a permit. 30

## Cantonal practice notes

### F. Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** is particularly useful for route-selection drafting because its official pages and forms expressly divide cases into local hire, posting and self-employment, and then further separate intra-group transfers from assignments into a third-party Swiss business. Zurich defines a Swiss "Stellenantritt"by reference to a Swiss employer, describes postings as temporary and usually project-based while the foreign employment contract continues, and requires route-specific evidence. For third-party postings, Zurich asks for the Swiss service/work contract and project description. For intra-group cases, Zurich requires identification of the home company and host company and confirmation that no third-party company is involved. Zurich's employer flowchart also visually separates "Lokalanstellung" and "Entsendung". 31

**Geneva** offers a helpful institutional split. The canton states that an EU/AELE worker engaged by a Swiss enterprise for more than three months must apply for a residence/work authorisation,whereas short employment uses the SEM notification procedure. Geneva also distinguishes EU/AELE undertakings or independents providing services in Geneva from third-country undertakings or independents doing the same. The OCIRT's foreign-labour division states that it decides authorisations for third-country nationals working in Geneva for Swiss employers or sent by non-EU/EFTA structures, and also authorisationns for EU/AELE undertakings or independents wishing to post staff or provide services in Geneva for more than 90 days. 32

**Fribourg** is useful because it formulates the service-provider route in classic AFMP terms. The canton states that EU/AELE nationals providing cross-border services through contracts or contracts for work of up to 90 effective working days, whether as posted workers or as self-employed providers, are subject to notification, not a permit. For services over 90 days outside a specific service agreement, Fribourg says there is no right under the AFMP, a residence authorisationis required in advance, and admission depends on the FNIA conditions of precedence, salary/work-condition review, qualifications and quotas; it also requires the Swiss contract and a posting convention. 33

<!-- 8 -->

**Ticino** confirms the same logic in Italian-language practice. The canton states that EU/EFTA citizens and workers posted by undertakings established in the EU/EFTA do not need a permit for work under 90days, but must nnotify. In other sectors there is generally notification only once work exceeds eight days in the calendar year, and for third-country nationals posted by a company established in the EU/EFTA Ticino repeats the twelve-month prior admission requirement to the regular labour market of an EU/ EFTA state. Ticino also notes, in separate guidance, that cases outside the notification regime are permit cases subject to labour-market examination. 34

Taken together, the cantonal material does not contradict the federal framework; instead it shows how classification is operationalised. The cantonal authorities focus heavily on the same issues that recur in the federal guidance: who the employer is, whether there is a Swiss host or a Swiss client, whether there is a genuine third-party service contract, whether the case is intra-group, how long the activity will last, and whether the applicant has documented salary, expenses and social-security arrangements in a way consistent with the proposed route. 35

## Practical analysis and drafting cautions

### G. Practical legal analysis

For internal content generation, the safest drafting method is to classify on the basis of **control,** **integration** **and** **responsibility** **for output,** not on labels alone. If the Swiss entity is the worker's real operational home, gives ordinary day-to-day instructions, sets the timetable, provides the tools and workplace, and receives labour rather than a defined contractual result, the arrangement is drifting away from posting and towards either Swiss employment or labour leasing. Swiss official material is quite explicit that labour leasing exists where the Swiss user undertaking has the essential instruction and monitoring rights and the worker is integrated into its organisation, even if the paperwork says "service contract" or "assignment". 20

**A** **Swiss** **local** **hire** is the safer classification where there is a Swiss employment contract, a Swiss employer entity, ordinary Swiss reporting lines, and an open-ended or business-as-usual role within Swiss operations. Zurich's official material treats this routeas "Stellenantritt Schweiz", and Geneva does the same for EU/AELE workers engaged by a Swiss enterprise. Short duration does not change the underlying classification; it only changes whether a notification channel is available instead of a residence/work permit. For EU/EFTA nationals employed by a Swiss employer, notification may be available up to three months. For third-country nationals, the ordinary permit route applies from the outset. 36

**A** **genuine** **posted-worker** **/** **third-party** **service** case is stronger where the worker remains employed by the foreign company, the Swiss-side role is that of client or recipient under a contract for services or a contract for work, the assignment istemporary and project-specific, the foreign employer remains responsible for the work and the worker's employment terms, and the foreign employer bears the commercial risk of delivering the contracted result. Zurich's third-party posting evidence list is especially helpful here because it requires precisely thedocuments that show this structure: the project description, the Swiss contract, the assignment contract, salary in the home state, allowance, expenses and social-security arrangements. 37

**A** **genuine** **intra-company** **transfer** is stronger where there is a documented group relationship, no third-party Swiss recipient, a route-specific business case such as management continuity, specialist deployment or knowledge transfer, and the worker remains anchored in the group rather than being transferred into an ordinary Swiss recruitment need. SEM's guidance on Article 46 OASA and GATS-

<!-- 9 -->

based ICTs makes prior employment abroad and a real Swiss group entity important formal indicators. Zurich's intra-group checklist reinforces that the home company / host company architecture must be spelled out. If the worker will in substance fill a normal Swiss vacancy and be absorbed into ordinary Swiss operations, a canton may see the case less as ICT and more as Swiss employment or ordinary third-country admission. That latter point is an inference from the structure of the sources and should be drafted as a conservative practice assessment, not as a statutory bright line. 38

**A** **notification** route should be described as available only where thefacts already fit one of the recognised substantive categories. For EU/EFTA cross-border services, the official rule is upo to 90effective working days per calendar year, counted both for the company and the individual. For posted workers and self-employed providers, eight-day advance notice is generally required, with first-day notification in the listed sensitive sectors and limited emergency exceptions. For Swiss short-term hires, the Swiss employer notifies no later than the day before work begins. Beyond 90 days, or where the relevant person/employer is outside the recognised categories, the matter becomes a permit case. 39

The main **red flags that the wrong route has been selected** are these. Calling a case "ICT" where there is no real Swiss branch/subsidiary/establishment or no genuine group link. Calling a case "posting" where the Swiss entity controls the worker's day-to-day tasks and the foreign undertaking is effectively supplying manpower. Using a Swiss shell company to re-label what is really a foreign posting as a Swiss hire; SEM/VFP guidance expressly warns cantonal authorities to test whether a Swiss company has a real infrastructure and operates on its own account rather than being used to circumvent the 90-day service limits. Using notification wvhen the service is plainly intended to exceed 90days. Treating a third-country employer outside the EU/EFTA structure as if it could use the EU/EFTA notification regime. Claiming self-employed service provision without the documents and market-independence indicators required by SECO. 40

**On** **payroll, salary, expenses and** **social security,** the official material supports a careful but limited proposition. Foreign payroll, home-country base salary, posting allowance, A1 coverage and employer-paid travel/accommodation/meal costs are consistent with a posting/assignment model; Swiss payroll and a full Swiss employment contract are more coonsistent with local hire. Zurich's forms ask for those exact items when assessing postings. But none of those elements is independently decisive if the supervision and integration facts point the other way. Payroll and A1 are therefore better treated as evidential indicators than as conclusive route determinants. That is a conservative inference from the official sources rather than a single express statutory rule. 41

### H. Drafting cautions

Future route-selection content should state expressly that Swiss law classifies arrangements by substance and legal framework, not by convenience. Avoid wording such as "the employer may choose the easiest route"; the better formulation is that the legally correct route depends on the employer location, the Swiss-side role, the existence or absence of a genuine group link, the level of Swiss-side control, the nature of the activity, and the applicable nationality/employer-establishment rules. 42

Use the terms **legal employer, payroll employer, Swiss host, Swiss client/recipient,** and **group host** distinctly. In particular, avoid collapsing "host company" and "Swiss employer". Zurich's own evidence lists separate the home company from the host company and distinguish intra-group files from third-party recipient files; failure to preserve those distinctions wvill lead to poor route analysis. 6

Do not treat **short duration** as decisive. It is relevant to whether notification is available, but not to whether the arrangement is local hire, ICT, posting or labour leasing. This point is strong enough to be built into template language. A sound sentence is that "short duration may affect the procedure, but it does not by itself determine the legal classification of the work arrangement". 21

<!-- 10 -->

Reserve **ICT** for genuine intra-group transfers into a Swiss branch, subsidiary or establishment, especially management and specialist deployments. Reserve **posted** **worker** **/** **cross-border** **service** **provider** for cases where the worker remains employed abroad and is sent to provide a defined service. Reserve **local hire / taking up employment in Switzerland** for Swiss-employer employment. Use **notification** only as the procedural wrapper for those short-term categories that are officially eligible. Use **labour** **leasing** where the Swiss entity receives labour under its own operational control. 43

Official materialsare more explicit on notification, EU/EFTA services, postings, self-employed service provision and labour leasing than on some border cases between a foreign assignment and practical integration into Swiss operations. In those thinner areas, draft conservatively and say that the answer is fact-sensitive; avoid pretending that one formal element, such as payroll alone or a group-company label alone, settles the matter. The safest approach is to state the official indicators and then explain the likely classification if those indicators point predominantly one way. 44
