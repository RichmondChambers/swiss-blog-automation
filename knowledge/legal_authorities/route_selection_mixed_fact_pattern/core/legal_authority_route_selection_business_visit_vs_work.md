# Swiss legal authority pack on business visits

## versus work in Switzerland

## Executive and classification framework

### A.Executive legal summary

In Swiss immigration law, the decisive question is not whether a trip is described as a "business trip" in commercial language. The real legal question is whether the foreign national will carry out **gainful** **activity** in Switzerland within the meaning of Swiss migration law. SEM's current guidance explains that this concept is interpreted broadly; it is assessed on **objective** **criteria,** turns on whether the activity is one that is **ordinarily** **remunerated** on the Swiss labour market, and can still amount to gainful activity even if the individual is unpaid or receives only minimal compensation. SEM also states that the **duration** of the activity and whether it is a main or side activity do not decide that threshold question. 1

The safest official examples of a **non-working** **business visit** are narrow and passive: representative visits by senior staff, contract negotiations and contract signing, non-binding relationship-maintenance meetings, internal strategy or kick-off meetings limited to coordination and information exchange, passive attendance at conferences or workshops, and theoretical or technical courses that do **not** integrate the visitor into a productive work process. By contrast, official materials treat the following as **work** **/** **gainful activity**: giving presentations, delivering training, on-the-job induction, internships and trainee placements, client-specific consulting, project planning meetings tied to execution, taking measurements, acceptance of works, software implementation, reorganisation work, repair and maintenance, installation and commissioning, and other hands-on or productive contributions made in Switzerland. 2

Short duration is not a "business visitor" safe harbour. It only changes the **route.** For EU/EFTA nationals and qualifying posted or self-employed service providers, gainful activity of up to 90 days or three months in a calendar year commonly falls into the **notification** regime, with an eight-day threshold for many service assignments, first-day notification in specified sectors, and first-day notification for Swiss local job starts. For third-country nationals outside free mnovement, work for a foreign employer or self-employed activity in Switzerland may be permit-free only up to **eight** **days** in a calendar year outside specified first-day sectors; work for a Swiss employer, a Swiss establishment, or the erection of structures or installations in Switzerland requires pre-entry authorisation analysis. A separate short-term **120-day** **/** **four-month** route exists for certain third-country assignments, but it remains a work-permit route with ordinary labour-market scrutiny. 3

Entry permission and work authorisation must be kept separate. Swiss official visa guidance states that a Schengen visa or visa-free admission only addresses **entry** **and** **stay;** if the stay involves gainful employment, a **work** **permit** is **also** **required.** Official FDFA guidance further states that, for longer-term work, the entry visa is granted only after the required Swiss work authorisations have been issued. 4

<!-- 1 -->

OfficialSwiss material on **remote work while physically present in Switzerland** is comparatively thin and is aimed mainly at residence/home-office cases rather than classic visitor trips. SEM's 2022 circular nonetheless shows the main distinction: under the FZA, EU/EFTA nationals working from Switzerland for a foreign employer with **no Swiss labour-market link and no Swiss customer contact** may be treated as non-economically active rather than as workers in Switzerland; but the same circular says Swiss-market-facing or Swiss-customer-facing activity is work, and for third-country nationals the employer concept is broader and the place where the salary is paid is irrelevant. For business-visitor drafting, that circular should therefore be treated as a **narrow, fact-specific** **administrative** **point,** not a general permission for "digital nomad" activity during visitor stays. 5

Official practice also shows that labels such as "meeting", "conference", "training", "workshop" or "support" do not settle the classification. Authorities look instead at the **actuaI substance** of the visit, the deliverables, and the supporting documents, including contracts, secondment confirmations, proof of purpose, training concepts, and project descriptions. 6

### B. Structural overview of business visit versus work classification

The Swiss analysis is best run in three stages. First ask whether the visitor will do anything that amounts **to** **productive** **contribution** in Switzerland or otherwise affects the Swiss labour market; that is the gainful-activity question. Secondly, if the answer is yes, choose the correct route by nationality, employer structure, duration and sector: FZA notification, AIG eight-day rule, 120-day permit, or ordinary permit. Thirdly, deal separately with **entry** / visa and, where relevant, **registration** **/ residence** formalities. 7

## Authorities and administrative materials

## D.Primary legal texts

The federal legal core is the **Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (AIG / FNIA / LEI,** SR 142.20) together with the **Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment (VZAE / OASA / OASA in** **French** **nomenclature,** SR 142.201). SEM's legal-basis pages identify these as the central statutes and ordinances, and SEM's chapter on employment operationalises the core provisions used for this subject: the definition of gainful activity, the canton's competence to classify borderline cases, the eight-day rule for work without local job start, and the short assignment route of up to four months / 120 days. 24

For EU/EFTA nationals and qualifying service providers, the next layer is the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement of Persons** and **Annex I,** together with the **Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons** **(VFP,** SR 142.203). SEM's current VFP guidance citesthe treaty and ordinance provisions that matter here:notification for work not exceeding three months /90 days, permit requirements once that threshold is crossed, and the absence of a full, EU-style freedom to provide services in all sectors. 25

<!-- 7 -->

Where the activity amounts to a posting or notified cross-border service, Swiss labour-law compliance is also anchored in the **Posted Workers Act (EntsG,** SR 823.20) and the **Posted Workers Ordinance** **(EntsV,** SR 823.201). SEM and SECO pages rely expressly on these texts for the notification mechanism, day-one sectors, and mninimum Swiss labour and salary conditions. 26

Entry law is a separate layer. For short stays, SEM's annex on the concept of gainful activity expressly links certain non-working stays to **visa** **issuance** **under the** **Visa** **Code** **/** **VEV** **framework,** while FDFA visa guidance states that, if the stay involves gainful employment, a work permit is still required. 27

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

For third-country and general AIG analysis, the most important operational source is **SEM,** **Weisungen** **AIG, chapter** **4** **"Aufenthalt** **mit Erwerbstätigkeit"** (current edition dated 1 January 2026 in the source materials used here). It sets out the broad Swiss concept of gainful activity, distinguishes **work** **with** **local job** **start** from **work** **without local job** **start,** explains the eight-day rule and the 120-day / four-month route, and gives additional compliance points including employer responsibility. 28

The most useful activity-level examples on the AIG side are in SEM's separate annex **"Begriff der** **Erwerbstätigkeit".** It is especially valuable for distinguishing theoretical training from on-the-job training, passive conference attendance from active speaking, representative or negotiation visits from project work, and pure business discussions from recurring management or operational activity. 29

For EU/EFTA and nnotification analysis, the key sources are **SEM's VFP** **guidance** **and Annex** **5** to those instructions. Annex 5 is especially important because it expressly says the table is **illustrative, not** **exhaustive,** and gives **a** high-value practical principle**: productive activities are to be notified.** **It** then classifies a wide range of meetings, conferences, project steps, technical interventions and customer-facing tasks as either notifiable or non-notifiable. 30

SEM's public **notification-procedure** pages and service-provision factsheets are useful for route consequences rather than activity definitions. They explain who can use the notification regime, the 90-day ceiling, the eight-day rule, first-day sectors, and the fact that long-term or excluded service activity falls outside the simple notification model. 31

SECO's public material is important where classification intersects with **posted-worker** **compliance,** especially proof of self-employment, minimum Swiss labour conditions, and sanctions. These materials show the practical control package that authorities expect to see when a person claims to be a self-employed service provider rather than an employee. 32

The SEM **home-office** **circular** **of** 5 **April 2022** is relevant but should be used with care. It is not a visitor guide; it addresses the immigration qualification of home-office situations under the FZA and for third-country nationals, and its main value here is to show how Swiss authorities analyse the combination of physical presence in Switzerland, employer location, Swiss market link and cuustomer contact. 5

### F. Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal materials do not create the federal legal test, but they are useful evidence of **implementation** **practice.** Zurich's notification page is particularly helpful. It states that all activities become notifiable if they exceed eight days in the calendar year, sets out the first-day sectors, points directly to **Annex** **5** for the distinction between notifiable and non-notifiable activities, requires advance notification, and lists

the documents needed to prove self-employment on inspection. It also states that assignments above 90 days require a permit and that Swiss labour conditions apply. 33

Basel-Stadt's practice page confirms the same federal structure from a cantonal perspective: the first eight days are normally free of notification or permit in EU/EFTA service assignments, but specified sectors are day-one cases. 34

Geneva's French-language guidance is useful for route selection. For EU/EFTA companies or self-employed persons offering services in Geneva, the activity must either be **notified** to **the** **Confederation** or authorised by the canton depending on duration, and only actual working days count. Geneva's third-countrypage is stricter: a company or independent professional based outside the EU/EFTA that wants to post staff or offer services in Geneva **must obtain** **cantonal authorisation,** with the file submitted 6-8 weeks in advance. 35

Ticino's Italian-language pages align with the same federal model: EU/EFTA citizens and workers posted by EU/EFTA undertakings can perform gainful activity of less than 90 days without a permit, but they must use the **notification procedure.** 36

## Practical legal analysis

## G. Practical legal analysis

**The key Swiss dividing line is productive contribution, not commercial motive.** SEM's current AIG guidance says that the decisive issue is whether the activity is ordinarily performed for remuneration on the Swiss labour market and influences that market. The VFP annex adds the practical formulation that **productive** **activities** **are** **to** **be** **notified.** For internal drafting, that matters more than labels such as "business trip", "site visit" or "strategic meeting". If the traveller is contributing labour,know-how or deliverables that a Swiss or foreign business is using in Switzerland, the case is moving into work analysis. 37

**Meetings, negotiations and conferences sit on both sides of the line.** Official non-work examples include contract negotiations and signing, representative visits, non-binding customer meetings to maintain relationships,internal strategy sessions, kick-off meetings, and meetings limited to coordination or information exchange. Passive attendance at conferences or workshops is also treated as non-work. But the same official materials make clear that once there is a **presentation,** **knowledge** **transfer** as **trainer,** customer-specificadvisory discussion, or project planning tied to deliverables, the matter becomes gainful activity. The distinction is therefore not "meeting" versus "no meeting"; it is **passive attendance or exchange** versus **delivery, advice or execution.** 38

**Client-facing and project-specific work is one of the clearest danger areas.** VFP Annex **5** treats as notifiable work a foreign interior designer advising a Swiss client on an office fit-out, a foreign kitchen builder discussing next steps and planning a kitchen project, a foreign contractor attending in Switzerland to accept its subcontractor's works, and even measurements taken before or around contract finalisation. The annex also treats internal group work at the Swiss headquarters as work where the visitor is actually working on a project, even though purely internal strategy and coordination meetings may remain non-work. For content drafting, the safest rule is that **client-delivery** **meetings** **and internal project-delivery** **meetings** are not the same thing as general corporate discussions. 39

### Technical support, implementation and hands-on interventions are firmly on the work side.

Official examples include maintenance on machinery in Switzerland, software implementation at the

<!-- 9 -->

Swiss head office, reorganisation work at a Swiss subsidiary, installation and end checks linked to systems or machinery, commissioning oversight and instruction of operating staff, and service assignments in engineering and software projects. These are classic cases of productive wvork performed in Switzerland, even if the worker remains employed abroad and even if the assignment is broken into periodic visits over several months. 40

**Training and onboarding require a careful split between receiving and delivering, and between** **classroom** **and** **productive** **integration.** SEM's annex treats seminars, theoretical and technical courses without productive integration, school-type teaching, and workshop participation as non-work.By contrast, "trainings on the job", induction integrated into the work process, trainee placements, and giving training in a Swiss affiliate are treated as work. The same logic should be applied to onboarding, observation and shadowing: if the individual is merely receiving classroom-style instruction or observing without productive participation, the case is closer to a non-working visit; once the person uses Swiss systems, works under supervision at Swiss workstations or otherwise joins the productive process, it is work. Official materials on pure "shadowing" are thin, so caution is needed. 41

**A limited exception exists for very short trial work, but it is narrow.** SEM cites a Federal Supreme Court judgment explaining that short trial work used only to assess suitability for a role, as part of an evaluation or negotiation process and before a true employment relationship exists, may fall outside gainful activity because it is not directed at producing output. SEM explicitly distinguishes this from a normal probationary employment period under labour law and says longer deployments remain subject to work authorisation rules. This should be drafted as a narrow exception rather than a general category of "observation". 42

**Trade fairs and goods-related visits deserve particularly careful wording.** Official mmaterials clearly treat an exhibitor at an international fair as work without local job start under the AIG chapter, treat customer acquisition including fair and sales conversations as notifiable work under the VFP annex, and require day-one notification for stand-building work that falls within the construction trades. There is, however, one route-specific tension: Annex 5 to the VFP guidance lists "pure goods deliveries (including unloading)" as non-notifiable, while SEM's AIG chapter uses a foreign "goods supplier" as an example of gainful activity without local job start governed by the eight-day rule. The safest internal reading is that **bare** **logistics** **should** **not** **be** **over-generalised,** and that any Swiss-side installation, end checks, sales activity, customer acquisition, stand operation or technical follow-on work should be treated as work. Purely passive attendance at a fair without a stand, sales or service delivery is not expressly worked through in the official sources and should therefore be described cautiously. 43

**Employer location matters for route selection, but not always for the work question.** Under the AIG framework, work for a Swiss employer or Swiss establishment is "with local job start"; work for a foreign employer can still be gainful activity, but as "without local job start", which is what engages the Article 14 VZAE eight-day structure. SEM's home-office circular reinforces that, for third-country nationals, the employer can be seated in Switzerland **or** **abroad,** and the place of payment of the salary is irrelevant. At the same time, the circular shows that for EU/EFTA free-movement cases, work done from Switzerland for a foreign employer with no Swiss market link and no Swiss customer contact may in some residence scenarios be classified as non-economic inactivity rather than Swiss employment. That difference means content should never compress the whole subject into a single slogan such as "foreign employer means no Swiss work permit". 17

**Duration and frequency matter for the route, not for the underlying classification.** Repeated visits of only a few days can still be work if what is done on each visit is productive; SEM's annex gives the example of a global manager who comes regularly to Switzerland to conduct staff appraisals, feedback discussions or target setting and treats that as work. Conversely, a short passive conference attendance or negotiation visit can remain outside work classification. This is why internal content should distinguish between **what happens on the day** and **how many days there are.** 44

<!-- 10 -->

**Once theactivity is classed as work, the route consequences are highly structured.** Under the FZA, gainful activity of up to 90 days or three months generally goes through the notification system; posted or self-employed service providers are usually notified after eight days, with first-day notification in listed sectors, while local Swiss job starts are notified from the first day. Above 90 days, or in areas excluded from the limited service liberalisation, a permit route is required. Under the AIG for third-country nationals, work for a foreign employer or self-employed activity in Switzerland may be permit-free only up to eight days in the calendar year outside the first-day sectors; beyond that, or in the day-one sectors, work authorisation is needed and must generally be awaited abroad. Separate from this, certain third-country assignments can use the short **120-day** **/** **four-month** route, but that route remains subject to labour-market review and documented purpose.45

**Entry permissions do not cure work-authorisation defects.** FDFA states that a stay involving gainful employment also requires a work permit, and SEM's French FAQ states that even the SEM approval decision does not itself authorise entry and commencement of work before thne cantonal migration authority has handled the visa step. This is an important drafting point for mixed fact patterns: a person can be lawfully admitted for travel purposes and still be **unauthorised** **to** **work.** 4

**Evidence** is **central** to **classification.** SEM says duration and purpose must be checked by written employment contracts or secondment confirmations for short-assignment permits, while the visa-entry rules require proof of the purpose and circumstances of stay. For theoretical training without work, SEM's annex says the foreign post should verify duration, content and place of training and confirm that no gainful activity is planned. For self-employed service providers, SECO and Zurich require the traveller to be able to show notification or permit proof, an A1 certificate, and a contract or written order confirmation. The documentary picture should therefore match the legal route being claimed. 46

**Swiss authorities and Swiss recipients also have compliance exposure. SEM's** handbook states that the employer concept in immigration law is factual: the relevant employer is the person or business that actually directs the foreign worker, even if there is no written contract. The handbook also states that persons who receive a cross-border service must ensure that the foreign persons providing it are entitled to carry out gainful activity in Switzerland. While a domestic principal may not always have a formal control duty where it has contracted with a Swiss intermediary, SEM still recommends verification to avoid control issues. Cantonal and SECO materials also show that failures around notification and self-employment proof can lead to fines, service bans and work stoppages. 47

## Drafting cautions

## H. Drafting cautions

**Do not write that a short stay is enough.** Swiss official materials expressly separate the **classification** question from the **route** **threshold** question. A one-day or two-day visit can still be work if the visitor is delivering services, giving training, taking measurements, performing repairs or otherwise contributing productive output in Switzerland. 48

**Do not write that unpaid work or foreign-paid work falls outside Swiss work-authorisation rules.** SEM's guidance says that ordinarily remunerated activity can be gainful activity even if the specific activity is unpaid, and SEM's home-office circular says that, for third-country nationals, it does not matter whether the salary is paid in Switzerland or abroad. 49

<!-- 11 -->

**Do not collapse all "meetings" into business visits.** Official examples split meetings into two categories: passive negotiations, relationship-maintenance and internal coordination may be non-work, but project planning, advisory discussions, works acceptance and project work in Switzerland are treated as work. Drafting should therefore use precise activity verbs, not generic nouns. 38

**Do not collapse all "training" or "workshops" into business visits.** Receiving theoretical training may be non-work; giving training, speaking at conferences, or taking part in on-the-job induction is work. Internal content should always distinguish between **attendance** and **delivery,** and between **classroom** **instruction** and **productive integration.** 50

**Do not present remote work as a settled visitor permissionn.** The most relevant official source is **a** 2022 home-office circular aimed at residence scenarios, not short visitor trips. It is useful for principles, but thin for business-visitor use cases. If remote work is raised in content, the analysis should spell out nationality, employer location, Swiss customer contact, Swiss market link and the limited scope of the official source. 5

**Treat trade fairs, goods delivery and logistics as drafting danger zones.** Official sources are more nuanced here than in other categories. They clearly treat exhibitor activity, customer acquisition, stand-building and technical follow-on activity as work, but differ in how they describe pure goods deliveries. Content should therefore avoid broad statements such as "attending a trade fair is fine"or "delivery is always outside work-permit rules". 43

**Where** **official** **materials are** **thin, say** **so.** That is especially true for short-term visitor remote work, some forms of observation or shadowing, and purely passive trade-fair attendance. A conservative internal pack should mark these categories asfact-sensitive rather than market them as settled exemptions. 51
