# ChatGPTSwiss legal authority pack on EU and EFTA family members and derived residence rights in Switzerland

# Legal summary and structure

## A. Executive legal summary

This pack addresses the **derived residence position in Switzerland of family members of EU/EFTA** **nationals who are themselves exercising free movement rights in Switzerland.** The core legal basis is the **EU-Switzerlannd Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons** and, for EFTA sponsors, the **revised** **EFTA Convention,** both of which recognise residence rights for qualifying family members irrespective of nationality. In Swiss administrative guidance, the same framework is applied to EU and EFTA sponsors, subject to special rules for Liechtenstein. 1

The key doctrinal point is that the family member's residence right is ordinarily **derived, not** **autonomous.** Swiss official guidance states expressly that the family right presupposes an **original** **right** **of residence** held by the EU/EFTA sponsor under the AFMP/ALCP, and that the validity of the family member's status is therefore generally conditional on the continuing existence of the sponsor's original status. Swiss SEM guidance also makes clear that this right is reserved to family members of EU/EFTA nationals who are actually within the scope of free movement law in Switzerland; it does **not** automatically arise in purely internal Swiss situations, and it is not available to family members of frontier workers or persons present only under the short-term notification procedure if they seek residence in Switzerland on that basis. 2

The **closed** **core** **family** **group** recognised as having a legal entitlement consists of: the spouse; descendants in the descending line who are under 21 or are dependent; and dependent ascendants in the ascending line of the sponsor or of the spouse. For students, the circle is narrower: spouseand dependent children only. Other relatives and certain partners may benefit only from a duty of **facilitated** **admission,** not from a direct entitlement under Annex I Article 3. Swiss guidance is careful on this distinction and directs "other family members" cases to the FNIA guidance and, in exceptional hardship situations, to Article 20 OLCP; that route does **not** itself create a legal right to admission. 3

**For** **third-country** **family** **members,** the treaty text and SEM guidance are clear that nationality is not, in itself, a barrier if the person falls within the recognised family categories. Swiss guidance also states that family members of EU/EFTA nationals do not need to show prior durable residence in another EU/ EFTA state before relying on Article 3 Annex I, which reflects the post-Metock Swiss position recognised by SEM. However, visa-required third-country family members may still need an entry visa, although the treaty requires Switzerland to give them every facility to obtain it; current SEM materials further state that non-EU/EFTAfamily members holding a valid residence permit issued by a Schengen state are exempt from the visa requirement. 4

**On** **documentation,** Annex I Article 3(3) is restrictive at the treaty level: the state may require only the entry document, proof of family relationship, and-where relevant-proof that a person is dependent or part of the sponsor's household. In practice, cantonal authorities often ask for additional supporting material to verify treaty conditions, including lease documents, marriage and birth certificates, custody decisions, consent of the other parent, evidence of financial support, translations and, in some cantons, legalisation or apostille. That does not make documentary proof optional; it reinforces that relationship and dependency evidence must be properly substantiated. 5

**On** **work** **access,** the treaty and SEM guidance distinguish sharply between categories of family members. The spouse and dependent children or children under 21 have a right to pursue an economic activity whatever their nationality. SEM further states that, for family members of EU/EFTA nationals, this right exists **without** **notification** **or** **a** **separate** **work** **permit,** and even where the sponsoring EU/ EFTA national is not themselves authorised to work. By contrast, SEM's family-reunification factsheet states that ascendants admitted by family reunification do **not** have a right to work. 6

The effect of the sponsor's status matters. For **employed** **sponsors,** suitable accommodation is required, but Swiss official materials say family reunification is not contingent on proving financial means separately. For **self-employed sponsors** and **economically inactive sponsors,** SEM states that sufficient resources are required. For **students,** the family circle is limited and sufficient means must still be shown. For **jobseekers,** the sponsor's own status is narrower and more fragile: SEM says a jobseeker may obtain a short-stay permit if they have sufficient means, and official guidance indicates that persons who no longer work, have lost worker status, or are seeking work must have funds enabling them to maintain family members; where social assistance is or would be needed, SEM states that family reunification cannot be recognised under the AFMP and residence rights may lapse. This is one of the most fact-sensitive parts of the regime and should be drafted cautiously. 7

Retention after **separation, divorce, death, departure or sponsor-status change** is also an area where Swiss official materials require careful drafting. SEM states that a spouse's derived right does not automatically end upon a mere separation, but its continuation depends on a real and effectively lived marital community; abuse of rights arises where only a formal marriage remains. SEM further states that, in principle, the derived right ends upon divorce or the death of the sponsor, unless the family member can point to an **independentAFMP** right of their own. For third-country spouses, continued residence after divorce or death is generally governed by the FNIA and its implementing provisions, subject to possible rights to remain and to the precondition that the sponsor still had an AFMP right at the relevant time. In death cases involving an economically active sponsor, SEM's factsheet summarises limited situations in which family members may remain. 8

## B. Structural overview of EU/EFTA family-member derived rights

The Swiss structure is best understood as a **four-layer** **hierarchy.**

First, the **treaty** **layer**: AFMP Article 7(d) and (e) recognises residence rights for family members and their access to economic activity, while Annex I Articles 1 to 4 and 24 set the substantive content for entry, residence, family membership, documentation, permit duration, work access, right to stay, and the conditions applicable to non-active persons. The EFTA Convention's Appendix on movement of persons mirrors these family-member rules for EFTA spoonsors. 9

Second, the **Swiss implementing** **layer**: SEM's legal-basis page identifies the AFMP, the OLCP/ Ordonnance on the Introduction of Free Movement of Persons, and the SEM directives and circulars as the relevant Swiss implementing materials. The OLCP sits alongside the treaty as the main domestic ordinance framework. 10

<!-- 2 -->

Third, the **administrative** **interpretation** **layer**: the SEM OLCP directives are the central operational source for Swiss practice. They explain the derivative nature of family rights, the personal scope of the AFMP, the absence of a prior-EU/EFTA-residence requirement, the position of stepchildren and dependent ascendants, the sponsor-status-specific conditions, and the interaction with the FNIA/OASA for abuse, revocation, hardship and post-breakdown residence. ⑪1

Fourth, the **cantonal evidential and** **procedural** **layer**: cantons administer applications and often publish forms or guidance stating which documents they will actually request. Zurich, Geneva and Basel-Stadt all require practical proof of relationship and housing; Zurich and Basel-Stadt specifically identify custody and parental-consent evidence for children, and Geneva expressly lists lease and relationship documents and supporting financial evidence. These materials are important for operational drafting, but they do not override the treaty limits and must be read as practice on evidence and procedure rather than as independent sources of substantive entitlement. 12

# Authorities and practice materials

## D. Primary legal texts

The primary treaty sources are the **AFMP** **itself** **and** **Annex** I. AFMP Article 7 expressly requires the contracting parties to provide for the right of residence for family members and their right to pursue an economic activity. Annex I Article 1 governs entry and visas; Article 2 governs residence documentation; Article 3 defines family members, documentary requirements, permit duration and work access; Article 4 concerns the right to stay after the end of economic activity; and Article 24 sets the sufficient-means and insurance rules for non-active persons. 3⁰

For **EFTA** **sponsors,** the revised **EFTA** **Convention** contains materially parallel wording in Appendix I on movement of persons, including entry, family membership, documentation, permit duration, work

<!-- 7 -->

access and right to stay. For internal drafting, this supports a single EU/EFTA route treatment in Switzerland unless a source flags a specific EFTA exception, with Liechtenstein being the main formally noted special case in SEM materials. 31

On the Swiss domestic side, SEM's legal-basis page identifies the **OLCP** as the principal federal implementing ordinance for free movement, alongside the AFMP and SEM directives. The same SEM materials also show that Swiss practice supplements the treaty through the **FNIA** and **OASA** where issues such as abuse, hardship, data exchange on social assistance, and post-breakdown residence arise. 32

A current drafting caution is that SEM now publishes materials on the **application** **of** **the** **current** **AFMP** and separately on an **updated** **AFMP** **that** is **not yet** in **force.** Internal content should therefore stay anchored to the **current** legal framework and should not import later EU free-movement concepts unless and until they are actually in force for Switzerland. 33

## E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful Swiss official interpretive source is the **SEM** **OLCP** **Directives,** especially chapter II.7 on family reunification. Those directives provide the clearest administrative statements on the derivative nature of the right, the recognised family categories, the narrowed student category, the absence of a prior EU/EFTA residence requirement, the exclusion oof frontier-worker and notification-procedure families from AFMP-derived residence in Switzerland, the evidential requirements for dependency,work access for spouses and qualifying children, and the interaction with abuse, separation, divorce and Article 50 FNIA. 11

SEM's short **Family** **reunification** **factsheet** is valuable because it distils the doctrine into operational propositions: who may accompany the sponsor; the housing and means conditions; the derivative nature of the right; the basic documents for a residence permit; the work right for spouse and qualifying children; the absence of work rights for ascendants; and the limited official summary on divorce and death. 34

The **SEM** **FAQ** **-** **Free** **Movement** of **Persons** is a useful current operational source for concise formulations and for public-facing administrative positions. It confirms the family-members list, the housing condition, and the work right for spouses and children; it also provides an up-to-date official statement of the permit architecture for EU/EFTA nationals generally. 35

**For** **entry** **and** **visa** **process,** the currennt SEM visa page confirms that a **national** D **visa** is the relevant form for long stays of more than three months that are subject to authorisation, including family reunification, while the SEM visa-requirements list confirms that third-country nationals holding a valid Schengen residence permit or valid D visa and a valid travel document are exempt from the visa requirement in the categories described there. AFMP Annex I adds the treaty-level requirement that every facility be given for any necessary visa. 36

**For residence** **cards,** the SEM biometric permit page confirms that third-country nationals who are family members of EU/EFTA citizens are issued biometric L/B/C residence permits and that, when presented with a valid national passport, the Swiss biometric residence permit allows visa-free travel within the Schengen area. That point is secondary to the derived-right analysis, but it is useful for document-status content. 26

## F. Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** publishes a practical page for family reunification under the free movement agreement. It states that EU/EFTA nationals may bring family members regardless of nationality, requires an appropriate family home, warns against abuse, and lists required documents including the entry application, marriage certificate, lease, recent salary slips and, for children, birth certificate plus formal custody proof and-where there is joint custody-a written consent to the child's move to Switzerland. Zurich also states expressly that a simple declaration by the parent remaining abroad is insufficient where a judicial or administrative custody position must be shown. 37

**Geneva** provides a current English-language family-reunification page for EU/EFTA nationals. It confirms the recognised categories, the narrower student category, the need for adequate accommodation, and the need to prove dependency for children over 21 and ascendants before entry. It lists attachments including identity documents, lease evidence, marriage and birth certificates, parental-authority evidence and supporting financial documents. Geneva also separately states that family members-except ascendants-who benefit from family reunification have the right to work or be self-employed throughout Switzerland regardless of nationality. 38

**Basel-Stadt** provides a current family-reunification information page and, more importantly,a November 2025 application form. The page confirms that EU/EFTA citizens are governed primarily by the AFMP, that visa-required applicants must lodge a personal entry application at the Swiss representation abroad and wait abroad for the decision, and that EU/EFTA spouses moving together to Basel-Stadt may register directly without a prior family-reunification application. The form itself shows the breadth of evidence the canton may request in practice: passports, marriagJe and birth certificates, divorce and custody decisions, parental consent, rent agreement, salary and tax records, health-insurance documents, proof of financial commitments and, for EU/EFTA nationals and their family members, an exemption from the otherwise standard A1 German language requirement. 39

These cantonal sources are best treated as **practice** **notes** **on** **evidence** **and** **process,** not as the primary source of entitlement. They are especially useful for content-system drafting on likely evidential requests, translations, apostille/legalisation, custody proof, and the need for the visa-required family member to remain abroad pending the entry decision. 12

# Analysis, cautions and sources

## G. Practical legal analysis

For internal content, the safest framing is that **EU/EFTA** **family** **members** in **Switzerland** **do** **not** **receive a free-standing "family permit" in the abstract.** They receive residence status only because a qualifyingsponsor has an original AFMP/EFTA right and because the family relationship falls within the treaty definition or, at most, within the non-entitlement "facilitate admission" zone. This should be stated expressly in any route-specific content. 40

The term **"family** **member"** **must** be **used** **carefully.** It clearly covers spouses and recognised registered partners, and it covers descendants in the descending line who are under 21 or dependent. It also covers dependent ascendants. It does not mean that all relatives qualify. Siblings, nephews, nieces, uncles, aunts and cohabiting partners fall outside the entitlement category, though some may be helped under the facilitated-admission limb or, exceptionally, through Article 20 OLCP hardship practice. Those routes are discretionary and should not be written up as rights. 41

<!-- 9 -->

**For children** **and** **descendants,** the most important qualification is that age21 is a legal threshold for automatic descendant status, after which dependency must be shown. Swiss guidance also confirms that **stepchildren** qualify, but partial family reunification requires proper custody analysis and evidence. Zurich and Basel-Stadt both require custody judgments and, where relevant, the foreign parent's formal consent;Geneva asks for parental-authority evidence. Internal drafting should therefore avoid any suggestion that a birth certificate alone will suffice where family authority arrangements are contested or incomplete. 29

**For dependency,** Swiss official guidance is conservative. SEM says the dependent person's indigence must be real and proven, and that application authorities may require state-of-origin documentation proving both the family relationship and, where relevant, the support provided. It also emphasises that dependency is a factual matter and that support should in principle be provided by the sponsor; weak or episodic support can indicate abuse. This matters most for ascendants and descendants aged 21 or over. 42

**On** **means** **and** **accommodation,** the material distinction is between sponsors who are working and those who are not. Workers are mainly tested through the housing requirement. Self-employed persons, non-active persons, persons who have lost worker status, and jobseekers must show sufficient resources. The official sources do not present a single, simple formula for every scenario. In particular, "unemployed" status is not a standalone bright-line category in the sources; the correct analysis depends on whether the sponsor still retains worker status, has become a jobseeker, or is now simpoly a non-active person. That is why future content should prefer conditional phrasing tied to the subsistence of the sponsor's AFMP status rather than categorical statements aboout "unemployment". 21

The **permit** **outcome** should also be drafted with precision. The treaty says the family member's permit has the same validity as the sponsor's permit. SEM adds that the samne type of permit is issued, but then qualifiesthat where the sponsor has settlement status, some family members may still receive a B EU/ EFTA permit by analogy unless the FNIA is more favourable. Content that says simply "family members get the same permit as the sponsor" is therefore serviceable only as a starting point; it needs a qualification for settlement cases. 43

**On** **work** **access,** internal drafting should make three distinctions. First, spouses and qualifying children have a work right and may also work self-employed. Second, that right exists regardless of nationality. Third, ascendants do not have this right. Geneva and SEM say the point clearly, and SEM further states that no separate notification or work permit is required for family members of EU/EFTA sponsors. 6

**For retention after relationship breakdown,** the safest rule is: **do not promise retained rights under** **AFMP** **unless** **the** **source** is **explicit.** Swiss official materials say separation is not automatically fatal, but a real marital community must still exist and abuse can be found. Divorce or death usually ends the derivative right; any continuation then depends either on the family member having their own AFMP basis or on separate FNIA provisions. The SEM factsheet gives only a limited death-based right-to-stay summary, and the directives stress that third-country spouses move onto FNIA analysis after dissolution. That is a much narrower and more conditional picture than some practitioners might assume from later EU law. 8

Finally, **Swiss-citizen comparators should be handled carefully.** SEM's official line is **that,** save for specific cross-border situations or where the family member independently qualifies under AFMP, family members of Swiss citizens are governed by the FNIA, not directly by the AFMP. That matters because route-specific content should not silently transpose AFMP rules from EU/EFTA sponsors to Swiss sponsors. 44

## H. Drafting cautions

Future content for this route should use the following drafting discipline.

State expressly that the family member's status is **derived** **from** **the** **sponsor's** **AFMP/EFTA** **status,** and normally lasts only while that original status subsists. Avoid language implying a general, freestanding right to remain. 40

Do not say that "family members" generally qualify. Say instead that the AFMP recognises a **limited** **core** **group:** spouse/recognised registered partner, descendants under 21 or dependent, and dependent ascendants; for students, spouse and dependent children only. 45

Do not treat **other** **relatives** **or** **unmarried** **partners** as having an entitlement. The correct formulation is that Swiss authorities must facilitate admission of certain other dependent or household members, but this is not the same as a direct treaty right, and Article 20 OLCP hardship practice is discretionary only.46

Do not imply that documentary proof of relationship or dependency is optional, or that authorities may insist on whatever documents they like without limit. The treaty limits what may be required at the level of principle, but the cantons commonly request additional documentary material to verify housing, dependency, custody, consent and genuineness. State both points together. 47

Do not say that all **family** **members** **may** **work.** Limit the work right to spouses and qualifying children, and say expressly that ascendants admitted by family reunification do not have a work right. 48

Do not treat **jobseekers** **and** **unemployed** **sponsors** as straightforward categories. Use cautious language linked to whether the sponsor still has an AFMP residence right and sufficient means; official materials are explicit that welfare dependence defeats AFMP-based family reunification for non-active or residual-status sponsors. 22

Do not overstate **retained rights after separation, divorce, death or sponsor departure.** The official materials are narrower and more fact-sensitive than a broad EU-law summary might suggest. For third-country spouses especially, post-breakdown residence often moves outside the AFMP and into FNIA analysis. 28

Do not import concepts from the **updated** **AFMP** **framework** **that** is **not yet** **in** **force.** Internal content should stay tied to the current agreement and current SEM materials unless the legal position changes formally. 33

**Open** **questions** **and** **limitations.** Fedlex's JavaScript-heavy current-law interface was not fully extractable in this research environment, so the treaty text and SEM's official directives/factsheets have been used as the principaI source-grounding for operative rules, supplemented by official cantonal materials and official court summaries where needed. Current official materials are strong on the existence and limits of derived rights, but thinner on certain edge cases such as the exact boundary between retained worker status, jobseeker status and family rights after abrupt labour-market changes. Those areas should be treated as fact-sensitive rather than settled bright-line rules. 49
