A Executive legal summary
For EU/EFTA nationals and EU/EFTA-based service providers, Swiss law does not have a single “short-
term work” route. It has three distinct legal patterns that must be kept separate: short-term
employment with a Swiss employer, posting by an EU/EFTA employer, and genuine self-employed
cross-border service provision from an EU/EFTA state. Each pattern rests on a different combination
of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU, Annex I to that
Agreement, the Swiss Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons, and the Posted Workers legislation.
Notification is available only for specified categories and within specified limits; it is not a universal
substitute for residence registration or a permit.
The basic federal position is clear. EU/EFTA nationals employed by a Swiss employer for up to three
months in a calendar year do not need a residence permit, but the Swiss employer must make an
online notification no later than the day before work starts. If the employment will exceed three
months in the calendar year, a residence permit is required instead. Official SEM material aligns this
with the AFMP and the Swiss implementing ordinance.
For posted workers and self-employed service providers established in an EU/EFTA state, the AFMP
liberalises service provision only up to 90 days of actual work per calendar year. In ordinary sectors,
notification is required only once the work in Switzerland exceeds eight days in the calendar year; in
listed higher-control sectors, notification is required from the first day. These sectors include
construction, building ancillary trades, gardening/landscaping, hospitality, cleaning, security, itinerant
trade and the sex industry, although official materials phrase the itinerant-trade exception slightly
differently in different places.
The decisive boundary between a notifiable activity and a non-notifiable business visit is substance, not
label. SEM’s official directives treat seminars, conference attendance without speaking, representative
appearances, contract negotiations, internal strategy meetings and pure goods delivery as examples of
non-notifiable activity. By contrast, on-the-job training integrated into the work process, speaking at a
conference, consulting, project implementation, internal project work, measurements for a concrete
project, repairs, maintenance, installation and customer-acquisition activity are treated as productive,
and therefore notifiable where the immigration rules otherwise apply. The directives say expressly that
the table is illustrative and that, as a general principle, productive activities must be notified.
Notification can also be unavailable even for EU/EFTA-linked services. The AFMP service liberalisation
does not extend to temporary/interim agency activities, staff leasing from abroad, or certain
financial services requiring prior authorisation. SEM’s official material states that direct and indirect
staffing supply from abroad is not permitted and that the notification procedure does not apply in those
cases.
Residence registration must also be handled carefully. Federal SEM guidance clearly requires EU/EFTA
nationals who take up residence in Switzerland for gainful activity of more than three months to

For posted workers and self-employed service providers, the day-counting rules are technical and
important. Official SEM guidance states that both the eight-day notification-free period and the 90-
day maximum are counted against the posting company and the individual worker. The company’s
quota is not multiplied by the number of workers sent on the same day: three workers deployed
simultaneously for five working days use five company days, not fifteen. By contrast, if they are sent in
sequence on different days, the company uses the full number of sequential working days. SEM also
states that if Saturdays, Sundays or public holidays are included in the notification, those days are
debited too, even if no work is actually performed on them.
For short-term employment with a Swiss employer, the logic is different. The limit is tied to the
worker’s employment in Switzerland for up to three months per calendar year; SEM annex material
says that a Swiss employer may notify several EU/EFTA employees, provided each employee’s own
contract is limited to three months in the calendar year. The company-wide “90-day account” logic
belongs to the posting/service-provider side, not to Swiss employment as such.

D Primary legal texts
The core treaty source is the AFMP. Article 5 of the Agreement gives service providers a right to provide
services for up to 90 days of actual work in a calendar year. Annex I then breaks the regime down
further: Article 17 defines the protected service-provider category, Article 20 removes the need for a
residence permit for service providers within the 90-day window, Article 21 fixes the 90 actual
workdays per calendar year ceiling, and Article 22 preserves host-state rules on posted-worker
conditions while excluding temporary/interim agencies and certain financial services from the
liberalised regime. On the worker side, Annex I Article 6 says that an employed person hired by an
employer in the host state needs no residence permit if employed for up to three months, but does
need one if employed for more than three months.
The principal Swiss implementing ordinance is the VFP/OLCP. The current consolidated text available
through a Swiss law-text mirror dated 1 January 2026 provides that EU/EFTA nationals who work in
Switzerland for not more than three months in a calendar year do not need a short-stay permit, and
that, for Swiss employment up to three months and for self-employed service provision up to 90
workdays, the notification procedure under the Posted Workers legislation applies mutatis mutandis.
The same article states expressly that, for Swiss employment of up to three months, the notification
must be filed at the latest on the day before the activity begins. The same consolidated text also
contains a specific offence provision imposing a fine of up to CHF 5,000 for breach of the notification
duty under article 9(1bis).
For postings, the decisive domestic labour-market text is the Posted Workers Act. Article 1 defines
posting by reference to a foreign employer sending employees to Switzerland either to perform work
under a contract with the service recipient or to work in a Swiss establishment or undertaking
belonging to the same corporate group. Article 6 requires advance notification before the assignment,
including identity, wage, the activity in Switzerland and the place of performance, and states that work
may begin only eight days after notification, subject to ordinance-level exceptions. Article 2, as
summarised by SECO, covers minimum pay, working time and rest, holidays, occupational safety and
health, protection of vulnerable workers and equal treatment.

SECO’s foreign-employment and posting pages provide the labour-market overlay. They distinguish

point to the posting platform and sanctions architecture. The official posting platform also clarifies that
notification for a genuine self-employed service provider is possible, while minimum posted-worker
conditions do not generally apply to a genuinely self-employed person; it also warns about false self-

Two SEM factsheets remain useful even though they are not all recent. The January 2025 factsheet on
residing in Switzerland and taking up gainful activity states the basic three-month employment rule and
the permit consequences beyond three months. The February 2023 factsheet on cross-border service
provision remains listed by SEM in January 2026 and is still the clearest concise official explanation of
what falls inside and outside the AFMP service regime, including the fact that service provision beyond
90 days falls out of the AFMP and into FNIA-based labour-market control and quotas. Because this
service-provider factsheet is older, it should be treated as useful but not as the sole source for time-
sensitive operational detail.
F Cantonal practice notes
Cantonal sources do not alter the federal legal framework, but they matter for process, local
registration and the tone of enforcement. Zürich’s official page confirms the classic three-category
structure, the first-day sectors, the strict eight-day prenotification requirement for service provision,
and the need to distinguish notifiable from non-notifiable activity by reference to the AFMP directives. It
also states plainly that work before the expiry of the eight-day period is prohibited.
Geneva’s official business portal is helpful on threshold framing. It treats missions by an EU/EFTA
undertaking or independent provider of 90 days per year or 3 consecutive months or less as
notifiable to the Confederation, while longer assignments require a cantonal authorisation and should
be filed 6 to 8 weeks before the mission starts. Geneva therefore illustrates a practical point worth
reflecting in internal drafting: the legal analysis should look not only at the abstract AFMP service right,
but also at the operational consequences once an assignment is expected to run beyond the
notification window.

G Practical legal analysis and H Drafting cautions
The safest analytical sequence for any short-term EU/EFTA case is this. First, identify the legal character
of the activity: Swiss employment, posting, self-employed service provision, or a non-productive
business presence. Secondly, identify whether the person is taking up residence in Switzerland or
remains based abroad. Thirdly, identify whether the planned activity falls within the AFMP service
liberalisation or outside it. Fourthly, apply the correct day-counting and deadline rule for that
category. If any of those steps is skipped, the risk of misclassification is high.
Where there is a Swiss employer, the analysis should start from the Swiss-employment rule, not from
the posting rules. The worker is entering the Swiss labour market directly. If the employment is for up
to three months in the calendar year, the route is notification; if longer, the worker needs the
appropriate residence permit, and if resident in Switzerland must register with the commune within 14
days and before starting work. The route does not become “posted work” just because the work is brief.
Where there is a foreign employer and the foreign employment relationship remains in place, the
question is whether the person is being posted to provide a service in Switzerland. The presence of a
Swiss client strongly points to posting, but a Swiss external client is not essential: the Posted Workers
Act also covers work in a Swiss group entity. The official definition therefore focuses on who remains the
employer, who bears the cost and direction, and whether the assignment is carried out under a service
relationship or within a group company.
Where the provider is self-employed, the key question is whether the person is genuinely carrying on
an independent cross-border service activity from an EU/EFTA base, rather than working as a disguised
employee. Official posting-platform material expressly warns about pseudo-self-employment. That
matters because genuine self-employed service providers can use the notification route within the
AFMP window, whereas false self-employment can trigger both immigration and labour-law problems.
The role of the Swiss recipient or client should be described carefully. A Swiss counterparty may
matter in at least three different ways. First, it may be the Swiss employer in a direct employment case.

Secondly, it may be the Swiss service recipient in a posting case. Thirdly, it may simply be the Swiss

therefore appear in factually similar scenarios that produce very different immigration consequences.
The decisive factors are not the presence of a Swiss recipient alone, but whether there is a Swiss
employment contract, whether the foreign employer remains in charge, and whether the activity in
Switzerland is actually productive.
On business visits versus productive work, internal content should be deliberately conservative.
SEM’s annex examples show that many familiar short-term commercial activities are not notifiable:
attending seminars, joining workshops as a participant, product launches by senior executives, contract
negotiations, customer relationship meetings, internal strategy meetings and pure goods delivery. But
once the person starts contributing productive value in Switzerland — speaking or training, carrying out
consulting, working on an internal project, performing measurements, repairs, maintenance,
implementation, assembly or installation, or conducting active customer acquisition — the official
examples move to the notifiable side. Mixed-purpose visits are especially sensitive. If the person will do
both meeting activity and productive activity, the safer internal position is usually to treat the trip as
work for Swiss notification/permit analysis.
On when notification is sufficient, the answer is narrow. Notification is sufficient only where the
correct category is engaged, the relevant threshold is not exceeded, the service is not one of the
excluded types, the correct deadline is respected, and no separate local resident-registration or
residence-permit duty has been triggered by the person’s actual residence pattern. It is therefore wrong
to say that “up to 90 days equals notification” without more. That shorthand fits only part of the law.
On when notification is unavailable or insufficient, the main triggers are: the activity is actually Swiss
employment beyond three months; the service provision exceeds 90 actual workdays in the calendar
year; the case involves staff leasing, private employment agency activity or certain licensed financial
services; the third-country posted worker lacks the EU/EFTA labour-market integration condition; or the
person has moved from mere short-term work into local residence requiring commune registration and
permit processing. For service provision beyond 90 days, SEM’s official factsheet says the case falls
outside the AFMP and into the FNIA regime with labour-market checks and quotas.
On salary and working conditions, drafting should not suggest that online notification constitutes
substantive wage approval. SEM’s directives say expressly that notification confirmation does not
confirm compliance with Swiss minimum wage and working conditions. For posted workers, those
conditions derive from the Posted Workers regime and can include minimum wage, working and rest
time, holidays, occupational health and safety and other protected matters. Swiss-employment cases
are of course governed by Swiss labour law too, but VFP article 9(1bis) notably says that, for Swiss-
employment notifications, the wage itself does not have to be reported in the notification.
On sector-specific overlays, two points matter. First, certain sectors trigger first-day notification
rather than the eight-day-free period. Secondly, regulated professions involve a separate SERI
declaration and qualification-checking process for EU/EFTA service providers even where the
immigration side stays within the 90-day route. Internal content should therefore avoid suggesting that
immigration notification is the only compliance step.
Drafting cautions for future internal content

Use short-term Swiss-employment wording only when there is a Swiss employer and the worker is not
being posted by a foreign employer.
Do not collapse “up to three months” and “up to 90 workdays” into a single universal rule. The former
is the primary Swiss-employment threshold; the latter is the service-provider threshold under the AFMP.
Do not treat the eight-day exemption as a general rule for all work. It applies to postings and self-
employed service provision in ordinary sectors, not to Swiss employment, and not to first-day sectors.
Do not rely on “business visit” labels where the facts show productive activity in Switzerland. The
official SEM annex uses substance-based examples, not titles.
Do not imply that notification disposes of all residence-registration questions. Federal and cantonal
materials show that local resident registration may still matter where residence in Switzerland is
actually taken up.
Do not imply that services beyond 90 days carry an automatic right to a permit. Official SEM material
says there is no entitlement in such service cases; beyond the AFMP window, the FNIA framework
applies.
Do not present notification confirmation as wage clearance or as authorisation for filing late. SEM says
the confirmation does not validate deadline breaches and does not confirm wage conformity.
Open questions and limitations
Federal materials are strongest on the notification categories, day limits and deadlines, but thinner on
the interaction between notification-based work and local resident registration where a person in
fact lives in Switzerland for part of the assignment. On that point, cantonal practice is important and
may vary procedurally; the Vaud circular is therefore best used as an official practice example rather
than as a statement of uniform pan-Swiss black-letter law. In addition, the SEM cross-border service-
provider factsheet currently listed by SEM is dated February 2023; it remains useful and appears still
current, but time-sensitive operational details should be cross-checked against the 2026 notification
page and user guide.
