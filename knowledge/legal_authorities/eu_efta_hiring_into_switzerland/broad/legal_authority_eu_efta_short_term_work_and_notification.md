# ChatGPTSwiss legal authority pack on short-term EU/ EFTA work and notification in Switzerland

## A Executive legal summary

For EU/EFTA nationals and EU/EFTA-based service providers, Swiss law does **not** have a single "short-term work" route. It has three distinct legal patterns that must be kept separate: **short-term** **employment with a Swiss employer, posting by an EU/EFTA employer,** and **genuine self-employed** **cross-border service provision from an EU/EFTA state.** Each pattern rests on a different combination of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU, Annex I to that Agreement, the Swiss Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons, and the Posted Workers legislation. Notification is available only for specified categories and within specified limits; it is not a universal substitute for residence registration or a permit.1

The basic federal position is clear. EU/EFTAnationals employed **by** **a** **Swiss employer** **for** **up** **to** **three** **months** **in** **a** **calendar** **year** do not need a residence permit, but the Swiss employer must make an online notification **no** **later** **than** **the** **day** **before** work starts. If the employment will exceed three months in the calendar year, a residence permit is required instead. Official SEM material aligns this with the AFMP and the Swiss implementing ordinance. 2

**For posted workers** and **self-employed service providers** established in an EU/EFTA state,the AFMP liberalises service provision only up to **90** **days** **of** **actual work** **per calendar year.** In ordinary sectors, notification is required only once the work in Switzerland exceeds **eight days** **in** **thecalendar** **year;** in listed higher-control sectors, notification is required **from** **the** **first** **day.** These sectors include construction, building ancillary trades, gardening/landscaping, hospitality, cleaning, security, itinerant trade and the sex industry, although official materials phrase the itinerant-trade exception sligghtly differently in different places. 3

The decisive boundary between a notifiable activity and a non-notifiable business visit is **substance,** **not** **label.** SEM's official directives treat seminars, conference attendance without speaking, representative appearances, contract negotiations, internal strategy meetings and pure goods delivery as examples of **non-notifiable** activity. By contrast, on-the-job training integrated into the work process, speaking at a conference, consulting, project implementation, internal project work, measurements for a concrete project, repairs, maintenance, installation and customer-acquisition activity are treated as **productive,** and therefore notifiable where the immigration rules otherwise apply. The directives say expressly that the table is illustrative and that, as a general principle, **productive** **activities** **must** **be** **notified.** 4

Notification can also be **unavailable** even for EU/EFTA-linked services. The AFMP service liberalisation does not extend to **temporary/interim agency activities, staff leasing** from abroad,or certain **financial services** **requiring** **prior** **authorisation.** SEM's official material states that direct and indirect staffing supply from abroad is not permitted and that the notification procedure does not apply in those cases. 5

Residence registration must also be handled carefully. Federal SEM guidance clearly requires EU/EFTA nationals who **take up** **residence** in **Switzerland** for gainful activity of more than three months to

register with the commune **within 14 days of arrival and before starting work,** and then obtain the appropriate permit. Federal material is thinner on local resident-registration duties where a person works under notification but in practice also resides in Switzerland; cantonal guidance therefore matters. Vaud, for example, states that where consecutive online notifications across year-end mean actual residence in the commune goes beyond 90 days, commune registration becomes mandatory from day 91. This is best treated as a **cantonal practice** point, not as a universally expressed fedderal rule. 6

**For posted workers** and **self-employed service providers,** the day-counting rules are technical and important. Official SEM guidance states that both the **eight-day** **notification-free** **period** and the **90-day maximum** are counted against **the posting company and the individual worker.** The company's quota is not multiplied by the number of workers sent on the same day: three workers deployed simultaneously for five working days use **five** **company** **days,** not fifteen. By contrast, if they are sent in sequence on different days, the company uses the full number of sequential working days. SEM also states that if Saturdays, Sundays or public holidays are included in the notification,those days are debited too, even if no work is actually performed on them. 21

For **short-term employment with a Swiss employer,** the logic is different. The limit is tied to the worker's employment in Switzerland for up to **three** **months** **per** **calendar** **year;** SEM annex material says that a Swiss employer may notify several EU/EFTA employees, provided each employee's own contract is limited to three months in the calendar year. The company-wide "90-day account" logic belongs to the posting/service-provider side, not to Swiss employment as such. 22

The Swiss day-counting and notificationn rules must not be confused with Schengen short-stay counting. AFMP service limits are expressed as **90** **days** of **actual work** in **a** **calendar year,** while Swiss-

## D Primary legal texts and E Official guidance / administrative materials

## D Primary legal texts

The core treaty source is the **AFMP.** Article 5 of the Agreement gives service providers a right to provide services for up to **90** **days** of **actual work** **in** **a** **calendar** **year.** Annex I then breaks the regime down further: Article 17 defines the protected service-provider category, Article 20 removes the need for a residence permmit for service providers within the 90-day window, Article 21 fixes the **90** **actual** **workdays** **per** **calendar** **year** ceiling, and Article 22 preserves host-staterules on posted-worker conditions while excluding temporary/interim agencies and certain financial services from the liberalised regime. On the worker side, Annex I Article 6 says that an employed person hired by an employer in the host state needs no residence permit if employed for **up** **to** **three** **months,** but does need one if employed for more than three months. 38

The principal Swiss implementing ordinance is the **VFP/OLCP.** The current consolidated text available through a Swiss law-text mirror dated **1 January** **2026** provides that EU/EFTA nationals who work in Switzerland for **not** **more** **than** **three** **months** **in** **a** **calendar year** do not need a short-stay permit,and that, for Swiss employment up to three months and for self-employed service provision up to 90workdays, the notification procedure under the Posted Workers legislation applies mutatis mutandis. The same article states expressly that, for Swiss employment of up to three months, the notification must be filed **at the** **latest** **on** **the** **day** **before** the activity begins. The same consolidated text also contains a specific offence provision imposing a fine of up to **CHF** **5,000** for breach of the notification duty under article 9(1bis). 39

For postings, the decisive domestic labour-market text is the **Posted** **Workers** **Act.** Article 1 defines posting by reference to a foreign employer sending employees to Switzerland either to perform work under a contract with the service recipient or to work in a Swiss establishment or undertaking belonging to the same corporate group. Article 6 requires advance notification before the assignment, including identity, wage, the activity in Switzerland and the place of performance, and states that work may begin only **eight** **days** **after** notification, subject to ordinance-level exceptions. Article 2, as summarised by SECO, covers minimum pay, working time and rest, holidays, occupational safety and health, protection of vulnerable workers and equal treatment. 40

## E Official guidance and administrative materials

The most operationally useful federal source is SEM's 2026 page **"Notification** **procedure for** **short-term** **work** **in** **Switzerland"** and the linked **2026** **user** **guide.** These sources identify the three eligible categories, explain the difference between the Swiss-employment deadline and the posting/service-provider deadline, set out the eight-day notification-free period and the first-day sectors, explain emergency filing, and confirm that the competent authority is the **canton** **of the** **place** **of** **work/service** **in** **Switzerland.** They are administrative guidance, not legislation, but they are the clearest current official explanations of the system. 41

Also important are the **SEM** **2026** **directives** and the 2024 annexes that remain linked from the 2026notification page. These materials are particularly valuable on two technical points that public-facing summaries often leave vague: first, the dual day-counting logic for postings; second, the distinction between **notifiable productive activity and non-notifiable meetings/training/business presence.** The annex material says expressly that the table is not exhaustive and that productive activities are, as a matter of principle, notifiable. For internal drafting, this annex is the best official source on the "business visit versus productive work" boundary, but it should still be used conservatively because it is illustrative rather than codified black-letter law. 42

SECO's foreign-employment and posting pages provide the labour-market overlay. They distinguish **Swiss** **employment** from **posting,** confirm that foreign employers posting workers must comply with Swiss minimum working and salary conditions, explain the 8-day rule and the first-day sectors, and point to the posting platform and sanctions architecture. The official posting platform also clarifies that notification for a genuine self-employed service provider is possible, while minimum posted-worker conditions do not generally apply to a genuinely self-employed person; it also warns about **false** **self-employment.** 43

Two SEM factsheets remain useful even though they are not all recent. The **January** **2025** factsheet on residing in Switzerland and taking up gainful activity states the basic three-month employment rule and the permit consequences beyond three months. The **February 2023** factsheet on cross-border service provision remains listed by SEMin January 2026 and is still the clearest concise official explanation of what falls inside and outside the AFMP service regime, including the fact that service provision beyond 90 days falls out of the AFMP and into **FNIA-based** **labour-market** **control and** qjuotas.Because this service-provider factsheet is older, it shouldd be treated as useful but not as the sole source for time-sensitive operational detail. 44

## F Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal sources do not alter the federal legal framework, but they matter for process, local registration and the tone of enforcement. Zürich's official page coonfirms the classic three-category structure, the first-day sectors, the strict eight-day prenotification requirement for service provision, and the need to distinguish notifiable from non-notifiable activity by reference to the AFMP directives. It also states plainly that wvork before the expiry of the eight-day period is prohibited.45

Geneva's official business portal is helpful on threshold framing. It treats missions by an EU/EFTA undertaking or independent provider of **90 days per year or 3 consecutive months or less** as notifiable to the Confederation, while longer assignments require **a** **cantonal authorisation** and should be filed **6** **to** **8** **weeks** before the mission starts. Geneva therefore illustrates a practical point worth reflecting in internal drafting: the legal analysis should look not only at the abstract AFMP service right, but also at the operational consequences once an assignment is expected to run beyond the notification window. 46

Basel-Stadt's official pages neatly separate the regimes. One page says that posted workers and self-employed persons from the EU/EFTA do not need a work permit for up to **90** **effective** **working days** **per** **calendar** **year** and are merely subject to notification, while another page says that EU/EFTA nationals taking up employment in Switzerland for up to **90** **days** require no permit but the employer must notify. Basel-Stadt also expressly reminds people who **take** **up** **residence** in the canton that they must register with the Einwohneramt within **14** **days.** 47

<!-- 8 -->

Ticino's official page is a useful Italian-language confirmation of the same federal structure. It states that EU/EFTA citizens and posted workers from EU/EFTA companies do not need a permit for gainful activity lasting under 90 days, but must use the notification procedure; for Swiss employment, the Swiss employer must notify **at** **least the** **day** **before** the activity begins; and the listed sectors trigger first-day notification. 48

Vaud's internal circular to communal resident-registration offices is especially important as a **practice** **point** on residence registration. It states that, in the rare case of **two** **successive** **SEM** **online** **notifications** straddling the end and beginning of a calendar year, the person must register with the commune from the **91st** day, even if no residence-permit application has yet been filed. This does not rewrite federal notification law, but it is a significant warning against treating notification as if it automatically discharges alI local residence-registration duties. 49

## G Practical legal analysis and H Drafting cautions

The safest analytical sequence for any short-term EU/EFTA case is this. First, identify the **legal character** of the activity: Swiss employment, posting, self-employed service provision, or a non-productive business presence. Secondly, identify whether the **person** is **taking** **up** **residence** **in** **Switzerland** or remains based abroad. Thirdly, identify whether the planned activity falls within the **AFMP** **service** **liberalisation** or outside it. Fourthly, apply the correct **day-counting** **and** **deadline** **rule** for that category. If any of those steps is skipped, the risk of misclassification is high. 50

Where there is a **Swiss** **employer,** the analysis should start from the Swiss-employment rule, not from the posting rules. The worker is entering the Swiss labour market directly. If the employment is for up to three months in the calendar year, the route is notification; if longer, the worker needs the appropriate residence permit, and if resident in Switzerland must register with the commune within 14days and before starting work. The route does not become "posted work" just because the work is brief. 7

Where there is a **foreign** **employer** and the foreign employment relationship remains in place, the question is whether the person is being posted to provide a service in Switzerland. The presence of a Swiss client strongly points to posting, but a Swiss external client is not essential: the Posted Workers Act also covers work in a Swiss group entity. The official definition therefore focuses on who remains the employer, who bears the cost and direction, and whether the assignment is carried out under a service relationship or within a group company. 51

Where theprovider is **self-employed,** the key question is whether the person is genuinely carrying on an independent cross-border service activity from an EU/EFTA base, rather than working as a disguised employee. Official posting-platform material expressly warns about pseudo-self-employment.That matters because genuine self-employed service providers can use the notification route within the AFMP window, whereas false self-employment can trigger both immigration and labour-law problems. 18

**The** role **of** **the Swiss** **recipient** or **client** should be described carefully. A Swiss counterparty may matter in at least three different ways. First, it may be the Swiss employer in a direct employment case. Secondly, it may be the Swiss service recipient in a posting case. Thirdly, it may simply be the Swiss meeting counterparty or host in a non-productive business-visit case.The same Swiss entity can therefore appear in factually similar scenarios that produce very different immigration consequences. The decisive factors are not the presence of a Swiss recipient alone, but whether there is a Swiss employment contract, whether the foreign employer remains in charge, and whether the activity in Switzerland is actually productive. 52

<!-- 9 -->

On **business** **visits** **versus** **productive** **work,** internal content should be deliberately conservative. SEM's annex examples show that many familiar short-term commercial activities are **not** notifiable: attending seminars, joining workshops as a participant, product launches by senior executives, contract negotiations, customer relationship meetings, internal strategy meetings and pure goods delivery. But once the person starts contributing productive value in Switzerland-speaking or training, carrying out consulting,working on an internal project, performing measurements, repairs, maintenance, implementation, assembly or installation, or conducting active customer acquisition-the official examples move to the notifiable side. Mixed-purpose visits are especially sensitive. If the person will do both meeting activity and productive activity, the safer internal position is usually to treat the trip as work for Swiss notification/permit analysis. 4

**On** **when** **notification** is **sufficient,** the answer is narrow. Notification is sufficient only where the correct category is engaged, the relevant threshold is not exceeded, the service is not one of the excluded types, the correct deadline is respected, and no separate local resident-registration or residence-permit dduty has been triggered by the person's actual residence pattern. It is therefore wrong to say that "up to 90 days equals notification" without more. That shorthand fits only part of the law. 53

**On when notification is unavailable or insufficient,** the main triggers are**:** the activity is actually Swiss employment beyond three months; the service provision exceeds 90 actual workdays in the calendar year; the case involves staff leasing, private employment agency activity or certain licensed financial services; the third-country posted worker lacks the EU/EFTA labour-market integration condition; or the person has moved from mere short-term work into local residence requiring commune registration and permit processing. For service provision beyond 90 days, SEM's official factsheet says the case falls outside the AFMP and into the FNIA regime with labour-market checks and quotas. 54

**On** **salary** **and** **working** **conditions,** drafting should not suggest that online notification constitutes substantive wage approval. SEM's directives say expressly that notification confirmation does **not** confirm compliance with Swiss minimum wage and working conditions. For posted workers, those conditions derive from the Posted Workers regime and can include minimum wage, working and rest time, holidays, occupational health and safety and other protected matters. Swiss-employment cases are of course governed by Swiss labour law too, but VFP article 9(1bis) notably says that, for Swiss-employment notifications, the wage itself does **not** have to be reported in the notification.55

**On** **sector-specific** **overlays,** two points matter. First, certain sectors trigger **first-day** **notification** rather than the eight-day-free period. Secondly, regulated professions involve a separate **SERI** **declaration and qualification-checking process** for EU/EFTA service providers even where the immigration side stays within the 90-day route. Internal content should therefore avoid suggesting that immigration notification is the only compliance step. 56

**Drafting cautions for future internal content**

<!-- 10 -->

Use short-term Swiss-employment wording only when there is a **Swiss** **employer** and the worker is not being posted by a foreign employer. 10

Do not collapse **"up** **to** **three** **months"** and **"up** **to** **90** **workdays"** into a single universal rule. The former is the primary Swiss-employment threshold; the latter is the service-provider threshold under the AFMP. 57

Do not treat the **eight-day** **exemption** as a general rule for all work. It applies to postings and self-employed service provision in ordinary sectors, not to Swiss employment, and not to first-day sectors. 17

Do not rely on "business visit" labels where the facts show **productive** **activity** in Switzerland. The official SEM annex uses substance-based examples, not titles. 20

Do not imnply that notification disposes of all **residence-registration** questions. Federal and cantonal materials show that local resident registration may still matter where residence in Switzerland is actually taken up. 36

Do not imply that services beyond 90 days carry an **automatic** **right** to a permit. Official SEM material says there is **no** **entitlement** in such service cases; beyond the AFMP window, the FNIA framework applies. 58

Do not present notification confirmation as wage clearance or as authorisation for filing late. SEM says the confirmation does not validate deadline breaches and does not confirm wage conformity. 59

## Open questions and limitations

Federal materials are strongest on the notification categories, day limits and deadlines, but thinner on the interaction between **notification-based work and local resident registration** where **a** person in fact lives in Switzerland for part of the assignment. On that point, cantonal practice is important and may vary procedurally; the Vaud circular is therefore best used as an official practice example rather than as a statement of uniform pan-Swiss black-letter law.In addition, the SEM cross-border service-provider factsheet currently listed by SEM is dated **February 2023;** it remains useful and appears still currennt, but time-sensitive operational details should be cross-checked against the 2026 notification page and user guide. 60
