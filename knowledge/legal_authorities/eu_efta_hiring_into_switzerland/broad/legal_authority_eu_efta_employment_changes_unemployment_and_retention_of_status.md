# Swiss legal authority pack on EU/EFTAemployment changes, unemployment andretention of status in Switzerland

# Executive legal summary

Swiss residence rights for EU/EFTA nationals who live in Switzerland for work are not based on an unlimited freedom to stay regardless of circumstances. They are anchored principally in the Agreement on the Free Movemment of Persons, especially Article 4 and Article 7 of the Agreement and Annex I, and are then administered in Switzerland through the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons and, subsidiarily, the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration where the Agreement does not provide a more favourable rule. The SEM's 2026 OLCP Directives expressly state that the Federal Act applies only subsidiarily to EU/EFTA cases, while the Agreement remains the primary source of residence rights. 1

For an EU/EFTA worker, the baseline position is generous but conditional. A worker with an employment contract of at least one year is entitled to a residence permit lasting at least five years; a worker whose employment lasts more thanthree months but less than one year receives a permit corresponding to the contract length. The permit is valid throughout the issuing State's territory, and Annex I protects professional and geographical mobility, including changing employer, changing occupation, and moving from salaried work to self-employment. Swiss administrative guidance reflects that position and states that, for employed EU/EFTA nationals, changing canton, employer or profession does not in itself require a new authorisation. 2

That freedom of movement does **not** mean that job loss is immigration-neutral. Switzerland now uses Article 61a FNIA to define when residence rights end after involuntary unemployment, and the SEM Directives implement that provision in detail. The effect depends on whether the employment ceased within the first twelve months of stay or later, whether the unemployment was involuntary, whether the person is registered with the employment office, whether unemployment benefits continue, and whether a new status under the Agreement exists. When employment stops during the first twelve months, residence usually continues for six months for job search, or until unemployment benefits end if they continue longer; when employment stops after the first twelve months, residence continues for six months from the cessation, or six months after unemployment benefits end if those benefits outlast the initial six months. If no new job is found by the statutory deadline, the cantonal authority is to revoke the permit or formally declare the end of the residence right. 3

Swiss law and practice therefore distinguish sharply between **worker status,** **temporary** **continuation** **after involuntary unemployment, pure jobseeker status, self-employed residence, economically** **inactive** **residence,** and the distinct **right** to **remain** after the end of economic activity. Internal drafting should preserve those distinctions. In particular, it would be wrong to say simply that an EU/ EFTA permit holder may always remain in Switzerland after losing work, or that reliance on social assistance is always irrelevant. Both propositions are materially misleading in Swiss practice. 4

# Structural overview of employment changes and retention of status

The cleanest way to analyse these cases is as asequence of status questions rather than as a single "permit renewal" question. First, is the person still a **worker** within the meaning of the Agreement because they hold genuine and effective employment? Secondly, if not, do they still benefit from the Agreement because they are within a period of **temporary continuation** **after** **involuntary** **unemployment?** Thirdly, if not, can they remain as **a jobseeker** or as an **economically inactive** person with sufficient means and health insurance? Fourthly, do they fall within the distinct **right** to **remain** after the end of economic activity? The SEM Directives and the treaty structure both support that layered approach. 5

For current workers, Switzerland gives broad professional and geographical mobility. Annex I protects mobility, and SEM guidance says that EU/EFTA short-stay permits, residence permnits and settlement permits are valid throughout Switzerland. A move to another canton does not require a fresh authorisation procedure; the person need only present the existing permit when declaring arrival at the new place of residence. Likewise, SEM guidance says that a change of canton, employer or profession does not require authorisation for employed EU/EFTA nationals. That is the core legal reason why ordinary employer changes are usually administratively light. 6

The position is more qualified for a move into self-employment. The Agreement protects transition from salaried work to self-employment as a form of mobility, but Swiss administration still requires proof of an **effective** independent activity. SEM guidance says that self-employed EU/EFTA nationals obtain an initial five-year permit only if they prove that they are established or wish genuinely to establish themselves for that purpose; cantons may later demand further proof and revoke the permit if the conditions cease to be met. The decisive elements are a genuine economic activity in Switzerland, regular income, and non-dependence on social assistance. Geneva's official guidance illustrates the administrative handling: an EU/EFTA national already resident in Geneva with a valid B or L permit who wishes to work as self-employed must notify the OCPM so the permit can be updated. 7

Reduced work is not automatically fatal, but it is one of the main points at which an apparently straightforward case becomes vulnerable. SEM guidance states that part-time or short-term work must be examined carefully and that activity may fail to qualify if it is merely marginal and ancillary. The guidance, drawing on case law, treats irregularity, short duration and low remuneration as relevant indicators. It also makes clear that multiple part-time jobs may be aggregated, and that the question is whether the activity remains real andd effective and sufficient, in context, to preserve worker status rather than becoming a nominal foothold. 8

The concept often described as "retained worker status" is therefore time-limited and context-specific in Swiss law. Annex I prevents withdrawal of a valid worker permit **solely** because the person is involuntarily unemployed or temporarily incapacitated, and it allows the first renewal to be limited to one year where the person has been involuntarily unemployed for more than twelve consecutive months. Swiss law then operationalises those ideas through Article 61a FNIA. The FederaI Supreme Court has treated Article 61a as compatible with the Agreement, despitethe apparent tension between the six-month statutory deadlines and Annex I Article 6(6). Internal content should therefore state the Swiss rule exactly as applied, not as an abstract or unlimited treaty entitlement. 9

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

## Primary legal texts

The core treaty framework is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. Article 4 guarantees the right of residence and access to economic activity under Annex I, and Article 7 lists the associated rights of equal treatment, professional and geographical mobility, the right to remain after the end of economic activity, and family-member residence and economic activity. Those are the treaty foundations for the relative ease of job mobility and for the need to distinguish between ongoing worker rights and later "right to remain" cases. 28

Within Annex I, the most important provisions for workers are the rules giving a five-year title to workers employed for at least one year, a contract-length title to workers employed for more than three months but less than one year, territorial validity of the title, and protection against withdrawal of a

<!-- 9 -->

valid permit **solely** because of involuntary unemployment duly recorded by the competent employment office or temporary incapacity caused by illness or accident. Annex I also allows the first renewal to be limited to one year when involuntary unemployment has lasted more than twelve consecutive months. 10

Annex I also expressly protects mobility. For workers, mobility includes changing employer, employment,profession, and moving from salaried work to self-employed activity; equal treatment extends to conditions of employment and work, including re-employment and vocational reintegration where a person has fallen unemployed. For independent persons, Annex I gives five-year residence titles on proof of independent activity and protects movement from self-employment back to salaried work. 29

The relevant Swiss implementing texts are the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons and the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. SEM guidance identifies Article 18 OLCP as the key provision on residence for job search, Article 16 OLCP as the main rule on sufficient means, and Article 23 OLCP as the administrative basis for revocation or non-renewal when the factual conditions for the permit are no longer met. SEM also states that the Federal Act applies only subsidiarily in EU/EFTA cases. 30

A central Swiss statutory provision is Article 61a FNIA, which governs the end of residence rights for EU/ EFTA permit holders after involuntary cessation of employment. Although the present research environment did not return a clean Fedlex article text, the official federal explanatory report and the SEM Directives provide a sufficiently reliable statement of the rule: different deadlines apply depending on whether employment ceases during the first twelve months of stay or later, and unemployment benefits can prolong the relevant period. 31

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The single most useful administrative source is the SEM **Directives** **et** **commentaires concernant** **l'ordonnance** **sur** la **libre** **circulation** **des** **personnes,** edition OLCP-1/2026. The directives integrate the current Swiss administrative reading of the treaty, the ordinance and the main case law. They are especially valuable on mobility, part-time and marginal work, self-employment proof, renewal outcomes, fixed-term contract expiry, and post-unemployment timelines. 32

SEM's fact sheets remain useful as concise official summaries. The 2025 fact sheet on residence for gainful activity states that an EU/EFTA permit for work is valid throughout Switzerland and that its holder may change work and employer; the 2023 fact sheet on residence without gainful activity summarises the sufficient-means test, the automatic renewal logic for non-active B permits, and the separate rules for jobseekers; the 2024 family reunification fact sheet explains the derivative and duration-linked nature of family residence. 33

For legislative intent on social-assistance consequences, the official federal explanatory report to the 2022 consultation on restricting social-assistance benefits is important. It records that, since 1 July 2018, first-time EU/EFTA entrants for job-search are excluded from social assistance, and that after involuntary termination of employment EU/EFTA nationals retain worker status, and therefore equal-treatment rights, only for the periods laid down in Article 61a FNIA. Although the consultation concerned another reform project, those passages accurately summarise the existing legal framework the Confederation was working from. 34

## Cantonal practice notes

Official cantonal materials generally confirm the federal picture of broad mobility coupled with administrative notification and renewal practices. Zurich's public guidance says that EU/EFTA nationals need a residence permit only where employment lasts more than three months, and that permits are issued via communal registration; for self-employed residence, Zurich emphasises proof of effective and existence-sustaining business activity. 35

Luzern is unusually explicit about job changes. Its migration office states that, for EU-27/EFTA nationals with a valid L, B or C permit for work, **a change of job is possible without a permit and without** **notification.** Luzern also states, on its L-permit renewal page, that a person staying only to seek work after entry may obtain a three-month short-stay permit, extendable up to one year on proof of means, search efforts and realistic prospects, and that extension for gainful employment is possible where there is a guaranteed workload of at least twelve hours per week. That last hourly threshold should be treated as a cantonal practice point, not as a federal treaty rule. 36

Bern's migration service states that an EU/EFTA national who loses their job or whose fixed-term contract ends may generally remain in Swwitzerland for six months as a jobseeker, and that an EU/EFTA L-holder changing job should submit the new contract through the commune so the migration service can verify the case and, if appropriate, issue a new short-stay permit. That is consistent with the federal rule that a new contract shorter than one year usually leads to an L permit rather than continued B-permit residence. 37

Geneva's official pages are helpful on administrative handling. Geneva states that holders of a durable B permit do not need to announce changes of employment. It also states that an EU/EFTA national already resident in Geneva with a valid B or L permit who wants to become self-employed must notify the OCPM to have the permit updated. On renewal, Geneva says that a B-durable or C holder normally receives an expiry notice one month before expiry; if the renewal request is filed in time, a permit holder whose expired document had already allowed work may continue working pending the decision. Geneva also requires L and "B conditionné" renewal requests to be filed at least fifteen days before expiry. 38

Ticino's older but still official notice for EU/EFTA L-permit holders illustrates the continuing role of cantonal notification duties even where substantive mobility rights are broad. Ticino states that the L permit is valid throughout Switzerland, but requires the holder to notify the migration office within fourteen days of changes of address, civil status, family composition, or transfer to another commune or canton, and to request renewal at least fourteen days before expiry. Because this notice dates from 2017, it is best used as a practice example rather than as a standalone authority on current federal law. 39

## Practical legal analysis

The decisive point for internal drafting is that Swiss EU/EFTA residence cases should be framed around the **current** **residence** **basis,** not just the label on the permit. A B permit issued for a five-year period does not guarantee a right to remain for that whole period if the underlying Agreement conditions fall away. Swiss law allows the authorities to revoke or decline to prolong EU/EFTA permits where,because the facts have changed, the requirements for the permit are no longer met. In that sense, the permit evidences a status; it does not freeze it. 40

<!-- 11 -->

For ordinary employer changes during ongoing genuine employment, the Agreement is liberal. The internal content system can safely state that EU/EFTA workers generally enjoy free professional mobility and do not usually need prior immigration authorisation to change employer, role or canton. What it should not say is that nothing needs to be done administratively. Official canton pages still often require registration, updated documentation, or permit replacement where the newv employment basis changes the correct permit type. That is most visible whena B holder who becomes unemployed then takes a contract of less than one y/ear; federal guidance treats that as a switch onto an L permnit rather than a simple continuation of the B. 41

Part-time cases need especially careful drafting. Switzerland does not appear to apply a single statutory percentage or salary threshold at federal level. Instead, SEM tells decision-makers to ask whether the work is real and effective or whether it has become merely marginal and ancillary. It gives case-law illustrations at both ends of the spectrum and says that several part-time jobs can be combined in the assessment. This means internal content should avoid crude rules such as "any part-time job is enough" or "below x hours is not enough". The legally safer formulation is that reduced work may still support worker status, but the lower and more precarious the activity becomes, the stronger the case for reviewing whether the person must instead qualify under another basis such as jobseeker or economically inactive residence. 8

Fixed-term contract expiry is another trap. SEM expressly says that the end of a fixed-term contract, without a replacement contract, is not treated as involuntary cessation. For internal drafting, the safest way to express the consequence is not to say that the person "must leave immediately", because the next step may be a jobseeker or non-active analysis, but rather that the straightforward worker case has ended and the person's right to remain must be reassessed under the unemployment and sufficient-means framework. 42

In unemployment cases, the timeline matters. If the work ceases involuntarily during the first twelve months of stay, Swiss law is materially stricter: residence continues only for the statutory job-search period and social assistance is excluded from the moment work ends. After the first twelve months, the person is better placed: official federal material and SEM guidance indicate that worker-related equal-treatment attributes continue during the Article 61a(4) period, provided the person is duly registered with the ORP and remains within the statutory timeframe. That is why internal guidance must not collapse all unemployment cases into a single rule. 43

Pure jobseeker residence is yet another category. An EU/EFTA national coming to Switzerland solely to look for work can stay permit-free for up to three months; beyond that, a short-stay permit depends on sufficient means and can be extended only on proof of ongoing search efforts and realistic employment prospects. SEM also says that resort to social assistance ends the right of stay for these jobseekers. Accordingly, once a former worker falls out of the protected post-unemployment worker framework, the case often becomes a jobseeker or economically inactive case with a strongerfocus on means and weaker protection against removal. 44

Self-employment should also be treated cautiously in immigration content. The Agreement recognises it and protects movement into it, but Swiss authorities ask for evidence of a genuine business activity in Switzerland that yields regularincome and does not require social assistance. In practicalterms, a move from employment to self-employment can solve a residence problem only where the new self-employed basis is real and documented. A merely aspirational or low-income independent activity may leave the person outside both the worker and the self-employed categories. 45

Lastly, the distinct "right to remain" aftereconomic activity should not be confused with temporary post-unemployment continuation. SEM explains that this right allows former workers or self-employed

<!-- 12 -->

persons to keep their residence after ceasing activity in specific retirement, incapacity or related situations, and that beneficiaries keep their acquired worker-based rights even though they no longer have worker status in the ordinary sense. It is, however, a separate and limited category; it is not the default answer to ordinary unemployment. 46

### Drafting cautions

Any future internal content should avoid saying that EU/EFTA nationals may "freely stay in Switzerland as long as they want" once a permit has been issued. The safer proposition is that EU/EFTA workers enjoy a strong treaty-based residence right **while** the conditions for worker residence remain satisfied and, after involuntary unemployment, for the further periods specifically recognised by Swiss law and the Agreement. 47

It should also avoid saying that "job loss does not affect immigration status". That is wrong in Swiss law. Job loss may trigger a statutory countdown, may require ORP registration, may convert a B case into an L case, may push the person into a jobseeker or non-active category requiring sufficient means,and may lead to revocation or non-renewal if the conditions for residence are no longer met. 48

Do not treat social assistance and unemployment insurance as interchangeable. Official materials distinguish them sharply. In some first-year post-employment cases, social assistance is excluded from the date work ends. In later involuntary-unemployment cases, worker-related equal treatment may continue for the statutory period. For pure jobseekers and economically inactive residents, resort to social assistance is a central risk because sufficient means are part of the residence condition. Unemployment benefits, by contrast, may extend thestatutory deadline or count in the means calculation. 49

Do not convert cantonal process points into national substantive rules. Geneva's no-announcement rule for durable B permit holders, Luzern's no-notification approach to job changes for EU/EFTA L/B/C holders, Luzern's twelve-hours-per-week practice for L-permit extension in work cases,and Ticino's fourteen-day notification windows are helpful illustrations of administration, but they do not replace the treaty or SEM framework. 50

### Open questions and limitations

This pack is strongest on the current federal framework and on the cantonal materials expressly retrieved here. It is thinner on directly citable, article-level Fedlex text for FNIA Article 61a becausethe accessible federal-law interface did not return a clean article page in this research run; the rule has therefore been groundded through official SEM directives, official federal explanatory material, and Federal Supreme Court summaries instead. The resulting propositions are still high-confidence, but where litigation-level drafting is needed it would be sensible to cite the RS text of Article 61a directly alongside the authorities already listed here. 51

There is also a genuine complexity around the interaction between Annex I Article 6(6), Annex I Article 6(1), and FNIA Article 61a. The Federal Supreme Court has accepted the Swiss statutory approach, but internal content should still avoid over-simplified formulas about "retention of worker status" and instead describe the specific Swiss timelines. 52
