Executive legal summary
Current position. As at 24 May 2026, Switzerland has no current Croatia-specific quotas, safeguard
restrictions, labour-market priority test, or Croatia-only permit procedure in force for employment
or residence under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons. Official federal materials show
that quota provisions for Croatian workers were removed for 2025, that the final possible 2026
safeguard would only have been reactivated if 2025 admissions crossed treaty thresholds, and that
those thresholds were not reached. SEM’s 2026 overview factsheet now states that the AFMP applies to
Croatian nationals without restrictions since 1 January 2026.
For ordinary current EU/EFTA hiring into Switzerland, the governing scheme is again the standard
AFMP/EFTA one: employment of up to three months / 90 working days per calendar year is generally
handled through the notification procedure rather than a residence permit; employment beyond
three months requires an L or B EU/EFTA permit based principally on proof of employment; self-
employed EU/EFTA nationals may obtain a B EU/EFTA permit if they show a genuine self-employed
activity. Those are current general rules, including for Croatian nationals.
There are still important controls, but they are not currently Croatia-specific. Posted workers and self-
employed service providers remain subject to the notification system and to Switzerland’s
accompanying wage and working-condition controls. Where services exceed the AFMP’s 90-day
framework and are outside a specific services agreement, Swiss law falls back to the FNIA/OASA system
with labour-market verification and quotas. In addition, the job registration requirement for
occupations with high unemployment remains in force as a nationality-neutral hiring control and
must not be confused with AFMP transitional or safeguard clauses.
The only clearly identifiable other EU/EFTA-related special category in current official materials is
Liechtenstein. That is a special treaty/protocol case, but on the Swiss side Liechtenstein nationals have

Historically, however, Croatia was subject to a dense transitional regime. Protocol III inserted a Croatia-
specific structure into Article 10 AFMP: initial annual quotas, labour-market priority and wage/working-
condition checks for the transitional period, then a treaty-based safeguard clause through the tenth
year. Switzerland used those mechanisms: the original Croatia transition started on 1 January 2017;
Croatian workers obtained full free movement in 2022; Switzerland then reintroduced quotas for 2023
and 2024 under the safeguard clause; quotas were removed again for 2025; and the final possible
safeguard for 2026 was not triggered because thresholds were not met. Those historical measures
should be treated as expired, not current.
Current structure and historical timeline
The current Swiss legal structure is best understood in three layers. First, the AFMP and the parallel
EFTA framework supply the core non-discrimination and market-access rules for EU/EFTA nationals,
their qualifying family members, and certain service providers. Second, the FMPO/OLCP/VFP and SEM
guidance organise permits, notification, and cantonal implementation. Third, Switzerland’s
accompanying measures, posted-workers rules, and the job registration requirement create labour-
market controls that are not the same thing as transitional quotas or safeguard clauses.
For present drafting purposes, the decisive current answer is simple: no EU or EFTA nationality
reviewed in the current federal materials is presently subject to a live Swiss transitional quota or
safeguard restriction for access to employment in Switzerland. Croatia was the last live
enlargement-related category; the materials reviewed now treat Croatia as aligned in practice with the
general EU/EFTA regime. Older Bulgaria/Romania safeguard references appear only in historical
chronology material, not as current operative rules. This is an inference from the current 2025–2026
federal materials and should be refreshed if new announcements appear.

Primary texts and administrative materials
Primary legal texts. The central treaty text is the Agreement of 21 June 1999 on the Free Movement
of Persons, cited by Swiss authorities as SR 0.142.112.681. Croatia entered via Protocol III of 4 March
2016, which modified Article 10 AFMP to insert Croatia-specific paragraphs on transitional quotas,
labour-market controls, and the safeguard clause. SEM’s 2026 guidelines also identify the FMPO/OLCP/
VFP as SR 142.203, the FNIA/AIG as SR 142.20, and the relationship whereby FNIA applies only
subsidiarily where AFMP/EFTA rules are not more favourable.
Protocol III is the key authority for distinguishing current law from expired historical Croatia
measures. Its text provided an initial two-year quota regime, permitted continuation up to year 5 and
then year 7, and then allowed a final safeguard-style unilateral quota mechanism through year 10
where admissions exceeded treaty formulas based on prior years’ averages. It also preserved acquired
rights and mobility for persons already authorised when the protocol entered into force.
On the services side, the relevant current Swiss legal framework is not just the AFMP and FMPO. SECO’s
accompanying-measures materials identify the Posted Workers Act as SR 823.20 and the Posted
Workers Ordinance as SR 823.201. work.swiss identifies the job registration requirement as
grounded in Article 121a of the Federal Constitution, Articles 21a and 117a FNIA, and Articles 53a
ff., 58a and 63 of the Recruitment Ordinance.
Official guidance and administrative materials. For current operational content, the most
authoritative administrative materials reviewed were SEM’s current 2025–2026 factsheets and FAQ
pages. Those materials state the present rules for ordinary EU/EFTA employment, self-employment,
family reunification, cross-border commuting, and the 90-day notification system. They should be
preferred over older archived Croatia circulars except where a historical position must be documented
expressly as historical.

For Croatia specifically, the current federal administrative picture is captured by the Federal Council’s
2024, 2025 and 2026 announcements and by SECO’s Croatia page. Together these show the legal
sequence from 2023–2024 safeguard quotas, to the 2025 repeal of quota provisions, to the January
2026 confirmation that the final possible safeguard was not triggered.
A further drafting trap is that SEM now publishes both material on the current AFMP and separate
material on an updated AFMP not yet in force. The factsheets page expressly separates those two
groups. Content aimed at current Swiss hiring rules should cite the current materials unless the
purpose is to discuss future reform or to flag possible incoming change.
Cantonal implementation and practical legal analysis
Cantonal practice notes. Cantons matter because they receive notifications, issue permits, and enforce
wage-protection measures. Zurich’s current official guidance is a useful illustration: it states that EU/
EFTA nationals enjoy full geographical and professional mobility, that employment beyond three
months or 90 days requires registration with the commune and a permit process, and that
Liechtenstein is a special treaty category with separate supplementary guidance.
Geneva’s current portal mirrors the same federal structure in practice. It has separate current pathways
for EU/AELE independent workers and for detachment/service supply, and explains that EU/AELE
service provision up to 90 effective working days per calendar year is generally subject only to
notification, whereas other cases require a cantonal work authorisation. That is useful confirmation
that the cantonal handling now follows the ordinary EU/EFTA model, including for Croatian nationals.
Vaud’s 2024 report by the cantonal tripartite commission is also valuable because it states, in substance,
that the employment in Switzerland of most European nationals no longer requires an a priori check
of pay/working conditions or domestic-labour priority, while also showing that the canton actively
polices wage dumping under the accompanying-measures framework. Ticino’s tripartite commission
materials similarly emphasise observation of the labour market and corrective wage-protection
mechanisms rather than nationality-specific admission controls.
Practical legal analysis. For current Swiss employer-facing content, the most important distinction is
between ordinary employment under the AFMP and service provision / posting / work outside the
AFMP framework. Ordinary EU/EFTA hiring is now procedurally light by Swiss immigration-law
standards: short assignments up to the notification threshold are handled by notification; longer
employment is handled by L/B permits based on proof of employment; and current official materials do
not identify any live Croatia-specific quotas or labour-market priority checks.
That does not mean that all controls disappear. Swiss wage protection remains central. Posted workers
and self-employed service providers must observe notification rules, first-day notification sectors, and
minimum Swiss pay/working-condition rules under the accompanying-measures regime. For services
outside the AFMP’s 90-day framework, Swiss law can require labour-market verification and quotas
under the FNIA/OASA system. Those controls are functional and sectoral, not currently country-specific
within EU/EFTA hiring.
Self-employed persons and family members also need careful handling. Current federal materials give
EU/EFTA self-employed persons a route to a B EU/EFTA permit if genuine self-employment is
demonstrated, and they give admitted spouses and children a right to work regardless of nationality.

Historical Croatian limitations on self-employment and family-related access should therefore not be
recycled into current text.
For cross-border commuters, the current rule is likewise the standard EU/EFTA one: the worker may
hold a G EU/EFTA permit and must return to the main foreign residence at least weekly. SEM FAQ
materials say that no border-zone restrictions apply to EU/EFTA nationals. That is important because
one historical Croatian issue was the treatment of frontier workers during transition; that historical
point is no longer a current rule.
Finally, content writers should separate the job registration requirement from immigration
permission rules. The vacancy-reporting obligation is a domestic labour-market tool that can affect
recruitment timing and process even where the employee is an EU/EFTA national with AFMP rights. It
creates an internal Swiss priority mechanism for certain occupations, but it is not a Croatia safeguard
clause, not a quota, and not a nationality-based permit restriction.
Drafting cautions and source list
Drafting cautions. Do not state or imply that Croatian nationals still need a permit from day one for
ordinary Swiss employment, that they cannot use the ordinary EU/EFTA notification route for short
employment, or that they remain subject to Swiss-priority or Croatia-only wage checks. Those
propositions were true during the historical transition documented in the 2016 Croatia circular and
Protocol III, but they are not the current rule.
Do not stop the analysis at the 2024 repeal of quotas. A content item updated in late 2024 but not
refreshed in January 2026 could miss the further point that a final safeguard was still legally possible for
2026 and then was not triggered. The safest current formulation is: “No Croatia-specific quotas or
safeguard restrictions are in force as at [date].”
Do not conflate current law with the updated AFMP not yet in force. SEM’s own factsheets page
separates current materials from future-update materials. This matters especially in family-reunification
content, where SEM now publishes side-by-side explanations of the current and updated positions.
Do not write as though all EU/EFTA situations are always identical. Current law distinguishes at least
between ordinary employees, self-employed persons, posted workers, self-employed service providers,
cross-border commuters, family members, and the special Liechtenstein category. The present pack’s
main conclusion is that there are no current live Croatia-specific restrictions, not that every EU/EFTA
pathway is procedurally identical.
