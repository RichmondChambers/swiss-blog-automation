# Swiss legal authority pack on EU/EFTA employee registration and residence procedure

## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

For EU nationals, the core legal basis is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU, especially its basic provisions on entry, residence, access to employed work, services and equal treatment, together with Annex I on employed persons. For EFTA nationals, Swiss official materials state that substantially the same regime applies through the revised EFTA Convention and Swiss implementing rules. Swiss SEM guidance also confirms that the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act applies only subsidiarily where free-movement law does not provide otherwise or where Swiss domestic law is more favourable. As at 1 January 2026, Croatia is treated under the AFMP without the former transitional restrictions. 1

The most important conceptual distinction is between the **substantive** **right** to work and reside under free movement law, and the **administrative** **formalities** used by Switzerland to evidence, register and document that right. For an employed person, Annex I to the AFMP ties permit length mainly to the length of the employment relationship: contracts of one year or more, or of indefinite duration, correspond to a residence permit oof at least five years; contracts of more than three months but less than one year correspond to a permit for the contract period; employment of up to three months does not require a residence permit. Swiss SEM permit pages mirror this by describing the L EU/EFTA permit for contracts from three to twelve months and the B EU/EFTA permit for contracts of at least twelve months or indefinite duration. 2

**For short-term work with a Swiss employer** of up to three months,Switzerland uses the **notification** **procedure,** not the residence-permit route. SEM states that Swiss employershiring EU/EFTA nationals for up to three months must nnotify online no later than one day before the first working day;the general eight-day prior notice is instead the standard rule for posted workers and self-employed service providers, subject to sector-specific exceptions. Zürich's official guidance states the same distinction in clear terms. This short-term track must be kept separate from residence-based employment and from cross-border service provision or posting. 3

**For employment of more than three months with residence in Switzerland,** SEM's official pages say the EU/EFTA employee must register with the local authorities of the commune of residence within 14days of arrival **and** **before** **actually** **taking** **up** **work,** presenting a valid passport or identity card and written proof of the employment relationship. Bern, Zürich and other cantonal materials reflect this as the normal rule, although the exact intake point and documentary package vary by canton and commune. 4

**On** **start-of-work** **timing,** the treaty and SEM materials support a careful but important point: the employee does not need to wait for the physical permit card to be printed and delivered before beginning work, provided that the person is in the correct residence-permit track and the formal application/registration has been duly lodged. Annex I, Article 6(7), states that completion of residence-permit formalities must not prevent immediate employment under the contract already concluded. SEM's 2026 directives addd that, outside the short-term notification track, gainful activity may begin after the competent cantonal authorities have received the application, and Geneva's official practice states that for an EU/EFTA employee with a contract of more than four months, work may begin as soon as the complete file has been sent to the cantonal migration office, subject to separate licensing rules for regulated professions. That said, this is **not** a licence to skip registration: the filing itself remains mandatory.5

The permit system remains decentralised in practice. SEM states that the cantons are responsible for issuing permits and that application and card-issuance procedures follow cantonal practice. Vaud's official materials show a commune-first workflowwith later cantonal processing and a biometrics invitation; Ticino uses a direct cantonal migration-office route for certain applications; Geneva has its own online or postal filing routes; Bern and Zürich continue to emphasise registration at the local residents' office for residence-based workers. Missed or delayed formalities can trigger administrative fines for notification breaches, and if a required ppermit cannot in fact be issued because conditions are not met, broader FNIA/LEI sanctions may also come into play. 6

## Procedure architecture and practical legal analysis

### B. Structural overview of EU/EFTA employee registration and residence procedure

The cleanest way to structure the Swiss position is to begin with **four** **separate** **tracks.** First, there is **residence-based employment with a Swiss employer for more than three months,** which requires arrival registration and an L or B permit depending mainly on contract length. Secondly, there is **short-term employment with a Swiss employer for up to three months,** which runs through the online notification procedure and not the residence-permit route. Thirdly, there is **cross-border** **posting** **or** **service** **provision,** which is legally different and is governed by the AFMP services provisions, Swiss notification rules and, in some cases, salary and sector controls. Fourthly, there is **frontier** **work,** which concerns permit G and should not be conflated with residence in Switzerland. Both SEM and Zürich expressly warn, in substance, against treating direct hire, posting and frontier work as interchangeable categories. 7

For the **residence-based** **employee** **route,** the treaty baseline is relatively lean. Annex I, Article 6(3), says that a contracting state may not require more than the entry document and a contractual statement from the employer or written confirmation of engagement when issuing the residence permit. SEM's FAQ operationalises this by referring to a valid passport or identity card plus written confirmation of employment, including the duration of employment and the number of working hours. Bern's official guidance is in the same vein and specifically asks for an employment confirmation or contract showing the exact start date, contract duration and workload. 8

In practice, however, the employee will often have to complete **more** **than** **the** **treaty** **minimum** because Switzerland combines migration formalities with local residents-register administration and physical card production. Geneva asks for identity documents and proof of accommodation. Vaud's T1form requires personal details, Swiss address, employer identity, start date, duration, weekly hours and salary, and it lists documents such as ID and the employment contract or mission contract.Vaud's arrival form also captures date of entry, current address in Switzerland and employer details. These additional items should therefore be treated as **administrative** **filing** **practice,** not as a rewriting of the treaty's minimum permit-issuance standard. 9

The **permit** **type** is driven chniefly by the duration of the employment relationship. The AFMP itself distinguishes one year or more, more than three months but less than one year, and up to three months. SEM's permit pages express the same distinction via the B EU/EFTA permit and L EU/EFTA permit. Geneva's official page does likewise: a contract up to 364 days gives rise to L EU/EFTA, while a contract of at least one year or of indefinite duration yields B EU/EFTA valid for five years. 10

Fixed-term, open-ended and part-time work need separate treatment. **Fixed-term** **versus** **open-ended** is crucial because the length of the engagement determines L versus B. SEM's 2026 directives add an important agency-work nuance: for staff leasing or placement, authorities look to the duration of the mission or concrete employment relationship, not merely the duration of the framework agreement. **Part-time** **work** is not excluded, but SEM states that very small or very short work may fail if it is merely marginal and ancillary; the real question is whether the activity is "genuine and effective".The same directives note that multiple part-time jobs can be added together when assessing the situation. 11

**The** **start-of-work** **rule** should be drafted with great care. The strongest formulation supported by the sources is this: for residence-based EU/EFTA employment, work may generally start once the employee has entered the proper registration/permit process and the competent authority has received the relevant filing; the employee does not have to wait for the plastic permit card to arrive. That proposition is grounded in AFMP Annex I, Article 6(7), SEM's 2026 directive that activities may begin after receipt of the application by the competent cantonal authority, and cantonal practice such as Geneva's statement that work may start once the complete file has been sent. But the converse is also true: sources do **not** support saying that an EU/EFTA employee may simply beggin work first and regularise later. SEM's public guidance still says registration must be completed within 14 days of arrival and before work begins, and Bern and Ticino publish similarly strict filing-before-work instructions. 12

### G. Practical legal analysis

The best internal synthesis is that Switzerland treats the AFMP/EFTA right as the substantive source of entitlement, while using cantonal and communal procedures to verify identity, residence, address, employment evidence and document manufacture. This is why the treaty can both limit the evidence required to issue the permit and simultaneously insist that formalities not block immediate employment. It also explains why public practice materials frequently ask for housing information,local registration forms, photographs or biometrics even though the treaty's worker-status proof is narrower. In other words, the permit card is evidential and administrative in function, but the filing route is still mandatory. 13

Renewal follows the same logic. SEM's permit pages say the B permitis normally renewed for five years if the requirements remain satisfied, while first renewal may be limited to one year if the holder has been involuntarily unemployed for more than twelve consecutive months. SEM's 2026 free-movement directives add that an employer's declaration of engagement or work attestation is sufficient for B renewal when conditions are met, and similarly require an employer declaration or work certificate for extension or renewal of an L permit. If a new contract is at least one year or indefinite, the case moves into B-permit territory; if it is shorter, the person remains in or returns to the L category. 14

Address changes are nationally important but operationally local. SEM's FAQ says EU/EFTA L, B and C permits are valid across the whole of Switzerland and that a move to another canton does not require a new permit; however, the person must register with the authorities of the new canton and have the new postal address entered in the permit. The same applies to changes within the same canton or commune. Vaud's official arrival form illustrates how local register law can add stricter local timelines: it states that every move and certain status changes must be reported within eight days under cantonal

residents-register law. Content for general use should therefore state the federal rule first and present local reporting deadlines as canton- or commune-specific. 15

The **notification** **procedure** must also be kept conceptually separate from residence registration. It covers, among other categories, EU/EFTA nationals directly hired by Swiss employers for up to three months, posted workers from EU/EFTA-based undertakings, and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers. For Swiss employer hires in that short-term category, SEMI requires notification by the day before the first working day. For posted workers and self-employed service providers, the standard rule is eight days' prior notification, with sector-specific rules and emergency exceptions. Zürich's practice materials mirror this and expressly distinguish direct hire, posting and self-employment. 16

The legal risk of **missing** **or** **misusing** **the** **short-term** **track** is not merely administrative untidiness. SEM's directives state that where an EU/EFTA national in a non-authorisation-required stay breaches only the notification obligation, a sanction under Article 32a OLCP may follow, with a fine of upo to CHF 5,000. The same directives say that, for EU/EFTA nationals, breaches normally concern arrival declarations or notification duties; but where a permit cannot be issued because the substantive conditions are not met, LEI/FNIA sanctions including Articles 115 and 118 remain applicable. Ticino also warns expressly on its official website that late filing may lead to a fine. 17

#### Primary legal texts and official administrative materials

### D. Primary legal texts

The primary legal instruments for this topicare: the **Agreement** **on** **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons** between Switzerland and the EU, especially Articles 1, 4, 5 and 7 of the agreement and Article 6 of Annex I for employed persons; the **EFTA** **Convention,** especially Chapter VIII and Annex K / Appendix 1for the parallel EFTA regime; the Swiss **Ordonnance sur la libre circulation des personnes /** **Verordnung über den freien Personenverkehr (OLCP/VFP),** particularly the provisions referred to in SEM's 2026 directives on admission, notification and sanctions; and the **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** **Act (LEI/AIG/FNIA),** which applies subsidiarily and supplies, among other things,the current framework&lt; for job-loss consequences and certain sanctions. 38

For worker admission and residence, the most legally important treaty provisions are AFMP Annex I, Article 6(1) to (3) and (7): they fix the contract-length thresholds for permit duration, restrict document demands to the core worker-status evidence, and prevent residence-permit formalities from blocking immediate commencement of work. AFMP Article 5 separately frames short-duration service provision and explains why posting and cross-border services must not be conflated with direct Swiss employment. 39

<!-- 7 -->

For the EFTA side, the official EFTA Convention materials show that freedom of movement among EFTA states pursues the same four objectives as the AFMP - entry, residence, access to employed work or self-employment, brief service provision and equal treatment-and the EFTA Annex K / Appendix 1materials reflect the same residence-permit logic for employed persons. In practical Swiss administration, SEM therefore groups EU and EFTA nationals together in a single operational category. 40

##### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful federal administrative materials are the SEM pages **"Working** **in** **Switzerland", "FAQ** -**Free Movement of Persons", "Notification procedure for short-term work in Switzerland",** and the permit pages for **B** **EU/EFTA** **and** **L** **EU/EFTA.** Together they provide the public-facing administrative statement of the filing deadlines, principal evidence, short-term notification rules and permit categories. 41

The most important technical administrative source is the **SEM** **2026** **directives** **on** **the** **OLCP/VFP,** because they link public-facing rules to the underlying Swiss ordinance framework and clarify several points that do not appear as clearly on short web pages: start of work after receipt of the application, treatment of mission contracts, wvorker status in part-time cases, renewal evidence, inter-cantonal mobility and sanctions. For internal drafting work, these directives are the strongest single Swiss administrative source after the treaty text itself. 42

Official forms are also useful because they show what front-line authorities actually ask for. Vaud's T1**form** records employer identity, start date, duration, hours and salary and must be handed to the commune on registration; Vaud's **arrival form** requires entry date, present Swiss address and employer details; Geneva's page lists identity and accommodation documents; the SEM AA19-EU/EFTA flyer explains that permit manufacture and delivery follow cantonal procedure and occur after the authority records the relevant personal data. These materials are valuable not because they displace primary law, but because they evidence the practical filing architecture that employee-registration content must describe accurately. 43

## Cantonal practice notes and drafting cautions

### F. Cantonal practice notes

**Zürich.** Zürich distinguishes sharply between direct Swiss employment, posting and self-employment. For direct Swiss employment, up to 90 days per calendar year runs through the notification procedure, with filing no later than one day before work; more than 90 days requires a residence permit issued via registration at the competent commune of residence. Zürich also emphasises that EU/EFTA permit holders enjoy professional and geographical mobility, while permit issuance and residence handling remain cantonal. 44

**Bern.** Bern presents the residence-based worker route in a classic commune-first form. An EU/EFTA employee who will work for more than 90 days must prepare ID plus employment evidence showing exact start date, contract duration and workload, then register in person with the residents' office of the municipality of residence within 14 days after entry and before taking up the job. This is a useful model of the "standard" residence-based pathway. 45

<!-- 8 -->

**Vaud.** Vaud's material shows a particularly clear split between the commune and the cantonal Service de la population. The commune receives the arrival declaration and the T1 permit form with annexes; the SPOP then processes the application. Vaud also shows the later document-production stage: after first registration or renewal, the applicant waits for the SPOP invitation and then attends biometrics in Lausanne. Vaud's forms also demonstrate how local materials operationalise address reporting and factual data capture beyond the treaty minimum. 46

**Geneva.** Geneva's official page states that any EU/EFTA nnational engaged by a Swiss company on a contract of more than three months must make a residence-and-work application before starting the activity. It then gives a specifically Genevan practice point for contracts of more than four months: the future employee or employer may file, and work may begin as soon as the complete dossier has been sent to the cantonal office, unless the profession is regulated and requires a separate professional authorisation. Geneva also publishes a distinct local route for contracts of 120 days or up to four months. That is helpful evidence of cantonal variation, but it should not be generalised into a nationwide rule. 47

**Ticino.** Ticino's official site for the B permit states that workers must submit the request before starting the activity, that non-working applicants must file within 14 days from entry, and that late filing may attract a fine. Ticino thereforeillustrates a more direct cantonal filing route and an express warning about late compliance. 48

### H. Drafting cautions

Future employee-registration content should avoid five recurring errors. First, do not write that EU/EFTA employees may "work without a permit" unless the specific context is the short-term notification procedure; once the case is on the residence route, registration and permit application remain mandatory. Secondly, do **not** describe the physical card as the legal source of the right to start work; the safer formulation is that work may usually begin once the correct filing has been duly lodged with the competent authority, subject to local practice and any separate licensing requirement for regulated professions. Thirdly, do **not** standardise the responsible filing office nationwide: some cantons are commune-first, some are canton-first, and some split the process. Fourthly, do not treat posted workers, self-employed service providers and Swiss-employer hires as a single category.Fifthly,do not imply that every part-time or on-call arrangement automatically confers worker status; SEM requires the activity to be genuine and effective, not merely marginal or ancillary. 49

A further drafting caution concerns documents. The treaty baseline is narrower than the real-world filing package. Internal content should therefore distinguish between **core** **legal evidence** of **worker** status-valid ID and employer confirmation/contract-and **locally** **requested** **administrative** **documents** such as lease papers, host attestations, photographs, biometrics appointments and local forms. Stating simply that "only two documents are needed" would be too absolute for operational use, even though Article 6(3) remains the correct treaty anchor. 50

### Open questions and limitations

This pack is intentionally conservative and is not an exhaustive survey of all 26 cantons and all communes. It is strongest on federal law and on selected cantonal practice examples from Zürich, Bern, Vaud, Geneva and Ticino. Where a point is likely to vary locally - especially the intake office, the exact document set, and the sequencing between commune registration, cantonal decision and card production -the pack marks this expressly as a practice point rather than a uniform national rule.
