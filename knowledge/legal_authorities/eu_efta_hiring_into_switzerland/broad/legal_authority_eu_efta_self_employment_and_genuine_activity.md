

Swiss Legal Authority Pack on EU and EFTA Self-
Employment and Genuine Economic Activity in
## Switzerland
Internal authority pack for immigration-law content use. Scope: current Swiss law and official
administrative practice on EU/EFTA nationals seeking residence in Switzerland as self-employed
persons, with emphasis on residence rights, evidential expectations, and the distinction between
genuine self-employment, employment, sham activity, and economically inactive residence. The analysis
is based on the current AFMP framework, SEM guidance and selected official cantonal materials in force
or current as at 24 May 2026. SEM also publishes separate materials on an updated AFMP that is not
yet in force; those future-law materials should not be mixed into current-route content.
Executive legal summary
The core residence right for EU nationals who establish themselves in Switzerland as self-employed
persons arises under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, especially Article 4 of the main
Agreement and Annex I, Chapter III, Article 12. That provision grants a residence permit valid for at
least five years to a national of a contracting party wishing to become established in the other state in
order to pursue a self-employed activity, provided the person produces evidence to the competent
authorities that he or she is established or wishes to become so; renewal is for at least five years on
proof that a self-employed economic activity is being pursued. The same basic structure exists for EFTA
nationals through Annex K to the EFTA Convention and Appendix 1, Chapter III, Article 11, and SEM
states expressly that the same rules apply to EFTA citizens as to EU citizens, subject to special rules for
## Liechtenstein.
In current Swiss administrative practice, however, the route is not treated as satisfied by a mere
statement of future intention. SEM’s 2026 VFP directives state that EU/EFTA nationals entering
Switzerland to take up self-employment receive an initial permit only if, when the application is
submitted, they can prove the self-employed activity; the directives add that, where there are serious
doubts about the real and sustainable exercise of the activity and about the generation of a regular,
subsistence-level income, the authorities may require further proof during the permit’s validity or
revoke it if the conditions are no longer met. SEM’s public factsheet and FAQ use the same operative
language: a B EU/EFTA permit is issued if the person can show an effective self-employed activity, and
self-employment documents must show an activity that is subsistence-generating for the applicant
and family members.
The decisive immigration-law test is therefore whether there is a real, active and economically viable
self-employed activity in Switzerland, not merely a business shell. SEM’s directives say that proof is
supplied by establishing an undertaking or place of business in Switzerland with effective and
subsistence-generating business activity, evidenced by business books, accounts, orders and similar
materials. They also say that commercial-register entry is generally expected for commercial
undertakings or legal entities, but cannot be required in every case, notably for certain liberal
professions and artists. The cantons may not impose prohibitive evidential hurdles, but they may insist
on enough evidence to show active business operations, regular income and freedom from social-
assistance dependence.

Swiss practice also insists on a real distinction between self-employment and employment. SEM’s
directives say the question is case-specific, but decisive factors are that the activity is carried on for
one’s own account and at one’s own risk, without being bound by third-party instructions, integrated
into another undertaking’s organisation, or placed in a subordinate relationship. SECO’s official
guidance uses materially the same indicators: investment, own name and own account, own premises,
bearing costs and loss risk, freedom over working methods and hours, and work for multiple clients
point towards self-employment; acting in another’s name, following instructions, fixed hours, assigned
workplace and regular work for the same employer point towards employment.
The route must also be kept distinct from both cross-border service provision and economically
inactive residence. EU/EFTA nationals established in another EU/EFTA state may provide services in
Switzerland for up to 90 days through the notification regime, but that is not the same as becoming
established in Switzerland as a self-employed resident. For the service-provider route, SECO requires
control documents such as the notification or permit, an A1 social-security form, and a contract or client
confirmation; this is specifically aimed at verifying self-employed status and detecting sham self-
employment. By contrast, if a person in Switzerland is no longer genuinely self-employed and is not
covered by some other AFMP status, the case moves towards the rules for persons not pursuing an
economic activity, where sufficient means become central.
Failure, low income or cessation matters because SEM distinguishes the position of workers from that
of self-employed or non-active persons. For self-employed persons who have ceased work and are not
covered by another retained status, SEM states that sufficient financial means are a condition of
residence; if such persons draw public social assistance, their residence right lapses and an existing
permit may be withdrawn. At the same time, the treaty and SEM both preserve residence where the
person is temporarily unable to work because of illness or accident.
For family members, SEM states that EU/EFTA nationals holding a Swiss short-stay or residence permit
as employees, self-employed persons or economically inactive persons may bring qualifying family
members, but self-employed persons and economically inactive persons must have the financial
means necessary to support them, and suitable accommodation is required. The family members’ rights
are derivative and normally last only while the principal’s underlying AFMP right continues.
Structural overview of EU and EFTA self-employment and
genuine activity
A. Legal basis. The residence right flows from the AFMP for EU nationals and from the EFTA Convention
for EFTA nationals. The main operative treaty provisions are Article 4 AFMP and Annex I, Chapter III,
Articles 12 to 15 AFMP for self-employed persons, mirrored for EFTA nationals in Annex K and Appendix
1, Chapter III, Articles 11 to 14. Article 7 AFMP and Article 7 Annex K also matter because they secure
equal treatment, occupational and geographical mobility, post-activity stay rights and family rights.
B. Implementing structure. Swiss implementation is operationalised through the Ordinance on Free
Movement of Persons and, in practice, through SEM’s current VFP directives and public factsheets/FAQs.
In this route, the directives are especially important because they state how the treaty standard is
applied by cantonal authorities: proof of existing self-employment, effective and subsistence-generating
activity, continuing review of genuineness, and loss of status if conditions cease to be met.
C. Immigration-law question being asked. The real question is not whether the person has opened a
company in the abstract, but whether they have established a genuine economic activity in
Switzerland that is being pursued independently, on their own account and risk, and at a level capable

D. Primary legal texts
The principal treaty texts are: the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland
and the EU, especially Article 4 and Annex I, Chapter III, Articles 12 to 15; the parallel EFTA Convention
materials in Annex K and Appendix 1, Chapter III, Articles 11 to 14; and the Swiss implementing
framework reflected in the current VFP/OLCP directives and federal factsheets. Article 7 AFMP and
Article 7 Annex K are also central because they extend equal treatment, mobility, post-activity stay rights
and family rights to persons within the agreement’s scope.
The Swiss implementing ordinance itself is the Verordnung über den freien Personenverkehr /
Ordonnance sur la libre circulation des personnes / Ordinanza sulla libera circolazione delle
persone (SR 142.203), repeatedly identified as the governing implementing instrument by SEM and
official cantonal pages. In this pack, operative propositions from that ordinance are grounded
principally through the current SEM directives and cantonal materials, which are the most usable official
implementation sources in this record.

Official guidance and cantonal practice
E. Official guidance / administrative materials
SEM’s 2025 federal factsheet says EU/EFTA citizens wishing to work in a self-employed capacity in
Switzerland initially receive a B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years if they can show an effective self-
employed activity when they submit the application. SEM’s FAQ adds that EU/EFTA nationals must
register with the commune within 14 days after arrival and request residence authorisation, producing
identity documents plus documents proving that they exercise or will exercise a self-employed activity
that is subsistence-generating for themselves and their family; the activity may be taken up only after
the documents required by the canton have been produced.
SEM’s 2026 VFP directives are the most precise federal administrative source. They state that self-
employment must be proved by a Swiss undertaking or place of business with effective and
subsistence-generating business activity, evidenced through business books, including accounting
and orders; they state that commercial-register entry usually accompanies commercial undertakings or
legal entities, but is not universally mandatory; they prohibit cantons from imposing prohibitive
evidential hurdles; and they make regular income and freedom from social-assistance dependency
decisive both for grant and for ongoing maintenance of status.
SEM also provides the main federal statement on what happens if the route fails over time. The
directives say that self-employed persons lose residence rights where they can no longer maintain
themselves and become dependent on social assistance, and that persons admitted as self-employed
but no longer economically active or job-seeking must meet the sufficient-means standard applicable
under the AFMP. By contrast, workers retain a different and generally more protective position under
the worker provisions.
SECO’s official materials are especially valuable on status verification and sham self-employment. For
temporary self-employed service providers, SECO requires a copy of the notification or permit, an A1
form from the home-country social-security authority, and a client contract or equivalent client
confirmation. SECO’s distinction guidance further states that self-employment points towards work
under one’s own name and account, with investments, own premises, loss risk, independence from
instructions, control over working time and multiple clients, whereas employment points towards
subordination, working in another’s name, fixed hours, assigned workplace and regular work for the
same employer.
Official AHV/OASI guidance is relevant, but only as evidence and status-indicator material, not as a
substitute for immigration-law residence rules. The AHV leaflet defines self-employed persons for
social-insurance purposes as persons who work under their own name and account, operate
independently and bear their own economic risk; it emphasises case-by-case assessment based on
economic facts rather than contractual labels. That aligns closely with SEM and SECO’s immigration-
law distinction and explains why cantonal migration offices commonly ask for AHV affiliation or
recognition, while still requiring immigration-specific proof of genuine Swiss activity.

Aargau is one of the clearest German-language cantonal authorities. It says that newly arriving EU/EFTA

validity or revoke the permit if conditions cease to be met. Its guidance requires sufficient financial
means and comprehensive health/accident insurance, and gives evidential examples including
commercial-register extract, business plan, company accounting, market analysis, a motivational
explanation, a lease or purchase contract for business premises, bank and insurance documents, and
AHV compensation-office registration.
Schaffhausen similarly states that self-employed applicants must prove an active and subsistence-
securing activity and that the canton may demand further evidence whenever doubts arise. Its
document examples are particularly useful for future drafting because they include business plan,
order volume, bookkeeping extracts, bank records, asset evidence, lease of business premises,
commercial-register extract, and health/accident insurance, with sector-specific permits where
necessary.
Ticino confirms the same basic pattern in Italian. It describes a five-year B UE/AELS permit for
independent activity, states that in case of serious doubts about the effective and durable character of
the activity the authority may demand new means of proof during validity and revoke the document if
the original conditions are no longer met, and stresses that separate obligations towards tax, social-
insurance and municipal authorities still apply. Ticino also notes, for family reunion, that the sponsor
must have sufficient financial means and suitable housing.
The safest general proposition is therefore this: cantonal evidence lists differ in detail, but the
recurring official themes are business plan/project description, business address or premises,
proof of actual or imminent operations, financial-resource material, insurance, social-security or
compensation-fund evidence, commercial-register evidence where relevant, and profession-
specific authorisations where the activity is regulated.

The immigration-law treatment of EU/EFTA self-employment in Switzerland is best understood as a two-
level enquiry. The first level asks whether the person falls within the AFMP/EFTA establishment route at
all: are they genuinely pursuing an economic activity in Switzerland as a self-employed person? The
second level asks whether the activity is real enough to sustain residence, rather than a nominal set-
up used to obtain a permit. SEM’s language of “effective”, “active”, “regular” and “subsistence-
generating” activity captures that second enquiry.
On the evidence, the official materials support a layered approach rather than a single mandatory
checklist. The strongest files will usually show several kinds of objective material together: identity and
registration material; a concrete business model and market explanation; evidence of business
premises or a plausible professional address; operational documents such as contracts, orders,
invoicing chains, accounting extracts or planned accounting structure; financial evidence such as start-
up capital, bank records or other resources; social-security evidence such as AHV/AVS affiliation where
applicable; insurance; and profession-specific licences where required. That is why it is wrong to present
company registration alone as sufficient, and equally wrong to suggest that there is one standard
federal list of documents binding every canton in every case.
The distinction from employment is legally central. Swiss official sources converge on classic
subordination indicators: if the person works in another entity’s operational structure, follows
instructions, has fixed working methods or times imposed by others, bears little real economic risk, or
effectively serves one principal in a dependent manner, the case may be classified as employment or
sham self-employment. By contrast, independent pricing, real commercial risk, own investment,
autonomy over operations, own client base, and the ability to accept or refuse projects point towards
genuine self-employment. The label chosen by the parties is not decisive; economic reality is.
That same logic explains why platform-based or single-client models need careful drafting. SEM’s
directives expressly note that persons offering taxi or food-delivery services through digital platforms
are not treated as self-employed in the relevant example from Swiss case law, and SECO’s own markers
emphasise multiple clients and freedom from instruction. Content for internal use should therefore
avoid broad statements that “one can simply freelance through a platform” without examining
operational control and subordination.
The distinction from economically inactive residence is equally important. A person who is not
genuinely self-employed cannot repair the self-employed route by pointing to capital, savings or private
wealth alone; that is a different AFMP residence basis with different conditions. Conversely, official
canton materials often request financial statements or bank evidence from self-employed applicants
not because the route is economically inactive residence, but because the canton is testing whether the
claimed business is viable enough to avoid welfare dependence. In other words, financial resources
may be relevant both as proof of business viability and, if the business fails, as the bridge to a different
residence category.
On permit duration and renewal, the route is straightforward in principle but strict in operation. The
treaty model is a permit of at least five years, renewable for at least five years on proof that self-
employed economic activity continues. Official Swiss materials reflect that five-year structure. But
because SEM allows the cantonal authorities to revisit the evidence if there are serious doubts about
genuine activity and subsistence-level income, the five-year permit should not be described as insulated
from later review. It is better understood as recognition of a right that continues only while the route
conditions remain met.
On business failure, low income and cessation, the safest statement is that there is no single
published federal income threshold for the self-employed route equivalent to a fixed salary floor.

Instead, the official materials focus on whether the business is effective, real and sufficient to support
the person without social assistance. If self-employment ceases and the person is not protected by
temporary incapacity or some other retained-status rule, SEM treats the case by reference to sufficient
means; if public social assistance is then required, the residence right can lapse and the permit can be
revoked. That makes the concept of financial viability real, but practice-led rather than reducible to a
single national numeric threshold in the materials reviewed here.
On family members, the route is available but should be drafted with care. SEM’s family factsheet
makes clear that self-employed sponsors need suitable accommodation and the financial means to
support the family members they sponsor. The family members’ residence position is derivative and
normally lasts only while the principal AFMP right exists. Family members who qualify through reunion
gain labour-market access, including self-employment, but relatives in the ascending line do not acquire
a right to work.
Drafting cautions and source list
H. Drafting cautions
Do not say that an EU/EFTA national qualifies simply by saying they intend to become self-employed.
Current Swiss official practice requires documentary proof of an effective and subsistence-generating
self-employed activity, and authorities may revisit the evidence later.
Do not say that a Swiss company, sole proprietorship or commercial-register entry by itself proves the
immigration-law test. Official Swiss guidance treats registration as relevant but not conclusive, and
many cantons ask for much more: business plans, premises, orders, bookkeeping, bank evidence,
insurance and compensation-fund material.
Do not conflate settled self-employment in Switzerland with the separate regime for cross-border
service providers operating up to 90 days in a calendar year. The latter uses the notification route and
SECO’s control documents, including A1 and client-contract evidence.
Do not blur immigration status with tax, AHV/social-security, VAT or company-law compliance.
Those areas matter as evidence of reality and independence, but none of them alone determines
residence rights. AHV recognition is evidentially important, not dispositive; professional-sector
authorisations are separate from residence permission.
Do not universalise canton-specific document checklists. The official materials show a strong national
core, but the exact supporting documents and process steps remain partly canton-led. Use wording
such as “authorities commonly ask for” or “official canton materials indicate”, rather than “Swiss law
always requires”.
Do not assume that low income is harmless merely because the person has a permit. SEM’s worker case
law is more tolerant of low income and some public support than the self-employed/non-active line. For
self-employed persons who cease activity or cannot maintain themselves, sufficient means become
decisive and social-assistance dependence may break the residence right.
Do not overlook the possibility of disguised employment, especially where there is one client, tight
operational control, platform dependency, fixed schedules or little genuine commercial risk. The official
Swiss sources treat that as a substantive classification issue, not a drafting technicality.

Open questions / limitations
The published official materials are strongest on the existence of a genuine, active and subsistence-
generating business and weaker on precise federal numeric thresholds for “viability” short of social-
assistance dependence. The most accurate current drafting line is therefore that viability is assessed
case by case by reference to actual business activity, regular income and independence from welfare,
with canton-specific evidential practice playing a significant role.
