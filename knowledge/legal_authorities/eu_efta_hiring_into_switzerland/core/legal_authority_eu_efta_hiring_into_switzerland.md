# Swiss legal authority pack for EU and EFTA hiring into Switzerland

# Executive legal summary

Swiss hiring of EU and EFTA nationals is built on a treaty-based free movement framework, not on the ordinary third-country labour-market admission model. The core basis is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU, whose objectives include entry, residence, access to employed work, establishment on a self-employed basis, brief cross-border services, residence for non-economically active persons, and equal treatment. SEM's current overview states that the same rules also apply to nationals of EFTA member states. Swiss domestic law confirms that the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act applies to EU and EFTA nationals only insofar as the free movement instruments do not provide otherwise, or where Swiss lawv is more favourable.1

That treaty entitlement is broad, but it is not a licence to ignore Swiss administrative formalities. For EU and EFTA nationals taking employment in Switzerland for more thanthree months, SEM's current guidance says they must register with the commune of residence within 14 days of arrival and before starting work, and must present a passport or identity card together with written proof of employment. SEM also states that, for EU and EFTA nationals, the residence permit doubles as the work permit. At treaty level, however, Annex I to the Agreement says that completion of residence-permit formalities must not prevent an applicant from immediately taking up employment under the contract already concluded. The careful internal position is therefore that free movement creates the underlying right, but Swiss registration and permit formalities remain mandatory and should be completed on time;the final issue of the permit card should not boe described as a discretionary labour-market approval in the ordinary case. 2

Permit category follows the length and nature of the stay. An employed EU or EFTA national with a contract of one year or more, or an indefinite contract, receives a B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years; a contract of more than three months but less than one year leads to an L EU/EFTA permnit for the duration of the contract; employment of up to three months does not require a residence permit. For frontier workers residing in another EU or EFTA state and returning home at least weekly, Switzerland uses the G EU/EFTA cross-border commuter permit. 3

Short-term work is one of the most important drafting traps. There is a distinct notification regime for work of up to three months or 90 working days per calendar year. For EU and EFTA nationals employed by a Swiss employer for up to three months, the Swiss employer submits the notification, generally no later than one day before the first day of work. For posted workers from EU/EFTA-based undertakings and for EU/EFTA self-employed service providers based in an EU/EFTA state, notification is generally required at least eight days before work starts, once the activity exceeds eight days in a calendar year; in specified sectors such as construction, landscaping, hospitality, cleaning, security, itinerant trade and the sex industry, notification is required from the first day. Breaching these requirements is not a harmless technicality. 4

Not every assignment falls within permit-free notification. SEM's guidance states that services lasting more than 90 days or three months in a calendar year require a permit application before work starts. SEM's 2026 directives go further: temporary service provision exceeding 90 working days per calendar year, outside the special services agreements, does not fall within the scope of the bilateral free movement agreement, so there is no treaty-based legal entitlement; instead, the case falls back into the domestic FNIA/OASA framework. SEM also states that job placement, staff leasing and financial services are outside the notification liberalisation; in these areas, prior permit application is always required and there is no entitlement to a permit. 5

Self-employment is also more exacting than short-form marketing content often suggests. Annex I gives EU and EFTA nationals a route to Swiss self-employment, but SEM's 2026 directives require proof of real and sustainable activity. The directives say the canton may require evidence that the activity is actually and durably pursued and generates regular income sufficient for subsistence; proof can include an undertaking or business establishment in Switzerland with effective, viable commercial activity, supported by business books, accounts and contracts. Zurich's official cantonal guidance expresses the same idea in more practical terms: actual, economically viable business activity should be substantiated through accounting records. 6

Jobseeking and unemployment are two areas where content systems should remain conservative. Annex I allows EU and EFTA nationals to stay for a reasonable period, which may be up to six months, to seek employment, and allows equal access to employment-service assistance while permitting exclusion from social security schemes during thnat period. SEM's FAQ says, as a general rule, a person who loses a job may stay in Switzerland for at least six months to look for new work, but must apply to the cantonal migration authority for a job-seeker permit. Swiss legislation and SEMI's 2026 directives then add more granular rules on when residence rights lapse after involuntary job loss and how long social assistance is excluded. These rules are not well captured by simplistic statements such as "EU nationals can simply stay until they find another job". 7

Family members enjoy strong but derived rights. Annex I covers spouses, descendants under 21 or dependent, and dependent ascendants, whatever their nationality. The spouse and dependent children or children under 21 have a right to work regardless of nationality. SEM's family reunification materials emphasise that the family member's right to stay is derived from the EU/EFTA national's own underlying right of residence, and normally lasts only as long as that primary right continues. For workers,the key family-reunification condition is adequate housing; for economically inactive persons, jobseekers and other non-worker categories, sufficient means become more important. Third-country family members may need a visa for entry if their nationality requires one, although SEM states that non-EU/EFTA family members holding a valid residence permit issued by a Schengen state are exempt from the visa requirement for entry into Switzerland. 8

There are currently no Swiss quota restrictions for Croatian nationals in 2026. Switzerland had the ability to reactivate the safeguard clause for Croatia one final time for 2026 if the 2025 thresholds were exceeded, but the Federal Council announced in January 2026 that those thresholds were not reached and Croatian nationals would therefore not be subject to quotas in 2026. Internal content should nevertheless treat country-specific safeguards as time-sensitive and should not hard-code them without date control. 9

# Structural overview of EU and EFTA hiring into Switzerland

The cleanest way to structure the Swiss route is to separate five legal pathways that are often conflated: employed residence in Switzerland; short-term Swiss-employer work under notification; cross-border posting or self-employed service provision; Swiss-based self-employment; and family-derived or

<!-- 2 -->

jobseeker residence. Zurich's cantonal guidance explicitly separates employed commencements of work, postings and self-employment, which matches the federal structure. 10

For an EU or EFTA national who will live in Switzerland and work for a Swiss employer for more than three months, the ordinary route is residence registration with the local commune followed by an L or B permit, depending on contract length. The person must show identity and a written employment confirmation or contract. The permit evidences the right to live and work, but the underlying legal basis is the treaty entitlement rather than a quota-based labour-market discretion. 2

For up to three months with a Swiss employer, the ordinary residence permit disappears, but the notification regime usually takes its place. SEM's notification page says the online system also applies to Swiss companies employing foreign workers for up to three months, and that the notification for such hires must be made no later than one day before the first day of work. Geneva's and Zurich's cantonal pages repeat that three months or 90 days with a Swiss employer does not require a permit but still requires notification. 11

For cross-border service provision from another EU or EFTA state, the regime changes again. The Agreemennt liberalises brief service provision up to 90 days of actual work in a calendar year, and Annex I sets the same 90-day ceiling. SEM and cantonal guidance translate that into the notification regime for posted workers and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers, with the eight-day advance rule and the eight-day annual threshold, subject to the listed high-risk sectors where day-one notification applies. Once the service crosses the 90-day threshold outside special sectoral agreements, there is no treaty-based right to provide it. 12

For Swiss-based seIf-employment, the decisive question is not merely whether a company has been formed, but whether the activity is genuine, economically real and able to sustain the person. SEM's 2026 directives state that the canton may ask for continuing proof and may revoke the permit if the conditions cease to be met. This is a classic evidential route rather than a route that should be described as automatic. 13

Family and jobseeker cases are conceptually different again. Family members rely on the EU/EFTA national's primary status unless and until they can establish an original right in their own name. Jobseekers may enter and remain for a reasonable period, but their position is not identical to that of workers, especially on means and social assistance. These are the two route variants most likely to become fact-sensitive despite the overall free movement framework. 14

# Primary legal texts and official guidance

## Primary legal texts

The central primary text is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EU. The most important provisions for this route are the Agreement's objectives in Article 1; the rights of entry, residence and economic activity in Articles 3 to 5; the non-discrimination principle in Article 2; Annex I Article 2 on residence and jobseeking; Annex I Article 3 on family members; Annex I Article 5 on public order, public security and public health restrictions; Annex I Article 6 on employed persons; Annex I Article 12 on self-employed persons; Annex I Articles 17 to 22 on services; and Annex I Article 24 on persons not pursuing an economic activity. The Croatia protocol and related transitional machinery sit within the same treaty architecture. 40

For EFTA nationals, the relevant parallel framework is the EFTA Convention, especially the movement-of-persons provisions in Annex K and its appendices. Official EFTA materials describe the Convention as progressively liberalising free movement of persons and parallel legal texts reproduce the right of residence, work, self-employment and frontier work, including rules corresponding to the Swiss-EU framework. Swiss official guidance nonetheless treats the practical rule set for EU and EFTA citizens as the same. 41

Swiss federal law matters chiefly in two ways. First, Article 2 FNIA/AIG confirms the residual and supplementary role of Swiss domestic immigration law in EU/EFTA cases. Secondly, Article 61a FNIA/AIG now regulates lapse of residence rights for EU/EFTA nationals after job loss, including six-month periods, possible continuation where unemployment insurance continues, exclusion from social assistance during the interim, and exceptions for temporary incapacity or retained rights of stay. 42

Swiss implementing legislation also includes the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons and the Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment. SEM's own legal-basis pages and legal-link pages identify these ordinances, together with the AFMP itself, as the core federal instruments governing this field. 43

# Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful current administrative materials are the SEM overview page on free movement; the SEM FAQ on living and working in Switzerland; the SEM "Working in Switzerland" page; the SEM permit pages for L, B and G categories; the SEM notification-procedure page; the January 2026 SEM directives on the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons; and the SEM factsheets on family reunification and the current application of the AFMP. Together, these materials give the clearest official working picture of registration timing, short-term notification, permit categories, self-employment evidence,family members, job mobility, unemployment and the current Croatia position. 44

One further interpretive point matters for case-law use. Article 16 of the AFMP states that, where the Agreement uses concepts of EU law, account shall be taken of Court of Justice case law delivered before signature, with later case law to be brought to Switzerland's attention through the Joint Committee mechanism. For internal drafting, this means that case law may be relevant, but the safest route remains to anchor operational guidance in the treaty text and current SEM instructions unless a case is truly load-bearing. 45

# Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal and communal practice does not replace the federal and treaty framework, but it often determines how a compliant application must be routed in real life. Zurich's official English-language guidance is especially clear. It distinguishes commencement of work with a Swiss employer, postings and self-employment; states that EU/EFTA citizens working for up to 90 days for a Swiss employer do not need a permit but must be notified by the Swiss employer at the latest one day before work starts; and says that Swiss-based self-employment requires evidence of actual, economically viable activity, usually by accounting records. For self-employment over 90 days, Zurich says a residence permit is issued through registration with the competent commune of residence. 46

Geneva's official pages confirm the same broad structure but show the local routing logic. Geneva states that an EU/AELE national engaged by a Swiss employer for up to three months or 90 days per year does not need a work permit but must use the SEM online notification system; for work of more than three months, the worker or employer must submit an application for a residence authorisation with gainful activity. Geneva's service-provider page adds that an EU/AELE undertaking or independent based abroad must either notify the Confederation or obtain cantonal authorisation depending on mission length, and must count effective working days only. 47

Ticino's official material shows why internal drafting should avoid over-universalising cantonal forms. One Ticino page for a new L permit for an EU/AELS citizen "person abroad" says the employer must make the request while the foreign national waits abroad for entry authorisation. That should be read as a route-specific administrative workflow rather than as a statement of general substantive law for all EU/EFTA hires, especially given SEM's federal guidance that permit formalities may be completed after arrival in ordinary free movement cases. Ticino separately reiterates the short-term notification rule for EU/AELS nationals and posted workers and repeats the rule that third-country posted workers must already have been admitted permanently, usually for at least 12 months, to an EU/EFTA labour market. 48

The safest internal practice point is therefore that cantons and communes vary in front-end workflow, forms, routing and evidential checklists, while the governing legal basis remains federal and treaty-based. Content that is meant to be reusable across Switzerland should frame cantonal steps as implementation detail and should avoid implying that one canton's specific form logic is a universal national rule unless a federal source says so. 49

# Practical legal analysis

The main analytical point is that Swiss free movement law is generous on substantive entitlement but strict on categorisation and filing discipline. That is why internal content should lead with the distinction between "right to work and residde under free movement" and "mandatory Swiss formalities proving and activating that right". Saying that an EU/EFTA national can "work automatically" is legally sloppy.Saying instead that theperson generally has a treaty-based right, subject to registration, notification, proof of status and the recognised grounds of restriction, is much closer to the authorities' own materials. 50

The most important drafting distinction is between employed residence in Switzerland and cross-border service provision. A Swiss employer hiring an EU/EFTA national for ordinary work in Switzerland is not using the same route as an EU/EFTA undertaking posting staff for a project, and neither route is the same as an EU/EFTA individual coming to Switzerland as a Swiss-based self-employed person.The notification procedure appears across several of these routes, but it does different work in each of them and has different filing deadlines. Internal copy should therefore never use "notification" as a synonym for "EU/EFTA hiring" in general. 51

<!-- 11 -->

The short-term threshold also needs careful handling. For Swiss-employer hires, "up to three months" and "up to 90 days in a calendar year" function as the legal threshold for moving from notification into the ordinary permit system. For postings and self-employed cross-border services, the same 90-day outer limit applies, but there is also the separate eight-day annual trigger for notification in most sectors and a first-day rule in prescribed sectors. As a result, content should not simply say "under 90days no formalities"; that is wrong. The correct internal statement is that under-90-day work may avoid a residence permit, but usually not notification. 25

Self-employment is another area where marketing language often overstates certainty.The relevant question is not just whether the EU/EFTA national says they are self-employed, or even whether a company has been registered. The authorities lookfor activity on one's own account and at one's own risk, not under another person's instructions or organisational control; they also look for real, viable, income-generating activity. These features are especially important in platform-work and service-provider cases, in undercapitalised start-ups, and where the person may in substance be a worker or a posted worker rather than a genuinely self-employed operator. 52

Family-member cases require the same discipline. The family route is generous in that it covers family members irrespective of nationality and does not require a prior lawful residence in another EU/EFTA state for a family member of an EU/EFTA national relying on Article 3 Annex I. But that does not make it unconditional. The derived right still depends on the principal person's status; adequate housing is required; visa formalities may still apply at entry; and for non-worker principal applicants, sufficiency of means becomes a real gatekeeper. Content systems should also distinguish between the stronger labour-market right of spouses and dependent children under 21, and ascendant relatives who do not benefit from the same automatic right to work. 53

Unemployment is the hardest part of the route to summarise accurately. Article 6(6) of Annex I protects workers against withdrawal merely because of temporary inability to work or involuntary unemployment. But Swiss legislation and SEM guidance now provide detailed rules on when residence rights expire after involuntary job loss, how long unemployment-insurance payments extend those rights, and when social assistance is unavailable. The practical result is that unemployment may begin as a state of retained worker protection but later become a question whether the person still has an AFMP basis to remain as a worker, jobseeker, economically inactive person with sufficient means, or a person with a retained right to stay. Internal content should therefore avoid bright-line claims such as "the permit remains valid until expiry regardless of unemployment" or "the right is lost immediately when the job ends"; both can be wrong depending on the facts. 54

Country-specific restrictions are currently limited. For practical purposes in May 2026, Croatian nationals should be treated as benefiting from full free movement in Switzerland because the safeguard thresholds for reintroducing quotas were not met for 2026. But this is precisely the kind of rule that should be date-controlled in any content system, because it is both time-sensitive and country-specific. 55

Do not say that EU/EFTA hiring into Switzerland is "automatic". The safer formulation is that most EU/ EFTA hires benefit from a treaty-based entitlement under free movement, but must still comply with Swiss registration, permit or notification formalities, and may still face scrutiny on public-order grounds, abuse, sham arrangements, sufficient means, family evidence, and category-specific rules. 56

Do not collapse permit-free short work, posted work, and Swiss-employer work into one category. A short Swiss-employer hire, a posted worker, and an EU/EFTA self-employed service provider may all use notification, but the filing timing, sector triggers, and follow-on authorisation rules differ. Do not omit the one-day rule for Swiss-employer hires or the eight-day rule and first-day sectors for postings and self-employed cross-border services. 57

Do not present self-employment as satisfied by coompany formation alone. The official guidance requires effective, economically viable business activity and allows the canton to ask for further proof or revoke the permit if the conditions cease to be met. 29

Do not treat family members of EU/EFTA nationals as interchangeable with EU/EFTA nationals themselves. Third-country family members may still need entry visas depending on nationality,their right is normally derived from the principal's status, and the labour-market right is strongJest for spouses and dependent children under 21 rather than all family members. 58

Do not oversimplify unemployment. Internal copy should say that involuntary unemployment does not necessarily end the right immediately, but ongoing residence after job loss is fact-sensitive and regulated by both the AFMP and Swiss domestic lapse rules. 59

Do not freeze country-specific safeguard rules into evergreen copy. Croatia is unrestricted for 2026 in practice, but this required a January 2026 confirmation by the Federal Council and was inherently time-sensitive. 55

## Open questions and limitations

Cantonal routing, form language and documentary practice remain administratively variable. Zurich, Geneva andTicino all reflect the same federal structure, but they do not present it in identical workflows. Where a cantonal page appears more restrictive thanthe federal general guidance, this pack treats the cantonal page as evidence of local administrative handling rather than as a universal national substantive rule. 49

This pack is focused on immigration, residence, work authorisation and notification. It does not separately map Swiss tax treatment, full social-security coordination, or regulated-profession licensing beyond noting where official sources reserve those issues. 64
