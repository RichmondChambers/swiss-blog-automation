# Core Swiss Business Founder and Entrepreneur Framework Pack

# Executive legal summary

Switzerland does not operate a single statutory "entrepreneur visa" or "business founder visa".What exists instead is a set of residence and work-authorisation routes that depend first on nationality and then on the legal character of the proposed activity. For EU/EFTA nationals, the relevant framework is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its implementing Swiss administration. For non-EU/EFTA nationals, the route sits within the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the corresponding admission ordinance and SEM practice. Official Swiss sources therefore frame founder cases as questions of residence, labour-market access, self-employment, establishment and business activity, not as a standalone investor or start-up visa class.1

The EU/EFTA route is materially more rights-based. SEM states that EU/EFTA citizens who wish to work in a self-employed capacity in Switzerland will initially receive a B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years if they can show an effectiveself-employed activity when applying. They must register wvith the commune within 14 days of arrival, apply for the residence permit, and provide documents showing that they are or will be self-employed and can support themselves and their family. Cantonal pages underline that the activity must be real, effective and economically self-supporting, and that AHV/OASI registration as self-employed is a core evidential step. The self-employment analysis is not purely formal: Swiss social-security guidance says self-employment is assessed case by case by the compensation office on the basis of economic reality, nnot contractual labels, and typical indicators include acting in one's own name, bearing one's own economic risk, organising the business independently and usually working for multiple clients. 2

The non-EU/EFTA route is substantially more discretionary and evidence-heavy. Switzerland applies a dual admission system under which third-country nationals are admitted only on a limited basis and,as a rule, only if highly qualified. SEM's official guidance says admission must be in the interests of Switzerland and the Swiss economy as a whole; the authorities consider the labour-market situation, sustainable economic development and the applicant's integration prospects. Cantonal entrepreneur pages translate that into practical criteria: a durable benefit to the Swiss labour market, contribution to regional or sectoral diversification, creation or preservation of local jobs, substantial investment, sufficient autonomous income, adequate capitalisation, and proof that the operational and financial conditions for the business are met. Quotas apply: for 2026, the Federal Council left the annual totals for third-country workers at 4,500 B permits and 4,000 L permits. 3

Across both routes, active involvement matters. Official cantonal and federal materials consistently describe the relevant activity as self-employed work carried on through one's own organisation,under one's own direction and risk, or as a start-up/business operation requiring the applicant's presence. The SEM application-document checklist for non-EU/EFTA business start-ups requires a deed of foundation or commercial-register entry together with a three-year business plan covering organisation, staff development, financing, budgeting and market/competition analysis. These materials support a conservative internal rule: what is tested is not passive capital placement as such, but whether the applicant is genuinely establishing and running an economically meaningful Swiss operation. That is why company-law capital minima are relevant but not sufficient. An SA/AG and an Sàrl/GmbH have

statutory capital requirements, yet immigration authorities separately assess viability, economic value and the necessity of the applicant's presence. 4

The process is also multi-level. For third-country cases, the file normally starts with the cantonal labour-market or immigration authority, moves to cantonal pre-assessment, and then goes to SEM for federal approval. For visa nationals, the Swiss representation abroad issues the entry visa after approval; registration in Switzerland follows after entry, and work may begin only after registration. Geneva's current official guidance adds that the file is reviewed by the cantonal foreign-labour directorate and, depending oon the case, presented to the tripartite commission for the economy.EU/EFTA cases are simpler procedurally but still document-driven and canton-facing. 5

Renewal and long-term planning remain central risk points. EU/EFTA B permits are renewable if conditions continue to be met, although official SEM material shows that first renewal can be limited to one year in some unemployment scenarios. Third-country entrepreneur files are even more exposed to post-grant review, because the original case is built around forecasts of business performance and public economic benefit. Official cantonal and federal materials do not promise automatic renewal. Over time, settlement and citizenship planning also diverge: the AFMP itself does not regulate the C permit, settlement for EU/EFTA nationals depends on the AIG and settlement treaties,and ordinary naturalisation requires ten years of residence and a C permit.6

## Structural overview of the Swiss business founder and entrepreneur framework

**Legal nature of entrepreneur and self-employment residence.** Swiss law does not create a freestanding founder visa label. In official materials, thhe operative categories are residence permits, work authorisations, self-employment, labour-market admission andd AFMP residence rights. That distinction should be preserved in all later drafting. 7

**EU/EFTA** **self-employment route.** EU/EFTA nationals benefit from AFMP rights. They register after arrival, show that they are or will be genuinely self-employed, and if they can prove an effective self-employed activity they initially receive a five-year B EU/EFTA permit. For cross-border establishment without Swiss residence, a G permnit may be available if the person remains domiciled in an EU/EFTA state and returns there at least weekly. Short-term cross-border service provision without establishment is separately liberalised up to 90 working days per calenddar year, subject to the notification regime. 8

**Non-EU/EFTA entrepreneur and self-employment route.** Third-country founders are assessed under the AIG/FNIA framework for gainful activity and self-employment. The route is quota-limited, restricted in principle to highly qualified persons, and depends on showing that the activity serves Switzerland's overall economic interest. Cantonal materials then translate that standard into business-plan evidence, autonomous income, capitalisation, investment, job creation and regional economic benefit. 9

**Competent authorities and approval sequence.** EU/EFTA cases are commune and canton led, with registration and permit issuance after arrival. Third-country cases move througgh the cantonal employment or migration authority, then to SEM for federal approval, then to visa issuance where required, followed by local registration after entry. Certain cantons add internal economic review bodies or labour-office pre-assessment. 10

**Business activity in the interests of the Swiss economy.** Official federal guidance says third-country admission requires consistency with Switzerland's overall economic interest. Official cantonal

explanations show the kinds of facts used to satisfy that test: durable labour-market benefit, diversification, local jobs, substantial investment and new business for the Swiss economy. 11

**Business** **plan** **and** **financial evidence.** SEM's apoplication-document list for business start-ups requires the deed of foundation or commercial-register entry and a three-year business plan covering organisation, staffing, financing, budgeting and market/competition analysis. Cantonal pages add proof of sufficient and autonomous income, business premises, and other supporting operational documents. 12

**Active involvement versus passive investment.** Official materials define the relevant activity by operational control, organisation and economic risk, not by ownership alone. Social-insurance guidance further makes clear that AG/GmbH shareholders are ordinarily emnployees of their own company rather than self-employed persons for AHV purposes. The immigration analysis therefore centres on active entrepreneurial activity, not passive holding of capital. This is an inference from the official framework, but it is a strong one. 13

**Company formation, permits, renewal and long-term planning.** Company-law formation, commercial registration, tax and social-insurance steps matter as evidence of seriousness, but they do not in themselves create residence rights. AG/SA and GmbH/Sàrl structures also require at least one Swiss-resident representative with valid Swiss residence/work status. Permit duration differs sharply by route, renewal is conditional, and later settlement or citizenship planning depends on maintaining lawful residence in the correct category over time. 14

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal administrative materials are SEM's AFMP factsheets and FAQs, SEM's non-EU/EFTA labour-market pages, the SEM application-document checklist, the SEM residence-permit pages, AHV/OASI leaflets on self-employment, and the SECO SME portal pages on foreign founders and legal forms. Taken together, these sources provide the best accessible official picture of the route's architecture, evidence and procedure. 42

For EU/EFTA self-employmennt, the strongest sources are the SEM factsheet on residing in Switzerland and taking up gainful activity, the SEM AFMP FAQs, the SEM family-reunification factsheet, and the AHV/ OASI leaflets. These sources establish the five-year B permit starting point, the registration and proof requirements, the need to show genuine self-employed activity and self-support, and the derived rights of family members. They also show why social-insurance recognition and disguised-employment analysis are legally significant rather than merely administrative formalities. 43

For non-EU/EFTA founders, the most important federal materials are SEM's "Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market" page and the "Application documents" page. The first source explains the dual system, economic-interest standard, quotas, qualifications, salary/employment requirements, housing requirement and integration criteria. The second is especially valuable because it gives a current official picture of what a business-start-up file should contain in practice: deed of foundation or commercial-register entry, plus a three-year business plan with organisation, staff development, financing and market analysis. 44

Procedure is set out clearly by SEM: the file goes to the cantonal employment or immigration authority, the canton takes thepreliminary decision, approved files go to SEM for final approval, visa issuance follows where required, and the applicant then registers locally after entry. Geneva's official material shows how this federal sequence is complemented by cantonal review, including labour-office scrutiny and possible presentation to the tripartite commission for the economy. 45

On company formation and infrastructure, the official SECO SME portal is valuable but should be treated as explanatory rather than primary law. It confirms practical points highly relevant in founder files: foreign founders need to match immigration status to the intended legal form; AG/SA and GmbH/ Sàrl require a Swiss-resident representative; legal-form choice shapes commercial-register obligations, tax treatment and governance; and company-law capital minima are CHF 100,000 for SA/AG and CHF 20,000 for Sàrl/GmbH. 46

On renewal and long-term planning, official sources are thinner than on initial admission. SEM's EU/ EFTA B-permit page confirms five-year renewability where conditions remain met, with limited first renewal in the involuntary-unemployment scenario. SEM's settlement and naturalisation pages confirm that the AFMP itself does not govern settlement, that C-permit access for EU/EFTA nationals depends on settlement treaties and the AIG framework, and that ordinary naturalisation requires ten years of residence and a C permit. 47

Published official case-law material located in this session was more useful on self-employment classification than on entrepreneur admission under AIG art. 19 specifically. Federal Supreme Court materials reinforce that AHV self-employment status does not turn on contractual form and that AHV classification is not automatically decisive for all other legal contexts. No equally accessible body of entrepreneur-specific federal immigration case law was identified from official databases during this session, so later drafting should avoid over-claiming jurisprudential certainty on points that are largely driven by administrative discretion and cantonal practice. 48

### Practical legal analysis

The decisive divide is between AFMP establishment rights and AIG/FNIA labour-market admission. EU/ EFTA founder cases start from a position of treaty-based labour-market access, so the main legal question is whether the activity is genuinely self-employed and whether the person is effectively establishing themselves in Switzerland within the treaty framework. Non-EU/EFTA founder cases start from the opposite position: no facilitated market access, no general right to establish a business by virtue of capital alone, quota control, and a need to justify admission in terms of Switzerland's broader economic interest. That is why the same commercial project can look routine for an EU/EFTA founder yet demanding and discretionary for a third-country founder. 55

The non-EU/EFTA route is significantly more discretion-laden because the official test is not merely whether the person can support themselves privately. SEM explicitly frames the issue as whether admission serves Switzerland and the Swiss economy as a whole. Cantonal pages then operationalise that with public-value indicators: joobs for the local labour market, diversification, meaningful investment, new mandates and durable benefit to regional economic structures. The practical consequence is that a founder who merely wishes to run a profitable boutique business for personal income may not satisfy the standard as easily as a founder who can show wider labour-market or innovation effects. That does not mean every successful case must be venture-capital-backed or tech-led; it does mean the legal narrative must be framed in public economic terms rather than only private commercial ambition. 11

This also explains why there is no fixed statutory minimum investment threshold, yet undercapitalisation remains dangerous. Swiss company law imposes formal capital minima for SA/AG and Sàrl/GmbH structures, but immigration guidance asks a different question: whether the business is adequately financed,operationally credible and likely to achieve the economic outcomes promised. A founder cannot cure a weak immigration case simply by pointing to CHF 20,000 or CHF 100,000 of share capital if the business plan shows no realistic route to sustainable operations, no autonomous income, or no wider economic contribution. Conversely, the absence of a statutory "entrepreneur investment minimum" should not be misread as evidential leniency. 56

Passive investment does not fit comfortably within this framework. The official sources focus on self-employed work, establishment, business activity, organisational control, entrepreneurial risk and the applicant's active function. Zurich defines self-employment by one's own organisation, authority and risk. AHV guidance tests whether the person works in their own name, bears economic risk and typically serves multiple clients. From that official framing, it follows that passive shareholding, simple capital injection or real-estate acquisition without active operational involvement is not, by itself, the relevant immigration case theory. That is an inference rather than an express statutory sentence, but it is a strong one and should guide drafting. 57

The applicant's physical and managerial presence in Switzerland must usually be shown to be necessary for the business. This is plainest in third-country files, because the law is deciding whether this particular foreign national should be admitted to the Swiss labour market at all. The applicant's qualifications, specialist role and integration prospects are therefore central. That is also why solo consultancy and small-footprint freelance cases can be harder than they first appear. Official sources do not say they are impossible, but they do demand autonomous income, high qualifications and durable economic benefit, while social-insurance guidance warns against single-client models that look like employment in disguise. A consultancy with several real clients, specialist market need and strong Swiss integration evidence may still be approvable; a thin one-client advisory concept is structurally weaker. 58

Canton selection and sectoral fit matter because the legal test is applied through cantonal institutions before SEM approval. Geneva expressly recommends prior contact with its cantonal economy and innovation office in order to assess fit with the local economic context. Zurich and Solothurn each present their own explanatory emphases, especially around financing, business plans and regional economic contribution. This does not mean cantons can rewrite federal law. It does mean venue strategy, local networks, premises, partnerships and the plausibility of the business in the chosen canton are important parts of the file. 54

The business plan is both a grant-stage and renewal-stage document. SEM's owvn application checklist for business start-ups requires a three-year plan with organisation, staffing, financing and market analysis. Once a case is granted on that basis, the same themes logically structure renewal: has the business actually launched, was it capitalised as proposed, were jobs created or orders generated, and is the founder still operating the enterprise in the way represented? Official sources found here do not provide a single national "renewal scorecard" for entrepreneur permits, but they clearly do not support any assumption of automatic renewal, especially in the third-country context. 59

Family planning is route-dependent. Under the AFMP, family members' rights are strong and derivative: qualifying spouses and descendants may work anywhere in Switzerland and may be self-employed.For long-term planning, however, the AFMP does not itself regulate settlement. SEM's residence pages show that C-permit access for EU/EFTA nationals depends on the AIG and settlement treaties, and ordinary naturalisation then requires ten years of residence and a C permit, with L-permit time not counted federally. For third-country founder families, the position is governed by the ordinary AIG family-reunification architecture rather than a special entrepreneur regime; the exact conditions should therefore be checked against the sponsor's permit category in any later topic-specific drafting. 60

Finally, not every wealthy founder or principal should be analysed through the entrepreneur route. Official Swiss materials separately treat residence without gainful employment for persons with sufficient means and insurance. Where the real objective is Swiss residence, family relocation or tax residence rather than active Swiss operational entrepreneurship, a non-gainful route may be the cleaner legal comparison point. This research pack did not separately investigate lump-sum taxation in depth, so later drafting should treat tax-residence options as distinct planning topics rather than variants of founder work authorisation. 61

### Drafting cautions and source list

#### Later content generation should keep the following cautions in view:

·Do not describe the route as a simple "Swiss entrepreneur visa" without explaining that Swiss law works through residence permits, work authorisation and AFMP/AIG frameworks. 62

·Do not flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA positions into one route. The distinction is thecentral structural divide. 63

·Do not suggest that forming a company, opening a bank account or paying in share capital by itself creates residence rights. 64

·Do not state that Switzerland has a fixed entrepreneur investment threshold. Official sources found here do not support that proposition. 12

·Do not confuse SA/AG or Sàrl/GmbH capital requirements with immigration eligibility. 65

·Do not present passive investment, passive shareholdings or real-estate purchase as sufficient founder-route facts without an active operational case theory. This is at most a strong inference from the official framework, not an express investor route. 66

·Do not omit the "interests of the Swiss economy as a whole" requirement in third-country cases. 21

·Do not overstate the strength of solo consultancy or freelancer files, especially where there is little capital, little labour-market benefit or only one client. 67

·Do not ignore quotas for non-EU/EFTA founders. 32

·Do not omit SEM approval in third-country cases. 68

·Do not present cantonal economic-development preferences as if they were federal law. 54

·Do not imply that an initial permit will be renewed automatically. 69

· Do not ignore the founder's own qualifications, necessity of presence and integration profilein third-country files. 33

·Do not omit AHV/OASI registration and disguised-employment analysis in EU/EFTA self-employment cases. 70

· Do not treat the business plan as marketing copy. In official materials it is legal evidence. 71

·Do not state that language is a standalone universal initial founder requirement. Official souurces found here support language as an integration factor in certain third-country residence decisions and as relevant later for settlement/naturalisation, not as a single founder-visa gatekeeper across the board. 72
