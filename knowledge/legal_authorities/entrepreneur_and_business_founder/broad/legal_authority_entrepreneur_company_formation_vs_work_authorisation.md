## Executive legal summary

### A. Executive legal summary

Swiss law draws a sharp distinction between the company-law ability to create, acquire, own, capitalise, or register a Swiss business and the immigration-law permission to reside in Switzerland or perform work there. The clearest federal rule for present purposes is in the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Gainful Employment: no claim in a permit procedure can be derived merely fromn steps such as entering into a lease, signing an employment contract, or engaging in "business formation or participation". In other words, incorporating a Swiss GmbH or AG, taking shares, or funding a business is not itself an immigration route. 1

The immigration side starts from a different premise. Under the Federal Act onForeign Nationals and Integration, foreign nationals who wish to carry on a gainful activity in **Switzerland** require authorisation regardless of the length of stay; "gainful activity" covers both employed and self-employed work and may be treated as such even where the concrete activity is unpaid, provided it is the kind of activity usually performed for remuneration. The SEM's 2026 directivesstress that the concept is interpreted broadly andd turns on the activity's objective effect on the Swiss labour market, not on the person's subjective intentions. 2

That is why founder, shareholder, director, employee, and self-employed analyses must be kept separate. A passive owner is not necessarily "working". SEM guidance expressly says that ordinary steps connected with management of one's own assets are not gainful activity, provided they do not take on a commercial or markedly speculative character. By contrast, if the person is actually operating the business, directing staff, acquiring clients, or otherwise carrying out activity on the Swiss labour market, immigration authorisation issues are engaged. The classification is functional and case-specific. 3

Swiss administrative guidance is equally clear that legal form, job title,and commercial-register presentation are not decisive. Foor immigration purposes, the SEM states that whether activity is self-employed or employed is assessed case by case; employment status under other branches of law and even liability structure, such as a GmbH, are not decisive. The directives add that a title such as president or CEO, the existence of an employment contract, or commercial-register entry-even with sole signatory powers - is not enough on its own to determine whether activity is employed or self-employed. That point is especially important for founder-directors and founder-shareholders. 4

For EU/EFTA nationals, the position is easier but still not automatic. Under the free movement regime, they may move to Switzerland for employment or self-employment, but the right to stay is still tied to the reason for residence and the relevant formalities. Official SEM and SECO guidance says that EU/EFTA nationals who want to take up self-employment must register and prove effective self-employed activity. If evidence is already available when the application is filed, a first B EU/EFTA permit is issued for five years; for short-term work, separate notification rules may apply. Incorporation can therefore be part of the evidence, but it is not the right itself. 5

For non-EU nationals, the difficulty is materially ggreater. If the founder will be treated as an employee of the Swiss company, the ordinary admission rules for employed work apply: an employer application, economic interest, labour-market priority, salary and working-condition compliance, and the other statutory criteria. If the founder is truly self-employed, the self-employment route applies instead: economic interest, financial and operational foundations, an adequate independent livelihood, quotas, and -as a rule - high qualification or a recognised entrepreneur/investor case that preserves or creates jobs. Official SEM, SECO, Zurich, and Geneva materials all point in the same direction: the proposed business must deliver a durable benefit to the Swiss labour market and must be supported by real evidence, not merely by a certificate of incorporation. 6

Company-law requirements can also mislead if read out of context. Official SECO/KMU guidance explains that an AG or GmbH must be representable by at least one person resident in Switzerland who holds a valid Swiss residence and work authorisation. That is a corporate-representation requirement, not an immigration entitlement for the foreign owner or founder. The opposite is true for a sole proprietorship: because the business is inseparable from the natural person, labour-market rules for that person apply directly, and a residence/work permit is required to work in Switzerland. 7

The safest internal drafting rule is therefore simple: **Swiss** **incorporation** **may** **createa vehicle,** **evidence, or platform for a permit application, but it does not itself create a right to move to** **Switzerland** **or** **work** **there.** Any future content should analyse nationality, existing status, actual function, place of work, Swiss labour-market effect, and the relevant legal route separately, and should avoid "start a company and move to Switzerland" wording. 8

## Structural overview

### B. Structural overview

**Company** **formation** **and** **ownership.** Official federal SME guidance accepts that foreign nationals can set up Swiss companies, but it treats formation as a company-law matter and immigration as a separate labour-market matter. For AGs and GmbHs, the company must be representable by at least one Switzerland-resident authorised person with a valid Swiss residence and work authorisation; for a sole proprietorship, by contrast, the proprietor personally needs the relevant residence/work entitlement to work in Switzerland. Those rules show that share ownership, capital contribution, and corporate registration are not the same as immigration permission. 9

**Residence** **and** **work** **authorisation.** The immigration question starts with whether the person will carry out a gainful activity in Switzerland. AIG Article 11 makes authorisation mandatory for foreign nationals who wish to exercise gainful activity in Switzerland, regardless of duration, and VZAE Article 6(2) expressly prevents permit claims from being derived from business formation or business participation alone. 10

**Shareholder, director, employee, and self-employed roles.** SEM guidance makes clear that the analysis is functional. Ordinary ownership and ordinary management of one's own assets are not gainful activity; but operational management, labour-market-facing work, and activities objectively performed on the Swiss labour market can be. Whether a founder is an employee or a self-employed person is not settled by company form, a CEO title, an employment contract, or a commercial-register entry. 11

<!-- 2 -->

**EU/EFTA** **and** **non-EU** **distinctions.** EU/EFTA nationals benefit from the free movement regime, but they must still register, and for self-employment they must prove effective activity; a five-year B EU/EFTA permit follows only where the evidence is in place. Non-EU nationals do not enjoy that facilitated access: employed founders fall under the ordinary employed-work rules, and self-employed founders fall under the separate self-employment rules, both of which are restrictive. 12

**Swiss company as sponsor or business platform.** **A** Swiss company can be the employer named in an application, or it can operate as the platform through which a founder tries to prove self-employment. But the company's existence does not displace the underlying admission criteria. For non-EU employed applications there must still be an employer application and compliance with Articles 20-25 AIG;for non-EU self-employment there must still be economic interest, financial/operational basis, and an independent livelihood. 13

**Practical** **immigration** **assessment.** In practice the authorities ask: who is the applicant; what will they actually do; where will it be done; is the activity employed, self-employed, or a cross-border service; what evidence exists; and does the proposed activity satisfy the relevant nationality-specific route? VZAE Article 4 gives the cantonal labour-market authority the initial role in deciding whether conduct amounts to gainful activity, with doubtful cases referred to the SEM. 14

**Drafting** **implications.** Future content must not collapse company law into immigration law. Incorporation, shareholding, directorship, and economic investment should be described as potentially relevant evidence or structure, not as automatic permit triggers. 15

#### Official guidance and administrative materials

### E. Official guidance / administrative materials

The official guidance is unusually important here because it explains how the authorities distinguish ownership from work, and employed from self-employed activity.

<!-- 10 -->

The SEM's **AIG Directives, Chapter 4 "Residence with Gainful Activity",** current to 1 January 2026, are the principal federal administrative source. They say the concept of gainful activity is interpreted widely; the decisive question is whether the activity is of a kind ordinarily performed on the Swiss labour market for remuneration; ordinary management of one's own assets is not gainful activity; and the distinction between employed and self-employed activity is case-specific rather than label-driven. Those directives are the strongest official answer to founder/director/shareholder confusion. 41

The SEM's short guidance sheet **"Begriff der** **Erwerbstätigkeit",** version 1 January 2025, is useful for border cases. It states that short business meetings do not count as gainful activity under AIG Article 11(2), and gives examples such as a foreign investor visiting Switzerland to investigate and negotiate the purchase of a Swiss company, contract negotiations, and certain short strategy meetings.That helps show that not every contact with a Swiss company is "work", but it is narrow, fact-sensitive guidance and should not be over-read. 42

For third-country nationals, the SEM page **"Nicht-EUJ/EFTA-Angehörige"** summarises the restrictive federal approach: only well-qualified persons are generally admitted; the employer must show laboour-market priority; and Swiss salary and working conditions must be met. This page is best read alongside AIG Articles 18-23 and the SEM directives when founder-controlled companies try to present a founder as an employee.43

For self-employment, SECO's page **"Selbständigerwerbende** **von** **Drittstaaten"** says expressly that third-country self-emnployment with residence in Switzerland is permit-requiring from the first day and is subject to the cantonal and federal permit procedure. SECO's page **"Selbständigerwerbende** **der EU/** **EFTA"** says EU/EFTA nationals must register within 14 days and apply for residence, with proof that they do or will carry on self-employment sufficient for themselves and their families; the activity may begin only after the requested cantonal documents have been submitted. 44

The SEM's 2025 factsheet **"Residence in Switzerland with Gainful Employment"** adds the key EU/EFTA residence point: if EU/EFTA nationals enter Switzerland to take up self-employment and can already prove effective self-employed activity at the time of application, they receive an initial B EU/EFTA permit valid for five years. The 2026 **VFP** **Directives** add that proof is satisfied by establishing a business or establishment in Switzerland with effective, income-generating activity, evidenced by business books, orders, and similar records. 45

SECO's page **"Self-employed** **or employed** **activity?"** is also useful because it lists practical indicators. It emphasises own name, own account, independence, own economic risk, own premises, and freedom from instructions on the self-employed side, and subordination, acting in another's name, lack of investment power, and regular work for the same employer on the employed side. That is not immigration legislation, but it aligns with SEM direction and is helpful for founder-role analysis. 46

The SEM pagJe **"Notification** **procedure** **for** **short-term** **work"** is relevant for founders who remain abroad while performing short periods of activity in Switzerland. It explains who may use the EU/EFTA notification regime, including EU/EFTA nationals with a short Swiss job and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers based in the EU/EFTA, and confirms that self-employed persons must file the notification themselves. 47

Finally, the SEM circular **"Home office; immigration-law classification; FZA and third states"** is the most useful official source for remote-management questions. It says that for EU/EFTA persons, mere physical presence in Switzerland with a laptop does not by itself create Swiss worker status if the work is for a foreign employer, without Swiss clients and without a direct link to the Swiss labour market. It also says that self-employed home-office work can be treated similarly where there is no direct Swiss labour-market connection. But where there is a direct impact on the Swiss labour market-for example Swiss client-facing activity - a different immigration analysis follows, and for third-country nationals home office with Swiss labour-market impact is permit-requiring. 28

<!-- 11 -->

The only official company-formation materials used here are the SECO/KMU Portal pages on foreign nationals setting up businesses. They are **not** immigration law in themselves, but they are useful in drawing the boundary: they state thatEU/EFTA nationals may become self-employed, that non-EU entrepreneurs must satisfy the labour-market rules, and that sole proprietorship, AG, and GmbH raise different corporate-representation issues. Crucially, they also show that company-law requirements such as a Swiss-resident representative are not the same as immigration entitlements. 48

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

### G. Practical legal analysis

Incorporation is not an immigration route because the Swiss legal system allocates company-formation questions and immigration questions to different rule sets. Federal immigration law asks whether a foreign national will exercise gainful activity in Switzerland and, if so, under what route. Federal company-formation guidance asks what corporate form can be used and which representation formalities mnust be satisfied. The federal ordinance then explicitly joins the dots by saying that no permit claim arises merely from "business formation or participation". A Swiss company can therefore be the setting for an application, but not the legal source of the right. 54

Ownership does not equal permission to work because shareholding and labour-market activity are distinct. A foreign person can be a passive investor, and ordinary management of one's own assets is not normally gainful activity. But once the same person starts actually running the enterprise in Switzerland - managing staff, performing services, acquiring clients, or otherwise doing work objectively aimed at the Swiss labour market - the immigration rules apply. The SEM's wide definition of gainful activity is designed precisely to stop formal distinctions from defeating labour-market control. 55

A founder's role must therefore be analysed functionally. "Founder", "director", "shareholder", "CEO", and "board member" are not immigration categories. The SEM says expressly that company liability structure, employment contract, corporate title, and commercial-register entry are not decisive on their own. The real questions are whether the person works in their own freely chosen organisation, bears entrepreneurial risk, acts independently, and falls outside subordination, or instead works under employment-type conditions for an employer entity. This is why "self-sponsorship" is more complex than ordinary employer sponsorship. If the founder controls the business and bears the risk, the self-

<!-- 13 -->

employment route is the natural legal frame. If the company is presented as the employer, the authorities may still scrutinise whether that is a genuine employed relationship or only a formal dressing of founder self-management. 56

Non-EU founder cases are materially more difficult than ordinary company formation because the restrictive admission system remains in place. On the employed route there must still be an employer application, overall economic interest, labour-market priority and compliant terms. On the self-employed route there must still be economic interest, sufficient financial and operational basis,and independent means of support, with quota exposure. Official Zurich and Geneva material, echoing federal guidance, shows what this means in practice: business plans, projected accounts, start-up capital, evidence of real market activity, and a persuasive case that the venture brings a durable benefit to the Swiss labour market. A certificate of incorporation by itself proves almost none of that. 57

EU/EFTA founder cases are easier, but automated content must still avoid overstatement. The free movement regime simplifies entry and residence for work and selIf-employment, yet it still asks for registration and proof of effective activity. Official SEM materials say that a first B EU/EFTA permit for self-employment follows where effective self-employed activity can be shown. That is not the same as saying that anyone who files articles of association for a Swiss company thereby acquires a right to move. The legal right arises from the free movement regime, coupled with proof of genuine activity, not from company registration alone. 58

The same caution applies to branches, representative offices, and remote-management models. Official sources are much fuller on self-employment and cross-border services than on "representative offices" as a discrete immigration category. The safest legal view is that the wrapper does not decide the answer: the question is still what activity the person will carry out, where, and with what impact on the Swiss labour market. Short negotiations and some brief strategy meetings may fall outside gainful activity; sustained Swiss-market-facing work generally does not. Remote management from abroad will usually not generate a Swiss residence right merely because the company is Swiss; Swiss immigration issues arise when the person actually works in Switzerland or performs Swiss-market-linked activity falling within the gainful-activity rules. 59

### H. Drafting cautions

·Do **not** say that forming a Swiss GmbH, AG, branch, or sole proprietorship gives a foreign national the right to live in Switzerland. 16

**·Do** **not** imply that acquiring shares in a Swiss company is the same thing as being authorised to work there. 60

·Do **not** imply that appointment as director, board member, president, or CEO automatically authorises work. 4

·Do **not** confuse commercial registration, regulatory licensing, or professional authorisation with immigration approval. 61

·Do **not** describe self-sponsorship by one's own Swiss company as automatic or straightforward; identify whether the legal route is employed work or self-employment and analyse it accordingly. 62

·Do **not** flatten EU/EFTA and non-EU cases into one founder route. 63

·Do not ignore the functional question whether the person will actually perform gainful activity in **Switzerland.** 64

·Do **not** assume that remote management from abroad is a Swiss residence route; analyse whether any Swiss labour-market activity is actually being carried on in Switzerland. 28

·Do **not** present a commercial-register entry, business plan, or incorporation document as anything more than evidence that may support an application. 65

·Do **not** rely on law-firm blogs, company-incorporation agents, or relocation marketing pages as immigration authority. The controlling sources are federal law, ordinnances, SEM/SECO guidance, and relevant cantonal materials. 66
