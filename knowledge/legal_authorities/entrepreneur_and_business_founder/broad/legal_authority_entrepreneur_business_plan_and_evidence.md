## Employment Work Authorisation Cases
Executive legal summary
In Swiss entrepreneur and founder cases, the crucial point is that a business plan is not an end in itself.
For third-country nationals, the governing legal question is whether admission to self-employment is in
Switzerland’s overall economic interest, whether the necessary financial and operational conditions are
met, whether the applicant has an adequate and independent source of income, and whether the
further statutory conditions tied into Article 19 FNIA are satisfied. The statute itself does not prescribe a
universal “entrepreneur document checklist”; rather, it sets legal criteria, and the business plan and
supporting documents function as evidence used by cantonal and federal authorities to test those
criteria.
That is why bare assertions are usually inadequate. The SEM directives state that, beyond the applicant’s
personal qualifications, the authorities must be able to verify the enterprise’s financial and operational
prerequisites and the applicant’s independent livelihood, and there must be proof that the planned
business activity will have sustainably positive effects on the Swiss labour market. The directives then
explain what that means in practice: a new business may be beneficial where it diversifies the regional
economy in its sector, preserves or creates jobs for local workers, makes substantial investments, and
generates new mandates for the Swiss economy; for start-ups, innovation capacity and transfer of
academic research may also count.
The role of the business plan is therefore evidential and criterion-linked. The SEM expressly requires, as
an application attachment, information on the business planning that gives insight into the proposed
activities, financing, market opportunity, development of personnel capacity and recruitment
possibilities, planned investments, expected turnover and profits. Existing organisational links with
other undertakings and binding financing commitments from external investors must be disclosed, and
the deed of incorporation and/or commercial register entry should in principle be filed, subject to a
narrow start-up-programme exception. This is the clearest federal administrative statement on what a
business plan must prove in immigration-law terms.
Official cantonal materials broadly track that logic, but they do not do so identically. Geneva requires a
complete project dossier or business plan covering the nature of the activity, organisation of the
business, and detailed three-year financial forecasts including turnover, costs, profits and specific
staffing; it also asks for proof of sufficient and autonomous income, a CV, diplomas, work certificates
and a motivation letter, while reserving the right to request further proof. Zurich states that, for third-
country self-employment, there must be enough start capital and that a business plan or projected
balance sheet is compulsory, with lease or credit agreements being helpful. Vaud’s official forms for EU/
EFTA self-employment ask for a business plan, proof of financial resources, a lease for business
premises, and detailed explanations of completed or planned administrative steps and professional
authorisations. Basel-Stadt’s official self-employment page for third-country nationals links both to a
dedicated self-employed application form and to a specific business-plan checklist; the search-preview

A central drafting caution is that not every founder case is legally classified as self-employment. The
SEM directives make clear that the classification is fact-sensitive. A title such as CEO, the existence of an
employment contract, or a commercial-register entry does not by itself determine whether the activity is
self-employed or salaried. What matters is the real organisation of the activity: control, absence of
subordination, power of direction, ownership position, and exposure to entrepreneurial and financial
risk. This matters because salary and local employment conditions are a formal admission criterion for
salaried-employment cases under Article 22 FNIA, but not for self-employment under Article 19 FNIA. In
founder cases conducted through a Swiss company, evidence often has to show not only that the
business is viable, but also why the founder’s role should be treated as self-employed or, if it is really
salaried, why salary and labour-market requirements are met.
Another important point for content drafting is evidential credibility over time. The SEM directives say
that self-employment and start-up permits should initially be limited to a maximum of two years and
that renewal should depend on the timely realisation of the promised sustainable positive effects;
renewal may be refused, for example, if the goals stated in the business plan are not achieved. That
means projected turnover, headcount, investment and market assumptions are not decorative. They
can become benchmarks against which renewal is judged. Blog content should therefore avoid treating
forecasts as mere presentational material.
For EU/EFTA nationals, the framework is materially different. Their case is not built around the third-
country “overall economic interest” test in Article 19 FNIA. Instead, official materials say they must prove
genuine intended or effective self-employment when applying for residence, and examples of proof
include a UID number, social-insurance registration as a self-employed person, a business plan,
accounting figures or a commercial-register entry. Cantonal forms, such as Vaud’s, show that even in
the EU/EFTA context authorities still expect documentary proof of business reality and financial means.
The safest drafting position for internal content is therefore this: Swiss law sets criteria, official federal
guidance gives a fairly specific picture of what those criteria must be evidenced by, and cantonal
materials show recurring document categories but not a single exhaustive national checklist. Any future
blog content should distinguish between formal legal requirements, officially described evidential
expectations, and prudent practical supporting evidence.
Structural overview
Purpose of business evidence. Business evidence is used to prove statutory admission criteria,
especially for third-country self-employment under Article 19 FNIA. The evidence must allow the
authorities to assess financial and operational readiness, the applicant’s independent livelihood, and
whether the project brings sustainably positive labour-market effects to Switzerland.
Business plan. The business plan is not expressly listed in the FNIA as a statutory form requirement,
but the SEM directives make it a core application attachment because it must explain planned activities,
financing, market opportunities, staffing and recruitment, investments, turnover and profits. Zurich and
Geneva also treat the business plan or equivalent projected financials as central.
Financial evidence. Relevant official materials point to proof of sufficient and autonomous income,
start capital, projected balance sheets or forecasts, binding financing commitments, bank statements

Operational evidence. Official materials indicate the relevance of premises, regulatory readiness and

Market and customer evidence. Federal guidance requires the business plan to address market
opportunity, and Zurich’s official explanatory PDF gives “market chances/comparison” and “orders” as
examples of supporting material. Official sources are thinner on exactly which customer-facing
documents are mandatory, so customer contracts, letters of intent, pipeline schedules and customer
lists should be treated as strong practice evidence rather than universally mandatory documents unless
a canton says otherwise.
Founder evidence. Founder qualifications matter because Article 19 incorporates Article 23 FNIA, and
the SEM states that founders and business leaders of labour-market-significant businesses may satisfy
the qualification test through the role they perform, but evidence is still needed. Geneva asks for CV,
diplomas and work certificates, and Zurich identifies training, employment certificates and CV as
relevant proof in third-country work cases.
Employment and salary evidence. Job-creation plans matter because preserving or creating jobs for
Swiss/local workers is expressly part of the “sustainably positive impact” analysis. Salary and local
employment conditions are a formal criterion for salaried-employment cases under Article 22 FNIA, not
the self-employment criterion in Article 19 FNIA. In founder cases involving a Swiss company, evidence
should therefore address both headcount/job impact and the legal classification of the founder’s own
role. Geneva’s checklist asks for precise staffing forecasts; SEM guidance on Article 22 requires the
canton to verify local, occupational and sectoral salary conditions for employees.
Canton-sensitive documentation. There is no single national checklist. Geneva, Zurich, Vaud and
Basel-Stadt all publish different official materials, and Zurich expressly says authorities may require
additional documents beyond the basic statutory criteria. Geneva likewise reserves the right to request
additional proof.
Common weak points. Weak cases commonly arise where the file contains only a polished narrative
without evidence of funding, market traction, regulatory readiness, founder qualifications or the
founder’s actual necessity and legal role. The SEM’s renewal guidance shows that unsupported
projections may later undermine renewal if business-plan targets are not met.
Drafting implications. Automated content should organise evidence by legal function and evidence
category, not by universal checklist language. It should say when an item is a statutory rule, official
guidance, canton-specific practice, or reasoned evidential strategy.

Primary legal texts
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration of 16 December 2005, SR 142.20, Article 11.
Foreign nationals who wish to work in Switzerland need a permit regardless of the length of stay, and
“gainful employment” includes both salaried and self-employed activity. In salaried cases the employer

files the application. This matters because founder cases start with classification: is the person applying
as self-employed, or as an employee of a Swiss company?
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 18. Salaried employment is admissible only
where it is in the interests of the economy as a whole, an employer application has been submitted, and
the requirements of Articles 20–25 are met. This matters because some founder cases are in substance
employee cases, especially where a Swiss company employs the founder under Swiss-law employment
terms.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 19. Self-employment is admissible only if it
is in the interests of the economy as a whole, the necessary financial and operational requirements are
fulfilled, the applicant has an adequate and independent source of income, and the requirements of
Articles 20 and 23–25 are met. This is the anchor provision for third-country founder and entrepreneur
work-authorisation cases and explains why business, financial, founder and accommodation evidence
matter.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 20. The Federal Council may limit first-time
L and B work permits by quota, and SEM allocates within the federal quota framework. This matters
because even a strong evidence bundle does not remove quota constraints in third-country cases.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 21. Foreign nationals may only be
permitted to work if no suitable domestic or free-movement-area workers can be found for the job. This
matters directly in salaried founder cases, but not as a separate criterion in self-employment under
Article 19 after the current statutory wording; practitioners should therefore avoid mechanically
importing employee-style resident labour market evidence into every self-employment case.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 22. Foreign nationals may only be
admitted to work if salary and employment conditions customary for the location, profession and sector
are satisfied. This matters where the founder is admitted as a salaried employee, rather than as a
genuinely self-employed person. It does not appear in Article 19’s self-employment condition-set.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 23. Short-stay and residence permits for
work may only be granted to managers, specialists and other qualified workers. This matters because
Article 19 imports Article 23; founder qualifications, sector experience and the necessity of the founder’s
personal role are therefore legally relevant.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 24. Suitable accommodation must be
available. In business-evidence terms this is peripheral, but it remains a formal admissibility
requirement imported into Article 19 via Articles 23–25.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 40. The cantons grant the relevant permits,
but the Confederation remains responsible for quotas and the approval procedure; where a foreign
national has no pre-existing right to work, the cantonal authority must issue a preliminary labour-
market decision for employment, change of job, or change to self-employment. This matters because
entrepreneur cases are structurally canton-led but federally supervised.
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, Article 99. The Federal Council determines which
cantonal permit grants and cantonal preliminary labour-market decisions must be submitted to SEM for
approval. This matters because self-employment decisions for third-country nationals are not purely
cantonal.

Official guidance and cantonal practice notes
At federal level, the SEM AIG Directives are the most important source on entrepreneur evidence. They
explain that the immigration-law concept of self-employment is autonomous and not identical to social-
security or labour-law classifications; that the authority must assess, case by case, whether genuinely
self-employed activity exists; and that real indicators include work in one’s own organisation, absence of
hierarchical subordination, real decision-making power, and exposure to financial/entrepreneurial risk.
The directives also explain the substantive economic-interest test, list typical indicators of positive
labour-market impact, require a business plan covering the main commercial and financial
assumptions, and tie renewal to delivery against the business-plan promises.
The SECO SME Portal is useful, but it should be treated as official guidance rather than binding primary
law. In its French and Italian pages for third-country entrepreneurs, it states that the business must
show a positive and durable effect on the Swiss labour market and explains that this may exist where
the enterprise diversifies the regional economy, creates jobs for locals, makes significant investments
and generates new mandates. It further says that entrepreneurs should already have a well-developed
business idea before relocating, that a convincing business plan is a good basis for a positive
assessment, that organisational links with other businesses should already exist, and that the
application should include incorporation papers and/or commercial-register proof. Its EU/EFTA pages
say that applicants must prove the intended self-employed activity and give examples such as UID
number, social-insurance registration, business plan, accounting figures and commercial-register entry.
For cantonal materials, the safest use is to treat them as canton-specific illustrations of evidential
expectations rather than as national law. Geneva’s third-country self-employed page is strong and
directly relevant. It states that the applicant must show an economic interest for the Swiss labour
market and that this can exist where the company diversifies the regional economy in its sector, obtains
or creates jobs for local labour, makes substantial investments and generates new mandates for the
Swiss economy. Geneva asks for a complete project dossier/business plan describing the nature of the
activity, business organisation, and detailed three-year forecasts including turnover, charges, profits and
specific staffing, along with proof of sufficient and autonomous income, CV, diplomas and work
certificates, and a motivation letter; it also says the administration reserves the right to require
additional proof. Procedurally, the Geneva page recommends prior contact with the canton’s economic
and innovation office to assess the project’s fit with Geneva’s economic context.
Zurich publishes two different types of relevant material. First, its public page for third-country
nationals says that, in self-employment cases, the activity must serve the overall economic interest,

Practical legal analysis
A Swiss entrepreneur business plan has to connect to immigration-law criteria because the authorities
are not assessing whether the proposal is an interesting venture in the abstract. They are assessing
whether the legal conditions for admission are evidenced. The SEM’s own formulation of required
business-plan content maps directly to Article 19 FNIA: activities and investments go to operational
readiness, financing and independent income go to solvency and self-maintenance, market opportunity
and turnover/profit forecasts go to viability, staffing and recruitment go to labour-market effects, and
organisational links plus investor commitments go to seriousness and substance. This is why future
blog drafting should always explain what a document proves, not merely what the document is called.

A generic pitch deck may therefore be insufficient. A pitch deck is often investor-facing and high level;
Swiss immigration guidance expects evidence that permits assessment of legal criteria. Federal
guidance specifically expects information on financing, market opportunity, staffing/recruitment,
investments, turnover and profits, and Geneva wants detailed three-year figures including specific
staffing. A polished deck without operational assumptions, financial substantiation, founder-proof and
formal corporate documents may not allow the authority to test the legal case. This is an inference from
the official evidential requirements, but it is a strong one.
The evidence should address both business viability and founder-role necessity. That follows from the
combined effect of Articles 19 and 23 FNIA and the SEM’s classification guidance. Authorities are
interested not only in whether a real business could exist, but in why this particular person should be
admitted to conduct it and whether they are in truth acting as a self-employed founder rather than
simply holding an executive title in a company that could function without them. Evidence should
therefore connect the founder’s qualifications, industry experience, network, ownership, control rights
and financial risk to the actual business model.
Financial forecasts require supporting assumptions because the SEM explicitly allows renewal to be
tested against whether the promised positive impacts and business-plan goals are actually delivered. A
forecast that is not tied to assumptions on customers, contracts, pricing, premises, staff, capital
expenditure and compliance readiness is not merely weak persuasion; it can become a later credibility
problem. Zurich’s public materials and Geneva’s checklist reinforce this by expecting projected
financials, start capital and, in Geneva, detailed three-year forecasts.
Official sources are thinner on exactly how authorities want market demand to be evidenced than on
financing and structure. The SEM directives require proof of market opportunity and positive labour-
market impact, and Zurich refers to market comparison and orders. From that, it is reasonable to infer
that customer contracts, framework agreements, letters of intent, pipeline schedules, customer lists
and evidence of active sales or pilot arrangements are useful corroborative material. But because the
official texts do not set a universal list, automated content should describe these as practical supporting
evidence, not mandatory documents in every case.
Cantonal document lists should not be treated as exhaustive. Three official sources point the same way:
FNIA Article 13 allows the competent authority to request further documents as needed; Zurich says
that depending on the facts additional documents may be required; and Geneva expressly reserves the
right to demand more proof. That means a canton’s published list is better understood as a starting
point or common minimum, not a closed evidential code.
For automated legal content, the most defensible architecture is to use evidence categories rather than
definitive universal checklists. A category-driven approach can say, for example, that official sources
indicate the relevance of: business-model evidence; financial and funding proof; market-demand proof;
operational readiness documents; founder qualification and role evidence; job-creation/employment
planning; and corporate-structure documents. Within each category, the content can distinguish
between what the SEM expressly identifies, what particular cantons publicly ask for, and what is prudent
but inferential corroboration. That is substantially safer than telling readers there is one fixed Swiss
entrepreneur-permit file universally required across all cantons and all nationality frameworks.
There is also an important nationality and route limitation. The evidence-heavy Article 19 FNIA analysis
principally concerns third-country nationals. EU/EFTA self-employed applicants operate under the free-
movement framework and must prove genuine self-employment, but not the same “overall economic
interest” threshold. Because public-facing content often collapses these routes into “Swiss entrepreneur

Open questions and limitations. The most detailed official federal guidance is concentrated on third-

demand proof, letters of intent, founder payroll arrangements, and corporate-governance evidence in

checklist, were only partially accessible through crawler snippets, so their use should remain explicitly
canton-specific and cautious.
Drafting cautions and source list
Drafting cautions
Do not present a single universal Swiss entrepreneur-permit document checklist unless a specific
official source says so for a defined canton or route.
Do not imply that a polished business plan is sufficient on its own; it must be supported by
documents proving financing, market opportunity, operational readiness, founder status and
qualifications.
Do not confuse investor pitch material with immigration evidence. The official focus is on
statutory proof, not fundraising presentation quality.
Do not overstate job creation as a formal minimum requirement. It is an important indicator of
positive labour-market impact, but the official texts present it as one of several ways to show
sustainable benefit.
Do not ignore founder qualifications and role necessity. Article 19 imports Article 23 FNIA, and
the SEM expressly ties qualification analysis to founder and business-leader functions.
Do not describe local salary compliance as a universal self-employment criterion. It is a formal
rule for salaried-employment cases; in founder matters it becomes relevant only where the route
is really salaried employment or where the founder’s structure makes salary evidence material.
Do not describe cantonal checklists as federal legal requirements without qualification. Geneva,
Zurich, Vaud and Basel-Stadt publish different materials for different routes.
Do not say any one document guarantees approval. Zurich expressly states there is no legal
entitlement to a permit and that cases are examined individually.
Do not ignore later credibility. The SEM says renewal may be refused if business-plan targets are
not achieved.
Do not merge EU/EFTA and third-country routes into one evidential standard. The legal tests
differ.
