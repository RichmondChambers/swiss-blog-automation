

## Swiss Founder And Entrepreneur Work
## Authorisation Authority Pack
Executive legal summary
Swiss founder and entrepreneur work-authorisation cases are unusually canton-sensitive because the
decisive federal rules are framed at a relatively high level of generality, while the first-instance
procedural pathway, the composition of the cantonal decision-makers, the evidence bundle, and many
practical sequencing questions are handled locally. For third-country nationals, the core federal route is
admission to self-employment under Article 19 AIG, read together with the general requirements in
Articles 20 to 25 AIG and the cantonal labour-market preliminary-decision machinery in Article 40 AIG
and Article 83 VZAE. Current official federal guidance also states that self-employed third-country cases
with residence in Switzerland are permit-required from day one and are subject to both cantonal and
federal procedure.
The federal criteria are demanding but not mechanically reducible to a single checklist. The file must
show, at minimum, that the project serves Switzerland’s overall economic interest, that the necessary
financial and operational conditions are met, that there is an autonomous and sufficient basis of
subsistence, and that the personal-qualification rules for third-country nationals are satisfied. The SEM’s
current directives make clear that the “overall economic interest” test in founder cases turns on
sustainable labour-market benefit, such as regional or sectoral diversification, job creation or retention,
significant investment, and new mandates for the Swiss economy; for start-ups, innovation capacity and
the commercialisation of academic research may also be relevant.
The canton-sensitive element arises because those federal criteria are operationalised differently across
cantons. Geneva expressly routes the file through OCPM and OCIRT, states that OCIRT gives the labour-
market preliminary decision and may seek the opinion of the tripartite economic commission, and
recommends prior contact with the cantonal economic and innovation office to assess the fit of the
project with the Geneva economic context. Zurich places third-country labour-market admissions within
the Office for Economy and publishes its own explanatory note requiring a specific application
justification, business plan, accounts or projected balance sheet, and often rental or credit documents.
Solothurn publishes an especially concrete list including market and cost analysis, Swiss cooperation
contracts, and evidence of acquired customers, and even publishes a service duration of three to six
weeks. Uri issues a detailed factsheet requiring, among other things, a three-year business plan,
criminal-record extract and translations into German, and states that the applicant must generally await
the procedure abroad. These are not differences in federal substantive law; they are differences in how
cantons structure proof and process around the same federal legal test.
Economic-development involvement must therefore be described carefully. It exists in some cantons,
but not always as a formal decisional step. Geneva is the clearest official example: prior contact with
OCEI is recommended before a third-country independent-permit filing, specifically to assess the
project’s fit with the local economy. Zurich’s business and economic development division and labour-
permit division sit within the same cantonal Office for Economy, which shows institutional proximity, but
Zurich’s published permit materials do not make economic-development endorsement a formal legal
requirement. Vaud and Zug both have official economic-promotion structures supporting business
creation and settlement, but the migration and labour-market materials reviewed here do not state that

Commercial-register sequencing is another area where over-generalisation is risky. SEM’s current

required nationally; it is that the permissible order of incorporation, business-plan finalisation and
immigration filing must be checked against the relevant canton’s own materials.
Finally, self-employment cases must be distinguished from freer-movement cases. The most
discretionary and evidence-heavy founder route is the third-country route. Cantonal EU/EFTA pages in
Bern, Solothurn and Basel-Stadt show a much lighter structure centred on registration and proof of a
genuine, effective self-employed activity. For internal content, that means “Swiss entrepreneur permit”
should never be treated as a single homogeneous route. If the case is a third-country founder case, the
content system should map the canton before generating procedural guidance.
Structural overview
Federal framework
The federal framework is built around Article 19 AIG for self-employment, Article 23 AIG for personal
qualifications, Article 40 AIG for cantonal permit authority and labour-market preliminary decisions, and
Article 99 AIG for SEM approval procedure. Article 83 VZAE assigns the first labour-market assessment
to the canton before first issuance of a work-related L or B permit, including self-employment under
Articles 18 to 25 AIG. Official SECO guidance confirms that third-country self-employment with Swiss
residence is permit-required from the first day and subject to a combined cantonal and federal
procedure.
Canton migration authority role
The migration authority is the residence-permit authority, but its exact procedural role varies by canton.
In Geneva, OCPM receives the file and issues the permit only if the labour-market arm has first accepted
it. In Bern, the Migration Service issues the residence permit after approval and indicates that renewals
generally also require approval from the cantonal economy office and, depending on the case, SEM. In
Vaud, the communal foreigner’s office acts as the reception and transmission desk for SPOP, and the
permit form itself routes filings differently depending on whether the applicant is already resident in
Vaud, resident in another canton, or abroad.
Labour-market authority role
The labour-market authority is often the real gatekeeper in founder cases. Federal law requires a
cantonal labour-market preliminary decision where there is no right to work. Geneva identifies OCIRT’s
foreign-labour directorate as the body competent to render the preliminary decision; Vaud’s form
contains a field for the prior decision of DISMAT; Bern requires filing with AWI; Zurich’s Office for
Economy handles third-country labour-permit applications; Zug’s Office for Economy and Labour

SEM and federal approval
SEM approval remains central in founder cases and must not be omitted in route-specific content. The
current SEM directives state that cantonal preliminary decisions admitting third-country nationals to
self-employment under Article 19 AIG are approval-subject irrespective of sector or qualification, by
reference to Article 1 letter a item 1 ZV-EJPD. Geneva’s official page similarly states that the cantonal
decision is then transmitted to SEM for approval. Under Article 85 VZAE, cantonal labour-market and
migration authorities may also refer cases to SEM where federal-law compliance needs to be reviewed.
Economic-development involvement
Economic-development involvement is not uniform. Geneva expressly recommends prior contact with
OCEI for third-country independent cases in order to evaluate the fit of the project with the Geneva
economic context. Zurich’s official organisational chart shows that Standortförderung and the labour-
permit division sit within the same Office for Economy, and Zurich’s settlement pages confirm that
cantonal business promotion accompanies firms from location evaluation to launch. Zug’s Office for
Economy and Labour formally contains both labour-force permits and economic-promotion functions,
and its business-contact point describes itself as a one-stop shop assisting with company formation and
required authorisations. Vaud’s SPEI officially supports implantation, creation and SME development,
but the Vaud permit materials reviewed here do not state that SPEI gives a binding immigration
assessment.
Document expectations
SEM’s founder guidance requires a business plan covering activities, financing, market opportunity,
staffing development and recruitment, planned investments, turnover and revenue, and asks for
organisational links, external investor commitments, and the deed of incorporation and/or trade-
register entry in principle. Zurich requires a specific justification, business plan, accounts or projected
balance sheet and often rental or credit documents. Solothurn asks for a business plan with market and
cost analysis, trade-register extract, proof of financial security, Swiss cooperation contracts and
evidence of acquired customers. Uri requires a three-year business plan, links to Swiss businesses, a
criminal-record extract and German translations. Geneva asks for a full project dossier with three-year
financial projections, proof of autonomous income, CV, diplomas, labour certificates and a motivation
letter. Basel-Stadt’s official self-employed page links to a distinct self-employed application and a
business-plan checklist; the searchable snippets from those official documents show that Basel expects
a detailed project sketch and, in its business-plan checklist, AHV compensation-office confirmation of
the self-employed activity.
Timing and sequencing
Published timing is sparse and must be qualified. Geneva states that the file should be lodged six to
eight weeks before the intended start date, and the same lead-time appears on Geneva’s frontier self-
employment page. Solothurn publishes a three- to six-week service duration. Bern’s third-country
employment page asks for filings at least eight weeks before the intended start date, which is not a
founder-specific statement but does show that some cantons publish advance-filing expectations. SEM’s
general FAQ on gainful employment says that the entry-residence-employment process can take six to
eight weeks, but that is not founder-specific and should be treated as a general federal orientation

Local variation
The practical route differs materially by canton. Geneva has the clearest published two-office model and
the strongest formal statement about economic-office pre-contact. Zurich publishes canton-specific
explanatory notes and runs the filing through its labour-permit platform. Solothurn and Uri publish
particularly detailed documentary expectations. Vaud splits reception and transmission across
communes, SPOP and the cantonal labour-market authority. Basel-Stadt distinguishes sharply between
a self-employed permit application and a business-plan checklist. Zug publishes a lighter substantive
page but embeds labour-force and economic-promotion functions in the same office. Ticino’s official
materials clearly identify a “third countries and labour market” service and a digital permit portal, but
publicly available founder-specific guidance appears comparatively thin.
Drafting implications
Future content must separate federal criteria from cantonal handling. It should say which points are
fixed by AIG/VZAE and SEM directives, and which points come from a particular canton’s filing route,
checklist or institutional setup. It should also avoid stating that economic-promotion input is a universal
or determinative element, because the official record shows that Geneva describes it expressly, while
other cantons more often describe it as business support rather than as a formal permit stage.

Primary legal texts
AIG article 19
Provision identified: Article 19, Bundesgesetz über die Ausländerinnen und Ausländer und über die
Integration (AIG; SR 142.20). The accessible official text states that foreign nationals may be admitted to
self-employment where this corresponds to the overall economic interest, the necessary financial and
business conditions are met, and an adequate autonomous basis of subsistence exists. Current federal
and cantonal guidance also applies the wider Articles 20 to 25 AIG framework to such cases, especially
quotas and personal qualifications. This matters because Article 19 is the anchor provision for third-
country founder admission.

AIG article 23
Provision identified: Article 23 AIG. The accessible official text states that work-related short-stay and
residence permits may generally be granted only to executives, specialists and other qualified workers.
This matters because federal law does not create a blanket entrepreneur exemption: even founder
cases are usually assessed through the third-country qualification filter unless a different legal basis
applies.
AIG article 38
Provision identified: Article 38 AIG, especially paragraph 3 on switching into self-employment. SEM’s
current directives explain that a person already holding a B permit without a work entitlement may
apply to the competent canton to switch from employed to self-employed activity, and that for
unrestricted B-permit holders the “overall economic interest” standard may be assessed more flexibly
than for newly arriving founders. This matters because founder pathways differ materially between new
entry and in-country status change.
AIG article 40
Provision identified: Article 40 AIG. The accessible official text states that the cantons issue the
relevant permits, but where there is no work entitlement a labour-market preliminary decision by the
competent cantonal authority is required for admission to work, job changes, and switching into self-
employment. This matters because founder cases often involve at least two cantonal actors: the
residence-permit authority and the labour-market authority.
AIG article 99
Provision identified: Article 99 AIG. The accessible official text states that the Federal Council
determines which permits and cantonal labour-market preliminary decisions must be submitted to SEM
for approval, and that SEM may refuse approval or restrict the cantonal decision. This matters because
positive cantonal handling does not end the matter in approval-subject founder cases.
VZAE article 83
Provision identified: Article 83, Verordnung über Zulassung, Aufenthalt und Erwerbstätigkeit (VZAE; SR
142.201). The accessible official text states that before the first issuance of a short-stay or residence
permit for gainful activity, the canton decides whether the requirements are met for self-employed or
employed activity under Articles 18 to 25 AIG. This matters because it is the regulatory basis for the
cantonal labour-market gatekeeping step.
VZAE articles 84 and 85
Provision identified: Articles 84 and 85 VZAE. Article 84 states that cantonal labour-market preliminary
decisions are valid for six months and can be extended for important reasons. Article 85 states that SEM
is competent for approval in the approval-required cases and that cantonal labour-market and
migration authorities may refer a cantonal decision to SEM to check compliance with federal law. This
matters because founder files may need re-filing or approval routing if implementation is delayed, and
because referral power exists even in doubtful cases.

VZAE article 88 and ZV-EJPD article 1
Provision identified: Article 88 VZAE on cantonal enforcement authorities, and Article 1 of the
Verordnung des EJPD über die dem Zustimmungsverfahren unterliegenden ausländerrechtlichen
Bewilligungen und Vorentscheide (ZV-EJPD), as described in the current SEM directives. Article 88 leaves it
to the cantons to designate the competent authorities. The SEM directives state that self-employment
admissions under Article 19 AIG are approval-subject under Article 1 letter a item 1 ZV-EJPD, regardless
of sector or qualification. This matters because institutional layouts differ by canton, but the federal
approval overlay remains nationally important.
Official federal guidance
The most important federal guidance instrument is the SEM’s current Weisungen und Erläuterungen im
Ausländerbereich, chapter on stay with gainful activity, current to 1 January 2026. For founder matters, it
does five things of practical importance. First, it clarifies the immigration-law definition of self-
employment and notes that titles such as president or CEO, or even trade-register entry alone, do not
by themselves settle the self-employed versus employed distinction. Secondly, it gives substantive
content to the “overall economic interest” test in founder/start-up files. Thirdly, it states that positive
cantonal preliminary decisions under Article 19 AIG are approval-subject. Fourthly, it directs that first
founder permits should usually be limited to a maximum of two years in the start-up phase, with
extension tied to actual delivery against the plan. Fifthly, it specifies the expected business-plan
evidence and the limited possibility of filing incorporation or trade-register evidence later for supported
start-ups.
The SEM directives also matter on status changes. Their current treatment of Article 38 paragraph 3 AIG
says that a switch from employed to self-employed activity by a B-permit holder may be assessed more
generously where the permit was not subject to restrictive conditions and the person is already
integrated into Swiss society and the labour market. That is a route-specific nuance which should be
preserved in internal content, because it is not the same as the new-entry entrepreneur route.
SEM’s public FAQ on gainful employment gives general procedural orientation. It states that for third-
country employment the application is lodged in the canton of work, and that if the canton approves,
the authorisation is transmitted to the Swiss representation abroad; it also gives a general lead-time of
around six to eight weeks. That guidance is not founder-specific and should not be treated as a
published service standard for self-employed start-up files, but it is still useful as federal-level context
on the canton-plus-federation sequencing model.
SECO’s page on self-employed third-country nationals provides a compact, current federal statement of
scope. It says that self-employment by third-country nationals who take up residence in Switzerland is,
in principle, permit-required from the first day and subject to the permit procedure of canton and
Confederation. This is particularly useful for keeping internal content tightly focused on the third-
country founder route and distinguishing it from EU/EFTA free-movement cases.

## Zurich
Authority and source. Zurich’s official materials sit mainly on the Office for Economy pages and related
migration pages. Filing route or procedure. Zurich publishes a canton-specific process note for self-
employed persons and routes labour-market filings through its work-permit system; its migration page
also shows that where the person lives in another canton, the Migration Office acts after receiving the
labour-market preliminary decision. Document expectations. Zurich’s entrepreneur guidance for third-
country nationals requires a specific application justification, business plan, balance sheet or projected
balance sheet, and may use rental or credit contracts as supporting evidence; the broader third-country
note says applications are examined individually and the authority may request additional papers.
Documents should be in German or English. Economic-development involvement. Zurich’s
organisational materials show that business-and-economic development and the labour-permit division
sit in the same Office for Economy, and the canton’s settlement pages say business promotion
accompanies firms from evaluation to start. But the entrepreneur permit materials do not make
business-promotion endorsement a mandatory immigration step. Timing information. No founder-
specific service time was identified in the reviewed Zurich entrepreneur materials. Classification.
Federal-law criteria appear on the Zurich pages, but the document package and institutional layout are
canton procedure/guidance.
## Solothurn
Authority and source. Solothurn publishes both a topic page and a service page through the
Migrationsamt. Filing route or procedure. The application is made in the canton where the company
seat is located; if the company is based in Solothurn, the applicant must submit the form and listed
documents to the cantonal authority. Document expectations. Solothurn’s service page is one of the
most document-rich official cantonal sources reviewed, requiring passport copy, business plan including
market and cost analysis, trade-register extract, rental contract or offer, CV, diplomas/qualifications, job
description, proof of financial security, cooperation contracts with Swiss companies, evidence of
acquired customers, and a reasoned cover letter. Economic-development involvement. No formal
economic-development role is described on the reviewed Solothurn entrepreneur pages. Timing
information. The service page publishes a duration of three to six weeks. Classification. This is official
cantonal procedure/guidance, not federal law.

## Zug
Authority and source. Zug’s official self-employment and labour-force pages sit within the Office for
Economy and Labour. Filing route or procedure. The self-employment page routes third-country
founders to the “B2” application for residence over four months and gives the Office for Economy and
Labour as the contact point. Document expectations. Zug’s entrepreneur page states that a business
plan or projected balance sheet is mandatory and that rental or credit agreements are helpful
supporting evidence; it also repeats the need for enough start capital and qualifying personal
characteristics. Economic-development involvement. Zug’s official organisational pages are notable
because they place work and residence permits and economic-promotion services in the same office,
and the economic-promotion contact point describes itself as a one-stop shop that helps with company
formation, required authorisations and settlement. That said, the entrepreneur page itself still frames
the immigration decision through the federal criteria, not through an economic-promotion
endorsement. Timing information. No founder-specific processing time was identified in the reviewed
Zug materials. Classification. Official cantonal guidance/procedure.

Bern and Ticino
Bern and Ticino are useful chiefly as cautionary contrasts. Bern’s reviewed official materials clearly show
the two-body structure for third-country labour cases: filing with AWI at least eight weeks in advance on
the labour side, and permit issuance by the Migration Service after approval on the migration side. But
the reviewed Bern founder-specific public material is thinner than Geneva’s or Solothurn’s. Ticino’s
official pages clearly identify a “third countries and labour market” service and a central permit portal,
yet public founder-specific narrative guidance in the reviewed sources is comparatively sparse; this itself
is a useful drafting point, because not every canton publishes the same level of entrepreneur-specific
operational detail.
Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
Entrepreneur cases require local procedural mapping because the federal test is only half the story.
“Overall economic interest”, “necessary financial and business conditions”, and “qualified worker” are
national concepts, but the authority that first evaluates them, the form in which they are proved, and
the degree to which the canton expects local market evidence, Swiss counterparties, regulatory-
licensing roadmaps, or advance contact all vary by canton. Geneva’s file architecture is not Zurich’s;
Solothurn’s published checklist is much more granular than many other cantons; Uri adds an await-
abroad rule and translation requirement; Vaud’s forms show a different allocation between commune,
migration office and labour-market authority.
That local variation affects both evidence and timing. A file that is legally viable in principle may still be
weak in practice if the selected canton expects a more developed market case, fuller local regulatory
mapping, or stronger evidence that the business is already operational or credibly funded. It also
affects chronology. SEM’s directives and several cantonal forms show that company formation and
immigration filing do not always follow a single mandatory national sequence: some files may be
lodged while the company is still being formed, while other cantonal materials demand a trade-register
extract or strongly prefer incorporation evidence up front. Any automated route guidance that collapses
these differences into one national sequence would be misleading.
Economic-development involvement should be stated conservatively. Officially described involvement
ranges from Geneva’s explicit recommendation of OCEI pre-contact, through Zurich’s and Zug’s
institutional proximity between economic-promotion and labour-permit functions, to cantons where the
reviewed migration materials do not formally place economic-promotion bodies in the permit workflow
at all. Support from a business-promotion body may strengthen the commercial credibility of a dossier,
but the reviewed official sources do not justify a general statement that such support guarantees,
strongly predicts, or formally substitutes for labour-market and migration approval.

A route may therefore be legally open yet practically difficult in a particular canton. The SEM directives
themselves show that first permits are time-limited and that extension depends on actual results
against the business plan. Cantons then add evidential and procedural texture. In internal drafting
terms, “possible under Article 19 AIG” and “practically strong in Canton X on the currently published
evidence model” are not the same proposition and should be kept separate.
The common weak points in founder files can be inferred from the official sources with reasonable
confidence. They include an under-developed business plan; failure to show why the project serves
Swiss rather than merely private economic interests; inadequate proof of start capital, operating
premises or autonomous subsistence; uncertainty about whether the applicant is genuinely self-
employed rather than functionally employed; missing or badly sequenced company-formation
evidence; missing local regulatory permissions; and failure to appreciate that SEM approval still sits
above the canton in approval-subject cases. Those weaknesses are not all listed in one federal text, but
they emerge consistently from the SEM directives and the cantonal checklists and forms reviewed here.
Drafting cautions
Do not say that entrepreneur or founder procedure is the same in every canton.
Do not treat cantonal forms, checklists or service pages as federal law. Use them as canton-
specific procedure or guidance only.
Do not omit SEM approval in new third-country self-employment cases.
Do not imply that economic-development support guarantees a permit outcome.
Do not nationalise one canton’s filing sequence, especially on whether trade-register evidence
must exist before filing.
Do not promise processing times unless the timing is officially published for the relevant canton
and expressly qualified.
Do not collapse new-entry founder cases into the different Article 38 paragraph 3 AIG switch-to-
self-employment route for existing B-permit holders.
Do not assume “self-employed” merely because the person is a shareholder, director or CEO; the
federal guidance requires real analysis of organisational control and entrepreneurial risk.
Do not ignore local regulatory authorisations outside immigration where a canton expressly asks
for them in the entrepreneur file.
Open questions and limitations
Official material is uneven across cantons. Geneva, Zurich, Solothurn and Uri publish comparatively
usable entrepreneur-specific guidance; Vaud is more form-based; Ticino’s entrepreneur-specific public
guidance appears thinner in the reviewed sources. Some official downloadable forms on Basel-Stadt
and Zug were indexed in search but could not be fully opened through the search interface, so this pack
relies on their searchable official snippets for the specific points attributed to them. In addition, the
Fedlex article texts available through the search index were not always the latest searchable
consolidation pages; accordingly, the pack uses those texts mainly for stable structural propositions
that are also reflected in current 2026 SEM or current cantonal guidance.
