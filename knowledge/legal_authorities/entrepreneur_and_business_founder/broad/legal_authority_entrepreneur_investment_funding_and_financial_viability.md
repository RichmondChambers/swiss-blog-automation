Executive legal summary
This pack concerns Swiss entrepreneur and founder work-authorisation cases, with the main focus on
third-country nationals, because that is where Swiss admission law expressly tests overall economic
interest, financial and operational substance, quotas, local salary conditions where relevant, and the
applicant’s own means of maintenance. EU/EFTA founders sit in a different legal framework under free
movement: they generally need to show an effective self-employed activity that is self-supporting,
rather than satisfy the stricter third-country economic-interest and quota regime.
For third-country founders, funding matters, but it matters as evidence within a cumulative legal test
rather than as an autonomous route to residence. The central statutory provision is Article 19 FNIA/AIG,
which permits self-employment only where the activity serves the overall economic interest, the
necessary financial and operational prerequisites are met, the founder has an adequate independent
basis of subsistence, and the other statutory conditions are also satisfied. SEM’s 2026 directives then
operationalise that test: the applicant must show durable positive effects on the Swiss labour market,
and the business plan must disclose activities, financing, market opportunities, staffing development,
recruitment possibilities, planned investments, turnover and earnings. Binding commitments from
external investors must also be shown where they exist.
Accordingly, official Swiss materials reviewed for this pack do not establish a fixed national minimum
investment threshold for entrepreneur work authorisation. What the sources ask for is functional
sufficiency: enough start capital, enough financing, enough operational infrastructure, enough proof of
market demand, and enough personal maintenance capacity to make the project credible. Cantonal
materials from Zurich, Zug, Geneva and Solothurn are consistent with that approach: they ask for
business plans, plan balance sheets, proof of financial security, contracts, leases, credit information,
customer evidence, and in Geneva a quantified three-year forecast.
Investment therefore helps only when it is tied to business substance. SEM’s directives expressly treat
substantial investment as one possible indicator of durable labour-market benefit, but place it
alongside wider factors such as regional diversification, job preservation or creation, and generation of
new orders for the Swiss economy. For start-ups, innovation and the commercialisation of academic
research can also count in the economic-interest analysis. Equally, start-up status does not displace the
need for evidence: initial permits should generally be limited to two years, and extension may be
refused if the business-plan objectives are not reached.
Personal maintenance requires separate analysis. Swiss law does not treat company money and the
founder’s own livelihood as interchangeable by default. Article 19 AIG expressly requires an “adequate
independent basis of subsistence”, and the cantonal materials reviewed repeatedly ask whether the
project will generate enough income to cover both business costs and living costs, or else require proof
of sufficient autonomous income. That means a well-capitalised company may still present a weak

Founder salary issues arise where the case is treated as employed activity through a Swiss company

an employment contract, a CEO title, or a GmbH structure does not by itself decide whether the founder
is self-employed or employed. If the founder is treated as employed by the Swiss company, Article 22

and SEM states that equity-based components may count only if direct funds needed for subsistence
are available and the equity remains proportionate to base salary. This is a major drafting point for
founder-content systems: salary compliance cannot be omitted simply because the applicant is “a
founder”.
Official materials are comparatively thin on a standalone “source-of-funds” test in entrepreneur work-
authorisation cases. I found no federal entrepreneur-specific rule in the reviewed materials requiring a
separate lawful-source affidavit in the style seen in some investment migration programmes. What the
official sources do show is an evidential expectation to demonstrate how the business is financed: own
and external capital, credit liabilities, guarantees, investor commitments, proof of financial security, and
related documents. Content should therefore present source-of-funds as an evidential credibility issue,
not as a clearly codified separate statutory limb unless a specific canton or case file justifies stronger
wording.
Structural overview
Legal and official basis
The governing federal framework is the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and the Ordinance on
Admission, Residence and Employment, supplemented by SEM’s official directives. For third-country
founders, Article 19 AIG/FNIA is the key self-employment provision; Articles 20 and 23–25 add quotas,
personal requirements, and ancillary conditions; Article 22 governs local salary and working conditions
where the case is treated as employed activity. SEM’s public “Basis for admission to the Swiss
employment market” page and its 2026 AIG directives are the main official interpretive materials.
Investment and capitalisation
Investment is relevant because SEM treats substantial investment as one possible indicator that a
project serves the overall economic interest, and because the business plan must address financing,
planned investments, turnover and earnings. Binding financing commitments from external investors
are expressly relevant in SEM’s 2026 directives. But investment is only one element in a wider
assessment that also looks at market demand, labour-market benefit, jobs, regional diversification and
business substance.
Financial viability
Swiss official materials focus on whether the business has the necessary financial and operational basis,
not on a fixed amount of capital. Zurich and Zug both state publicly that sufficient start capital must be
available and that the projected activity must generate enough income to cover business costs and
living costs; Geneva requires a complete business plan with detailed three-year financial forecasts;
Solothurn asks for proof of financial security, market and cost analysis, Swiss cooperation contracts and
acquired customers.

Personal maintenance and salary
Article 19 AIG includes an adequate independent basis of subsistence as a separate legal element.
Geneva asks for proof of sufficient and autonomous income; Zurich and Zug publicly frame the test in
terms of covering operating costs and living costs. Where the founder is instead treated as an employee
of a Swiss company, Article 22 AIG applies: local salary and working conditions must be respected, and
SEM requires a signed written contract. SEM also accepts some equity-style remuneration, but only if
direct financial means for subsistence are present and the equity remains proportionate to base salary.
Revenue and runway
The federal guidance does not use the venture-capital term “runway”, but it clearly asks for the
information from which runway is judged: financing, market chance, recruitment plans, planned
investments, turnover and earnings. Geneva’s official page requires detailed three-year quantitative
forecasts; Zurich requires a business plan and plan balance sheet. In practice, this means the authorities
are testing whether the project can sustain operations long enough to become viable, not simply
whether money has been injected at one point in time. That “runway” reading is an inference from the
official evidence requirements, not an express statutory term.
Source of funds
The reviewed official sources do not set out a separate federal entrepreneur “lawful source of funds”
article. They do, however, require evidence of financing structure and financial security. SEM asks for
binding external investor commitments where relevant. Solothurn asks for proof of financial security.
Basel-Stadt’s official form snippets ask for the share of own and borrowed capital, existing or planned
credit, and planned guarantees from private or public bodies. Source-of-funds should therefore be
treated as an evidential and credibility issue unless a file contains a specific money-origin concern or a
canton publishes stronger requirements.
Canton-sensitive evidence
Official cantonal expectations vary materially. Geneva is notably detailed on three-year forecasts and
autonomous income. Zurich and Zug openly require business plan or plan balance sheet and enough
start capital. Solothurn publishes a specific evidence list including Swiss contracts and customer
acquisition evidence. Lucerne says the complexity of these cases means the filing package should be
agreed in advance with the canton on a case-by-case basis. Basel-Stadt points applicants to a specific
business-plan checklist and official forms that ask for financing composition, AHV proof, lease evidence
and proof of orders.
Refusal risks
The official materials point to recurring refusal risk where the project is under-evidenced,
undercapitalised, weak on market proof, unable to sustain the founder personally, or misclassified
between self-employment and employed activity. SEM also says extension may be refused where
business-plan goals are not achieved. Inference: applications are particularly exposed where there is
capital but no convincing route to Swiss economic benefit, or where projections are speculative and
unsupported by contracts, customers, investor commitments, programme backing, or credible
operational documents.

Drafting implications
Internal content should describe funding as supportive evidence, not as a standalone residence route.
It should avoid any unsourced minimum-investment claim, should separate business financing from the
founder’s maintenance position, and should flag salary/local-conditions analysis whenever the founder
may in substance be employed by the Swiss company. It should also state expressly where official
material is thin, especially on source-of-funds formalities, runway terminology, and the treatment of
accelerators or grants.

Primary legal texts
Because the official Fedlex article texts were not directly retrievable in this interface, the article wording
or close paraphrases below rely on official SEM legal-basis pages and guidance, supplemented where
necessary by clearly identified fedlex-derived legal mirrors used only to reproduce the article text
conservatively.
Article 19 of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act
Provision: Article 19 AIG/FNIA, Ausübung einer selbständigen Erwerbstätigkeit / Self-employment, in the
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, SR 142.20. The provision states, in substance, that
foreign nationals may be admitted for self-employment where the activity serves the overall economic
interest, the necessary financial and operational prerequisites are met, an adequate independent basis
of subsistence exists, and the conditions in Articles 20 and 23–25 are fulfilled. It matters because it
makes funding, business viability and personal maintenance co-equal parts of the legal test rather than
optional supporting material.
Article 23 of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act
Provision: Article 23 AIG/FNIA, Persönliche Voraussetzungen / Personal requirements, same Act. The article
limits work-related residence permits in principle to managers, specialists and other qualified workers,
but paragraph 3 allows derogations for, among others, investors and entrepreneurs who maintain
existing jobs or create new jobs. It matters because founders sometimes rely on Article 23(3)(a), but the
provision only addresses personal requirements; it does not displace the separate Article 19 self-
employment requirements.
Article 22 of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act
Provision: Article 22 AIG/FNIA, Lohn- und Arbeitsbedingungen / Salary and employment conditions, same
Act. In substance, admission for work requires compliance with local, occupational and sector-standard
pay and working conditions; for posted workers, assignment-related expenses such as travel,
subsistence and accommodation must be reimbursed and do not count as salary. It matters where a
founder is treated as employed by a Swiss company, including a founder-controlled start-up, because
salary compliance must then be analysed on ordinary labour-market terms.

Article 20 of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act
Provision: Article 20 AIG/FNIA, Begrenzungsmassnahmen / Limitation measures, same Act. In substance,
the Federal Council limits the number of short-stay and residence permits for gainful employment,
taking account of cantonal needs and overall economic interests. It matters because even a financially
credible founder case can still be affected by quota availability in third-country cases.
Article 24 of the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act
Provision: Article 24 AIG/FNIA, Wohnung / Accommodation, same Act. In substance, foreign nationals
may be admitted for gainful employment only if they have accommodation appropriate to their needs.
It matters as part of the cross-reference in Article 19(d), though in entrepreneur drafting it is usually
secondary to business viability and maintenance.
Article 2 of the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment
Provision: Article 2 VZAE/OASA, Selbstständige Erwerbstätigkeit / Activité lucrative indépendante,
Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, SR 142.201. The provision defines self-
employment, in substance, as activity carried on within one’s own freely chosen organisation, for profit,
under one’s own material direction and at one’s own entrepreneurial risk. It matters because the
immigration-law concept is substance-based: the authorities look at actual organisation, control and
risk, not only company law labels.
Article 85 of the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment
Provision: Article 85(3) VZAE/OASA, same Ordinance. It provides that cantonal labour-market and
migration authorities may submit a cantonal decision to SEM so that the federal authority can review
compliance with federal conditions. It matters because SEM’s own directives additionally state that
cantonal advance decisions on third-country self-employment are approval-sensitive, which reinforces
the federal supervisory layer in founder cases.
Official guidance and cantonal practice notes
Official federal guidance and administrative materials
SEM’s public page on the admission of non-EU/EFTA workers is the clearest federal overview. It confirms
the dual system, states that third-country admission must serve the overall economic interest, explains
that quotas apply, and summarises Article 22 salary/local-conditions control and the need for a signed
contract. It is the safest public federal page for concise route descriptions.
SEM’s 2026 AIG directives, chapter 4, are the decisive administrative source for founder-work-
authorisation analysis. They state that for third-country self-employment the applicant must prove
durable positive effects on the Swiss labour market; explain that this can include regional
diversification, preservation or creation of jobs, significant investment, and new orders for the Swiss
economy; note that start-up innovation and research commercialisation can be considered; require a
business plan covering financing, market chance, staffing, recruitment, investments, turnover and
earnings; demand disclosure of binding external investor commitments; and state that initial permits
are generally limited to two years with renewal linked to business-plan delivery.

The same directives are also the key source for salary and founder-remuneration issues. They explain
that authorities must test local, occupational and sector-standard pay and working conditions; may use
statistical salary tools such as Salarium and the national wage calculator; require a signed written
contract for employed cases; and allow employee participation/equity elements to count only where
there are direct means for subsistence and the participation element remains proportionate to base
pay.
SEM’s annex on local salary and working conditions, together with the official salary tools of SECO and
the Federal Statistical Office, shows how Swiss authorities approach “local conditions” analysis. SECO’s
national wage calculator and FSO’s Salarium both expressly disclaim that their outputs are wage
recommendations, but they are official statistical tools used as reference points in salary assessment.
For EU/EFTA founders, SEM’s free-movement FAQ is the most practical official source. It requires EU/
EFTA self-employed persons to present documents proving that they are carrying on, or will carry on,
self-employed activity that is sufficient to support themselves and their family, and states that self-
employed persons receive a B permit on proof of effective self-employment. This is materially different
from the third-country entrepreneur test.
A more peripheral but still useful official federal source is the SECO SME Portal guidance on self-
employment. It is not migration guidance, but it confirms the administrative steps for recognition of
self-employed status and notes that in a limited company the company is a separate legal entity, which
helps explain why founder remuneration and immigration classification may require separate analysis.
It should be used as background only, not as the primary immigration authority.

The reviewed cantonal materials show a consistent pattern: Swiss entrepreneur cases are document-
heavy, business-plan-driven, and sensitive to whether the canton sees both a viable business and a
viable personal maintenance position. They also show meaningful canton-level variation in how
explicitly financial documents are itemised.
Practical legal analysis
Funding can strengthen a Swiss founder case, but the official sources do not support describing
funding as a self-standing residence route. The law asks whether the founder satisfies Article 19’s
cumulative test, and SEM’s directives ask whether the project will sustainably benefit the Swiss labour
market. Funding helps prove that the project is real, financed, and capable of delivery. It does not
dispense with the need to show economic interest, adequate subsistence, personal eligibility, quotas
where applicable, and genuine business substance. That is why Article 23(3)(a) must be handled with
care: it may relax the personal-qualification filter for certain investors and entrepreneurs, but it does
not eliminate Article 19.
Financial viability must connect to business substance because Swiss authorities ask for much more
than proof that money exists. The federal directives require a business plan covering activities,
financing, market chance, staffing, recruitment, planned investments, turnover and earnings. Cantons
then localise that into document requests such as plan balance sheets, market analyses, lease evidence,
credit information, contracts, orders and customer acquisition. In practical terms, money is persuasive
only if it is tied to a coherent operational story: what the business will do, who the customers are, why
Switzerland benefits, what costs will arise, and how those costs will be met over time.

A founder’s salary and personal maintenance often need a separate analysis because the statute
expressly separates the applicant’s independent subsistence from the business’s finance. Zurich and
Zug make that separation explicit by asking whether the activity will cover both operating costs and
living costs; Geneva requires proof of sufficient autonomous income in addition to a detailed business
plan. This strongly suggests that company capitalisation and personal maintenance are analytically
distinct. A company may be solvent on paper while the founder remains unable to show how they will
lawfully and sustainably support themselves during the start-up phase. That distinction becomes even
sharper if family members are included, because Geneva asks separately for supporting evidence of
family finances.
Early-stage and pre-revenue businesses are not excluded by the official materials, but they require
especially careful evidential treatment. SEM expressly recognises that start-ups may satisfy the
economic-interest test through innovation and implementation of academic research. It also allows
supported start-ups in cantonal or federal programmes to file incorporation or commercial-register
evidence shortly after filing rather than at the outset. At the same time, SEM expects the business plan
to contain financing, planned investment, turnover and earnings, and says that initial permits should
generally be limited to two years, with renewal tied to whether the promised impact is actually
delivered. The reasonable inference is that pre-revenue cases can succeed, but only if the non-revenue
evidence is unusually strong.
A useful drafting distinction is between evidence of capital and evidence of market reality. Swiss
clients, Swiss contracts, Swiss orders, customer acquisition, and fit with the regional economy all matter
because the legal test is about Swiss labour-market and economic benefit. Solothurn asks for
cooperation contracts with Swiss companies and evidence of acquired customers; Basel-Stadt asks for
proof of orders where available; Zurich wants business-plan material including orders; Geneva
recommends prior engagement with its economy and innovation office for local-context assessment.
None of these items is a statutory prerequisite in every case, but together they show what makes
financial projections look credible rather than speculative.
The official sources are notably thinner on “source of funds” in the narrow sense of tracing lawful origin.
What they do show is a practical need to evidence the financing structure. SEM asks for binding investor
commitments where relevant. Solothurn asks for proof of financial security. Basel-Stadt’s forms go
further, asking for the mix of own and borrowed capital, existing or planned loans, and guarantees from
private or public bodies. Internal content should therefore avoid importing investment-migration
language from other jurisdictions. In Swiss entrepreneur work-authorisation cases, the safer
formulation is that authorities need credible documentary evidence of financing and may scrutinise
where the money comes from if the file raises credibility issues, but the reviewed official materials do
not establish a standalone federal source-of-funds limb for this route.
Founder salary requires special caution where the founder works through a Swiss company. SEM’s
directives make clear that a founder is not automatically “self-employed” just because they are a
shareholder, manager or registered executive. The authorities look at control, subordination and
entrepreneurial risk. If the case is handled as employed activity, Article 22 AIG applies in full. SEM
requires a signed written contract, and it uses local salary analysis to protect against underpayment and
social dumping. Its allowance for equity-style remuneration is narrow and explicitly conditioned on the
founder still having direct funds for subsistence. This means founder content should never assume that
a low-cash/high-equity start-up salary is automatically acceptable for immigration purposes.
For content drafting, the most important negative proposition is this: official sources do not support
saying that “investing X francs in Switzerland gives a right to residence” in entrepreneur/self-
employment cases. The legal architecture is built around economic interest, operational and financial

Do not say that putting money into a Swiss business automatically gives a right to residence or
work authorisation.
Do not conflate Article 23(3)(a) “investors and entrepreneurs” with the whole admission test;
Article 19 still has to be met.
Do not treat capital alone as proof of economic interest; Swiss benefit, market demand, jobs,
diversification, orders and delivery capacity still matter.
Do not omit salary/local-conditions analysis where the founder may in substance be employed
by the Swiss company.
Do not ignore the founder’s own maintenance and, where relevant, family maintenance.
Company funds and personal subsistence are not the same issue.
Do not overstate the significance of venture-capital backing, grants or accelerator participation
without legal analysis. They are corroborative evidence, not automatic eligibility.
Do not use “runway” or “source of funds” as if they were statutory Swiss terms unless the text
makes clear that these are analytical labels derived from business-plan and financing evidence
requirements.
Do not transplant third-country entrepreneur language into EU/EFTA founder content. The legal
test is materially different.
