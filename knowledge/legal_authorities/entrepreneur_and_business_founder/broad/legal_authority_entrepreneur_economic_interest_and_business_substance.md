Executive legal summary
Swiss founder, entrepreneur and self-employment work-authorisation cases turn centrally on economic
interest and business substance because Switzerland does not operate a free-standing “entrepreneur
visa” in the materials reviewed. For third-country nationals, founder cases are folded into the general
labour-market admission regime for gainful activity, especially Article 19 AIG, read with Article 3 AIG,
the quota rules, the personal-qualification rules, and the implementing/approval framework. In other
words, the legal question is not simply whether a person has incorporated a company or can describe a
plausible business idea, but whether the proposed self-employed activity meets a source-based
immigration test tied to Switzerland’s overall economic interest, the business’s financial and operational
reality, and the founder’s ability to implement the project credibly.
For third-country applicants, Article 19 AIG requires more than a business concept. The applicant must
show that the activity serves the overall economic interests of Switzerland, that the necessary financial
and operational conditions are met, that there is an adequate independent basis of subsistence, and that
the relevant quota and personal-requirement rules are satisfied. The current SEM directives make the
point more concretely: the applicant must prove that the planned self-employed activity will have
sustainably positive effects on the Swiss labour market. SEM identifies examples rather than a closed list:
contribution to regional sectoral diversification, maintenance or creation of several jobs for local
workers, substantial investment, and the generation of new orders for the Swiss economy. For start-
ups, SEM further says that innovation capacity and the implementation of findings from academic
research may be considered.
“Business substance” is equally central because Swiss immigration law does not treat company form as
decisive. The implementing ordinance defines self-employment as activity carried out within one’s own
freely chosen organisation, aimed at generating income, under one’s own instructions, and with one’s
own entrepreneurial risk. The SEM directives then warn that, for immigration purposes, an employment
contract, a title such as president or CEO, or even a commercial-register entry with sole signatory power
do not by themselves prove the relevant status. Founders must show that they are not subordinated to
a superior body, that they act in their own organisation, and that they bear genuine entrepreneurial
risk. This is the clearest official basis for distinguishing a real operating business from a nominal or
paper company.
Commercial attractiveness is therefore not enough. The required evidence is explicitly immigration-
facing. SEM says the business plan must cover planned activities, financing, market opportunity,
staffing development, recruitment possibilities, planned investments, turnover and profits,
organisational links with other companies, and binding commitments from external investors;
incorporation documents and/or the commercial-register entry should normally be supplied as well.

Cantonal guidance goes in the same direction: Zurich asks for a business plan, forecast balance sheet,
and material such as debt and office-rental contracts; Vaud asks for a business plan, forecast budget, CV
and diplomas; Geneva asks for a full project dossier/business plan with detailed three-year financial
projections and proof of an autonomous source of income. These requirements are not decorative.
They are the evidence by which the authority tests whether the project is operationally credible rather
than speculative.
Founder credibility also matters as a separate legal question. Third-country self-employment cases
remain linked to Article 23 AIG on personal qualifications. The statute generally limits employment
admission to executives, specialists and other qualified workers, while expressly allowing investors and
entrepreneurs who preserve or create jobs as an exception to the ordinary qualification model. Current
SEM and cantonal materials continue to expect evidence of the founder’s training, work history and
implementation capacity through CVs, diplomas and work certificates. Where a person already holds a
Swiss B permit and seeks to switch from employment to self-employment, SEM says the “overall
economic interest” standard may be applied more flexibly if that B permit was unconditional; but the
financial and operational requirements still remain and the person’s training and professional
experience remain relevant.
The operational dimension continues after grant. SEM instructs that initial permits for self-employment
or business formation should, in the start-up phase, be limited to a maximum of two years, and that
extension should depend on the timely realisation of the promised sustainable positive effects. SEM
expressly states that renewal may be refused if the goals set out in the business plan have not been
achieved. This makes clear that the business plan is not a marketing document but a benchmark
against which operational delivery may later be tested.
EU/EFTA nationals sit in a different legal framework. They do not generally pass through the Article 19
AIG “economic interest” route when relying on free movement rights. Under current SEM materials on
the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, an EU/EFTA national seeking to establish as a self-
employed person receives an initial five-year permit if they can already prove self-employment when
applying. But the federal guidance still requires actual, effective and subsistence-generating activity in
Switzerland: if there are serious doubts about the real and sustainable exercise of the activity, or about
the generation of regular income sufficient for subsistence, the canton may demand further proof and
may revoke the permit if conditions are no longer met. The directives say that creating a company or
establishment with effective and income-securing business activity is enough, and that this should be
evidenced through business records such as accounts and orders. So even in the EU/EFTA context,
operational reality matters; the difference is that the governing legal test is proof of real self-
employment, not the third-country labour-market and economic-interest admission filter.
The upshot for drafting is strict. A business may be commercially attractive, innovative and even
incorporated in Switzerland, yet still fail under immigration law if the file does not show sustainable
Swiss benefit, genuine entrepreneurial structure, realistic implementation, adequate means,
appropriate qualifications, and an operational footprint supported by documents. Automated content
should therefore avoid suggesting that “a profitable Swiss company” or “a strong business idea” is
enough. Official Swiss materials point to a narrower, evidence-heavy, discretionary and canton-sensitive
work-authorisation analysis.

Structural overview
Legal basis
For third-country nationals, the core federal framework is Article 3 AIG on admission in the interests of
the economy, Article 19 AIG on self-employed activity, Article 20 AIG on quotas, Article 23 AIG on
personal qualifications, Article 24 AIG on adequate housing, and Articles 38(3) and 39(3) AIG on
switching into self-employment from an existing B or G permit. The implementing layer includes Article
2 VZAE/OASA on the definition of self-employment, Article 83 VZAE/OASA on the cantonal labour-market
preliminary decision, and Articles 85–86 VZAE/OASA on SEM approval. Current SEM guidance further
states that cantonal preliminary decisions on third-country self-employment cases are subject to SEM
approval under Article 1 ZV-EJPD. For EU/EFTA nationals, the relevant basis is the Free Movement
Agreement, especially Annex I Article 12, as applied through the VFP/OLCP guidance.
Economic-interest logic
Official Swiss materials frame the issue not as abstract entrepreneurship, but as a Swiss economic and
labour-market benefit test. SEM’s current directives require proof that the planned self-employed activity
has sustainably positive effects on the Swiss labour market, and identify regional diversification,
preservation or creation of local jobs, substantial investment and new mandates for the Swiss economy
as indicators. Cantonal materials echo this logic: Zurich speaks of strong demand without oversupply
and of jobs or investment; Geneva requires an economic interest for the Swiss labour market and
repeats the SEM examples; Vaud states the case must serve the country’s economic interests.
Business substance
Swiss authorities distinguish a genuine operating business from a paper vehicle by looking at structure
and risk, not merely form. Article 2 VZAE/OASA defines self-employment through one’s own
organisation, own instructions and entrepreneurial risk. SEM adds that founders must not be
subordinate to a superior organ and must bear the entrepreneurial risk; a CEO title, an employment
contract or a commercial-register entry on its own does not settle the question. SECO’s public guidance
on the employed/self-employed distinction reinforces the same practical markers: investments, own
premises, own name and account, freedom from instructions and loss risk point towards genuine self-
employment; subordination, lack of investment and periodic remuneration point the other way.
Commercial viability
Commercial viability is relevant because the law and guidance require the business to be financially and
operationally capable of sustaining itself and the founder. Article 19 AIG requires the necessary financial
and operational conditions and an independent basis of subsistence. SEM says the business plan must
cover financing, market opportunity, staffing, recruitment, investments, turnover and profits, while
Zurich states that the planned business must likely generate enough income to cover operating costs
and living costs and that sufficient start-up capital is needed. Geneva adds that the founder must show
a sufficient and autonomous source of income and detailed three-year projections.
Founder credibility
Official materials treat the founder’s background as part of the immigration-law analysis, not as an
optional extra. Article 23 AIG ties admission to executives, specialists and other qualified workers, with
an exception for investors and entrepreneurs who preserve or create jobs. SEM’s directions for changes

Operational evidence
The official materials point repeatedly to operational, document-backed evidence: business plan,
financing plan, market analysis, projected staffing, investor commitments, incorporation documents
and commercial-register evidence at federal level; plan balance sheet, debt commitments and office-
rental contracts in Zurich; detailed project dossier, proof of autonomous revenue and regulatory
authorisations where relevant in Geneva; and business plan plus budget in Vaud. For EU/EFTA self-
employment, SEM says effective and subsistence-securing activity should be shown through business
records such as accounts and orders.
Canton-sensitive assessment
The federal law is national, but the assessment is operationally canton-sensitive. Article 83 VZAE/OASA
places the labour-market preliminary decision at cantonal level, and Article 85 VZAE/OASA then routes
specified cases to SEM approval. Vaud says DGEM authorises the independent activity and, if
favourable, submits the case to SEM; Geneva says OCPM issues only if OCIRT first accepts the file, with
some cases going to the tripartite commission for the economy, and recommends early contact with
the cantonal economy and innovation office; Zurich and Basel-Stadt publish their own evidential
emphases and forms.
Refusal risks
The main risks emerging from the sources are lack of provable Swiss economic benefit, lack of genuine
entrepreneurial autonomy, insufficient funding or revenue basis, weak founder qualifications, generic
or thin business plans, and failure to deliver promised outcomes after admission. SEM expressly says
extension can be refused if the business-plan goals are not met. EU/EFTA files also face risk if the activity
is not real and sustained or does not produce regular subsistence income.
Drafting implications
Future content should present the route as a discretionary work-authorisation assessment, not as a soft
business-migration category. The language should remain tied to economic interest, sustainable
labour-market benefit, financial and operational readiness, founder qualifications, and evidence of
genuine activity. It should also distinguish carefully between third-country and EU/EFTA frameworks,
because the legal tests are materially different.

Official guidance and cantonal practice notes
Official federal guidance
The most important current federal administrative source is SEM, AIG Directives, Chapter 4. For third-
country self-employment and company formation, this guidance does four things of real practical
importance. First, it defines the immigration-law meaning of self-employment and refuses to treat
company form, title or register entry as decisive. Secondly, it interprets “economic interest” as a
requirement to prove sustainably positive effects on the Swiss labour market, giving examples such as
diversification, local jobs, substantial investment and new Swiss mandates, while also recognising
innovation and research transfer for start-ups. Thirdly, it turns the business plan into a structured
evidential document by specifying the matters it must address. Fourthly, it instructs that initial
permissions in the start-up phase should be capped at two years and that renewal depends on actual
delivery against the projected benefits.
The SEM page on non-EU/EFTA nationals provides the public-facing federal summary of the general
system. It reiterates that non-EU/EFTA applicants must be well qualified, that sustainable integration is
relevant for longer stays, and that the future employer must prove the absence of suitable domestic or
EU/EFTA candidates in employed-work cases; it also lists the AIG, VZAE and ZV-EJPD as the governing
legal bases. Although this page is not founder-specific, it is useful as an official statement that third-
country labour-market access is restrictive rather than presumptive.
For EU/EFTA self-employment, the key source is the SEM VFP/OLCP guidance together with the SEM
free-movement FAQ. These materials show that the legal question is proof of real self-employment, not
the third-country economic-interest test. The directives require effective and subsistence-securing
business activity in Switzerland, evidenced by business records such as accounts and orders, and allow
the cantons to seek further proof or withdraw the permit if the activity is not actually and sustainably

“operational reality” and “nominal company” arguments, provided it is clearly presented as general

Practical legal analysis
A Swiss business can exist without justifying residence. The clearest reason is structural: company law
form and immigration-law sufficiency are separate questions. Article 2 VZAE/OASA and the SEM AIG
directives focus on own organisation, entrepreneurial risk and absence of subordination. SEM expressly
says that a CEO title, employment contract or commercial-register entry alone does not decide the
matter. A founder who has incorporated a Swiss entity but cannot show genuine commercial autonomy,
risk-bearing, Swiss operations and admissible economic benefit may still fail. That is why “the company
exists” is not a complete immigration argument.
Economic interest must be evidenced, not asserted, because the official materials make documentary
proof integral to the legal test. SEM requires a business plan addressing activities, financing, market
chances, staffing, recruitment, investment, turnover and profits, plus links to other companies and

investor commitments. Zurich and Geneva go further in published practice by identifying forecast
balance sheets, debt and rental commitments, proof of autonomous income, and detailed multi-year
financials. Vaud requires the same basic package. In practice, this means that a generic statement such
as “the business will benefit Switzerland” carries little legal weight unless tied to sources showing
demand, financing, implementation and the Swiss economic effects relied upon.
Cantonal economic priorities may matter, but only in a controlled sense. Federal law supplies the
admissibility framework; the canton applies it first through the labour-market preliminary decision, and
the file may then require SEM approval. Within that structure, cantons may publish different emphases
about what they want to see. Zurich highlights demand/no oversupply, start capital and premises.
Geneva not only channels the file through OCIRT and, in some cases, a tripartite economy commission,
but also recommends prior contact with the cantonal economy and innovation office so that the project
can be assessed against the Geneva economic context. That does not turn cantonal development
preferences into federal law. It does, however, mean that evidence of regional fit and implementation
realism can matter sharply in practice.
Job creation is relevant, but it should not be overstated. The statute expressly singles out investors and
entrepreneurs who preserve or create jobs, and the SEM directives list the preservation or creation of
several local jobs as one indicator of sustainable benefit. But the same directives also recognise
substantial investment, regional diversification, new mandates and, for start-ups, innovation and
transfer from academic research. The safer legal statement is therefore that local employment is a
strong positive factor, sometimes central, but not the only officially accepted route to showing economic
interest. Content that turns job creation into a universal statutory minimum would go beyond the
reviewed sources.
Weak or generic business plans create legal risk for two reasons. First, they fail at the initial evidential
stage because the authorities are looking for concrete proof of market opportunity, financing, staffing
and implementability. Secondly, they create downstream risk because SEM instructs that initial self-
employment permissions should be limited in the start-up phase and that renewal may be refused if the
business-plan goals are not reached on time. That means over-ambitious, vague or unsupported
projections are not harmless sales language; they may later become the benchmark against which
renewal is judged.
Automated blog content must also avoid suggesting that any profitable business can qualify. Swiss law
does not treat private profitability, investor appeal or mere incorporation as sufficient. Third-country
founder cases are quota-bound, qualification-sensitive, canton-led, SEM-supervised and tied to Swiss
economic benefit and operational credibility. Even in the EU/EFTA framework, the official materials still
insist on proof of effective self-employment and regular subsistence income, with scope for later re-
checking. The proper drafting lesson is that immigration-law sufficiency depends on the quality of the
evidence and the public-law criteria, not on entrepreneurial enthusiasm alone.
A final caution concerns regulated activities and operational footprint. Geneva expressly states that
where the activity or profession is regulated, the linked additional authorisations must be obtained; the
free-movement guidance says the same in principle for EU/EFTA cases by reserving the health- and
economic-police rules applicable to Swiss nationals. Zurich’s materials specifically mention business
premises, while SEM and Geneva emphasise business records, orders, investor commitments and
detailed financial projections. The safest content model is therefore to explain that Swiss permits are
helped by concrete operational markers such as premises, records, funding commitments, customer
orders and sectoral authorisations, while also noting that the published official materials do not offer a
closed statutory checklist.

Drafting cautions
Do not say that any viable or profitable business can support Swiss residence. Third-country
founder cases are governed by Article 19 AIG and a labour-market/economic-interest framework,
not by a general profitability test.
Do not imply that investment alone proves economic interest. SEM treats investment as one
indicator among several, alongside diversification, jobs, new mandates and, for start-ups,
innovation.
Do not imply that incorporation, a commercial-register entry, or a CEO title proves business
substance. Official guidance says those factors do not by themselves settle the immigration-law
classification.
Do not describe job creation as universally mandatory unless the specific source and context
support that statement. It is important, but the reviewed materials present it as one strong route
rather than the only route.
Do not present cantonal preferences or advisory bodies as if they were federal law. Geneva’s
OCEI contact recommendation and tripartite-economy pre-opinion are practice features, not
national statutory criteria.
Do not use vague phrases such as “strong business idea” or “innovative concept” without
explaining the supporting evidence. Official materials expect market, financing, staffing and
operational proof.
Do not ignore founder credibility. Qualifications, training and professional experience remain
legally relevant, and cantons routinely require CVs and diplomas.
Do not describe the business plan as a formality. SEM ties extension of initial permissions to
delivery against the promised positive effects and says renewal may be refused if the plan’s
objectives are missed.
Do not collapse EU/EFTA and third-country routes into one explanation. EU/EFTA self-
employment is assessed under the free-movement framework and proof of actual self-
employment, not under the third-country Article 19 AIG filter.
Do not assume that tax yield, brand prestige or “Swiss company” status are independent
headline legal tests unless a source expressly says so. The reviewed official materials foreground
labour-market benefit, diversification, investment, mandates, innovation and self-sufficiency
instead.
