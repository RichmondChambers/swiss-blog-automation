Executive legal summary
Swiss founder and entrepreneur work-authorisation cases for third-country nationals are primarily
governed by the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission,
Period of Stay and Employment, as applied through SEM directives and cantonal labour-market
procedure. The core federal rule for genuine self-employment is Article 19 FNIA: self-employment may
be permitted only where it is expected to have a positive effect on the Swiss labour market, the financial
and operational requirements are met, and the personal requirements in Article 23 FNIA are satisfied.
For employed founder structures, the ordinary employed-work rules remain relevant, including overall
economic interest, employer sponsorship, precedence, salary and local conditions, and qualification
requirements. Swiss practice therefore depends first on correct legal characterisation: is the founder
truly self-employed, or in substance an employee of a Swiss company, even if he or she is a shareholder,
director or CEO on paper?
The main refusal risk is not a single technical defect but failure to persuade the authorities that the case
advances Switzerland’s overall economic interest in a concrete, durable and evidenced way. SEM’s
current directives say the applicant must show that the planned self-employed activity will have a
lasting positive impact on the Swiss labour market. That benefit is described in practical terms:
contribution to regional or sectoral diversification, retention or creation of jobs for local workers,
substantial investment, or generation of new orders for the Swiss economy; for start-ups, innovation
and implementation of academic research may also be relevant. Official SEM web guidance and
cantonal pages make the same point in simpler language: applicants must explain and document why
admission serves the overall economic interest, and Geneva expressly states that third-country
entrepreneurs must demonstrate the economic interest of the application.
That is why founder cases are often refused for evidential weakness rather than because some narrow
rule mechanically bars them. SEM requires a business plan showing intended activities, financing,
market opportunities, staffing development and recruitment, planned investments, turnover and profit,
together with organisational links to other businesses, external financing commitments, and usually
formation documents and a commercial register extract. SEM’s general application page adds that
incomplete applications are not processed, and documents in other languages usually need certified
translation into an official Swiss language or English. Zurich’s official materials add a practical viability
test: the venture should be expected to generate enough income to cover both business costs and
living costs, and sufficient start-up capital should be proven, ordinarily through a business plan or
opening balance sheet and, helpfully, leases or credit agreements.
A second cluster of refusals concerns founder-role ambiguity. SEM’s directives are explicit that titles, an
employment contract, or commercial-register entry alone do not decide whether activity is self-
employed or employed. Authorities look instead at the real structure: own organisation, ownership or

This matters because many founder cases sit in a grey zone. If the file presents the applicant as an
entrepreneur to avoid employed-work requirements, but the evidence shows subordination to
investors, a board or a parent company, the case may fail on the legal classification point before the
business merits are even reached.
Salary and local-conditions issues also require careful drafting. Article 22 FNIA and Article 22 OASA
protect against wage dumping and require local and occupationally customary conditions for admitted
foreign workers. SEM’s directives say the cantons must examine real local conditions, not just minimum
collective-agreement figures, and may use salary statistics. They also note that start-up-style equity
participation may count towards remuneration only if there are sufficient direct financial means for
subsistence and the participation remains proportionate to base pay. In a pure self-employment file, the
better analytical question is not always “salary”, but whether the founder has shown a viable
independent livelihood; however, where the founder will be employed by a Swiss vehicle, or where the
package is largely equity-based, remuneration becomes a live refusal issue.
Appeal analysis must therefore separate three things. First, legal or procedural challenge: wrong legal
test, misclassification of the role, failure to consider relevant evidence, or procedural unfairness.
Secondly, procedural correction: incomplete papers, translation defects, missing contracts, missing
corporate documents, or other curable omissions. Thirdly, substantive evidential rebuilding: weak
market evidence, speculative projections, uncommitted funding, unclear operational presence, or
unsupported economic-interest claims. The Federal Administrative Court reviews federal administrative
decisions under the federal public-law procedure statutes, and official SEM procedure materials show
that a positive cantonal assessment in these cases still goes to SEM for federal approval; but official
materials reviewed for this pack do not describe any entrepreneur-specific, easy reconsideration route.
In weak founder files, a substantially rebuilt re-filing is often more realistic than an appeal that simply
re-argues the same thin record. Content written for future publication should therefore avoid
suggesting that refusals are routinely or easily overturned.
Structural overview and practical legal analysis
B. Structural overview
Legal refusal framework. Third-country entrepreneur admission sits within Switzerland’s labour-
market admission system. For genuine self-employment, the key gateway is Article 19 FNIA; for
employed founder structures, the Article 18 FNIA track remains relevant. SEM’s directives add that self-
employment is defined autonomously in migration law and not simply by social-security or corporate-
law labelling. Company formation or participation, by itself, does not create an entitlement in permit
proceedings.
Economic-interest refusal risks. The authorities ask whether the case serves Switzerland’s overall
economic interest, not merely the applicant’s private interest. SEM links that enquiry to labour-market
conditions, sustainable economic development, and integration prospects; in self-employment cases it
requires a lasting positive effect on the Swiss labour market, such as diversification, job creation or
retention, investment, or orders for the Swiss economy. Thin statements about “innovation”,
“international expansion” or “future hiring” are risky unless backed by concrete Swiss-facing evidence.
Business-substance refusal risks. A speculative business model, generic slide-deck, or business plan
written at a high level without operational proof is vulnerable. SEM expects evidence about activities,

market opportunities, staffing, investments, revenue and profit forecasts, financing, corporate links,
and formation documents. Zurich adds that enough income should be expected to cover both business
and living costs. A file with no Swiss clients, no Swiss premises, no contracts, no licensing position, and
no explanation of why Switzerland is the real operating centre is structurally weak.
Financial and funding risks. The federal test expressly includes financial and operational
requirements. Zürich’s practice note requires sufficient start-up capital and supports this with business
plan, opening balance, lease or credit documentation. SEM’s directives call for binding investor
commitments where external finance is relied on. Assertions that funds are “available” or “to be raised”
without documentary commitment are therefore a major refusal risk.
Founder-role risks. Many cases fail because the applicant’s role is not properly characterised or
justified. SEM says titles, contracts and register entries are not enough; the real indicators are control,
absence of superiors, and entrepreneurial risk. A founder who appears dependent on a board, parent
or investor may be treated as employed, and then the Article 18 framework, employer application,
precedence and salary rules become relevant. Even where Article 19 is correctly invoked, the founder’s
necessity must still be evidenced: why this person, for this Swiss venture, at this stage?
Salary and employment-conditions risks. Salary/local-conditions objections arise most sharply where
the founder is really being employed by a Swiss entity, but they can also surface where remuneration is
unrealistic or largely equity-based. SEM requires local and occupationally customary conditions for
workers and allows equity to count only in a limited way where direct subsistence funds remain
sufficient. Drafting should therefore avoid implying that founders may simply take nominal pay because
they are “entrepreneurs”.
Procedural risks. SEM and cantonal authorities process only complete applications. Missing
translations, unsigned or incomplete contracts, absent formation records, unsubstantiated funding, or
internal contradictions across the business plan and corporate documents can sink the file before
merits are fully reached. OASA also states that company formation or participation alone creates no
claim in the permit process.
Appeal and re-filing. Procedure is two-stage: the canton screens labour-market conditions and, if
positive, forwards the case to SEM for federal approval. Official federal material confirms that SEM then
issues the formal decision. Federal public-law procedure is governed by VwVG, VGG and BGG; cantonal
procedure is governed separately at canton level. Official materials reviewed for this pack do not
describe a bespoke entrepreneur reconsideration route. That matters: many weak founder refusals are
better analysed as candidates for substantial evidential rebuilding and re-filing, not merely legal re-
argument.
Drafting implications. Future content should describe founder cases as evidence-heavy, highly
discretionary and quota-sensitive. It should not suggest that incorporation, capitalisation alone, or
“being a founder” creates a strong approval claim. Swiss authorities look for durable economic utility,
real operating readiness and a coherent legal characterisation of the role.
H. Practical legal analysis
Swiss entrepreneur cases commonly fail on cumulative evidential weakness because Article 19 FNIA is
framed as a compact statutory test while the real proof burden is spread across many practical
questions: market demand, Swiss benefit, funding, structure, founder control, professional
qualifications, admissible remuneration model, and operational readiness. The file must show not only

that the business could exist, but that this foreign founder’s admission should be preferred within a
controlled labour-market system designed for the interests of Switzerland as a whole. That is why
ostensibly small defects often compound into a “weak file” outcome. A vague plan, uncommitted
funding, no Swiss contracts, and overstated job-creation forecasts may each be survivable in isolation;
together they usually undermine the economic-interest narrative and the credibility of the venture.
Appeal is not always the best answer to that kind of refusal. A legal challenge makes sense where the
authority applied the wrong legal framework, misunderstood whether the founder was self-employed
or employed, ignored key evidence, or committed a procedural defect. But where the authority has
simply found the business case too thin, the better remedy is often a rebuilt record: committed finance,
clearer Swiss-market evidence, real premises, licensing position, letters of intent or contracts, a
corrected remuneration model, and a defensible explanation of why the founder must personally be in
Switzerland now. Swiss federal procedure gives review rights, but review does not turn a speculative file
into a proved one.
Re-filing also usually requires genuine change, not repetition. OASA expressly says that no claim can be
derived from company formation or share participation alone. SEM’s directives add that initial
entrepreneur permits should normally be limited to two years and that extension may be refused if the
promised targets in the business plan are not reached on time. Those texts point in the same direction:
Swiss authorities expect concrete performance against evidence, not circular reliance on the fact that a
company exists. A re-filed application that merely repackages the same plan, without stronger proof or
materially changed facts, remains exposed.
Funding claims and remuneration claims deserve particular caution in automated content. Founder
narratives often present low salary, future equity upside and investor interest as strengths. Swiss
guidance cuts through that rhetoric. Where employee-admission rules apply, local and sectoral
conditions must be respected, and equity only helps to a limited extent if direct means of subsistence
are present. Where the case is genuinely self-employed, Zürich still expects the activity to generate
enough income for operating and living costs and requires enough start-up capital. A blog engine that
says “you can just pay yourself little at the start because you are the founder” would therefore be
materially unsafe.
For the same reason, automated content should not promise that a refusal can readily be overturned.
Official materials reviewed here do not provide a founder-specific shortcut to reconsideration, and the
procedure remains multi-layered: canton, SEM approval, and then the applicable federal or cantonal
remedy route. Strong content should instead explain that some refusals are appeal-worthy because
something went legally wrong, while others are signals that the evidence, structure or timing of the
case was not yet good enough.

E. Official guidance and administrative materials
The main current federal administrative material is SEM’s Directives and Explanations, Chapter 4:
Residence with Gainful Employment (status 1 January 2026). The directives contain the most operational
guidance on entrepreneur cases. For self-employment and business start-ups, they set out: the
migration-law concept of self-employment; the need to prove a lasting positive effect on the Swiss
labour market; examples of qualifying benefit; documentary expectations for the business plan and
financing; and the normal practice of an initial permit limited to two years, extendable only if the
promised durable positive effects are realised on time. They also state that cantonal pre-decisions on
Article 19 FNIA cases are subject to SEM approval.
SEM’s public FAQ in English is especially useful because it states, in accessible terms, that third-country
nationals may be self-employed only after labour-market review under Article 19 FNIA and that they
must show a lasting positive impact on the Swiss labour market. It then lists the expected supporting
documents: business plan, market analysis, workforce development and recruitment plan, investments,
turnover and profit forecast, organisational links, articles of association and commercial-register
extract.
SEM’s general page on the Basis for admission to the Swiss employment market is useful for the wider
refusal architecture. It summarises overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, vacancy-notification,
salary/local conditions and personal requirements. It expressly says applicants must explain and
document why admission is in the overall economic interest, and notes that only limited numbers of
highly qualified third-country workers are admitted.

SEM’s Procedure and Application documents pages matter for procedural refusals. They explain the
canton-to-SEM approval sequence; confirm that the canton makes a preliminary decision and sends

applications submitted in full. The application-documents page also shows the general documentary

the application, and sector-specific additions.
On salary and conditions, SEM’s directives are the critical administrative source. They require cantons to
compare local and occupational conditions against legal rules, actual pay in the same workplace and
sector, and salary statistics; they also note that statistical averages usually operate as a floor in urban
contexts. The directives’ discussion of equity-style participation in start-ups is particularly relevant to
founder drafting.
For procedural and appeal framing, the Federal Office of Justice explains that federal public-law
procedure is governed by the Administrative Procedure Act, the Administrative Court Act and the
Federal Supreme Court Act, and that these laws regulate the making of administrative decisions and
parties’ procedural rights such as the right to be heard. This is the cleanest official federal source for
distinguishing federal appeal structure from cantonal procedural law.
Official materials reviewed for this pack are comparatively thin on entrepreneur-specific refusal letters,
reconsideration practice and re-filing guidance. The SEM legal-protection section exists within the
directives architecture, but the reviewed public materials did not expose a dedicated entrepreneur
reconsideration pathway. That absence should itself shape drafting: future content should not imply a
standard, easy post-refusal reset route unless the actual refusal notice or canton-specific procedure
says so.
Cantonal practice notes and case law notes
F. Cantonal practice notes
Canton Zurich. Zurich’s public page for third-country nationals defines self-employment in terms
tracking OASA and then lists cumulative conditions for third-country self-employment: the activity must
serve Switzerland’s overall economic interest; it should be expected to generate enough income to
cover operating costs and living costs; sufficient start-up capital must be shown, ordinarily by a business
plan or opening balance sheet; and only specialists and otherwise qualified workers can be authorised.
Zurich’s 2025 one-page procedure sheet for self-employed persons adds quota dependence and
distinguishes between residence-taking entrepreneurs and cross-border self-employed service
providers. Zurich’s broader explanatory note for third-country work permit cases also states expressly
that there is no legal entitlement to a permit or to extension, that first permits are quota-bound, and
that authorities may request additional documents. These are best treated as cantonal practice points
reflecting the national framework rather than free-standing canton-only rules. Appeal routes were not
described on the reviewed public pages. The points addressed are mainly evidential and discretionary,
with some procedural overlay.
Canton Geneva. Geneva’s official entrepreneur page states that any foreign national wishing to live in
Geneva and work there independently requires a residence authorisation with activity. For third-country
nationals, Geneva says there is no facilitated access to the Swiss labour market, that the applicant must
in particular demonstrate the economic interest of the application, that only managers, specialists and
qualified workers may be admitted, and that admission remains subject to availability within the
quotas. Geneva also explains the institutional route: the OCPM handles the filing, while the OCIRT

G. Case law notes
The case law below is included conservatively. In several instances the most accessible official or
officially cited material was the SEM directives’ citation to the judgment rather than the full public text of
the judgment itself. Where facts are thin in the reviewed sources, that is stated expressly.
Federal Administrative Court, 11 April 2013, C-2485/2011. The search extract identifies the appellant
as a Kazakh national who was founder and owner of a distribution company in the medical and
healthcare sector and was challenging SEM’s refusal to approve a cantonal labour-market pre-decision.
SEM’s current directives cite this judgment for the proposition that entrepreneur permits should initially
be time-limited and that extension may be refused if the business-plan targets and promised durable
positive effects are not realised in time. Drafting relevance: this is authority against any suggestion that
initial founder approval settles the case for the longer term, and it supports a hard distinction between
a start-up phase and a proved operating phase.
Federal Administrative Court, 9 May 2011, C-7286/2008. The publicly accessible search extract states
that SEM refused approval because the requirements for self-employed activity under Article 19 AuG/
FNIA were not met and contains a snippet showing the authority’s scepticism towards deliberately
conservative minimum projections designed to avoid allegations of excessive optimism. SEM’s directives
cite this judgment both for self-employment classification and for economic-interest analysis. Drafting
relevance: entrepreneur projections must be credible, not just numerically plausible; underpowered
forecasts can be as damaging as exaggerated ones if they fail to evidence meaningful Swiss benefit.
Federal Administrative Court, 11 August 2011, C-6135/2008. The case title and SEM directives show it
is one of the authorities relied on for overall economic interest and for refusal/extension analysis where
promised business effects are not realised. The facts were not fully retrievable from the reviewed public
extracts, so this pack uses it narrowly. Drafting relevance: use it as support for the proposition that
Swiss entrepreneur admission is not meant to support merely private or particular interests detached
from concrete Swiss labour-market benefit.

Drafting cautions, source list and limitations
I. Drafting cautions
Do not say that a refusal can always be appealed successfully. Swiss entrepreneur refusals often turn on
evidential weakness under a discretionary, quota-bound labour-market framework rather than on an
obvious legal error.
Do not imply that re-filing the same documents is enough. Official materials show there is no permit
claim merely from founding or participating in a company, and SEM ties later success to whether the
promised benefits are actually evidenced and achieved.
Do not understate economic interest. It is the centre of the statutory and administrative analysis, not a
cosmetic criterion.
Do not present founders as automatically “self-employed”. Swiss migration law applies its own
definition, and titles or register entries alone are insufficient.
Do not omit funding, operational readiness and role-necessity issues. Business plans, financing,
investor commitments, commercial-register materials and evidence of real Swiss operations are
expected.
Do not frame salary/local-conditions issues as irrelevant merely because the applicant is a founder. They
remain central where the case is employed in substance, and SEM expressly regulates the limited use of
start-up equity in remuneration analysis.
Do not treat quota or timing problems as purely administrative. Quotas are a substantive part of the
admission system and remain in force for 2026.
Do not present refusal grounds as exhaustive unless sourced. The reviewed official materials are rich on
criteria, but thinner on entrepreneur-specific appeal, reconsideration and re-filing routes. State that
clearly.

Open questions and limitations
Official federal and cantonal materials reviewed for this pack are strong on admission criteria and initial
procedure, but noticeably thinner on entrepreneur-specific reconsideration practice, re-filing standards
and canton-by-canton appeal routes after refusal. Those matters should therefore be verified against
the actual refusal notice, the issuing authority, and the applicable cantonal procedural law in the live
case. Case-law accessibility was also uneven: several important judgments were most readily available
through current SEM directive citations or search extracts rather than fully parsed official judgment text,
