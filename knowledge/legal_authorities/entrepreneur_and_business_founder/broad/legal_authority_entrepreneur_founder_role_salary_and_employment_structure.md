

Swiss founder role, salary and employment
structure authority pack
Executive legal summary
Swiss immigration law does not create a standalone work-authorisation category for a “founder”,
“shareholder” or “director”. The controlling question is what the person will actually do in or for
Switzerland, and under what legal and economic structure. The SEM’s guidance applies the concept of
gainful activity broadly: any employed or self-employed activity that normally produces gain counts as
gainful activity even if it is unpaid, and the assessment is objective rather than label-driven. The same
guidance also says that, for founders and associates, a title such as president or CEO, the existence of
an employment contract, or entry in the commercial register even with sole signatory power are not
sufficient, by themselves, to establish whether the activity is salaried or independent. Swiss official
materials are therefore clear that founder cases must be analysed by function, control, risk and the
actual work to be carried out in Switzerland, not by company-law labels alone.
Ownership by itself does not confer a right to work in Switzerland. Official SEM guidance distinguishes
passive situations from active work: the ordinary administration of one’s own assets may fall outside
gainful activity where it has no marked commercial or speculative character, and short business
meetings are treated as non-gainful in principle. By contrast, operational and management work in
Switzerland is generally treated as gainful activity even if the person calls themselves a founder, investor
or board member. The SEM’s “notion of gainful activity” annex gives examples: short CEO
representation, negotiations and certain strategic meetings can fall outside gainful activity, but regular
on-site management tasks, project implementation, integrated training, and repeated personnel
interviews and target-setting in Switzerland count as gainful activity. For founder cases, this means
passive shareholding is not the same as active entrepreneurial work, and directorship is not a safe
shorthand either way.
For third-country nationals, the structure matters immediately. If the founder will be employed by a
Swiss company, the case is assessed under the salaried-employment framework: the employer must
apply; the case must satisfy economic-interest, priority and vacancy-notification requirements where
applicable; local salary and working conditions must be met; personal qualifications must be shown;
and the authorities require a written, binding employment contract. The SEM also states that salary
review is real, including in founder and start-up contexts: authorities look first to legal norms,
comparable conditions in the same enterprise and branch, and current statistical materials; employee
participation schemes may be counted only if the person also has sufficient direct financial means and
the equity element remains proportionate to the base salary. The same guidance requires the labour-
market authority to ensure that employer and employee social-security contributions are paid and that
the person has adequate health and accident cover.
If the founder is genuinely self-employed, the case moves into the self-employment regime. The official
SEM directives identify typical indicators of independent founder activity: the work is carried out within
the person’s own freely chosen organisation, the founder is not subject to superior instructions, the
founder has significant power to instruct others, and the founder bears the entrepreneurial and
financial risk. Third-country self-employment also requires more than mere incorporation. The applicant
must show personal qualifications, financial and operational viability, an autonomous and sufficient

EU/EFTA nationals stand on a different legal footing because the Agreement on the Free Movement of
Persons gives them significantly easier access to the Swiss labour market. For employed activity above
three months, a contract or declaration of engagement from a Swiss employer is generally sufficient for
the permit framework described in the SEM factsheet; for self-employment, an EU/EFTA national may
obtain a B permit if they prove that they are actually exercising self-employed gainful activity in
Switzerland. That easier access, however, still does not make the founder label decisive: even under the
free-movement framework, the right depends on effective employed or self-employed activity, not on
symbolic founder status alone.
Swiss official sources are thinner on the specific phrase “self-sponsorship”. I did not locate any express
federal rule saying that a founder may automatically be sponsored by their own Swiss company, nor
any express federal rule flatly banning it. The safer reading of the official materials is more nuanced:
where the founder works through a Swiss AG or GmbH, Swiss social-insurance guidance normally treats
that person as employed for social-security purposes, while sole proprietorships and partnerships are
treated as self-employed; but compensation-fund classification still depends on the concrete case, and
immigration classification depends on the real structure of control, subordination and risk. In practice,
therefore, own-company sponsorship is possible only if the case is structured and evidenced
convincingly as a genuine salaried employment relationship; if the founder has no superior, exercises
dominant control and bears the business risk, the case may instead be treated as self-employment.
Official guidance is therefore strong on the underlying rules but thin on any standalone founder “self-
sponsorship” doctrine.
Structural overview
Ownership and shareholder status
Swiss official materials do not treat shareholding as a separate admission route. Passive ownership, or
the ordinary administration of one’s own assets without a marked commercial or speculative character,
may fall outside gainful activity; but active entrepreneurial work in or for a Swiss business is analysed
under the ordinary gainful-activity rules. Shareholding is therefore legally relevant mainly as evidence of
entrepreneurial risk and control, especially in self-employment analysis, not as an independent work
right.
Director and board roles
No dedicated federal founder/director work-authorisation track was located. Instead, the SEM guidance
says that function titles such as president or CEO, and commercial-register entry even with sole
signatory power, are not sufficient to classify the activity as employed or independent. The legal analysis
therefore turns on the substance of the role: a short board meeting or negotiation visit may be non-
gainful, but ongoing management or operational performance in Switzerland is work.

Employment by the Swiss company
Where the founder is to be employed by a Swiss company, third-country cases are analysed under the
salaried-employment framework. The employer must file the application, and the case must satisfy
economic-interest, priority and vacancy-notification rules where relevant, local salary and work
conditions, and personal-qualification requirements. Zurich’s official guidance states the same
essentials expressly for third-country local employment.
## Self-employment
Where the founder truly operates within their own organisation, is subject to no superior instructions,
and bears the entrepreneurial risk, the case belongs under the self-employment framework. The SEM
directives require evidence of qualifications, financial and operational conditions, a sufficient
autonomous source of income and a durable positive effect on the Swiss labour market. The business
plan and operational evidence are central, not incidental.
Salary and local conditions
If the founder is employed by the Swiss company, salary and local conditions remain a live issue. The
SEM explains that authorities look first to legal norms and comparable conditions in the same
undertaking and branch, then to statistical materials; it also states that equity or employee-participation
elements can count only where the applicant still has adequate direct means of support and where the
equity element is proportionate to the cash salary. The FSO’s Salarium tool and SECO’s national wage
calculator are official reference tools, although both stress that they are not wage recommendations.
Role necessity
Swiss official sources do not publish a discrete “founder necessity test”, but the general rules effectively
require one. In employee cases, the employer must justify economic interest, show why priority
candidates were not available, and demonstrate that the applicant’s qualifications match the role. In
self-employment cases, the business plan must explain what the founder will do, how the enterprise will
operate, and how it will benefit the Swiss labour market. Necessity is therefore assessed through the
actual role and its economic function, not through founder status as such.
Physical presence in Switzerland
Physical presence must be justified by the Swiss tasks to be performed. The SEM’s annex treats short
business meetings, negotiations and certain preparation visits as non-gainful, but treats repeated
management activity, integrated training and project work on Swiss soil as gainful activity. A founder
application should therefore explain why the person must be in Switzerland to perform genuine
entrepreneurial or managerial work there, rather than relying on abstract strategic oversight.
Self-sponsorship risks
Official sources are thin on the phrase “self-sponsorship”, but they point to the underlying risk factors. If
the founder works through a Swiss AG or GmbH, Swiss social-insurance guidance generally treats the
founder as employed; but the SEM immigration guidance says that lack of superior reporting, dominant
control, and business-risk assumption point toward independent activity. Own-company sponsorship is
therefore not equivalent to ordinary third-party sponsorship, and cases with concentrated founder
control are especially classification-sensitive.

Drafting implications
Founder content should separate ownership, office-holding, employment and self-employment. It
should describe the actual Swiss activities, reporting lines, control structure, salary mechanism, payroll
treatment, insurance position, and business need for on-site presence. Where the structure is
borderline, the content should say so plainly and avoid implying that company-law status alone
determines immigration status.

Primary legal texts
Federal legislation
Article 11 FNIA — Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, SR 142.20. Close paraphrase: a
foreign national who wants to exercise gainful activity in Switzerland needs a permit, and gainful activity
includes any employed or self-employed activity that normally yields gain, even where it is unpaid. This
matters because it prevents founders from arguing that unpaid management, shareholder functions or
director functions automatically fall outside work-authorisation law.
Article 18 FNIA — same Act. Close paraphrase: third-country nationals may be admitted for salaried
employment only where the admission is in Switzerland’s economic interests, an employer application
has been filed, and the further requirements in the Act are met. This is the starting point for founders
employed by their Swiss company. It shows that employee-route founder cases are still ordinary labour-
market admissions cases, not a special founder exception.
Article 19 FNIA — same Act. Close paraphrase: third-country nationals may be admitted for self-
employment if the activity serves Switzerland’s economic interests, the financial and operational
conditions are met, and the further statutory requirements apply. The SEM’s guidance adds that the
activity must produce sufficient autonomous income and durable positive effects on the Swiss labour
market. This matters because company creation alone is not enough; the business must be viable and
beneficial in Swiss labour-market terms.
Article 21 FNIA and Article 21a FNIA — same Act. Close paraphrase: in third-country salaried cases,
priority goes to Swiss and other priority-category workers, and vacancy-notification duties apply in the
relevant occupations. This matters because a founder presented as an employee is still vulnerable to

Ordinances and free-movement instruments
Articles 1 to 3 OASA / VZAE — Ordonnance relative à l’admission, au séjour et à l’exercice d’une
activité lucrative / Verordnung über Zulassung, Aufenthalt und Erwerbstätigkeit, SR 142.201.
Close paraphrase: the ordinance gives a deliberately broad concept of gainful activity and defines self-
employment as activity carried out within one’s own freely chosen organisation, aimed at generating
income, under one’s own authority, and with entrepreneurial risk borne by the person concerned. This
matters because it anchors the employee/self-employed distinction in control and risk rather than in
titles.
Article 22 paragraph 2 OASA / VZAE and Article 22a OASA / VZAE — same ordinance. Close
paraphrase: where work is to be performed in employment, the authority requires a written and
binding employment contract before approval; in posting contexts the ordinance also regulates the
employer’s obligation to reimburse assignment expenses. This matters because founder-employed
cases need a real contract and payroll structure, not only a board resolution or incorporation document.
Article 1 letter a number 1 OA-DFJP — FDJP ordinance on approval. Close paraphrase: cantonal pre-
decisions admitting third-country nationals for self-employment under Article 19 FNIA are subject to
federal approval. This matters because entrepreneur cases are not purely cantonal, and the SEM
remains a substantive checkpoint.
Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and corresponding SEM EU/EFTA guidance. Close
paraphrase: EU/EFTA nationals have a right of entry, stay and labour-market access under the free-
movement framework; employed persons generally rely on a Swiss contract or declaration of
engagement, while self-employed persons must prove that they actually exercise self-employed activity
in Switzerland. This matters because EU/EFTA founders are not assessed under the same restrictive
third-country admission logic, but still must prove actual economic activity.

The SEM’s annex on conditions of remuneration and work is narrower because it is framed around

The SEM’s public page on labour-market admission basics is a concise official summary of the dual
system, the economic-interest test, annual quotas, priority rules, vacancy-notification duties, local salary

founder employee cases remain ordinary work-admission cases unless they are properly structured as

For EU/EFTA distinctions, the SEM factsheets are the clearest official short-form materials. They confirm
that employed EU/EFTA nationals above the short-term threshold rely on a Swiss employment
attestation or contract, and that EU/EFTA self-employed persons obtain a B permit when they prove
effective self-employed activity in Switzerland. These factsheets are important because they show that
the easier EU/EFTA route is activity-based too.
For payroll and social-security structure, the most relevant official administrative materials are from the
BSV and the AHV/AVS information system. The BSV’s AHV FAQ says that self-employment exists where
the person works in their own name, on their own account, independently and at their own economic
risk; contractual wording is not decisive; and the compensation fund decides status case by case. The
BSV’s SME page adds an important legal-form rule of thumb: owners of an AG or GmbH are treated as
employees, while sole proprietorships and partnerships are treated as self-employed. Together, those
materials are highly useful for founder payroll design, although they are social-insurance guidance
rather than immigration law.
For salary benchmarking, the FSO’s Salarium and SECO’s national wage calculator are official
reference tools. Both disclaim that they are not wage recommendations, and the SEM directives treat
them as aids among several salary references rather than legally binding rates. That is the correct
drafting stance for founder cases as well: they are evidential tools, not safe-harbour numbers.

comprehensive explanatory note, it should be treated as a practice-sensitive evidential indicator rather

Practical legal analysis
The first practical point is that Swiss authorities are concerned with the legal character of the activity,
not the entrepreneur’s preferred label. That is why official guidance repeatedly turns back to control,
instructions, risk and actual tasks. A founder may be an employee for immigration purposes, self-
employed for immigration purposes, an employee for social insurance, or in a mixed or disputed
posture depending on the exact setup. This is also why directorship and register status are treated
cautiously: the SEM expressly says those formal markers are not enough. Where official sources are
thin, the safest reading is not to create new founder categories by implication.
The second point is that the actual work to be performed in Switzerland matters more than ownership
or board membership. The SEM’s gainful-activity materials show a spectrum. At one end are short
meetings, negotiations and other brief business contacts that can remain outside gainful activity. At the
other are repeated management and operational tasks on Swiss soil, which are treated as work. This
means a founder role description must say what the person will actually do in Switzerland: lead
recruitment, supervise staff, raise and deploy capital locally, manage Swiss client relationships, oversee
product roll-out, run operations, or similar. If the description only reaches occasional board presence,
investment monitoring and strategic conversations, the case may not line up well with a work-permit
narrative at all. That is an inference from the official examples, but it is a strong one.
The third point is that salary and local-conditions review does not disappear merely because the
applicant founded the company. If the structure chosen is employment by the Swiss company, Article 22
FNIA remains engaged. The SEM says the purpose is both to protect the foreign worker from abusive
terms and to prevent cheaper foreign labour being used against resident workers. In founder cases,

The fourth point is that physical presence in Switzerland must be justified by the role. Official sources do
not publish a dedicated federal “need to be physically present” test for founders, and that absence
should be stated openly. But the existing materials imply the question strongly. If some kinds of short
business travel are not gainful activity, then a founder seeking a Swiss work authorisation should
explain why the planned Swiss presence will involve actual gainful activity and why those tasks need to
be performed from Switzerland. In a founder or entrepreneur case, that usually means tying the
presence to on-the-ground company creation, management, hiring, product or service delivery, client
development or regulated operational functions, supported by the business plan and staffing plan.
Simply saying “the founder wishes to oversee the company” is too abstract.
The fifth point is that self-sponsorship is not the same thing as third-party sponsorship. A normal
employee case assumes a company distinct from, and genuinely directing, the worker. A founder-
controlled AG or GmbH may be a separate legal person and may also treat the founder as an employee
for social-insurance purposes, but the SEM’s immigration classification still asks whether the founder is
really subordinate to the company, or whether the founder is in substance acting independently and
bearing the entrepreneurial risk. That is why own-company cases can be viable but fragile. They must
be designed around real governance, genuine remuneration, payroll compliance and a credible
explanation of who directs whom. Any automated content system should therefore avoid saying either
that self-sponsorship is impossible or that it is routine; the truthful position is that official sources are
indirect and the answer is structure-dependent.
The sixth point is that automated public-facing content must not present directorship alone as a route.
That is unsupported by the official materials and risks serious legal inaccuracy. The better message is
that directorship may be part of the factual matrix, but Swiss work-authorisation law analyses the
underlying activity. A non-executive or occasional board role may, in some circumstances, resemble a
short business function that does not itself require a work permit; an executive or operational board
role in Switzerland may require full compliance with the salaried or self-employed framework. Because
no dedicated federal director/shareholder doctrine was located, content should say exactly that rather
than inventing certainty.
Drafting cautions
Do not say that a shareholder can work in Switzerland merely because they own shares. Official
sources treat passive asset administration and active gainful work differently, and they do not
identify shareholding as a standalone work right.
Do not say that appointment as a director, board member, president or CEO automatically gives
work rights. The SEM says those titles and register entries are not sufficient classification criteria.
Do not treat “founder”, “director”, “shareholder” or “investor” as legally decisive immigration
categories. The analysis turns on actual activity, control, reporting lines and risk.
Do not confuse passive investment or short business meetings with active entrepreneurial work
in Switzerland. Some short business activity can be non-gainful; repeated management and
operational activity is not.
