# Swiss founder role, salary and employment structure authority pack

## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law does not create a standalone work-authorisation category for a "founder", "shareholder" or "director". The controlling question is what the person will actually do in or for Switzerland, and under what legal and economic structure. The SEM's guidance applies the concept of gainful activity broadly: any employed or self-employed activity that normally produces gain counts as gainful activity even if it is unpaid, and the assessment is objective rather than label-driven. The same guidance also says that, for founders and associates, a title such as president or CEO, the existence of an employment contract, or entry in the commercial register even with sole signatory power are not sufficient, by themselves, to establish whether the activity is salaried or independent. Swiss official materials are therefore clear that founder cases must be analysed by function, control, risk and the actual work to be carried out in Switzerland, not by company-law labels alone.1

Ownership by itself does not confer a right to work in Switzerland. Official SEM guidance distinguishes passive situations from active work: the ordinary administration of one's own assets may fall outside gainful activity where it has no marked commercial or speculative character, and short business meetings are treated as non-gainful in principle. By contrast, operational and management work in Switzerland is generally treated as gainful activity even if the person calls themselves a founder, investor or board member. The SEM's "notion of gainful activity" annex gives examples: short CEO representation, negotiations and certain strategic meetings can fall outside gainful activity, but regular on-site management tasks, project implementation, integrated training, and repeated personnel interviews and target-setting in Switzerland count as gainful activity. For founnder cases, this means passive shareholding is not the same as active entrepreneurial work, and directorship is nota safe shorthand either way. 2

For third-country nationals, the structure matters immediately. If the founder will be employed by a Swiss company, the case is assessed under the salaried-employment framework: the employer must apply; the case must satisfy economic-interest, priority and vacancy-notification requirements where applicable; local salary and working conditions must be met; personal qualifications must be shown; and the authorities require a written, binding employment contract. The SEM also states that salary review is real, including in founder and start-up contexts: authorities look first to legal norms, comparable conditions in the same enterprise and branch, and current statistical materials; employee participation schemes may be counted only if the person also has sufficient direct financial means and the equity element remains proportionate to the base salary. The same gguidance requires the labour-market authority to ensure that employer and employee social-security contributions are paid and that the person has adequate health and accident cover. 3

If the founder is genuinely self-employed, the case moves into the self-employment regime. The official SEM directives identify typical indicators of independent founder activity: the work is carried out within the person's own freely chosen organisation, the founder is not subject to superior instructions, the founder has significant power to instruct others, and the founder bears the entrepreneurial and financial risk. Third-country self-employment also requires more than mere incorporation. The applicant must show personal qualifications, financial and operational viability, an autonomous and sufficient

source of income, and durable positive effects for the Swiss labour market. Official guidance expects a plan of operations or business plan covering activities, financing, market analysis, staffing development, recruitment possibilities, planned investments, expected turnover and profit, and organisational links or investor commitments. Initial permits for enterprise creation are normally granted for two years,with renewal depending on whether the promised positive impact is actually delivered. Cantonal pre-decisions in third-country self-employment cases are subject to SEM approval. 4

EU/EFTA nationals stand on a different legal footing because the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons gives them significantly easier access to the Swiss labour market. For employed activity above three months, a contract or declaration of engagement from a Swiss employer is generally sufficient for the permit framework described in the SEM factsheet; for self-employment, an EU/EFTA national may obtain a B permit if they prove that they are actually exercising self-employed gainful activity in Switzerland. That easier access, however, stilldoes not make the founder label decisive: even under the free-movement framework, the right depends on effective employed or self-employed activity,not on symbolic founder status alone. 5

Swiss official sources are thinner on the specific phrase "self-sponsorship". I did not locate any express federal rule saying that a founder may automatically be sponsored by their own Swiss company,nor any express federal rule flatly banning it. The safer reading of the official materials is more nuanced: where the founder works through a Swiss AG or GmbH, Swiss social-insurance guidance normally treats that person as employed for social-security purposes, while sole proprietorships and partnerships are treated as self-employed; but compensation-fund classification stilldepends on the concrete case, and immigration classification depends on the real structure of control, subordination and risk. In practice, therefore, own-company sponsorship is possible only if the case is structured and evidenced convincingly as a genuine salaried employment relationship; if the founder has no superior, exercises dominant control and bears the business risk, the case may instead be treated as self-employment. Official guidance is therefore strong on the underlying rules but thin on any standalone founder "self-sponsorship" doctrine. 6

## Structural overview

## Ownership and shareholder status

Swiss official materials do not treat shareholding as a separate admission route. Passive ownership, or the ordinary administration of one's own assets without a marked commercial or speculative character, may fall outside gainful activity; but active entrepreneurial work in or for a Swiss business is analysed under the ordinary gainful-activity rules. Shareholdling is therefore legally relevant mainly as evidence of entrepreneurial risk and control, especially in self-employment analysis, not as an independent work right. 1

## Director and board roles

No dedicated federal founder/director work-authorisation track was located. Instead, the SEM guidance says that function titles such as president or CEO, and commercial-register entry even with sole signatory power, are not sufficient to classify the activity as employed or independent. The legal analysis therefore turns on the substance of the role: a short board meeting or negotiation visit may be non-gainful, but ongoing management or operational performance in Switzerland is work. 7

## Employment by the Swiss company

Where the founder is to be employed by a Swiss company, third-country cases are analysed under the salaried-employment framework. The employer must file the application, and the case must satisfy economic-interest, priority and vacancy-notification rules where relevant, local salary and work conditions, and personal-qualification requirements. Zurich's official guidance states the same essentials expressly for third-country local emnployment. 8

## Self-employment

Where the founder truly operates within their own organisation, is subject to no superior instructions, and bears the entrepreneurial risk, the case belongs under the self-employment framework. The SEM directives require evidence of qualifications, financial and operational conditions, a sufficient autonomous source of income and a durable positive effect on the Swiss labour market. The business plan and operational evidence are central, not incidental. 9

## Salary and local conditions

If the founder is employed by the Swiss company, salary and local conditions remain a live issue. The SEM explains that authorities look first to legal norms and comparable conditions in the same undertaking and branch, then to statistical materials; it also states that equity or employee-participation elements can count only where the applicant still has adequate direct means of support and where the equity element is proportionate to the cash salary. The FSO's Salarium tool and SECO's national wage calculator are official reference tools, although both stress that they are not wage recommendations. 10

## Role necessity

Swiss official sources do not publish a discrete "founder necessity test", but the general rules effectively require one. In employee cases, the employer must justify economic interest, show why priority candidates were not available, and demonstrate that the applicant's qualifications match the role. In self-employment cases, the business plan must explain what the founder will do, how the enterprise will operate, and how it will benefit the Swiss labour mmarket. Necessity is therefore assessed through the actual role and its economic function, not through founder status as such. 1

### Physical presence in Switzerland

Physical presence must be justified by the Swiss tasks to be performed. The SEM's annex treats short business meetings, negotiations and certain preparation visits as non-gainful, but treats repeated management activity, integrated training and project work on Swiss soil as gainful activity. A founder application should therefore explain why the person must be in Switzerland to perform genuine entrepreneurial or managerial work there, rather than relying on abstract strategic oversight. 12

### Self-sponsorship risks

Official sources are thin on the phrase "self-sponsorship", but they point to the underlying risk factors. If the founder works through a Swiss AG or GmbH, Swiss social-insurance guidance generally treats the founder as employed; but the SEM immigration guidance says that lack of superior reporting, dominant control, and business-risk assumption point toward independent activity. Own-company sponsorship is therefore not equivalent to ordinary third-party sponsorship, and cases with concentrated founder control are especially classification-sensitive. 13

## Drafting implications

Founder content should separate ownership, office-holding, employment and self-employment. It should describe the actual Swiss activities, reporting lines, control structure, salary mechanism, payroll treatment, insurance position, and business need for on-site presence. Where the structure is borderline, the content should say so plainly and avoid implying that company-law status alone determines immigration status. 14

### Primary legal texts

### Federal legislation

**Article 11 FNIA-Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, SR 142.20.** Close paraphrase: **a** foreign national who wants to exercise gainful activity in Switzerland needs a permit, and gainful activity includes any employed or self-employed activity that normally yields gain, even where it is unpaid.This matters because it prevents founders from arguing that unpaid management, shareholder functions or director functions automatically fall outside work-authorisation law. 27

**Article** **18** **FNIA-same** **Act.** Close paraphrase: third-country nationals may be admitted for salaried employment only where the admission is in Switzerland's economic interests, an employer application has been filed, and the further requirements in the Act are met. This is the starting point for founders employed by their Swiss company. It shows that employee-route founder cases are still ordinary labour-market admissions cases, not a special founder exception. 19

**Article** **19** **FNIA-** **same** **Act.** Close paraphrase: third-country nationals may be admitted for self-employment if the activity serves Switzerland's economic interests, the financial and operational conditions are met, and the further statutory requirements apply. The SEM's guidance adds that the activity must produce sufficient autonomous income and durable positive effects on the Swiss labour market. This matters because company creation alone is nnot enough; the business must be viable and beneficial in Swiss labour-market terms. 28

**Article** **21** **FNIA** **and** **Article 21a** **FNIA** **-** **same** **Act.** Close paraphrase: in third-country salaried cases, priority goes to Swiss and other priority-category workers, and vacancy-notification duties apply in the relevant occupations. This matters because a founder presented as an employee is still vulnerable to

ordinary labour-market compliance objections unless the route is structured as genuine self-employment instead. 29

**Article** 22 **FNIA-same** **Act.** Close paraphrase: the foreign worker must receive salary and working conditions customary for the locality, profession and branch. The SEM explains that this protects both the foreign worker and the resident labour market, and that the review includes contractual salary elements, statistical benchmarks, social contributions, and insurance coverage. This matters directly to founder salary design. 30

**Article** **23** **FNIA-same** **Act.** Close paraphrase: admission for work is generally limited to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, subject to some statutory flexibilities. This matters because founders must still show that their qualifications and experience match the Swiss role they propose to perform. Founder status does not displace the qualifications test. 31

### Ordinances and free-movement instruments

**Articles 1 to 3 OASA / VZAE - Ordonnance relative à l'admission, au séjour et à l'exercice d'une** **activité lucrative / Verordnung über Zulassunng, Aufenthalt und Erwerbstätigkeit, SR 142.201.** Close paraphrase: the ordinance gives a deliberately broad concept of gainful activity and defines self-employment as activity carried out within one's own freely chosen organisation, aimed at generating income, under one's own authority, and with entrepreneurial risk borne by the person concerned. This matters because it anchors the employee/self-employed distinction in control and risk rather than in titles. 32

**Article 22 paragraph 2 OASA / VZAE and Article 22a OASA / VZAE-same ordinance.** Close paraphrase: where work is to be performed in employment, the authority requires a written and binding employment contract before approval; in posting contexts the ordinance also regulates the employer's obligation to reimburse assignment expenses. This matters because founder-employed cases need a real contract and payroll structure, not only a board resolution or incorporation document. 30

**Article 1 letter a number 1 OA-DFJP-FDJP ordinance on approval.** Close paraphrase: cantonal pre-decisions admitting third-country nationals for self-employment under Article 19 FNIA are subject to federal approval. This matters because entrepreneur cases are not purely cantonal, and the SEM remains a substantive checkpoint. 24

**Agreemennt on the Free Movement of Persons and corresponding SEM EU/EFTA guidance.** Close paraphrase: EU/EFTA nationals have a right of entry, stay and labour-market access under the free-movement framework; employed persons generally rely on a Swiss contract or declaration of engagement, while self-employed persons must prove that they actually exercise self-employed activity in Switzerland. This matters because EU/EFTA founders are not assessed under the same restrictive third-country admission logic, but still must prove actual economic activity. 5

#### Official federal guidance and administrative materials

The central federal administrative source is the SEM's Directives and comments, Foreign Nationals, Chapter 4: Stay with gainful activity (state 1 January 2026). For this topic it does most of the real work. It explains the broad meaning of gainful activity; the salary and labour-market review in employed cases; the

founder/associate indicators of self-employment; the business-plan and evidence expectations for company creation; the initial two-year period for new self-employed enterprises; and the federal approval requirement for Article 19 FNIA cases. For founder-role drafting, this is the most valuable official interpretive source after the statute and ordinance themselves. 33

The SEM's annex on the **notion** **of** **gainful activity** is especially important for board/director/ shareholder questions. It is the best official source I found for separating short non-gainful business contact from real work in Switzerland. It confirms that business meetings are, by definition, short and not usually treated as gainful activity, but it also lists management and project examples that do count as work. For founder cases, that annex is what prevents over-broad claims that board or founder travel is always permit-free. 16

The SEM's annex on **conditions** of **remuneration** **and** **work** is narrower because it is framed around detached workers, but it is still useful administratively. It shows the type of salary decomposition the authorities expect, distinguishes salary from expense reimbursements, and expressly includes Swiss social deductions and insurance items in the review. Read together with Chapter 4.3.4 of the directives, it reinforces that salary review is a concrete compliance exercise rather than a theme to be handled only at a high level. 34

The SEM's public page on **labour-market admission** **basics** is a concise official summary of the dual system, the economic-interest test, annual quotas, priority rules, vacancy-notification duties, local salary and conditions, and qualifications. It is not founder-specific, but it is very helpful in showing that founder employee cases remain ordinary work-admission cases unless they are properly structured as self-employment. 29

For EU/EFTA distinctions, the SEM **factsheets** are the clearest official short-form materials. They confirm that employed EU/EFTA nationals above the short-term threshold relyon a Swiss employment attestation or contract, and that EU/EFTA self-employed persons obtain a B permit when they prove effective self-employed activity in Switzerland. These factsheets are important because they show that the easier EU/EFTA route is activity-based too. 35

For payroll and social-security structure, the most relevant official administrative materials are from the BSV and the AHV/AVS information system. The BSV's AHV FAQ says that self-employment exists where the person works in their own name, on their own account, independently and at their own economic risk; contractual wording is not decisive; and the compensation fund decides status case by case.The BSV's SME page adds an important legal-form rule of thumb: owners of an AG or GmbH are treated as employees, while sole proprietorships and partnerships are treated as self-employed. Together,those materials are highly useful for founder payroll design, although they are social-insurance guidance rather than immigration law. 36

For salary benchmarking, the FSO's **Salarium** and SECO's **national wage** **calculator** are official reference tools. Both disclaim that they are not wvage recommendations, and the SEM directives treat them as aids among several salary references rather than legally binding rates. That is the correct drafting stance for founder cases as well: they are evidential tools, not safe-harbour numbers. 37

### Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich.** The Cantonof Zurich's official third-country guidance is useful on both the employee and self-employed sides. Its public page says that third-country local employment from day one requires a permit that only the employer can request, and that the employer must comply with local, occupational and sectoral salary and working conditions. Its January 2024 explanatory PDF goes further: for salaried work it requires a dated and signed employment contract; for self-employment it requires a business plan, projected balance sheet, evidence of start capital and premises, and states that only qualified managers, specialists and other qualified workers can be admitted. Zurich's one-page self-employment procedure chart also shows, in a compact official form, that third-country self-employed cases require proof of economic interest, financial and operational conditions, personal qualifications and quota availability. The Zurich material is partly legal summary, partly evidential checklist, and partly practice note; it is useful but not as authoritative as SEM guidance. 38

**Geneva.** Geneva's official material for third-country independent workers is more procedural and evidential, but very helpful. It states that non-EU/EFTA applicants do not enjoy facilitated labour-market access; that the project must be in the Swiss economic interest; that the business cannot start until the work authorisation is issued; that the OCPM may deliver an L permit up to 24 months or a B permit, though the competent authority will generally issue a shoort-term permit first; that the OCIRT examines the application in advance; and that the canton's decision is then sent to the SEM for approval. **Vaud.** Official Vaud form material located in search results, while not a full guidance page, asks for evidence such as a lease for business premises, detailed administrative steps planned for starting the independent activity, a business plan, proof of financial resources, and affiliation to a compensation fund among the planned or completed steps. Because this comes from form material rather than a comprehensive explanatory note, it should be treated as a practice-sensitive evidential indicator rather than a source of general legal propositions. 40

### Practical legal analysis

The first practical point is that Swiss authorities are concerned with the legal character of the activity, not the entrepreneur's preferred label. That is why official guidance repeatedly turns back to control, instructions, risk and actual tasks. A founder may be an employee for immigration purposes, self-employed for immigration purposes, an employee for social insurance, or in a mixed or disputed posture depending on the exact setup. This is also why directorship and register status are treated cautiously: the SEM expressly says those formal markers are not enough. Where official sources are thin, the safest reading is not to create new founder categories by implication. 41

The second point is that the actual work to be performed in Switzerland matters more than ownership or board membership. The SEM's gainful-activity materials show a spectrum1. At one end are short meetings, negotiations and other brief business contacts that can remain outside gainful activity. At the other are repeated management and operational tasks on Swiss soil, which are treated as work. This means a founder role description must say what the person will actually do in Switzerland: lead recruitment, supervise staff, raise and deploy capital locally, manage Swiss client relationships, oversee product roll-out, run operations, or similar. If the description only reaches occasional board presence, investment monitoring and strategic conversations, the case may not line up well with a work-permit narrative at all. That is an inference from the official examples, but it is a strong one. 12

The third point is that salary and local-conditions review does not disappear merely because the applicant founded the company. If the structure chosen is employment by the Swiss company, Article 22FNIA remains engaged. TheSEM says the purpose is both to protect the foreign worker from abusive terms and to prevent cheaper foreign labour being used against resident workers. In founder cases,

<!-- 10 -->

that means low-cash or no-cash salary packages are risky unless they are carefully justified within the SEM's participation-plan logic. Official guidance is helpful here: employee participation can count, but not as a complete substitute for reliable means of living. Payroll design, social contributions, health/ accident coverage, and in longer employment cases occupational pension enrolment all remain relevant. 21

The fourth point is that physical presence in Switzerland must be justified by the role. Official sources do not publish a dedicated federal "need to be physically present" test for founders, and that absence should be stated openly. But the existing materials imply the question strongly. If some kinds of short business travel are not gainful activity, then a founder seeking a Swisswork authorisation should explain why the planned Swiss presence will involve actual gainful activity and why those tasks need to be performed from Switzerland. In a founder or entrepreneur case, that usually means tying the presence to on-the-ground company creation, management, hiring, product or service delivery,client development or regulated operational functions, supported by the business plan and staffing plan. Simply saying "the founder wishes to oversee the company" is too abstract. 42

The fifth point is that self-sponsorship is not the same thing as third-party sponsorship. A normal employee case assumes a company distinct from, and genuinely directing, the worker. A founder-controlled AG or GmbH may be a separate legal person and may also treat the founder as an employee for social-insurance purposes, but the SEM's immigration classification still asks whether the founder is really subordinate to the company, or whether the founder is in substance acting independently and bearing the entrepreneurial risk. That is why own-company cases can be viable but fragile. They must be designed around real governance, genuine remuneration, payroll compliance and a credible explanation of who directs whom. Any automated content system should therefore avoid saying either that self-sponsorship is impossible or that it is routine; the truthful position is that official sources are indirect and the answer is structure-dependent. 43

The sixth point is that automated public-facing content must not present directorship alone as a route. That is unsupported by the official materials and risks serious legal inaccuracy. The better message is that directorship may be part of the factual matrix, but Swiss work-authorisation law analyses the underlying activity. A non-executive or occasional board role may, in some circumstances, resemble a short business function that does not itself require a work permit; an executive or operational board role in Switzerland may require full compliance with the salaried or self-employed framework. Because no dedicated federal director/shareholder doctrine was located, content should say exactly that rather than inventing certainty. 7

### Drafting cautions

·Do not say that a shareholder can work in Switzerland merely because they own shares. Official sources treat passive asset administration and active gainful work differently, and they do not identify shareholding as a standalone work right.

·Do not say that appointment as a director, board member, president or CEO automatically gives work rights. The SEM says those titles and register entries are not sufficient classification criteria. 18

·Do not treat "founder", "director", "shareholder" or "investor" as legally decisive immigration categories. The analysis turns on actual activity, control, reporting lines and risk. 44

·Do not confuse passive investment or short business meetings with active entrepreneurial work in Switzerland. Some short business activity can be non-gainful; repeated management and operational activity is not. 16

·Do not ignore salary and local-conditions issues where the founder is employed by the Swiss company. Article 22 review remains engaged, and equity-heavy packages need careful handling. 10

·Do not omit payroll, social-security and insurance consequences. Swiss authorities expect compliant social contributions and adequate health/accident cover; legal form also affects social-insurance treatment. 45

·Do not imply that self-sponsorship is automatic. Official sources are indirect, and own-company cases are classification-sensitive where the founder controls the business and bears the risk. 46

·Do not imply that self-sponsorship is impossible either. A Swiss AG or GmbH can in principle structure founder employment, but the case must still satisfy the ordinary employed-work tests and withstand scrutiny on subordination and reality. 47

·Do not omnit the need to justify what the founder will physically do in Switzerland. If the role could be reduced to occasional meetings or investor oversight, the permit narrative may be weak or conceptually mismatched. 48

·Do not forget that self-employed founder cases need a real operational dossier: financing, staffing, market analysis, expected turnover/profit, and evidence of labour-market benefit. 49
