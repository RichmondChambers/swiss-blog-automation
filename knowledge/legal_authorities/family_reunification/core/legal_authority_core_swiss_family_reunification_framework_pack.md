# Swiss Family Reunification Legal Authority Pack

## Executive legal summary

Swiss family reunification is not a single, uniform permission. It is a composite framework operating through different legal regimes depending on the sponsor's status: the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act for Swiss sponsors and most third-country sponsors; the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons for EU/EFTA sponsors; and a distinct, SEM-led framework for persons with provisional admission. The central drafting point is therefore structural: one cannot state a generic "Swiss family reunification rule" without first identifying the sponsor category, because entitlement, eligible relatives, deadlines, financial thresholds, language expectations, decision-maker, and the effect of family breakdown all change materially across routes.

For Swiss citizens, Article 42 AIG creates a statutory entitlement for foreign spouses and unmarried children under 18, subject to cohabitation. It also creates a distinct and easily missed route for certain wider relatives where the family member already holds a durable residence permit from a state with which Switzerland has a free movement agreement: spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent, and dependent ascendants. For settlement-permit sponsors (permit C), Article 43 AIG also creates an entitlement, but only if the family will live together, suitable accommodation exists, the family is not dependent on social assistance, the spouse can communicate in the local national language or is enrolled in language tuition for initial issuance, and the sponsor does not receive annual supplementary benefits under the ELG or become newly eligible because of the reunification. For residence-permit sponsors (permit B), Article 44 AIG is materially similar on conditions, but the permit "may" be granted rather than "must" be granted; SEM guidance expressly notes that there is no legal entitlement and that cantons may apply stricter admission requirements. For short-stay sponsors (permit L), Article 45 AIG allows family reunification on a still more limited, discretionary basis.

For EU/EFTA sponsors, the AFMP route is materially broader. Annex I Article 3 gives a right of family admission to the spouse; descendants under 21 or who are dependent; and dependent ascendants, regardless of the family member's nationality. It also requires the parties to facilitate admission of certain other dependent or household members, which is not the same thing as an unqualified right. SEM's official factsheet adds two practical qualifications of high drafting importance: students may reunite only with spouses and dependent children, and employees are not subjected to the same financial means test as self-employed or economically inactive sponsors, who must show sufficient resources. The family members' residence rights are derivative and normally last only as long as the principal EU/EFTA sponsor's own AFMP-based right continues.

Evidence is legally central. Official materials consistently require identity and civil-status evidence, proof of the family relationship, proof of housing, and proof of finances where relevant. For dependent relatives, the official public guidance requires evidence from the country of origin or provenance showing dependency or prior co-residence. For spouse routes subject to integration requirements, the key practical distinction is between initial issuance and later continuation: federal rules allow enrolment in a language-support offer to suffice for initial issuance in several routes, but SEM guidance states that within one year after arrival the spouse must show A1 oral competence in the local national language. Children under 18 are exempt from the language requirement. Cantonally published application guidance commonly demands lease documentation, recent payslips or tax records, civil-status certificates, translations, and - in separated-parent cases - formal custody or consent documents rather than informal letters.

Timing rules are often decisive. Under the domestic AIG route, the general rule is that reunification must be claimed within five years, but children over 12 must be brought within 12 months. Those deadlines do not apply to the special Article 42(2) route for certain family members of Swiss citizens holding a durable EU/EFTA residence status, and SEM's official FAQ says there are no deadlines under the EU/EFTA free-movement route. A subsequent or delayed child application is only available where major family reasons are shown; the implementing ordinance formulates this conservatively, by reference to the child's welfare being safeguardable only through reunification in Switzerland. SEM guidance further states that age is assessed when the application is lodged, not when the decision is issued, and explains how the one-year rule truncates rather than adds to the ordinary five-year period once a child turns 12.

Procedure also matters. The current SEM instruction on third-country family reunification states that the ordinary Article 42 ff. AIG process generally begins with a national visa D application at the Swiss representation in the foreign national's lawful district of residence. The representation conducts a preliminary check of essential documents and identity; once the file is decision-ready, it is transmitted to the competent cantonal migration authority for the substantive family-reunification assessment. The instruction also shows why document preparation is not a mere checklist exercise: representations may flag fraud indicators, sham marriage concerns, forced marriage, child marriage, human trafficking concerns, and may recommend further verification or a DNA test. For provisionally admitted sponsors, the procedure is different again: the application is filed with the canton, but the SEM makes the decision.

Refusals are commonly driven not by one missing document in isolation, but by a combination of legal fit, timing, and credibility. Domestic routes can fail because the wrong sponsor status is assumed, deadlines were missed, cohabitation or suitable accommodation is not established, social assistance or supplementary-benefit issues are overlooked, language requirements are misstated, or separated-parent evidence is incomplete. Applications can also be suspended where authorities suspect a civil invalidity ground in the marriage, and rights can extinguish where they are invoked abusively or where statutory revocation grounds are present. After separation or divorce, Article 50 AIG may preserve status, but only where the relationship lasted at least three years and integration criteria are met, or where important personal reasons require continued stay; that route has its own A1 oral language rule in the ordinance. Under AFMP, family members' rights are derivative and may fall away unless an independent residence basis exists after divorce or death.

## Framework and proposition map

### Structural overview

**Legal nature of family reunification.** Swiss law differentiates between entitlement-based and discretionary family reunification. Swiss sponsors and permit C sponsors generally have statutory entitlement routes if conditions are met. Permit B and permit L sponsors are materially more conditional, with permit B expressly treated in SEM guidance as discretionary and permit L as a non-entitlement route. EU/EFTA sponsors rely primarily on the AFMP, which is autonomous and usually takes precedence over AIG where it is more specific or more favourable. Provisionally admitted sponsors use a separate Article 85c AIG route, which is discretionary and SEM-decided.

**Main eligible categories.** Domestic AIG routes centre on spouses or registered partners and unmarried children under 18, with special expansions for Swiss citizens in Article 42(2) and for adoptive foster children in Article 48. The EU/EFTA route is broader: spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent, dependent ascendants, and facilitated admission of some additional dependent or household members. Temporary admission under Article 85c is narrower again: spouse and unmarried children under 18 only.

**Sponsor-related distinctions.** AIG routes turn heavily on the sponsor's permit category. The jump from permit B to permit C changes both legal force and settlement consequences. The EU/EFTA route turns instead on whether the principal holds an AFMP-based right, whether the person is an employee, self-employed, economically inactive, or a student, and whether the family member's status is purely derivative. A Swiss sponsor's special Article 42(2) route should not be confused with the ordinary spouse-and-minor-child route in Article 42(1).

**Core evidential requirements.** Across routes, the recurring evidence themes are identity, civil status, kinship, dependency where relevant, suitable housing, finances, cohabitation intention, and language evidence for spouses where the statute or ordinance imposes it. Official procedure materials also show that documentary authenticity itself is often a live issue: the authorities may require apostilles, official translations, custody orders, DNA evidence, or document verification through the Swiss representation abroad.

**Procedural and cantonal dimensions.** In the ordinary third-country regime, the consular post usually receives the visa D application, performs a preliminary check, and forwards the file to the canton for substantive decision-making. Cantons publish their own submission forms, documentary expectations, and timing indications, and SEM guidance expressly leaves room for cantonal variation, especially in discretionary permit B cases. In Article 85c cases, the canton runs the preliminary stage but the SEM decides.

**Refusal and longer-term planning significance.** The framework is highly sequencing-sensitive. Deadline expiry can close routes unless "major family reasons" are established. Weak custody evidence can sink child applications. Weak explanation of housing, finances, or sponsor status can create avoidable refusals. Family breakdown planning matters as well, because Article 50 AIG and AFMP post-divorce rules do not replicate the initial admission route and must be analysed separately.

### Official guidance and administrative materials

The SEM's 2026 AIG Directives are the principal federal administrative guide. They expressly distinguish the Swiss, permit C, permit B, permit L, recognised-refugee and provisionally admitted routes; state that permit-B family reunification lacks a legal entitlement and may be subject to stricter cantonal requirements; explain the five-year/12-month deadline structure; confirm that the child's age is measured at filing; and summarise the post-breakdown route, including the extension of Article 50 AIG from 1 January 2025 to additional categories. They are therefore indispensable as the federal administrative synthesis, while remaining guidance rather than statute.

SEM's instruction of 4 December 2025 on *Familiennachzugsverfahren von Drittstaatsangehörigen* is especially important for procedure and evidence. It states that ordinary Article 42 ff. AIG cases generally begin with the visa D application at the competent Swiss representation abroad; that the post performs a summary pre-check of essential documents, identity and relationship evidence; that the file is then sent to the canton for substantive examination; and that the representation may highlight indicators of document forgery, sham marriage, forced marriage, child marriage, trafficking, or recommend further document verification or DNA testing. It also addresses refugee-related identity-document problems and states that, in Article 85c cases, the SEM alone decides after the canton has carried out the preliminary stage.

For EU/EFTA cases, the SEM factsheet on family reunification is the clearest official public summary. It confirms the wider eligible categories, distinguishes employees from self-employed and economically inactive persons on finances, limits students to spouses and dependent children, states that family members' residence rights are derivative and generally co-terminate with the principal sponsor's right, lists the key entry documents, records labour-market access for spouses and younger descendants, and explains the position after divorce, death, or fraud.

SEM's language-requirements page and its Aufenthalt FAQ are useful official interpretive aids. They confirm that family-reunification applicants must prove a national language at the relevant minimum level where the law requires it; that children under 18 are exempt; that the spouse of a C-, B- or F-based sponsor must normally enrol in language support and then show A1 oral ability within one year after entry; and that the often-cited housing rule of thumb is "family members minus one equals number of rooms". Those latter points are administrative guidance, not black-letter statutory text, and should be labelled accordingly in later drafting.

The federal public-information portal ch.ch is also useful because it puts the statute into operational terms. It clearly warns that family reunification may be a right or a discretionary possibility depending on nationality and permit type; that permit B does not automatically confer a right; that recognised refugees may benefit but asylum seekers may not; that spouses, children and some parents may qualify depending on sponsor category; that a marriage or registered partnership must be recognised in Switzerland; and that applications ordinarily require a passport, visa if needed, relationship proof, dependency proof where relevant, and language-course enrolment for the spouse where necessary.

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

### Practical legal analysis

Swiss family reunification operates as a layered matrix. The first and most important step is to classify the sponsor correctly. A Swiss sponsor under Article 42(1), a Swiss sponsor under Article 42(2), a permit C sponsor, a permit B sponsor, a permit L sponsor, an EU/EFTA sponsor under AFMP, a recognised refugee with asylum, and a provisionally admitted person under Article 85c do not sit within the same legal architecture. That classification then determines not just who may qualify, but whether the route is entitlement-based or discretionary, whether the family member's right is autonomous or derivative, whether deadlines apply, how finances are assessed, whether the spouse must show or may defer language competence, and whether the canton or the SEM is the true decision-maker.

Evidence, sequencing and consistency matter because the Swiss system is procedurally distributed. In ordinary third-country cases, the representation abroad and the canton each have a role, but not the same role. The representation checks competence, identity, and the sufficiency of essential documents; the canton makes the substantive family-reunification assessment. That institutional split explains why cases can fail even where the legal route exists in principle: the file may never reach a persuasive substantive presentation if identity, civil-status evidence, custody papers, translations, or documentary authenticity are weak. The SEM's 2025 instruction makes clear that fraud indicators, forced-marriage concerns, sham-marriage concerns, and doubts about documents can trigger deeper checking or recommendations for DNA analysis.

Cantonal handling can materially affect outcomes even though cantons do not make federal law. Three points matter in practice. First, discretionary routes, especially permit-B cases, are explicitly left with room for stricter cantonal approaches in SEM guidance. Secondly, cantons shape the documentary package and the operational standard of proof: Zurich's requirements for apostilles, translations, formal custody evidence, payslips and proof of historic support illustrate how a canton can turn broad statutory concepts such as "relationship", "dependency" or "suitable housing" into concrete evidential demands. Thirdly, cantons materially affect timing, with Geneva publicly indicating six to eight months for family-reunification processing, Zurich warning of several months on at least one route, and Basel speaking of several weeks. These are not legal time limits, but they are highly relevant for strategic planning.

Refusals often arise from weak factual presentation rather than a single omitted document. By inference from the statutory and procedural materials, the high-risk patterns are: choosing the wrong route; overlooking a sponsor-status distinction; missing the five-year or 12-month deadline; assuming that a late child case can be cured without proving major family reasons; presenting weak evidence of dependency or cohabitation; submitting incomplete separated-parent evidence; underestimating the importance of supplementary benefits as distinct from social assistance; or treating language requirements as a generic pre-entry checklist rather than a route-specific condition that may operate differently at initial issuance, renewal, or post-breakdown retention. By contrast, re-filing is most likely to be realistic where the problem is curable and evidential - such as apostille, translation, custody, dependency, housing, or language-course enrolment - and much less likely to succeed where the statutory route itself is closed by deadline expiry, sponsor ineligibility, or a durable abuse finding. That is an inference from the legal materials rather than an extracted official formula, but it is strongly supported by the way the framework is built.

Refusal and appeal handling should therefore be framed cautiously in later content generation. At high level, the first-instance authority differs by route: for ordinary Article 42 ff. AIG cases, the canton is the substantive authority after the consular stage; for Article 85c cases, the SEM decides after the canton's preliminary stage. In practice, that means remedy path, forum and re-filing strategy depend on who made the operative decision and why. This pack does not separately compile the detailed appeal codification for every route and canton, so later drafting should avoid over-precise statements on appellate forum without checking the decision notice and the procedural law applicable to the deciding authority.

### Drafting cautions

- Do not collapse EU/EFTA, Swiss, permit C, permit B, permit L and provisional-admission routes into one framework. The legal basis, eligible relatives and conditions differ materially.
- Do not describe permit-B family reunification as an unconditional right. SEM guidance says there is no legal entitlement and that cantons may impose stricter conditions.
- Do not assume family reunification is only a spouse-and-minor-child route. Article 42(2), AFMP Annex I Article 3 and Article 48 AIG create additional categories.
- Do not state time limits generically. AFMP cases are treated differently from domestic AIG cases, and Article 42(2) also sits outside the ordinary deadline rule.
- Do not present cantonal housing formulae, document lists or processing times as if they were federal law. They are practice points and may vary by canton.
- Do not reduce language analysis to "A1 before entry". The statute, ordinance and SEM guidance distinguish between initial issuance, renewal and post-breakdown continuation. Children under 18 are different again.
- Do not assume all post-divorce cases are governed by Article 50 AIG. AFMP cases use derivative-right logic and may require a fresh independent residence basis.
- Do not assume a missing passport or imperfect documents always end the case. The official procedure instruction shows that identity, status and kinship may sometimes be established through broader evidential means, including verification or DNA, especially in refugee-related contexts.
- Do not write as if all recognised-partner questions are identical across all routes. Article 52 AIG handles registered partnerships by analogy in domestic law, but AFMP drafting should be checked separately if the precise route matters.

For article-level wording, this pack relied on consolidated text as retrieved through legal-text mirrors during Fedlex access instability, cross-checked against official SEM and ch.ch guidance.

Procedure materials also refer to the visa-entry ordinance (VEV/OEV) and the EJPD consent-procedure ordinance, but those texts were not separately extracted article-by-article in this pack.

**Official guidance.** SEM AIG Directives, version as of 1 January 2026; SEM instruction of 4 December 2025 on third-country family-reunification procedure; SEM EU/EFTA family-reunification factsheet; SEM language-requirements page; SEM Aufenthalt FAQ; ch.ch family-reunification guidance; SEM visa application guidance.

**Cantonal guidance.** Canton Zurich family-reunification overview and application pages; Canton Geneva OCPM family-reunification page and processing-times page; Canton Basel-Stadt family-reunification page.

**Secondary material.** No non-official secondary authority was relied on substantively for legal propositions. LexFind, Lawbrary and Droit bilingue were used only as access mirrors for consolidated legislative wording where direct Fedlex pages were unstable during research.

### Open questions and limitations

This pack is intentionally cautious on three points. First, it does not separately map the full asylum-law family-unity regime for recognised refugees under Article 51 AsylG, beyond noting official public guidance that recognised refugees may benefit and asylum seekers may not. Secondly, it does not compile the full appeal-route codification for each sponsor category and canton; later drafting should check the remedy notice on the operative decision and the specific procedure law of the deciding authority. Thirdly, because direct Fedlex access was intermittently unstable during research, some legislative wording was extracted through reputable legal-text mirrors rather than directly from Fedlex, and should be re-opened on Fedlex during any later final-publication check.
