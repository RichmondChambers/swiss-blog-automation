Executive legal summary
Swiss spouse and registered-partner family reunification is not a single route. The controlling
framework depends first on whether the case falls under the domestic Foreign Nationals and
Integration Act regime or the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons regime. Under domestic
law, the core provisions are in the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, especially Articles 42 to 52,
with implementing rules in the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment and detailed guidance in
the State Secretariat for Migration directives. Under the AFMP, EU/EFTA family reunification follows
treaty-based free-movement logic rather than the full domestic AIG/LEI structure, although the AIG may
still apply subsidiarily where the treaty does not regulate the point or where domestic law is more
favourable. 1


For domestic cases, Swiss sponsors under Article 42, C-permit sponsors under Article 43, B-permit
sponsors under Article 44, L-permit sponsors under Article 45, and temporarily admitted sponsors
under Article 85 and OASA Articles 74 and 74a sit under different statutory conditions. Articles 42 and 43
create rights claims where the statutory conditions are met; Articles 44 and 45 are discretionary
provisions for the cantonal authorities. Temporarily admitted persons and temporarily admitted
refugees have a separate statutory scheme and, unlike the ordinary B/C routes, face a waiting period
before reunification is possible. 2


Registered partnerships should not be handled loosely. Domestic law expressly equates the Chapter 7
spouse rules with registered partnerships of same-sex couples through Article 52 AIG. SEM guidance
also states that Swiss registered partnerships created before 1 July 2022 continue to exist, while new
Swiss registered partnerships can no longer be created after marriage equality took effect. Under the
current AFMP framework, SEM states that registered partners are already treated in practice as
equivalent to spouses by analogy or via subsidiary application of the more favourable FNIA/AIG rules,
but the codification of registered partners in the updated AFMP is described by SEM as a future change
and is not yet in force. That distinction is critical for later drafting. 3


At federal level, the most stable legal requirements for spouse/registered-partner cases are: genuine
cohabitation unless an accepted exception applies; category-specific checks on housing, social-
assistance risk, supplementary benefits and language ability; documentary proof of identity and family
relationship; and, in ordinary third-country cases, an application sequence that in principle starts
abroad with a long-stay D visa process through the competent Swiss representation. Official refusal
risks identifiable in federal materials include sham marriage or other abuse, false or incomplete
information, inability to prove identity or family relationship, lack of suitable accommodation where
required, dependence on social assistance or supplementary benefits where relevant, absent language
evidence in categories that require it, and public-order or revocation grounds. 4

Structural overview of spouse and registered-partner
reunification
For a Swiss sponsor who is not relying on the special EU/EFTA-linked rule in Article 42(2), the basic
spouse route is Article 42(1) AIG: the foreign spouse of a Swiss national is entitled to a residence permit
if the couple live together. Unlike Articles 43 to 45, Article 42(1) does not set out a standalone statutory
language requirement for the initial residence permit, and it does not expressly list “no social
assistance” or “adequate housing” as independent threshold conditions in the article itself. SEM
guidance, however, treats adequate accommodation as indirectly relevant through the cohabitation
requirement, and cantonal practice may request income and housing evidence at filing. After five years
of lawful, uninterrupted stay, the foreign spouse of a Swiss national is entitled to settlement if the
integration criteria are met, including the language thresholds in OASA Article 73b. Registered
partnerships are treated the same way domestically by Article 52. 5


Article 42(2) AIG creates a different Swiss-sponsor configuration where the foreign family member
already holds a durable residence entitlement in an EU/EFTA state linked to Switzerland by free
movement. That rule is broader than Article 42(1): it covers the spouse, descendants under 21 or
dependants, and dependent ascendants; SEM guidance explains that it mirrors free-movement logic
more closely and is not subject to the ordinary Article 47 time limits. This route should therefore be kept
doctrinally separate from the standard Swiss-national spouse route under Article 42(1). 6


For a sponsor with a C permit, Article 43 AIG creates a right to spouse reunification, but only if the
family cohabit, suitable accommodation is available, the family will not depend on social assistance, the
incoming spouse can communicate in the local national language or is registered for a qualifying
language course, and the sponsor is not receiving supplementary benefits and would not become
entitled to them because of reunification. SEM guidance treats future income and overall family
finances as relevant when assessing dependency risk. After five years of lawful, uninterrupted stay in
genuine family life, the spouse can move to settlement status if the integration criteria are met,
including A2 oral and A1 written language. Registered partnerships again follow by virtue of Article 52.




For a sponsor with a B permit, Article 44 AIG is structurally similar to Article 43 but materially weaker: it
is discretionary, not a statutory entitlement. The federal conditions are cohabitation, suitable
accommodation, no social-assistance dependency, A1 oral language or course enrolment, and no
supplementary benefits for the sponsor. SEM guidance also states that cantons may impose stricter
practice within the bounds of their discretion, and that future income is ordinarily not counted, save in
narrow situations where durable actual employment is highly probable. 8


For a sponsor with an L permit, Article 45 AIG is also discretionary. The statute allows the spouse of a
short-stay permit holder to receive a short-stay permit if the family live together, suitable
accommodation exists, the family does not depend on social assistance, and the sponsor is not
receiving supplementary benefits or made eligible for them because of the reunification. SEM guidance
states expressly that, apart from language competence, the conditions correspond to the B-permit
route, and the family member receives a permit with the same validity as the sponsor’s short-stay
permit. The continuation logic after breakdown is correspondingly weaker. 9


For temporarily admitted persons and temporarily admitted refugees, federal law uses a separate
route. Under Article 85 of the AIG as reflected in the consolidated text, family members may be reunited
at the earliest three years after the temporary-admission order if the family will live together, suitable
accommodation exists, the family does not depend on social assistance, the spouse can communicate






<!-- Page break -->

in the local national language, and the sponsor is not receiving supplementary benefits or made eligible
for them because of the reunification. OASA Articles 74 and 74a implement the filing route through the
cantonal migration authority and confirm that registered partnerships are treated correspondingly. This
route is relevant as an “other sponsor category”, but it should not be conflated with the ordinary B-
permit family route. 10


For EU/EFTA sponsors under the AFMP, the official SEM position is that EU/EFTA nationals holding a
Swiss residence or short-stay permit may bring their spouse, descendants under 21 or dependants, and
dependent ascendants, regardless of those relatives’ nationality; students are limited to spouses and
dependent children. Suitable accommodation is always required. Financial sufficiency is not an
employee-condition, but self-employed and economically inactive persons must have sufficient means
for the family. The spouse’s right of stay is derivative of the sponsor’s AFMP right. Under current SEM
guidance, registered partners are already treated in practice as equivalent to spouses, but the updated
AFMP material makes clear that the express treaty-level codification of registered partners belongs to
the future amended framework, not the current one. 11

Primary legal texts
The key domestic text is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. For spouse and
registered-partner work, the load-bearing provisions are Article 42 (Swiss sponsors), Article 43 (C-permit
sponsors), Article 44 (B-permit sponsors), Article 45 (L-permit sponsors), Article 47 (time limits), Article
49 (exceptions to cohabitation), Article 49a (language-waiver grounds), Article 50 (continued residence
after family breakdown), Article 51 (expiry of rights where there is abuse or revocation grounds), Article
52 (registered partnerships), and the general revocation grounds in Articles 62 and 63. The temporary-
admission regime sits later in the Act and includes family reunification conditions for that category. 23


The main implementing ordinance is the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment. Its family-
reunification provisions are particularly important for practice: Article 73 applies the five-year and
twelve-month filing limits to residence-permit sponsors and expressly applies those rules
correspondingly to registered partnerships; Article 73a fixes the language-course and renewal language
requirements for Articles 43 and 44; Article 73b sets the settlement-language thresholds for spouses of






<!-- Page break -->

Swiss nationals and C-permit holders; Article 74 regulates the temporary-admission filing path and
again extends it to registered partnerships; Article 74a sets the temporary-admission language rule;
Article 75 defines “important family reasons” for late child reunification; Article 76 gives examples of
“important reasons” for separate residences; and Article 77 sets the A1 oral language threshold for
Article 50(1)(a) continuance cases. 24


For EU/EFTA cases, the operative public-law framework is the Agreement on the Free Movement of
Persons and its Swiss implementing practice. In the evidence gathered here, the most directly usable
official material is SEM’s current family-reunification factsheet and the 2026 factsheet on the updated
AFMP package, which together identify the present spouse-based treaty scheme and the not-yet-in-
force future codification of registered partners. For later drafting, those AFMP materials should be read
together with the domestic Act’s Article 2 subsidiarity rule and the SEM free-movement guidance, rather
than collapsed into the domestic Articles 42 to 45 framework. 25


The entry/visa layer is also relevant. SEM’s 2025 federal family-reunification procedure directive
expressly treats ordinary Article 42 ff. AIG cases as combining a residence-permit application with an
entry/visa application. It cites the Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance and states, in principle, that
long-stay family reunification starts with a D-visa application at the competent Swiss representation
abroad. 26


Official guidance and administrative materials
The single most useful federal administrative source for domestic cases is the SEM Directives and
Explanatory Notes I. Foreign Nationals Sector, state as at 1 January 2026. Those directives do four
things that matter for future content: they distinguish sponsor categories with precision; they explain
when cohabitation, housing, finance, language and supplementary-benefits checks apply; they clarify
that Swiss same-sex registered partnerships remain covered after marriage equality through Article 52;
and they collect the administration’s reading of Article 50 after family breakdown. They are guidance,
not statute, but they are the clearest official statement of federal administrative reading. 27


A second federal source of particular drafting value is the SEM Directive on family-reunification
procedure for third-country nationals dated 4 December 2025. That directive is narrower but
procedurally decisive. It states that Article 42 to 45 cases are to be distinguished by sponsor status;
confirms that Articles 42 and 43 are claims but Articles 44 and 45 are discretionary; explains that the
procedure normally begins abroad at the Swiss representation competent for the applicant’s lawful
residence; identifies the representation’s summary screening role; describes the need for documents
proving identity and family relationship; and explains when further documents, document checks or
DNA tests may be used. It also confirms that, after the competent migration authority issues entry
authorisation, the representation’s role is largely reduced to identification and issuance of the D visa
unless serious doubts arise. 28


For EU/EFTA cases, the best federal operational materials are the SEM family-reunification factsheet
and the SEM 13 March 2026 AFMP update factsheet. The first states the current family-member
categories, housing and finance logic, entry documents, labour-market access and divorce/death
consequences. The second is especially important because it says openly that registered partners are
already treated in practice as equivalent under the current framework, but only the updated AFMP
would expressly codify them. That is a drafting warning, not merely background. 11

Practical legal analysis
A legally cautious internal framework should begin by separating entitlement routes from
discretionary routes. Swiss sponsors under Article 42(1) and C-permit sponsors under Article 43 sit in
the entitlement category, but Article 42(1) is materially lighter on express statutory conditions than
Article 43. B-permit and L-permit sponsors do not create a statutory right to reunification and therefore
leave more room for cantonal practice variation. That distinction affects not only merits analysis but
also how strongly later content may speak in terms of “eligibility”, “entitlement”, “usual practice” or
“cantonal discretion”. 30


On housing and cohabitation, the safest proposition is that cohabitation is a core federal requirement
for spouse cases across Swiss, C and B sponsor routes, with Article 49 and OASA Article 76 allowing
exceptions only for objectively weighty reasons while the family household genuinely continues. For B,
C, L and F sponsors, suitable or adequate accommodation is express in the legislation; for Swiss
sponsors under Article 42(1), federal guidance treats adequate accommodation as folded into the
requirement that the family really live together, rather than as a separately numbered statutory
condition. Content should therefore avoid saying that Article 42(1) has exactly the same housing test as
Article 43 or 44 without qualification. 31


On finance, Article 43, Article 44, Article 45 and the temporary-admission scheme all contain explicit “no
social assistance” logic, and Articles 43, 44 and temporary admission also now include express
supplementary-benefits restrictions. SEM guidance for Article 43 allows a forward-looking family-wide
financial assessment, whereas SEM guidance for Article 44 says future income is ordinarily disregarded
except where durable employment is highly probable. By contrast, Article 42(1) for Swiss sponsors does
not contain the same explicit front-end statutory finance test, although abuse and revocation rules
remain relevant and cantonal practice often still asks for income evidence. That asymmetry should be
stated with care. 32


On language, the domestic rule is again category-specific. For C-permit and B-permit sponsor cases,
the incoming spouse must either already have A1 oral competence in the local national language or at
least be enrolled in a qualifying course, with actual A1 oral proof required by the time of renewal. For
spouses of Swiss nationals, there is no equivalent express language requirement at the initial Article
42(1) residence-permit stage in the texts cited here, but there is an A2 oral / A1 written requirement for
settlement after five years. L-permit spouse cases do not carry the same federal language requirement
as B-permit cases, according to SEM guidance. Temporarily admitted spouse cases require A1 oral
competence or course registration under OASA Article 74a. 33

On evidence, the federal minimum is functional rather than closed-list. SEM’s 2025 procedure directive
says the essential documents are those that allow the authorities to establish the applicant’s identity
and family relationship to the sponsor, such as civil-status documents. The Swiss representation checks
whether the documents and information permit identity, civil-status and descent assessment, and
whether the travel document is valid and the photograph corresponds. Where justified doubts remain,
further documents may be demanded, document authenticity may be checked, and DNA can be used if
doubts about descent or identity cannot otherwise be resolved. In practice, cantons then translate this
functional standard into local filing lists. Zurich and Geneva show the usual pattern: marriage
certificate, translations where needed, lease, identity documents, financial evidence, and in some
cantons also criminal-record or debt-enforcement material. Internal drafting should therefore
distinguish between the federal evidential standard and the canton-specific filing bundle. 34


On procedure, ordinary third-country family reunification under Articles 42 ff. AIG should be described
as, in principle, an out-of-country process. SEM’s procedure directive says the long-stay family
reunification process normally begins with a D-visa application at the Swiss representation for the
applicant’s lawful place of residence, and Geneva and Ticino repeat the same practical rule. Non-EU/
EFTA family members under the AFMP who are visa nationals also need the entry step, but the SEM
AFMP factsheet notes that non-EU/EFTA family members who already hold a valid Schengen residence
permit are exempt from Swiss visa formalities. 35


On breakdown and refusal risk, the best federal synthesis is conservative. Under Article 50, Swiss/C/B
spouse cases may survive separation or divorce if there were at least three years of married/family life
in Switzerland plus integration, or if important personal reasons justify continued residence, such as
domestic violence, absence of free consent to the marriage, or seriously jeopardised reintegration in
the country of origin. SEM expressly reads those Article 50 rules across to registered partnerships
through Article 52. By contrast, SEM guidance states that L-permit family members do not acquire a
stronger residence claim through Article 50. For AFMP family members, the right is derivative and the
current SEM factsheet says that, in divorce cases, family members lose the derivative right and must
establish another basis of stay; on death, the spouse may keep residence in certain defined situations.
Separate from breakdown, official refusal risks include sham marriage, abuse of right, false statements,
unreliable or insufficient civil-status proof, and public-order/revocation grounds. 36


A further point for refugee-linked cases is worth keeping separate from ordinary sponsor categories.
SEM guidance states that if a recognised refugee marries only after receiving asylum in Switzerland and
the spouse remains abroad, the spouse’s admission follows the foreign-nationals regime rather than
asylum-family-unity rules. That avoids an easy but incorrect assumption that every refugee sponsor
uses the same family-reunification basis. 37


Drafting cautions and source list

Drafting cautions

Future content should not present “Swiss family reunification for spouses and registered partners” as a
single rule set. It should explicitly separate Article 42 Swiss cases, Article 43 C-permit cases, Article 44 B-
permit cases, Article 45 L-permit cases, temporary-admission cases, and AFMP/EU-EFTA cases.
Collapsing those routes would materially misstate both entitlement strength and documentary
conditions. 38


Do not say that the current AFMP text straightforwardly gives registered partners the same express
treaty entitlement as spouses. The safer formulation, grounded in current SEM material, is that






<!-- Page break -->

registered partners are already treated in practice as equivalent within the AFMP sphere by analogy or
subsidiary application of the more favourable AIG/FNIA rules, while express codification belongs to the
updated AFMP package and is not yet in force. 39


Do not imply that Swiss sponsors under Article 42(1) face the same explicit statutory language and
finance conditions as C- and B-permit sponsors. The domestic statute itself is lighter at the initial
residence-permit stage for Swiss sponsors, although cantonal practice may still ask for practical
evidence of housing and means. 40


Do not present cantonal filing lists as the federal minimum set of documents. Zurich and Geneva are
useful examples of evidential handling, but they are local administrative practice, not exhaustive
nationwide law. Later drafting should therefore label them as “selected cantonal practice notes” or
“illustrative filing practice”. 41


Do not assume that “registered partner” is a broad, current Swiss domestic registration category. The
official SEM directives say new Swiss registered partnerships can no longer be created after 1 July 2022,
but existing registered partnerships continue and Article 52 AIG still keeps them within the family-
reunification framework. Any drafting about foreign civil unions should be limited to officially
recognised registered partnerships unless a more specific source supports a broader statement. 42

Open questions and limitations

This pack deliberately prioritises federal law and federal guidance, with only selected cantonal evidence
pages. It does not map every cantonal filing list or fee schedule, and later drafting for operational use
should always verify the filing canton’s current forms and document checklist before publication. The
most important unresolved drafting sensitivity is AFMP treatment of registered partners: current SEM
material supports practical equivalence, but future content should still describe the current treaty
position conservatively until the updated AFMP provisions are actually in force. 46
