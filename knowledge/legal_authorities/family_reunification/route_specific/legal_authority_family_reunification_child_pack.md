# Swiss family reunification for children

Swiss family reunification for children
Suggested filename: legal_authority_family_reunification_child_pack.pdf


Executive legal summary
Swiss child reunification is not a single legal route. Current official materials show four distinct
frameworks that matter in practice: the general foreign-nationals regime under the Foreign Nationals
and Integration Act, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons route for EU/EFTA sponsors and a
narrower Swiss-citizen scenario linked to that agreement, the provisional-admission route for F-permit
holders, and the asylum-law route for recognised refugees with asylum. Each framework uses different
child categories, deadlines, dependency concepts, and evidential expectations. 1


For ordinary child cases under the general AIG/FNIA regime, official guidance treats the core child
category as the unmarried child under 18. The headline timing rule is strict: family reunification must
usually be requested within five years, but children over 12 must be brought within 12 months. The
decisive age is the child’s age on the date of filing, not on the date of decision, so a child who turns 18
during the procedure does not lose the claim if the application was filed in time. If the child turns 12
during the original five-year window, the one-year rule shortens the remaining period rather than
adding a new year on top. 2


Official materials also make clear that child cases cannot safely be reduced to age alone. In split-family
or one-parent cases, the authorities must examine where the future centre of family life will be, whether
the application is genuine, whether the sponsoring parent has the necessary parental authority or
legally sufficient power to live with the child, whether the other parent’s position is properly accounted
for, and whether the child’s welfare is protected. A mere private statement from the parent abroad
allowing the move is not enough in the federal guidance or in Zurich’s cantonal practice materials. 3


Delay has serious consequences. After the ordinary deadline, a child can be admitted only if there are
“important family reasons”. SEM’s current guidance says this exception is to be applied restrictively. In
child cases, the official starting point remains whether the child’s welfare can only be safeguarded by
moving to Switzerland, but SEM also says welfare is not the only criterion: the full circumstances matter,
including changed care arrangements in the home state, the possibility of the child remaining there,
the stage of the child’s socialisation abroad, and the anti-abuse purpose of the deadline rules. Economic
opportunity, general better prospects in Switzerland, sibling care, or the political situation in the
country of origin are not treated as sufficient stand-alone reasons in the guidance. 4


Under the AFMP route, child reunification is materially broader. Official SEM guidance states that
descendants under 21 qualify without any separate dependency test, while descendants aged 21 or
above qualify only if support is actually provided. This route applies irrespective of the child’s nationality,
but it is derived from the EU/EFTA sponsor’s own underlying right of residence. For students, the circle is
narrower: only the spouse and dependent children qualify. There is no general AIG-style Article 47
deadline for EU/EFTA family reunification, although as a matter of category logic, a non-dependent
descendant should not be allowed to age past 21 if relying solely on the under-21 status. 5

Provisionally admitted persons and provisionally admitted refugees are on a different track again. The
current official operating materials still state that the spouse and unmarried children under 18 may be
reunited only after three years of provisional admission, provided the family intends to live together,
suitable accommodation exists, the reunited family will not depend on social assistance, annual
supplementary benefits are not being received or triggered by the reunification, and the spouse meets
the language requirement. SEM’s asylum handbook also emphasises that this is a discretionary route,
with a proportionality assessment, not an automatic entitlement. A separate SEM legislative project
proposes cutting the waiting period to two years, but that proposal had not displaced the three-year
rule in the operative guidance relied on here. 6


Recognised refugees with asylum must be kept separate from the F-permit route. The asylum-law route
in Article 51 AsylG is tied to separation caused by flight and presupposes that the family relationship
already existed and was actually lived in the country of origin, generally in a common household, and
that the relationship remains current and “worthy of protection”. It is not the route for creating a new
household that never previously existed. Here too, minority is measured under Swiss law and, for that
criterion, by the child’s age at the time of filing. 7


On procedure and proof, the official federal material is detailed. Swiss representations abroad carry out
the initial completeness and identity review, then transmit the file to the competent canton for the
merits decision. Where civil-status evidence is weak or documents are unavailable, the canton can
decide whether to proceed on the file, ask for further checks, seek document authentication, order
carefully limited social or neighbourhood enquiries with written consent, or rely on DNA testing where
justified doubts about identity or parentage cannot otherwise be removed. DNA is not meant to be
imposed wholesale by nationality alone. Children over 14 may be heard where necessary, usually at the
Swiss representation abroad. Children do not face ordinary language requirements in family
reunification, although in late child cases SEM says a permit may be tied to attendance at an integration
or language course. 8

Primary legal texts
The operative federal child-reunification provisions identified in the current official materials are these.
Under the FNIA/AIG, the core family-reunification articles are Articles 42 to 47, with Article 49 dealing
with exceptions to the cohabitation requirement, Article 51 dealing with extinction of claims through
abuse or revocation grounds, Article 58b allowing integration or language-course conditions, and
Article 85c governing reunification for provisionally admitted persons. The SEM’s current AIG directives
and asylum handbook repeatedly use those provisions as the federal legal basis for child cases. 44


The implementing ordinance provisions currently relevant are the Ordinance on Admission, Residence
and Gainful Employment provisions on deadlines and late child cases, especially Articles 73 to 76,
together with Article 87 in the context of biometrics and DNA profiles. SEM’s 2026 AIG directives
expressly anchor the ordinary 5-year and 12-month deadlines in Article 73 for B-permit sponsors and
Article 74 for provisionally admitted persons, state that late child cases are assessed through Article 75,
and cite Article 76 on exceptions to cohabitation. The dedicated third-country instruction then explains
how DNA testing may be used where justified doubts about identity or parentage cannot otherwise be
resolved. 45


For EU/EFTA-based child reunification, the central text is Article 3 of Annex I to the Agreement on the
Free Movement of Persons. SEM’s current federal AFMP fact sheet and VFP directives use that provision
to define family members as including descendants under 21 or supported descendants, regardless of
nationality, and to require suitable accommodation and, where appropriate, sufficient resources. For
students, Article 24(4) of Annex I narrows the family circle to the spouse and dependent children. 46


For recognised refugees with asylum, the governing text is Article 51 AsylG. SEM’s asylum handbook
distinguishes Article 51 family asylum / asylum-law family reunification from the AIG route for
provisionally admitted persons, and confirms that Article 51(4) covers entry from abroad where family
members were separated by flight. The same handbook also relies on Asylum Ordinance 1 for the
definition of minority used in asylum family cases. 47


Official guidance and cantonal practice notes
The most important federal guidance document for ordinary child cases is SEM’s AIG directive chapter
on family reunification, in force as of 1 January 2026. It is the key official source for the structure of split-
family child cases, timing, the beginning of time limits, the consequences of turning 12, and the






<!-- Page break -->

restrictive treatment of late applications. It also gives the clearest official warning against reducing
difficult child cases to the child’s age alone. 48


The 4 December 2025 SEM instruction on third-country family reunification procedure is the main
federal administrative source on how child reunification files are handled in practice. It sets out the
three-step workflow of Swiss representation, canton and, where relevant, SEM approval; defines the
“essential” documents as those needed to establish identity and the family relationship; explains what
happens when documents are unavailable; allows carefully limited voluntary document checks and
social or neighbourhood enquiries with written consent; and explains when DNA testing is permissible
and when it is not. 49


The AFMP route is best tracked through SEM’s federal fact sheet on family reunification and the 2026
VFP directives. Those documents are especially useful for later drafting because they set out, in a
compact official form, the under-21/dependency distinction, the derivative character of the family
member’s right, the housing and finance requirements, the student exception, and the evidential
approach to actual dependency for older children. 46


For F-permit and asylum-law child cases, the controlling federal administrative material is SEM’s asylum
handbook. Article F7 is the key official source for provisional-admission reunification, its three-year
waiting period, its under-18 child category, and its discretionary/proportionality structure. Article F3 is
the key source for recognised refugees with asylum, including the requirement of separation by flight,
the prior actual family relationship, and the need in some solo-child cases to obtain the second parent’s
signed consent even where the parent in Switzerland is alone authorised to sign. 50


Cantonal practice notes are valuable mainly on evidence and procedure, not on nationwide legal tests.
Zurich’s public migration pages and practice note are particularly useful for child files: they require a
formal custody title for a child joining one parent, reject a mere private permission letter, require
written consent where there is joint custody, and ask for specific supporting material such as birth
certificates, custody judgments, housing documents and, for older children, confirmation of unmarried
status. Zurich also expressly says that child care in Switzerland must be secured. 51


Geneva’s OCPM pages are useful because they present the child route separately for Swiss sponsors,
non-EU/EFTA sponsors, EU/EFTA sponsors and F-permit holders. They are especially helpful on
operational attachments: parental-authority evidence where reunification concerns only children, proof
of financial means, civil-status documents with translation, and, for F-permit cases, a motivation letter
stating the current address of the child and the intention to live together. They also provide a practical
confirmation that, for EU/EFTA sponsors, the under-21 / dependency distinction is institutionally
handled as a real category split. 52


Ticino’s official materials are thinner on the substantive migration test, but still useful on practical
handling and family-status evidence. The migration office provides a dedicated online route for “new
permit for family reunification and birth”, requiring identity, visa-validity, parent/spouse/child data, and
information on the stay in Switzerland, including financial position. The civil-status pages then explain
how joint parental authority is handled for unmarried parents and how child recognition is registered,
which can matter where parent-child status or parental authority must later be evidenced in a migration
file. 53

Practical legal analysis
A straightforward minor-child case under the ordinary AIG route is narrower than it first appears. It is
not enough that the child is under 18 and unmarried. Official materials also expect the family’s future
centre of life to be in Switzerland, timely filing, genuine intended cohabitation, a legally sound parent-
child relationship, and no material abuse or revocation concerns. In practice, the child file is strongest
where it is filed in time, the child is clearly a minor, the civil-status evidence is clean, the sponsoring
parent can genuinely live with the child in Switzerland, and the future domestic arrangements are
coherent and documented. 54


One-parent and split-family cases need separate treatment in any later content system. SEM’s guidance
no longer insists that both parents and the child live together before a timely admission can be granted,
but it places real weight on the sponsoring parent’s legal position in relation to the child. The federal
and cantonal materials together support a conservative operational rule: in a child-joining-one-parent
case, later drafting should assume that the file must address formal custody or parental-authority
status, the other parent’s position, and the practical care arrangements in Switzerland. It should not
state that “consent from the other parent” is enough unless that sentence is carefully qualified by the
need for the sponsoring parent’s underlying legal authority and by the limits of a simple private
declaration. 36


Late child cases are the main danger area. Official SEM material says the exception is narrow, especially
where the young person has spent most of adolescence abroad and is close to majority. The guidance
requires a real explanation of what has changed in the origin country, especially in the child’s care
arrangements. Later drafting should therefore look for a change in the child’s actual care environment,
not merely a delayed parental wish. Stronger patterns in the official material include the disappearance
of the prior caregiver or a child’s disability that makes parental care in Switzerland genuinely necessary.
Weaker patterns include better life chances, help with younger siblings, household assistance, or
general instability abroad. 4


Household formation is also legally central. SEM’s family-reunification guidance starts by asking where
the future centre of family life will be; if the centre remains abroad, reunification is not made out. The
AFMP route similarly presupposes suitable accommodation and a genuine family household, while the
F-permit route requires an intention to live together and suitable accommodation as express
conditions. In the asylum-law route, the household question looks backwards as well as forwards: the
family relationship must have existed and been lived, generally in a common household, before flight,
and there must now be a real intention to re-establish family life in Switzerland. 55


Dependency should be handled with precision. It is not a general stand-alone test for ordinary minor
children under the AIG route. There, the official emphasis is on minority, family relationship, timeliness,
common household and genuine future family life. Dependency becomes central mainly in AFMP cases
involving descendants aged 21 or over, where the official materials require real need and actual
support, and in some difficult late AIG files where the child’s care needs are the reason advanced for
admission. Drafting that treats “dependency” as a universal threshold for all child cases would therefore
be over-broad. 56


The procedural handling of documentary weakness also deserves careful treatment. Federal guidance
does not assume that every child-reunification file will have perfect civil-status records. Instead, it builds
an escalation ladder: preliminary completeness review by the Swiss representation, canton-level merits
assessment, further document requests where possible, canton-led decisions in “blockade” situations
where papers cannot realistically be obtained, and only then targeted authenticity checks, local






<!-- Page break -->

enquiries with consent, or DNA where justified doubts remain and proportionality is respected. Later
content should therefore not suggest that absence of a document always ends the case immediately,
nor that DNA is routine. 49


The AFMP route can rescue some otherwise difficult age cases, but only if used accurately. Where the
sponsor falls under the AFMP and the child is under 21, the route is broader than the ordinary AIG
under-18 rule. Once the child is 21 or older, however, official federal material moves decisively into a
dependency analysis. Prior actual support is important. The existence of a legal maintenance duty is not
required. For students, ascendants are excluded entirely and only dependent children qualify. This
means that later drafting should not present the AFMP route as “no age limit” or “automatic” for adult
children. 57


F-permit and asylum-law cases require still more caution. F-permit child reunification remains slower
and more conditional than ordinary AIG or AFMP routes, and SEM’s asylum handbook expressly treats it
as discretionary. Recognised refugees with asylum, by contrast, may benefit from Article 51 AsylG, but
only where the child was separated by flight from a pre-existing, actually lived family relationship. Those
routes must not be collapsed into a single “refugee family reunification” category in future content. 58


Drafting cautions
Do not write Swiss child reunification as though every route uses the same age rule. Official sources
require a strict distinction between the ordinary under-18 AIG route, the under-21 / dependent AFMP
route, the under-18 provisional-admission route, and the minor-child asylum route. “Children” is
therefore a legally unstable shorthand unless the route is identified first. 59


Do not write that a sole-parent case is solved by “the other parent’s consent”. The federal and cantonal
materials are more demanding. They focus on formal parental authority or custody, the sponsoring
parent’s legal power to live with the child, and the reality of the intended move. A simple private
declaration is expressly treated as insufficient. 36


Do not treat late applications as a soft discretionary fairness exercise. The current SEM guidance treats
them as a narrow exception. Better education, better work opportunities, or a general wish to join the
parent later are not enough. Later content should ask what has changed in the child’s actual care
arrangements and why continued care abroad is no longer realistically possible, especially for
adolescents. 60


Do not state that the child’s best interests are the only consideration in late cases. SEM says the welfare
of the child matters, but not alone; the whole circumstances and the purpose of the deadline rules
remain relevant. At the same time, later drafting should not imply that officials can freely substitute
their own welfare view for the parents’ in every timely case: the official materials describe the
intervention space as limited. 61


Do not transpose spouse-language rules onto child cases. The federal FAQ is explicit that unmarried
children under 18 do not face ordinary language requirements. The nuance to preserve is different: in
late child cases SEM says the permit may be made conditional on attendance at an integration or
language course. 62


Do not publish a two-year F-permit waiting period unless the law has been rechecked. The official
operative materials relied on here still use three years, even though the SEM legislative project page






<!-- Page break -->

discusses a reduction to two years. This is exactly the kind of rule that should be re-verified immediately
before publication. 63


Do not over-generalise cantonal document lists into nationwide law. Zurich and Geneva are useful and
detailed, but they are practice notes. They are strongest as evidence of what authorities commonly
request in real files: birth certificates, custody orders, parental-authority evidence, written consent
where relevant, lease documents, salary slips, financial records, translations and supporting civil-status
documents. 64


Do not describe B-permit or F-permit child reunification in unconditional “right to reunification”
language. Official materials support a more careful formulation: the route exists and is handled
operationally, but the strength of the legal position is not identical across sponsor categories, and the F-
permit route is expressly discretionary. 65


Source list
The principal federal source used for the ordinary immigration route was the State Secretariat for
Migration, Weisungen und Erläuterungen I. Ausländerbereich, chapter 6 “Familiennachzug”, status 1
January 2026. It was used for the structure of child cases, partial-family reunification, time limits, late
applications, child hearings and the interaction between child welfare and anti-abuse concerns. 48


For procedural handling in third-country cases, the key federal administrative document was SEM’s
Weisung: Familiennachzugsverfahren von Drittstaatsangehörigen dated 4 December 2025, used for the
roles of the Swiss representation and the canton, essential documents, blockades caused by unavailable
documents, voluntary document checks, local enquiries and DNA. 66


For EU/EFTA and AFMP material, the pack relied on SEM’s federal fact sheet Family reunification and the
2026 Weisungen VFP. These were used for the under-21/dependency distinction, derivation from the
sponsor’s own right of residence, accommodation, finances, student exceptions and proof of
dependency. 46


For F-permit reunification and asylum-law reunification, the pack relied on the SEM asylum handbook:
Article F7 on family reunification for provisionally admitted persons and provisionally admitted
refugees, and Article F3 on family asylum / asylum-law family reunification. These were used for the
three-year waiting period, under-18 rule, discretionary nature of the F route, and the pre-flight family-
unity requirement in Article 51 AsylG cases. 67


For concise federal operational clarifications, the pack used SEM’s public FAQ on residence and
integration criteria, especially for the deadlines, the absence of ordinary language requirements for
children, and the current rule stated for provisionally admitted sponsors. 40


For cantonal evidence and procedure, the pack used Zurich’s migration practice note and public
application pages, Geneva’s OCPM family-reunification pages for Swiss, non-EU/EFTA, EU/EFTA and F-
permit sponsors, and Ticino’s migration and civil-status pages. Those sources were used only as
cantonal practice notes and not as substitutes for federal law. 68
