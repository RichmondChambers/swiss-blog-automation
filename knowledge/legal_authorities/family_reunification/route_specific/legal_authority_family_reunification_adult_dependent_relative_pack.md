Executive legal summary
A. Executive legal summary. Switzerland does not operate a broad, self-standing “adult dependent
relative” route comparable to the category used in some other jurisdictions. In current Swiss law, adult
dependency-based admissions arise mainly through three channels: first, the EU/EFTA free movement
regime, which recognises certain dependent ascendants and dependent descendants; secondly, the
parallel Swiss-national route in Article 42 paragraph 2 FNIA, but only where the foreign family
member already holds a durable or settlement-type residence permit from an EU/EFTA state covered by
free movement; and thirdly, exceptional Article 8 ECHR cases where a relationship outside the
ordinary core family is protected because of a special dependency relationship. Outside those
channels, the ordinary statutory family-reunification rules are built primarily around spouses or
partners and minor children. 1


For third-country sponsors under the ordinary FNIA framework, adult parents, grandparents, adult
children and other adult relatives do not enjoy a general entitlement-based reunification route. The
ordinary Swiss, C-permit, B-permit, L-permit and F-permit provisions centre on spouses and unmarried
children under 18, with the B-, L- and F-permit routes either discretionary or otherwise more restricted
than the core entitlement routes. The Swiss-national exception in Article 42 paragraph 2 is real, but
narrow: it is tied to the foreign family member’s existing residence status in an EU/EFTA state and
should not be confused with a general adult-dependent-relative category. 2


When practitioners turn to Article 8 ECHR, Swiss official guidance is explicit that there is no
unconditional right to admission. Outside the core family, Article 8 is engaged only where there is an
actual dependency relationship, usually because of physical or mental disability or serious illness
requiring permanent care, and the necessary care must be indispensably provided by the relative in
Switzerland. The same official guidance states that relations between adult children and parents
normally fall outside Article 8, and Federal Supreme Court authority indicates that strong emotional
ties alone or financial support alone are insufficient to establish the required special dependency. 3


The practical consequence is that future drafting for this topic should proceed on the basis that most
Swiss “adult dependent relative” arguments are either support-based free movement cases or
exceptional human-rights cases, rather than ordinary statutory family reunification. That distinction
should be stated plainly in internal content. 4


Structural overview of dependency-based arguments
B. Structural overview of dependency-based family-reunification arguments. The strongest and
clearest dependency-based route is the free movement route. SEM’s current AFMP factsheet states






<!-- Page break -->

that an EU/EFTA national with residence rights in Switzerland may be accompanied by a spouse,
descendants under 21 or dependent descendants, and dependent ascendants of the sponsor or
spouse, irrespective of those family members’ nationality. SEM also states that students are a special
case: for them, family reunification is limited to the spouse and dependent children. Suitable housing is
required in all cases; for workers the right is not tied to financial means, whereas for self-employed or
economically inactive sponsors sufficient means are required. 5


A second, narrower dependency route exists for Swiss nationals under Article 42 paragraph 2 FNIA.
The statutory text extends family reunification beyond spouse and minor children where the foreign
family member already holds a qualifying residence status from a state linked by a free-movement
agreement, covering spouses, descendants under 21 or dependants, and dependent ascendants. Article
47 paragraph 2 FNIA removes the usual family-reunification deadlines for this category. Zurich’s
published cantonal practice note confirms that this route is treated as a specific special channel and
distinguishes it from the broader AFMP jurisprudence. 6


The third channel is the exceptional Article 8 ECHR route. SEM’s immigration directives explain that
Article 8 can protect relationships outside the core family only where the person is in a real dependency
relationship with a sponsor enjoying a settled right of residence in Switzerland, and only after a
broader balancing exercise between private and public interests. The directives also say that if the
sponsor can reasonably live abroad with the applicant, Article 8 is normally not engaged; similarly,
Article 8 does not create a right where the proposed move serves essentially economic advancement or
causes a further splitting of the family. 7


A fourth and separate category comprises routes that should not be drafted as adult-dependent-
relative routes even though they are sometimes raised in “family” contexts. These include: ordinary
FNIA spouse/child routes under Articles 42 to 45; the F-permit route under Article 85c, which is limited
to spouse and unmarried children under 18 and is discretionary; the family asylum mechanism for
recognised refugees with asylum under Article 51 AsylA, which SEM itself treats as a distinct regime; and
discretionary cohabiting partner permissions, which cantonal guidance in Geneva expressly describes
as carrying no right to admission. 8

D. Primary legal texts. The core primary texts for this topic are the Foreign Nationals and
Integration Act and, within it, especially Articles 42 to 45, 47, 49, 49a, 51 and 85c. For adult
dependency arguments, the key provisions are Article 42(2) for Swiss sponsors with qualifying EU/EFTA-
based family members, Article 47(2) on the absence of normal deadlines for that category, and Article
85c for the separate F-permit regime. Articles 43 and 44 remain important mainly because they show
what the ordinary statutory routes do not cover. 19


The other key primary instrument is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons, particularly its
family-reunification rules as implemented in Swiss practice. SEM’s current AFMP factsheet summarises
the operative categories and conditions: dependent ascendants and certain dependent descendants
are expressly included, suitable housing is required, and support-based proof from the home or origin
authorities is expected for ascendants and adult descendants relying on dependency. 20


At the human-rights level, the governing legal basis is Article 8 ECHR, as interpreted in Swiss
immigration practice and case law. SEM’s directives treat Article 8 as a threshold-and-proportionality
framework rather than a free-standing family-reunification code: there must be a real family
relationship, a sponsor with settled residence rights, and a balancing exercise in which private interests
outweigh public interests. 21


A drafting point on source status matters. The legally binding Swiss federal texts are in German, French
and Italian. The English FNIA version used here is expressly identified as an informational translation
with no legal force. 22


Official guidance and cantonal practice
E. Official guidance / administrative materials. The most important federal administrative source is
SEM’s Directives and Explanatory Notes I. Foreign Nationals, especially section 6.17 on Article 8
ECHR, because this is where SEM states the official threshold for dependency outside the core family,
the settled-status requirement for the sponsor, and the main negative propositions: no unconditional
right, no Article 8 claim for merely economic migration, and no Article 8 claim where the family can
reasonably live abroad or the application would split the family further. 23


For the free-movement side, SEM’s current AFMP family-reunification factsheet is the key operational
source. It sets out the family categories, explains the housing and resource rules, confirms that work-






<!-- Page break -->

authorisation is generally available to accompanying spouses and younger/dependent children but not
to ascendants admitted under this route, and specifies the documentary expectations for dependency.




For provisionally admitted persons, the most useful federal materials are the SEM asylum manual
article F7 and SEM’s December 2025 procedural directive on family reunification by third-country
nationals. Together they make clear that Article 85c is a discretionary route, distinguish it from the
Article 51 AsylA family-asylum route for recognised refugees with asylum, explain the respective
competences of the cantons and SEM, and describe evidential possibilities including document checks
and, where necessary, DNA testing or other proof of identity and kinship. 24


SEM’s public FAQ on residence and integration criteria and the official ch.ch page are also useful for
front-end drafting controls. They repeat the key distinction between ordinary categories and the AFMP/
Swiss special categories, explain the ordinary deadlines, and flag the broad housing, means and
language conditions that frequently drive decisions in standard route cases. 25


F. Cantonal practice notes. The clearest cantonal authority located for dependency-based adult-
relative work is Zurich’s published Migrationsamt Familiennachzug practice note. It expressly explains
Article 42(2) FNIA for Swiss nationals whose foreign family members hold qualifying EU/EFTA residence
status; states that no Article 47 deadlines apply in that category; sets out the evidential focus on prior
support, need, and official home/origin-state certificates; and records Zurich’s published practice that
support may be financial or in kind, need not amount to total maintenance, but must be significant and
ongoing. 26


Geneva’s official OCPM pages are useful as practical cross-checks because they present the categories
separately by sponsor status. For EU/EFTA nationals, Geneva states that dependent ascendants and
dependent adult descendants are within the AFMP route and that their poverty must be proved before
entry. For Swiss nationals, Geneva likewise distinguishes between family members resident in an EU/
EFTA state with a durable residence permit and family members outside the EU/EFTA. For third-country
sponsors and F-permit holders, Geneva’s public guidance is limited to spouse and minor children. 27


Basel-Stadt’s public page is useful not because it develops a dependency doctrine, but because it
illustrates the ordinary cantonal front-end framing: spouse or registered partner and unmarried
children under 18, with general reference to AFMP rules for EU/EFTA cases. That is a practical reminder
that adult-dependent-relative arguments will usually need to be framed as an exception to standard
local intake assumptions, not as a routine family-reunification application. 28


Practical legal analysis
G. Practical legal analysis. The central drafting distinction is between support-based dependency and
care-based dependency. Under the AFMP and Article 42(2) FNIA, dependency is fundamentally about
maintenance: the relevant family member must be a dependant, and SEM expects documentary proof
from the home or origin authorities showing either that support is already being provided or that the
parties have been living together. Zurich’s practice note sharpens this by stating that the support must
pre-date the application, the need must be of some seriousness, and regular support covering a non-
negligible part of living costs can suffice; the support may also be in kind, such as board and lodging.
Federal Supreme Court authority in BGE 135 II 369, as reflected in the official case summary, treats the
assessment as one of the ascendant’s needs and support in light of present living conditions in
Switzerland. 29






<!-- Page break -->

By contrast, the Article 8 ECHR route is not about ordinary maintenance dependence. SEM’s directives
place the threshold much higher: the applicant must normally be outside the core family but dependent
because of bodily or mental invalidity or serious illness requiring permanent care, and that care must
be indispensably supplied by the relative in Switzerland. Read conservatively, this means that a file
must do more than prove affection, even very strong affection, or even regular remittances. It must
show a care relationship that is both medically serious and practically irreplaceable. 16


On evidence, official materials point in four directions. First, prove the family link by civil-status
documents. Secondly, prove the dependency itself: for AFMP-type cases, that means support records,
home/origin-state certificates, and evidence of prior co-residence where relevant; for F-permit and
other sensitive cases, SEM materials also contemplate broader proof such as identity documents, family
photographs and DNA analysis where necessary. Thirdly, prove the sponsor’s status and capacity:
residence status, housing, salary slips or other means evidence, and any required language materials.
Fourthly, in Article 8 care cases, prove the medical and practical care need with detailed medical
material and a concrete account of who gives care, what tasks are performed, and why they cannot
realistically be replicated elsewhere. 30


The relationship between dependency and the availability of care or support in the country of origin
or residence is not spelled out in a single federal checklist for adult relatives, but it follows from the
official standard. If the necessary care must be indispensably provided by the Swiss-based relative, a
claimant will ordinarily need to show why equivalent care is not realistically available through other
relatives, hired carers, local institutions, or other arrangements abroad. That is an inference from SEM’s
dependency test and the refusal-side case law holding that emotional closeness or financial help alone
is insufficient. 16


The most common legally identifiable weaknesses in these files are therefore predictable. They include:
trying to use ordinary spouse/minor-child provisions for adult relatives; relying on emotional closeness
without evidencing legal dependency; showing remittances but not real need; failing to prove that
support existed before the application; filing late ordinary applications without “important family
reasons”; presenting applications that appear aimed at education, work or general improvement of
living conditions; and, in Article 8 cases, failing to show that the sponsor has a sufficiently settled
residence right in Switzerland. Abuse or fraudulent use of family rules remains a separate refusal
ground. 31


A further practical point concerns sponsor status changes. In the F-permit context, SEM’s December
2025 procedural directive states that if the sponsor obtains a hardship-based residence permit while an
Article 85c case is pending, the Article 85c basis falls away and the matter passes to the cantonal
authorities to assess under Article 44 FNIA instead. That can materially weaken the legal footing of a
pending family-reunification case. 32


Drafting cautions and source list
H. Drafting cautions. Do not describe Switzerland as having a general “adult dependent relative route”.
The legally accurate formulation is that Swiss law contains: a free-movement dependency route for EU/
EFTA sponsors; a Swiss-national special provision tied to qualifying EU/EFTA residence status of the
family member; and a narrow Article 8 ECHR exception for special-dependency cases. Everything else
should be drafted as outside the ordinary adult-dependent framework unless a separate legal basis is
expressly identified. 4






<!-- Page break -->

Do not collapse AFMP support-dependency and Article 8 care-dependency into one test. Under
AFMP / Article 42(2), the focus is dependency by support and need. Under Article 8, the focus is
exceptional dependency caused by illness or disability and indispensable care from the Swiss-based
relative. These are different legal ideas and require different evidence bundles. 33


Do not present adult parent or adult child cases for third-country sponsors under Articles 43 to 45 FNIA
as if they were equivalent to spouse or minor-child applications. Official public guidance from Geneva
and Basel-Stadt, together with the statutory text, all point the other way. Adult-relative arguments in
such cases usually need either a free-movement hook or an exceptional Article 8 analysis. 34


Do not assume that future AFMP reforms are already operative. SEM’s 2026 material explains that, in
the course of updating the bilateral framework, family reunification under the AFMP is expected to be
broadened to include additional categories such as certain members of the same household, persons
requiring care and durable partners; but that is described as an update to the current agreement, not
the current law as such. Internal content should therefore keep current law and prospective reform
strictly separate. 35


Do not rely on English statutory wording without caveat. The English FNIA text used for convenience is
expressly informational and has no legal force; the binding Swiss texts are in German, French and
Italian. 22


Do not rebrand discretionary cohabiting-partner admissions as family-reunification rights. Geneva’s
official page says expressly that there is no right to such a permit, and SEM’s Article 8 guidance
recognises a legal claim for childless cohabitants only where the relationship is long-standing and
marriage is imminent. 18
