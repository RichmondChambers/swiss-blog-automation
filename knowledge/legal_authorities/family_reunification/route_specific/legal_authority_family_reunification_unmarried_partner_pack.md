Executive legal summary and structural overview

A Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law does not treat unmarried partners as the equivalent of spouses for ordinary
family reunification. The formal family reunification provisions under the AIG/LEI, as described by SEM,
are framed around spouses / registered partners and children, with sponsor-status distinctions:
Articles 42 and 43 AIG create an entitlement for the spouses and minor children of Swiss citizens and C-
permit holders, while Articles 44 and 45 AIG are discretionary (“Kann-Bestimmungen”) for the spouses
and minor children of B- and L-permit holders. None of those ordinary routes is framed as a general
unmarried-partner entitlement. 2


The principal federal route expressly described for unmarried partners is instead a hardship-style
derogation from the ordinary admission rules: SEM states that the cohabiting partners of Swiss
citizens, or of foreign nationals holding a B or C permit, may receive a residence permit under Article
30(1)(b) AIG in conjunction with Article 31 OASA/VZAE if cumulative conditions are met. For childless
couples, SEM requires a stable, long-term relationship, additional indicators of intensity, absence of
public-order issues, and intended cohabitation in Switzerland; for couples with children, SEM focuses on
co-residence, joint care and maintenance of the children, and public-order compliance. This is a
discretionary derogation, not an ordinary family reunification right. 3


A narrower human-rights backstop exists under Article 8 ECHR as applied in SEM guidance and
Federal Supreme Court case law, but it is materially stricter than the administrative hardship route. SEM
states that, for a childless unmarried relationship, an Article 8 ECHR right to family reunification exists
only where the partnership is long-lasting and stable and marriage is imminently planned; if removal
has already been ordered, Swiss practice requires proof of a settled marriage-like relationship or an
immediately forthcoming marriage / registered partnership. SEM also states that Article 8 protection
further requires the sponsor in Switzerland to hold a secure residence status. 4


Since 1 January 2025, SEM’s directives state that the post-breakdown protection in Article 50 AIG
applies not only to spouses and children in the ordinary family-reunification categories, but also to
unmarried partners who had already been admitted under Article 30(1)(b) AIG to stay with their
cohabiting partner. SEM further explains that the three-year period for those concubinage cases begins
with the Swiss filing / Swiss entry logic described in the directives; cohabitation abroad is relevant to the
initial assessment, but it does not itself count towards the three-year Swiss period. Initial admission
therefore remains discretionary even though continuation rights after breakdown are now expressly
addressed. 5

In practice, unmarried-partner cases are highly cantonal and evidence-heavy. Geneva, Vaud and
Zurich all publish official guidance showing that the route is handled as exceptional, discretionary and
proof-intensive. Zurich adopts an especially strict public-facing practice, requiring a relationship lived in
a marriage-like way for at least three years, prior common household, and close ties such as children
or reciprocal responsibility; Vaud and Geneva require extensive documentary proof and expressly state
that there is no right to the permit. 6


B Structural overview of unmarried partner routes or arguments

For internal drafting purposes, Swiss unmarried-partner cases should be analysed in four distinct
boxes, not blurred together. The first box is the ordinary family reunification chapter of the AIG,
which is for spouses / registered partners and children. The second is the Article 30(1)(b) AIG + Article
31 OASA discretionary hardship route for cohabiting partners, described by SEM under the heading
on “Konkubinatspaare”. The third is the Article 8 ECHR argument, which is narrower and generally
strongest where there are children or an immediately forthcoming marriage. The fourth is residence
planning outside family reunification, such as a permit to prepare marriage, or in some EU/EFTA
contexts a separate residence basis not equivalent to partner reunification.     7




That distinction matters because the legal consequences differ sharply. An ordinary spouse / registered-
partner case can involve a legal entitlement, especially under Articles 42 and 43 AIG or under the AFMP/
FZA for spouses / registered partners of EU/EFTA citizens. By contrast, a concubinage case under Article
30(1)(b) AIG is a derogation from the admission conditions; it depends on the exercise of discretion and,
in visible cantonal practice, often on SEM approval. Article 8 ECHR should be presented as a restrictive
fallback, not as a general unmarried-partner route. 8

Primary legal texts and official guidance

D Primary legal texts

For internal drafting, the most relevant black-letter provisions are these. Articles 42–45 AIG define the
ordinary family reunification architecture by sponsor status and, as summarised by SEM, cover foreign
spouses / registered partners and minor children of Swiss, C-, B- and L-permit sponsors respectively.
Article 52 AIG preserves equal treatment for existing registered partnerships. Article 30(1)(b) AIG
together with Article 31 OASA/VZAE supplies the derogation / hardship basis used by SEM for
unmarried-partner admission. Article 50 AIG, now including the extension discussed by SEM for
concubins admitted under Article 30(1)(b), governs retention after family or partnership breakdown.
Article 17 AIG governs the need to await the decision abroad where a temporary entrant later seeks
durable stay. Article 85c AIG governs family reunification for temporarily admitted persons, but official
SEM material describes that provision in spouse-and-children terms, not as a concubinage route. 18


The key international overlay is Article 8 ECHR, as interpreted in SEM’s family-life guidance and the
cited Federal Supreme Court case law. In the unmarried-partner context, SEM’s current guidance
sharply limits when Article 8 can produce a legal entitlement and requires a secure-status sponsor plus
a strong factual family-life case. For EU/EFTA sponsors, the relevant treaty text in practice is Annex I
AFMP/FZA, whose official SEM materials define “family members” as spouses / registered partners and
qualifying descendants / ascendants, not unregistered partners. 19


A separate but adjacent text is the federal basis for stay to prepare marriage. SEM states that a limited
permit may be issued under Article 30(1)(b) AIG and Article 31 OASA to prepare marriage with a Swiss
citizen or a B/C-permit holder, but only where the civil-status office confirms that the marriage process
has begun and the marriage can be expected within a reasonable time; the later family-reunification
conditions must also be met. This is a distinct route and should not be conflated with an unmarried-
partner residence route. 20


E Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal administrative source is SEM’s Directives and Commentaries, Foreign
Nationals Area, especially the sections on 5.6.3 childless cohabiting couples, 5.6.4 cohabiting
couples with children, 5.6.5 stay to prepare marriage, 6.15.1 duration of marriage or cohabitation,
and 6.17.2.1.3 fiancés / concubinage under Article 8 ECHR. Those sections do most of the doctrinal
work for unmarried-partner cases in practice. They are current, official, and considerably more useful
for drafting than a purely abstract citation to Article 30 AIG. 21


SEM’s 4 December 2025 instruction on family reunification procedures for third-country nationals
is also important, because it sharply distinguishes the ordinary family-reunification procedure under






<!-- Page break -->

Articles 42–45 AIG from other categories, explains which authority receives the application, and
confirms that the long-stay family route involves a residence-permit component and, where needed, a
D-visa component. That instruction underscores why an unmarried-partner case under Article 30 AIG
should not be casually described as “family reunification” in the same sense as a spouse case. 9


On the EU/EFTA side, SEM’s public pages and fact sheets are useful negative authorities: they
consistently frame AFMP family members as the spouse / registered partner plus qualifying
descendants and ascendants, and they publish separate materials for persons residing in Switzerland
without gainful activity on the basis of sufficient means and insurance. That separation supports
careful drafting: where an unmarried EU/EFTA partner can live in Switzerland, the legal basis may be a
self-standing AFMP residence basis, not a derivative partner-based reunification right. 22


Finally, for post-breakdown cases, SEM’s directives now expressly state that Article 50 AIG applies to
unmarried partners already admitted under Article 30(1)(b) AIG, and they explain how the three-year
period is calculated. The same directives also set out the official approach to domestic-violence
evidence, including medical reports, police reports, specialist confirmations and judicial protection
measures, and they note that a criminal conviction is not indispensable. 23


Cantonal practice notes

F Cantonal practice notes

Vaud is the clearest cantonal source for third-country unmarried-partner cases. Its official page states
that residence with a concubin is governed by Article 30(1)(b) LEI and Article 31 OASA, that there is no
right to such a permit because it is a derogation from the admission conditions, and that if a foreign
union or PACS-type partnership is equivalent to a registered partnership, the case should instead be
handled under ordinary family reunification. Vaud also publishes a detailed evidence list: passport and
visa copy, the Swiss partner’s ID/residence title, a letter explaining the need for the stay, proof of a
stable pre-existing relationship, financial evidence, the Swiss partner’s lease, and usually a foreign
criminal-record extract. For opposite-sex concubins, Vaud expressly asks for the reasons why marriage
is not envisaged. It also states that, if accepted by the cantonal population service, the file is submitted
to SEM for approval because the route is a derogation. 24


Geneva likewise publishes a dedicated “cohabiting partner” route for a non-EU/EFTA foreign national
joining a Swiss or foreign partner holding a valid B or C permit. Geneva states that there is no right to
the permit. For childless couples it requires a relationship that is stable and of some duration, proof of
intensity such as a cohabitation agreement, no public-order issues, a future common household in
Geneva, and sufficient means to avoid social assistance. Geneva also lists evidential examples in
unusually concrete terms: a motivation letter, proof of finances, the Swiss/Geneva partner’s lease and
status document, any cohabitation agreement, and proof of a stable lasting relationship such as
photos, letters from relatives or friends, a joint lease or joint bank account. Visa-subject applicants must
apply at the Swiss representation abroad and wait abroad for the decision. 25


Zurich publishes the strictest visible public-facing formulation among the cantonal materials reviewed.
Its public guidance states that a legal claim for cohabiting couples arises only where the relationship
has long been lived in a marriage-like way or where there are concrete clues of an immediately
forthcoming wedding. It then states that admission of cohabiting partners is possible only where there
is a settled partnership of at least three years, the couple has already lived together in a common
household, and there is a close bond such as children or reciprocal responsibility. Zurich therefore
appears to operationalise the federal “stable / durable / intense relationship” standard with a much






<!-- Page break -->

stricter front-end screen than SEM’s general directives. That should be treated as cantonal practice,
not as federal black-letter law. 26


Vaud’s EU/EFTA page is noteworthy because it maintains a separate “séjour auprès du concubin” page
under the EU/FZA section and cites Article 24 of Annex I AFMP/FZA, while requiring proof of identity,
lease, financial means or undertaking, and—if work will be taken up simultaneously—the relevant EU/
EFTA residence/employment form. Because the federal SEM materials reviewed do not present this as a
general unmarried-partner family reunification right, the safer internal reading is that this is a cantonal
residence-handling route in the AFMP context, not proof of a universal federal unmarried-partner
right. 27


Ticino is useful as a negative comparator. Its public page for third-country family reunification refers to
parents, spouses and registered partners, requires the application for entry authorisation to be filed via
the competent Swiss representation abroad, and says the family member must wait abroad for the
outcome, subject only to stated exceptions. The page does not identify a standard unmarried-partner
category. 28


Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

G Practical legal analysis

The central analytical distinction is between formal entitlement and discretionary tolerance. A
spouse or recognised registered partner may fit into a clearly codified family-reunification category with
a legal entitlement or, depending on the sponsor’s status, at least a codified discretionary framework.
An unmarried partner ordinarily does not. The partner instead depends on a hardship-type derogation
under Article 30(1)(b) AIG as described by SEM, or on a narrow Article 8 ECHR argument. Future drafting
should therefore describe the unmarried route as exceptional, discretionary and fact-sensitive, not
as a standard family-reunification channel. 29


The sponsor’s status matters materially. SEM’s concubinage sections for initial admission are drafted
for partners of Swiss citizens or holders of B or C permits. The ordinary family-reunification pages
separately cover L-permit holders for spouses and children, but the SEM concubinage guidance does
not mirror that for unmarried partners, and the F-permit family-reunification materials likewise speak in
spouse-and-child terms. For EU/EFTA sponsors, AFMP family reunification reaches the spouse /
registered partner, not the unregistered partner. A case involving an L sponsor, an F sponsor, or an EU/
EFTA sponsor should therefore be analysed with special caution and not by assumption. 30


The quality of the relationship evidence is decisive. SEM’s federal directives for childless cohabiting
couples emphasise a “settled and durable” relationship and require extra indicators of intensity,
expressly mentioning contractual assumption of reciprocal support obligations, such as a cohabitation
agreement, and the foreign partner’s willingness and ability to integrate. Geneva and Vaud then make
that evidential logic concrete by asking for cohabitation agreements, shared finances, joint leases,
photographs, letters, witness-type statements, motivation letters, and other proof of a stable pre-
existing relationship. Zurich’s public practice goes further by requiring a prior common household and
at least three years of settled partnership. 31


The strongest fact patterns are therefore likely to be those with actual cohabitation history, shared
children, shared finances, reciprocal support obligations, credible long-term plans, and a sponsor with
secure residence rights. The weakest fact patterns are likely to be those with little or no proved
common life, mostly remote contact, lack of financial organisation, unexplained refusal to marry where






<!-- Page break -->

marriage is realistically available, weak sponsor status, or the obvious possibility of maintaining the
relationship abroad or through visa-free visits. That last point matters because SEM’s directives
expressly make “it is unreasonable to expect the relationship to be maintained abroad and/or during
permit-free stays” part of the childless-concubinage analysis. 32


Applying from abroad is usually the safer procedural sequence. SEM’s general guidance states that
long-term residence without gainful activity must be applied for before entry; if a person lawfully enters
for a temporary stay and then applies for durable residence, the canton may require the person to
await the decision abroad unless the admission conditions are obviously met. Geneva’s cohabiting-
partner page is explicit that visa-subject applicants apply at the Swiss representation abroad and wait
abroad for the decision. In an unmarried-partner case, where the merits are discretionary rather than
clearly “obvious”, the practical risk of being told to wait abroad is higher than in a straightforward
entitlement case. 17


One important planning point is future marriage. Swiss law does recognise a distinct temporary route
to prepare a marriage, but SEM and Zurich both make clear that this is not a device to test cohabitation
in Switzerland. The marriage process must already be initiated, the civil-status office confirmation must
exist before entry, and the marriage must be expected within a reasonable time. Drafting should
therefore keep “unmarried partner residence” and “stay to prepare marriage” separate. 20


For post-breakdown cases, the law is now better than it used to be for unmarried partners who
successfully entered via Article 30(1)(b) AIG. SEM states that Article 50 AIG now applies to them as well.
But the continuation route remains structured by Swiss timing and by the ordinary Article 50 logic: the
three-year period is a Swiss period, not an overseas one; and where continuation is sought because of
major personal reasons such as domestic violence, the federal guidance still requires serious proof.
SEM’s current materials accept a broad range of evidence and state that a criminal conviction is not
indispensable, but they are also clear that mere relationship disappointment or lower-level conflict is
not enough. 23


H Drafting cautions

Use the following cautions in any downstream content.


Do not write that Switzerland has an “unmarried partner family reunification right” comparable to the
spouse route. The safer formulation is that Swiss law and SEM practice recognise a limited,
discretionary residence possibility for certain cohabiting partners under Article 30(1)(b) AIG and
Article 31 OASA, with a narrower human-rights backstop under Article 8 ECHR in exceptional cases. 33


Do not collapse all sponsor categories together. For initial concubinage admission, the federal SEM
guidance reviewed is drafted for Swiss, B-permit and C-permit sponsors. AFMP family reunification
covers spouses / registered partners, not ordinary cohabitants; and the official F-permit family-
reunification materials are spouse-and-child materials. 34


Do not treat a foreign PACS or similar status as automatically “unmarried”. If the foreign status is
recognised as equivalent to a registered partnership or marriage, the case can move into the ordinary
family-reunification framework; if not, it remains an unmarried-partner case. 35


Do not present the Article 8 ECHR route as broad. For a childless unmarried couple, SEM’s current
guidance requires a long-standing stable relationship and an imminently planned marriage; it is
materially narrower than the Article 30 discretionary route. 4






<!-- Page break -->

Do not imply that years of cohabitation abroad satisfy the Article 50 three-year threshold after
admission. SEM expressly says they do not count towards the Swiss three-year period, though they
remain relevant to the initial assessment of stability and intensity. 14


Do not understate cantonal variation. Zurich publicly applies a three-year / prior-cohabitation screen,
Vaud asks why marriage is not contemplated in opposite-sex cases, and Geneva specifies an evidence
package including shared finances, photos and letters. Any final drafting should therefore describe the
route as practice-sensitive and canton-dependent. 6


Do not assume an in-country switch is available merely because the person entered lawfully. SEM’s
general guidance allows the canton to require the applicant to wait abroad; Geneva’s cohabiting-partner
page expressly requires visa-subject applicants to apply abroad and wait abroad. 17


Open questions and limitations

This pack is high-confidence on the federal administrative architecture and on the cantonal practice
notes cited above. The area that remains most practice-sensitive is the exact handling of EU/EFTA
unmarried-partner residence planning outside formal family reunification, because the reviewed
cantonal Vaud page cites Article 24 Annex I AFMP, while the federal SEM materials reviewed do not
frame that as a general “partner reunification” right. The safer internal position is to treat such cases as
separate residence planning under AFMP conditions, not as ordinary unmarried-partner family
reunification. 36
