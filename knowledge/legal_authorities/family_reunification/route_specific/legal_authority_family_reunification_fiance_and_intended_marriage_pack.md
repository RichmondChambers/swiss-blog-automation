# Swiss legal authority pack on fiances, intended marriage, and post-marriage residence

Swiss legal authority pack on fiancés, intended
marriage, and post-marriage residence
Summary and route architecture

A Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not create a free-standing statutory “fiancé visa” or “intended spouse” family-
reunification right equivalent to the post-marriage rights found in the Foreign Nationals and Integration
Act. Before marriage, the framework is assembled from three bodies of law and practice: civil-status law
on the marriage preparation procedure, entry/visa law, and foreign-national stay rules pending a permit
decision. The Civil Code requires a marriage preparation procedure, allows refusal where the marriage
is obviously abusive or not freely intended, requires non-Swiss fiancés to prove lawful stay during the
preparation procedure, and provides that the marriage may be celebrated within three months after
positive completion of that procedure. The spouse-based family-reunification provisions, by contrast,
begin only once a marriage exists. 1


The most conservative legal characterisation is therefore this: a pre-marriage case is not a spouse
family-reunification case yet. It is a marriage-planning and entry/stay case which may, depending on
the facts, lead into a post-marriage residence application. SEM’s current directives recognise a
temporary stay arrangement for preparation of marriage in some cases, including for persons
otherwise in an irregular situation, but only where there are no indications of abuse and it is already
clearly apparent that the applicant would obviously satisfy the admission conditions after marriage.
SEM also states that this stay is generally regularised by a short-stay permit without gainful
employment. 2


Swiss law is also careful about sequence. A long stay in Switzerland is ordinarily permit-based and, if
applied for after entry following only a temporary lawful stay, the applicant must generally wait abroad
for the decision. Article 17 FNIA creates the key risk rule: someone who entered lawfully for a temporary
stay and then seeks a longer stay must wait for the decision abroad unless the canton considers that
the admission requirements are clearly fulfilled. SEM’s directives sharpen this further for marriage
cases: marriage concluded during a visit does not by itself extinguish the eventual family-reunification
claim, but if no positive first-instance permit decision is issued within the lawful visitor stay, the person
must normally await the outcome abroad unless Article 17 paragraph 2 is satisfied with high probability.




For future content, the legally safe position is that Swiss practice recognises planning for marriage in
Switzerland, including visa handling and, in some cases, a temporary stay to prepare the marriage; but
any statement that a visitor entry or Schengen stay can simply be “converted” into residence after
marriage is too broad unless the official sources and the case facts clearly support Article 17 paragraph
2 treatment. 4


B Structural overview of fiancé and intended-marriage route planning

The federal civil-status materials describe marriage in Switzerland as a two-step process: first the
marriage preparation procedure, then the ceremony itself. If one fiancé is domiciled in Switzerland, the





<!-- Page break -->

civil-status office at that domicile is competent for the preparation procedure; if both are abroad, the
civil-status office responsible for the planned ceremony is competent, and the documents may also be
channelled through the competent Swiss representation abroad. The parties must personally sign the
declaration on the marriage conditions, including abroad before the Swiss representation where
relevant. 5


For foreign nationals, the preparation stage carries an immigration consequence of its own: a
document proving the legality of stay in Switzerland up to the probable date of the ceremony must be
produced. The BJ/OFEC memento states this expressly, and SEM’s AIG directives explain that lawful stay
can consist, depending on the case, of a valid Swiss permit, a lawful permit-free stay, a valid visa or
Schengen residence title permitting short entry, or a still-running departure period. SEM then adds that
persons unlawfully present who wish to marry may seek a temporary residence authorisation to
prepare the marriage, but only under the restrictive conditions already noted. 6


Once the preparation procedure is successfully completed, the marriage must be celebrated within
three months. The marriage can be celebrated in the chosen civil-status district, and if the preparation
occurred elsewhere an authorisation to celebrate must be presented. These points appear both in the
Civil Code and in federal/cantonal civil-status guidance. 7


The residence step after marriage is separate. SEM’s 2025 directive on family reunification for third-
country nationals states that family reunification under Articles 42 to 45 FNIA is a long-stay procedure
with two components: the residence authorisation and the entry visa. It says that, in principle, the
procedure starts with a D-visa application at the competent Swiss representation abroad in the district
of the applicant’s lawful domicile; the representation conducts a preliminary review and forwards the
file to the competent cantonal migration authority, which decides the residence question, subject to any
SEM approval reserve. 8


A practical planning distinction therefore matters. If the foreign national intends only a short
attendance in Switzerland for the marriage and no immediate residence, the case remains within short-
stay entry law and civil-status formalities. If, however, the plan is to take up residence immediately
after the marriage, official FDFA guidance says that the visa and residence-permit application may be
submitted as part of the marriage preparation procedure, with the cantonal migration authority
deciding the residence aspect. Official FDFA visa guidance also distinguishes Schengen C visas for stays
up to 90 days from national D visas for stays of more than 90 days; long-stay D visas are subject to
cantonal migration authorisation. 9

Legislative framework

D Primary legal texts

The Swiss Civil Code is the starting point for intended-marriage cases. Article 97a empowers the civil-
status officer not to proceed where one fiancé obviously does not want a genuine marital community
but wishes to circumvent immigration law. Article 98 requires the marriage-preparation application,
personal appearance, documentary proof of identity and legal conditions, and proof of lawful stay for
non-Swiss fiancés. Article 99 requires the office to verify the application, identity, and marriage






<!-- Page break -->

conditions, including the absence of obvious lack of free will. Article 100 gives a three-month window
for the ceremony after completion of the preparation procedure. Article 105 treats sham or non-free
marriages as annulment grounds. 18


The FNIA supplies the core immigration logic. Article 10 states that no permit is needed for a stay
without gainful employment of up to three months, but longer stays require a permit in advance,
subject to Article 17 paragraph 2. Article 17 then provides the central sequencing rule: a person who
entered lawfully for a temporary stay and later seeks a longer stay must wait abroad for the decision
unless the canton decides the admission requirements are clearly fulfilled. Articles 32 and 33 define
short-stay permits and residence permits. Articles 42 to 45 then govern post-marriage family
reunification by sponsor status. Article 49 provides the cohabitation exception where there is good
cause for separate living and the family household continues to exist. Article 58a contains the
integration criteria relevant to longer-term status. 19


Within those post-marriage articles, the legal strength differs materially. Under Article 42, the foreign
spouse of a Swiss national who lives with the Swiss national is entitled to a residence permit; after five
years of lawful uninterrupted stay, settlement becomes available if the integration criteria are met.
Under Article 43, the spouse of a C-permit holder also has a right, but subject to cohabitation, suitable
accommodation, absence of social-assistance dependence, language ability, and the supplementary-
benefits condition. Under Article 44, the spouse of a B-permit holder may be granted a permit on
similar conditions; it is a discretionary provision. Under Article 45, the spouse of an L-permit holder
may be granted a short-stay permit, again on a discretionary basis. The SEM 2025 directive expressly
confirms that Articles 42 and 43 confer a right, whereas Articles 44 and 45 remain potestative. 20


The OASA fills in procedural detail. Article 5 provides for an entry authorisation where a short-stay or
residence permit has been approved and the person is still abroad; if no visa is required, the authority
can issue an assurance of the permit. Article 6 explains when Article 17 paragraph 2 FNIA may be
treated as obviously satisfied, and then warns in paragraph 2 that steps such as starting marriage or
family-law proceedings, renting accommodation, acquiring property, or concluding an employment
contract do not by themselves create claims in the permit procedure. Articles 10 and 11 address
change of stay purpose and visa extension timing. Articles 73, 73a and related provisions govern
deadlines and language matters in family-reunification cases. Articles 75 and 76 define, respectively,
“important family reasons” for late child admission and examples of good cause for living separately.




The VEV separates short and long-stay entry. It defines the Schengen C visa for short stays and the
national D visa for longer stays. It states that a longer stay requires, in addition to general border
conditions, a D visa where required and satisfaction of the immigration admission conditions for the
proposed purpose. It also provides that long-stay visas are issued for residence in Switzerland under
the permit rules, and that Swiss representations act in the name of the competent authorities, while
cantonal migration authorities are competent whenever the stay itself is to be authorised by the canton.




For EU/EFTA sponsors, the separate AFMP regime applies. SEM’s official factsheet states that an EU/
EFTA national who has acquired a right to stay in Switzerland may be joined by a spouse and certain
descendants and ascendants, regardless of the family member’s nationality, subject to suitable
accommodation and documentary proof of family relationship; the family member’s right is derivative
from the sponsor’s own residence right. This is a spouse/family-member regime, not a fiancé regime.

Official guidance and cantonal practice

E Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important administrative source for this topic is the SEM AIG directives, especially the sham-
marriage and marriage-preparation sections. These directives say expressly that from the opening of
the marriage-preparation procedure until the ceremony, the persons concerned must prove lawful stay
in Switzerland. They then recognise a possibility of requesting a permit to prepare the marriage for
persons unlawfully present, but only if there are no signs that the marriage is being used abusively and
it is clearly recognisable that the person will obviously satisfy the admission requirements after the
marriage. SEM also states that, in general, this stay is regularised by a short-stay permit without gainful
employment. 14


Those same directives are the clearest official source on the visitor-to-residence risk. They say that
marriage during a visitor-visa stay does not make the eventual reunification claim disappear; however, if
no positive first-instance decision is issued during the period of lawful visitor stay, the person must wait
abroad for the immigration decision unless the admission conditions under Article 17 paragraph 2 FNIA
can be regarded as met with a high degree of probability. This is one of the most important caution
points for future drafting. 14


The SEM directives also describe inter-authority information flows in suspected sham-marriage cases.
Swiss representations must pass on relevant indications to cantonal authorities, civil-status authorities
must transmit indications of sham marriage to migration authorities, and migration authorities may
take immigration measures independently of the civil status of the marriage. The directives further say
that entry applications are in principle lodged abroad at the competent Swiss representation; the
representation performs a summary review and the canton decides whether a visa authorisation should
be granted. 24


The SEM directive of 4 December 2025 on family-reunification procedure for third-country
nationals is the cleanest administrative source on the post-marriage route. It states that Articles 42 to
45 FNIA family reunification involves both the permit application and the entry application; that it is, as
a rule, a long-stay procedure; that the person envisaging a long stay must hold a D visa if required and
satisfy the conditions of admission for the intended purpose; and that the procedure generally begins
with the D-visa filing at the competent Swiss representation abroad, which sends the file to the cantonal
migration authority. It also emphasises the applicant’s duty to prove the family relationship and co-
operate by providing exact information, necessary evidence and valid identity documentation. 25


Federal civil-status guidance is equally important. The BJ/OFEC memento on marriage celebrated in
Switzerland says that marriage in Switzerland occurs in two stages, identifies the competent civil-status
office depending on domicile, requires foreign fiancés to produce identity, domicile, civil-status and
nationality documentation plus proof of legality of stay in Switzerland up to the probable date of the
ceremony, and states that the marriage may take place within three months after the positive decision
on the preparation procedure. It also states that the civil-status officer will not process a request where
one fiancé manifestly does not seek a common life or where free will is absent. 26


The BJ FAQ on marriage in Switzerland remains useful for procedural routing. It states that, where
required, the entry visa for marriage in Switzerland is filed with the competent Swiss representation
abroad, which forwards it via SEM to the competent cantonal migration authority. The same FAQ also
states that a spouse’s residence in Switzerland under family reunification requires a permit decision by






<!-- Page break -->

the competent cantonal migration authority, with the family-reunification application capable of being
channelled through the Swiss representation abroad. 27


FDFA/EDA guidance complements the SEM materials. On the official page for marriage in Switzerland,
FDFA states that a visa may be required to enter Switzerland for marriage, and that where residence in
Switzerland is planned immediately after the marriage, the visa and residence-permit application can be
submitted within the marriage preparation procedure, with the cantonal migration authority deciding
the residence issue. General FDFA visa guidance separately distinguishes the Schengen C visa for up to
90 days and the national D visa for longer stays, and notes that long-stay D visas are subject to cantonal
migration authorisation. 28


F Cantonal practice notes

Public cantonal guidance should be treated as procedural evidence of practice, not as a substitute for
federal law. Geneva’s public OCPM page is particularly valuable because it names a limited-duration
residence permit for the purpose of marriage. It states that a foreign national may apply for such a
permit in order to prepare marriage to a Swiss citizen or to a foreign national with a durable Swiss
permit; that the application must be filed from abroad before entry with the competent Swiss
representation; and that the application should include, among other things, a civil-registry certificate
confirming that the preparatory formalities have been completed and that the marriage can be
celebrated within a reasonable time, evidence of financial means, and a copy of the future spouse’s
Geneva lease. Those points are highly useful as practice markers, although they should not be over-
generalised beyond Geneva without checking local requirements. 29


Ticino’s official civil-status guidance is useful on the lawful-stay proof issue. It states that a non-Swiss
person must prove the legality of stay in Switzerland at least until the celebration of the marriage, and
that the proof is issued by the migration office of the canton of domicile or the competent Swiss
representation abroad. Ticino’s separate guidance on the ceremony also repeats that the marriage
cannot be celebrated until the preparation procedure has been concluded and that the marriage must
be celebrated within three months after closure of that procedure. 30


Zurich’s public migration pages are not fiancé-specific, but they are useful for illustrating post-marriage
family-reunification filing practice. For Swiss sponsors, Zurich’s page frames the post-marriage
process as an entry application for spouses and children, with deadlines, apartment requirements,
abuse screening, and supporting evidence such as the family certificate, lease and income material. For
sponsors with B or C permits, Zurich publishes a more extensive documentary list including the
marriage certificate, housing evidence, salary documents, debt enforcement extract, health-insurance
costing, and—where applicable—proof of A1 oral German or course registration. These are cantonal
practice points and should not be elevated into federal-law propositions, but they demonstrate how
document-heavy post-marriage cases are in practice. 31


Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

G Practical legal analysis

For internal immigration-planning content, the safest doctrinal framing is to treat Swiss fiancé cases as
a sequencing problem, not as a single immigration category. The key legal questions are: who is the
sponsor; where are the parties located; do they intend only to marry or also to settle immediately in
Switzerland; does the foreign national need a short-stay visa, a long-stay visa, or no visa; can lawful stay






<!-- Page break -->

be proved throughout the marriage-preparation stage; and, after marriage, under which sponsor-status
article will residence be pursued. 32


In a marry-and-settle case involving a third-country national, official sources point towards planning
the residence component before or during the marriage preparation procedure rather than assuming
that a ceremony on visitor status can later be regularised in-country. That conclusion is supported by
three strands of material: the long-stay D-visa rules in the VEV; FDFA’s statement that visa and
residence-permit applications may be submitted as part of the marriage preparation procedure where
residence is planned right after marriage; and SEM’s Article 17 guidance requiring the applicant to wait
abroad absent clearly satisfied admission conditions. 33


In a marriage-only short-stay case, the legal analysis is different. A person may be able to enter for a
lawful short stay under general short-stay rules if nationality and visa requirements allow it, and the
civil-status authorities may process the marriage provided the lawful-stay proof can be shown for the
preparation period. But that is not the same as being pre-cleared for residence after the wedding.
Content should therefore distinguish sharply between “entry to marry” and “entry to reside after
marriage”. 34


The evidential burden in fiancé and intended-marriage cases is broader than simply proving a
relationship. Before marriage, the evidence usually includes identity documents, domicile material, civil-
status documents, where relevant recognition/capacity material for foreign fiancés abroad, and proof of
lawful stay until the expected ceremony. Once the case moves into residence or family reunification, the
evidence expands to include proof of the marriage, cohabitation or intended cohabitation, appropriate
housing where the statute requires it, financial material, language evidence where applicable, and the
absence of abuse indicators. SEM’s procedure directive also underlines a general duty to provide exact
information and necessary evidence, and in difficult civil-status-document cases the representations
may require additional document checks and may recommend further verification. 35


The genuine-marriage screen is central. Civil-status officers may refuse to enter into the preparation
procedure where there is an obvious sham or lack of free will, and the SEM directives enumerate
common indicators such as a marriage linked to a removal situation, very short acquaintance, serious
communication barriers, inconsistent statements, or payment for the marriage. Even where the civil-
status process is not stopped, indications of sham marriage can still feed into the migration decision
later. This means that internal content should never reduce the enquiry to “are the parties legally free to
marry”; it must also account for abuse screening by both civil-status and migration authorities. 36


The nationality and sponsor-status split must also be explicit. For EU/EFTA sponsors, the AFMP family-
reunification framework is separate and more favourable once the sponsor has a Swiss right of stay; it
covers spouses and certain descendants and ascendants, regardless of nationality, with a derivative
residence right for the family member. For Swiss, B, C and L sponsors under the FNIA, the legal position
varies article by article, and those differences matter immediately for both route strength and evidence.
In particular, Swiss and C-permit sponsor cases are rights-based once the statutory conditions are met,
whereas B-permit and L-permit spouse cases remain discretionary. 37


A final point concerns cohabitation. The spouse-based provisions are structured around living together,
and Article 49 FNIA only relaxes the requirement where there is good cause for separate living and the
family household continues to exist. Future content should therefore avoid presenting Swiss spouse
migration as a purely status-based route detached from actual living arrangements. 38

H Drafting cautions

Do not describe Swiss law as containing a single statutory “fiancé visa” or “fiancé residence permit”. Pre-
marriage handling exists, but it is assembled from civil-status law, visa law, and administrative stay
practice rather than from a dedicated family-reunification article. 39


Do not collapse three separate stages into one: entry, marriage preparation and ceremony, and
post-marriage residence. Each stage has its own authority structure and legal tests. 40


Do not imply that a person who enters as a visitor, or without a visa for a short stay, can automatically
remain in Switzerland and obtain residence after the wedding. Article 17 FNIA and SEM’s directives point
in the opposite direction unless the admission conditions are already clearly met. 41


Do not omit sponsor-status analysis. Content must identify whether the marriage will lead, after the
wedding, into Article 42, 43, 44 or 45 FNIA, or into the AFMP regime, because the right/discretion
distinction and evidential demands differ materially.      42




Do not understate lawful-stay proof during the preparation period. Federal and cantonal materials
expressly require proof of lawful stay until the expected ceremony, and civil-status authorities may
refuse to proceed if it is not produced. 43


Do not frame the case solely in terms of relationship genuineness. Swiss authorities also test free will,
anti-sham-marriage concerns, future cohabitation, housing, financial independence, and sometimes
language ability depending on the post-marriage route pursued. 44


Do not elevate cantonal checklists into universal substantive law. Use Geneva, Ticino and Zurich
materials as practice notes and filing illustrations, not as binding federal norms for all cantons. 45


When using English-language legislative wording internally, mark it as an informational translation
only. Refworld’s official English translations are useful, but the legally authoritative texts are the official
Swiss-language versions. 46
