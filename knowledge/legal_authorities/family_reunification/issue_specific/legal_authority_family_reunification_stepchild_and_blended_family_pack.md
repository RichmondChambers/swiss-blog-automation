# Swiss family reunification in stepchild and blended-family cases

Suggested filename:
legal_authority_family_reunification_stepchild_and_blended_family_pack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

## Executive legal summary

Swiss authority materials do not support a blanket statement that all stepchildren can be treated as
ordinary “children” for family reunification purposes. Under the ordinary domestic framework in the
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, the child route is sponsor-centred; in the Swissnational context, the SEM’s 2026 directives expressly state that article 42 paragraph 1 refers only to the
Swiss national’s own unmarried children, and they add that the residence right requires a genuinely
lived family relationship. By contrast, under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its
implementing guidance, the spouse’s children can qualify as family members, and the Federal Supreme
Court has confirmed that stepchildren of the spouse may fall within that regime irrespective of
nationality.
The decisive axis in difficult blended-family cases is therefore usually not the social label “stepchild”, but
the combination of: the applicable immigration regime; the existence of a legally recognised parentchild relationship; the allocation of parental authority and residence decision-making under civil law;
and the evidence that the child will genuinely join a lawful household in Switzerland. Swiss civil law
forms the parent-child relationship through birth, marital presumption, recognition, court declaration
or adoption; parental responsibility generally belongs jointly to mother and father; and moving a child’s
residence abroad under joint parental responsibility requires the other parent’s consent or a judicial or
child-protection decision.
Official federal guidance consistently treats partial or later child reunification as a fact-sensitive
category. In the domestic AIG/FNIA regime, the SEM says that partial family reunification cannot be
granted without further examination, especially where the child has been living abroad with the other
parent or that parent’s family. It identifies three mandatory checks: abuse of rights, parental authority
and legally sufficient consent, and the child’s welfare. The same basic concerns reappear in the EU/EFTA
guidance for stepchildren, which requires compliance with civil-law custody rules, express consent or a
court/administrative decision where parental responsibility is joint, and a welfare assessment.
The evidence model is correspondingly demanding. The SEM’s 2025 procedural instruction requires core
documents establishing identity and the family relationship, allows further document demands
depending on local conditions, permits DNA testing only where serious doubts about descent or
identity cannot otherwise be resolved, and allows voluntary civil-status verification and even social or
neighbourhood investigations only with consent and subject to proportionality. Cantonal practice pages
then translate those principles into filing expectations: for children of a previous marriage or outside
marriage, Vaud requires filiation documents, proof of custody and/or parental authority, the written
consent of the parent remaining abroad, and even the written agreement of the spouse or partner in
Switzerland to take the child under the household roof.
Timing is often outcome-determinative. Under the domestic AIG/FNIA route, family reunification must
generally be exercised within five years, and children over twelve must be brought within twelve
months; later child reunification is allowed only for important family reasons, with article 75 OASA/VZAE
defining those reasons by reference to whether the child’s welfare can be safeguarded only by a move
to Switzerland, and children over fourteen may be heard where necessary. Under the EU/EFTA route,
SEM’s official FAQ says the Swiss statutory reunification deadlines do not apply in the same way, but
delayed cases remain exposed to scrutiny for abuse, true family life and the child’s welfare, especially
where the child is older.
For drafting purposes, the safe internal position is this: do not write that stepchild cases automatically
follow ordinary minor-child reunification logic. The legally relevant question is whether the case is really
one of a sponsor’s own legal child, a spouse’s child under the EU/EFTA free-movement route, a late or
partial reunification of a child who has long lived elsewhere, or a different category altogether such as
foster child/adoption routing or post-separation “reverse” family life.

## Case structure and legal propositions

## Structural overview of stepchild and blended-family reunification cases

The official materials support a practical three-part structure.
The first category is the straightforward legal-child case. Here, the sponsor is the child’s legal parent,
the child falls within the relevant age threshold, the time limits are met where the AIG/FNIA applies, the
household will be formed in Switzerland, and there is no unresolved issue as to custody, parental
responsibility or consent. In those cases, the ordinary child provisions in articles 42 to 45 AIG/FNIA do
most of the work, subject to the distinct right/discretion split between Swiss and C-permit sponsors on
the one hand and B-/L-permit sponsors on the other.
The second category is the stepchild under the EU/EFTA free-movement regime. This is the clearest
expressly recognised “stepchild” route in official Swiss materials. The SEM’s 2026 VFP/OLCP directives
state that the spouse’s children of an EU/EFTA national with an original right of residence are family
members for article 3 of Annex I to the AFMP/FZA, and the Supreme Court’s BGE 136 II 65 confirms that
point. But this route still requires civil-law authority, sufficient consent for the move, child-welfare
review, genuinely lived family life, adequate common housing and an absence of abuse.
The third category is the domestic blended-family case in which the legally relevant joining parent
is not the Swiss or settled spouse, but the child’s own parent. This is where later content is most
likely to go wrong. The federal and cantonal materials do not treat the existence of a step-relationship
alone as sufficient. Rather, they focus on the child’s filiation, the sponsor-parent’s parental authority, the
other parent’s rights, and whether the Swiss household will genuinely receive and care for the child.
Vaud’s official filing list exposes that structure with unusual clarity: the parent’s custody/authority
documents and the other parent’s consent are required first, and the Swiss spouse’s written agreement
to house the child is required separately.

## Primary legal texts

## Primary legal texts

The binding primary framework sits in four federal sources: the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and
Integration, the Admission, Residence and Employment Ordinance, Annex I to the Agreement on the
Free Movement of Persons, and the Swiss Civil Code. English statutory wording available through
Fedlex-derived access copies is only informational; the legally binding texts are the official German,
French and Italian versions.
Within the AIG/FNIA, articles 42 to 45 create the basic spouse-and-child routes. Article 42 governs Swissnational sponsors; article 43 covers sponsors with settlement permits; article 44 covers sponsors with
residence permits; and article 45 covers sponsors with short-stay permits. Articles 43 to 45 attach
cohabitation and housing conditions to the child route, and for B-/L-type sponsors the route remains
discretionary. Article 47 sets the five-year and twelve-month time limits and provides for later
reunification only for important family reasons; article 49 allows separated residences only if good
cause exists and the family household continues; article 51 causes family-reunification rights to lapse
where exercised abusively or where revocation grounds exist. Article 48 provides a separate route for
children fostered with a view to adoption, and article 85c creates the separate family-reunification
framework for provisionally admitted persons and their minor children.
For stepchild work, one domestic nuance matters especially. The SEM’s 2026 guidance for article 42
paragraph 1 says that the French text “ses enfants célibataires” refers only to the Swiss national’s own
children; that is a strong signal against treating the ordinary domestic Swiss-sponsor child route as an
unqualified stepchild route. There is, however, a separate article 42 paragraph 2 route, modelled on free
movement, for foreign family members of Swiss nationals who hold a durable EU/EFTA residence
permit; in that setting, the statute and SEM guidance work with the broader free-movement familymember concept.
For EU/EFTA sponsors, the key legal text is article 3 of Annex I to the AFMP/FZA. As summarised in the
SEM’s 2026 VFP/OLCP directives, that provision covers the spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent,
and dependent ascendants; the same directives and BGE 136 II 65 confirm that this family-member
concept extends to the spouse’s own children, including third-country stepchildren. The AFMP/FZA route
therefore gives the clearest primary-law foothold for true stepchild reunification in Switzerland.
The civil-law framework is what turns many “immigration” stepchild cases into mixed civil-status and
authority cases. ZGB article 252 defines the formation of the parent-child relationship; article 255
supplies the marital presumption of paternity; article 260 provides for recognition; article 296 states the
principle that parental responsibility serves the child’s best interests and generally belongs jointly to
mother and father; article 298 regulates allocation on divorce; article 298a governs joint parental
responsibility for unmarried parents, with sole parental responsibility remaining with the mother until a
joint declaration is made; article 301 permits the caring parent to decide alone only in routine or urgent
matters; article 301a makes an international move of the child consent-sensitive where parental
responsibility is joint; and article 273 secures reasonable contact for the non-resident parent.

## Official guidance and cantonal practice

## Official guidance / administrative materials

The most important federal administrative source is the SEM’s 2026 AIG directives on family
reunification. They treat partial family reunification as a distinct category rather than a routine child
case. Even after the Federal Supreme Court’s move away from the old requirement that the child live
with both parents, the SEM says authorities must still examine abuse of rights, parental authority and
adequate consent, and the child’s welfare; it adds that a mere declaration by the parent remaining
abroad is insufficient where civil law does not entitle the joining parent to live with the child. The same
directives also state that after the time limit has expired, the older case law on partial family
reunification still matters: there must generally be a material change in the circumstances in the
country of origin, especially in the child’s care possibilities, and the authorities must assess whether the
child can remain where he or she has been living.
The SEM’s 2026 VFP/OLCP directives perform the same function for EU/EFTA cases. They expressly
identify the spouse’s children as family members, confirm that stepchildren fall within the familymember concept, and then apply a package of controls: compliance with civil-law authority, express
consent from the other parent or a court/administrative decision where parental responsibility is joint,
child-welfare review, proof that the family relationship is actually lived, agreement of the EU/EFTA
spouse, adequate common housing and an absence of abuse. The directives also explain that the
derived right of residence is normally contingent and ceases with the original right, and that the
autonomous right to finish vocational training applies only to the EU/EFTA national’s own child, not to
stepchildren.
The SEM’s 4 December 2025 instruction on third-country family-reunification procedure is the main
federal evidence source. It says the Swiss representation must verify that the file contains the essential
documents needed to establish identity and the family relationship, may note signs of document
forgery or sham or forced or minor marriage, and may recommend civil-status verification or DNA
testing. It then provides for “voluntary” document verification, capable of checking authenticity and the
correctness of civil-status content, and even social or neighbourhood studies via trusted local counsel,
but only with consent and under the proportionality principle. DNA testing is allowed only where
justified doubts about parentage or identity cannot be removed otherwise and only with written
consent.
The SEM’s official public-facing guidance adds two practically important points. First, its FAQ on the EU/
EFTA regime says that family reunification under free movement is not subject to the Swiss statutory
family-reunification deadlines that apply to Swiss, C-permit, B-permit and F-permit sponsors under
domestic law. Secondly, its general AIG FAQ reminds users that, in domestic cases, children over twelve
must normally be brought within twelve months and later child reunification is exceptional.

## Cantonal practice notes

The canton-level materials are most useful on proof and procedure, rather than on the substantive
federal legal test. Vaud’s official page for family reunification of a spouse, partner and/or children is the
clearest official cantonal source for blended-family evidence. For children from a previous marriage or
born outside marriage, it asks for the birth certificate showing filiation, an official and legalised
document proving custody and/or parental authority in favour of the parent seeking reunification, the
legalised written agreement of the parent remaining abroad, the written agreement of the spouse or
partner in Switzerland to accommodate the child under that household roof, and the lease showing
who shares the accommodation. Where some minor children are not included in the application, Vaud
also asks for a letter explaining why the family is not being fully recomposed and what the future
intentions are for the children who remain abroad.
Zurich’s official page for family reunification by Swiss nationals restates the domestic time limits and
gives a practical housing rule of thumb: number of persons minus one equals minimum number of
rooms. Basel-Stadt’s official page emphasises that the child route requires not just a qualifying
relationship, but unmarried children under 18 “for whom” the parent or parents in Basel must care,
together with cohabitation and suitable accommodation. These are administrative indicators, but they
are useful indicators of how cantons operationalise the federal requirements of care responsibility and
household formation.
Geneva’s official EU/EFTA page shows the free-movement point from a different language setting: an
EU/EFTA sponsor may invoke family reunification for children under 21 or dependent, including those of
the sponsor’s spouse. Ticino’s official civil-law guidance on joint parental responsibility makes the
related family-law rule explicit in Italian: where parental responsibility is joint, moving the child’s place
of residence abroad requires both parents’ consent or a decision by the judge or child-protection
authority. Together, those pages are valuable for later drafting because they demonstrate the practical
interface between migration procedure and civil authority documentation across languages.

## Practical legal analysis

## Practical legal analysis

Legal treatment of stepchildren and reconstituted families. The safest synthesis of the official
materials is that Swiss law recognises some stepchild routes, but not a universal one. The EU/EFTA
route expressly treats the spouse’s child as a family member, subject to civil-law and welfare controls.
The ordinary domestic AIG/FNIA route, however, is much more tightly tied to the sponsor’s own spouseand-children structure. The fact that the SEM had to say expressly, in the article 42 context, that the
relevant wording refers only to the Swiss national’s own children is a warning against over-broad
drafting. If the sponsoring adult is merely a step-parent, later content should ask first which legal
route is actually doing the work: the legal parent’s route, the AFMP/FZA route, a foster/adoption
route, or a different family-life analysis.
Biological parentage, legal parenthood, custody, parental authority and consent. Official Swiss
materials focus on legal parenthood and civil authority, not merely on social ties. Parent-child status
may arise without proof of biology, through marital presumption, recognition, judicial declaration or
adoption, while a pure step-relationship does not itself create a legal parent-child bond. Once the legal
parent-child bond exists, the migration question immediately intersects with parental responsibility and
the right to decide the child’s place of residence. Where parental responsibility is joint, an international
move of the child requires the other parent’s consent or a judicial/child-protection decision. That is why
the SEM insists, in both the domestic and EU/EFTA stepchild contexts, on sole authority, express
consent, or a court/administrative decision, and why it says an informal permission letter does not
necessarily suffice.
Relationship between the sponsoring household and the child’s existing care arrangements. The
federal materials repeatedly compare the existing care structure abroad with the future proposed
household in Switzerland. In timely domestic partial child-reunification cases, the child’s welfare
remains a live issue where the child has been living with the other parent or that parent’s family. In late
domestic cases, the SEM says there must usually be a material change in the circumstances in the
country of origin, especially as to care possibilities; authorities must then ask whether the child can
remain at the existing place of residence. Vaud’s requirement for an explanation where other minor
children remain abroad shows the same concern in administrative form: the authority wants to
understand whether the Swiss application reflects a coherent family recomposition or only a selective
move.
Evidential expectations regarding actual family life and future living arrangements. In complex
stepchild or blended-family cases, the authorities appear to expect at least four evidential layers. First,
civil-status proof of the claimed relationships. Secondly, authority-and-consent proof showing who
may lawfully move the child. Thirdly, household proof, including who lives in the Swiss accommodation
and whether it is adequate. Fourthly, plausibility proof that the claimed family life is real: in the EU/
EFTA stepchild context, both the SEM and the Supreme Court insist that a social family life of at least
minimal intensity must actually exist, even if the family members have not yet been co-resident. The
spouse in Switzerland must also genuinely agree to the move and the shared household.
Procedural complexity and enhanced verification. The 2025 SEM procedure instruction is especially
important for authority-pack drafting because it shows what Swiss authorities may do when the
evidential picture is difficult. They may ask for additional civil-status documents depending on local
conditions; they may recommend DNA, document legalisation or deeper checks; they may carry out
“voluntary” document verification to confirm authenticity and registry registration; and - in suitable,
proportionate cases and with consent - they may carry out social or neighbourhood inquiries through
trusted local intermediaries. In other words, blended-family cases can become procedurally complex
not because Swiss law creates a separate “patchwork family” chapter, but because ordinary child and
spouse provisions are asked to operate across disputed parentage, weak registries, divided authority
and changing care arrangements.
Timing and age. Domestic AIG/FNIA cases are strongly shaped by the five-year rule and the shorter
twelve-month rule for children over twelve. The SEM’s 2026 guidance says the child’s age at the time of
application is decisive, the right does not lapse merely because the child turns older during the
proceedings, and the deadlines begin from the constitution of the family relationship; for a child, that is
generally at birth, but in some disputed or unknown parentage situations it may begin later on
recognition or final judgment, and in adoption cases it begins with the adoption. After the deadline,
article 75 OASA/VZAE and the SEM guidance push the analysis firmly towards child welfare, material
change and the practicability of remaining at the former place of residence. The older the child, and the
nearer adulthood, the more serious and well-supported the reasons must be.
The EU/EFTA route has a different formal age threshold - under 21, or older if dependent - and the
SEM’s public guidance says the Swiss statutory deadlines do not apply in the same way. But the absence
of statutory deadlines does not make older stepchild cases easy. The SEM’s VFP/OLCP directives warn
that abuse concerns intensify where the move is delayed without objective reason, and the Supreme
Court’s free-movement case law still expects genuine family life rather than a purely immigration-driven
move. That is why older child, long delay, long socialisation abroad and weak evidence of actual family
life are warning signs even in AFMP/FZA cases.
Common refusal risks and complexity points identifiable from official materials. The most visible
risks are: lack of a legally sufficient route for a mere step-parent under the domestic regime; no proof of
sole authority, or no court/KESB-style permission for the move abroad where authority is joint; a child
who has in reality been anchored for years in the other household abroad; very late applications
involving adolescents; motives that appear primarily educational, labour-market or economic rather
than genuinely family-based; inadequate housing; and abuse indicators such as sham or merely formal
marital ties. The SEM expressly says that economic reasons such as better prospects in Switzerland,
sibling care or household running should not dominate the analysis in late child-reunification cases, and
the free-movement materials likewise stress that the route must serve family life rather than
circumvention of admission rules.
Distinguishing straightforward minor-child reunification from fact-sensitive stepchild cases. The
dividing line is not formality for its own sake; it is whether the case can be resolved by the ordinary child
rule alone. A straightforward minor-child case usually has one clear legal parent, a clear route, a timely
deadline position, uncontested authority and an imminent shared home. A fact-sensitive blended-family
case typically has one or more of the following: the Swiss-resident adult is only a step-parent; the child’s
other parent remains abroad with ongoing rights; the child has been raised elsewhere for many years;
the Swiss spouse’s role is that of household receiver rather than legal parent; official documents are
weak or contested; or only some of the children are moving. In those cases, the internal drafting
baseline should be “separate the legal route, the civil-law authority and the household-evidence
narrative” rather than “apply the ordinary child rule with minor modifications”.

## Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

Do not use “stepchild” as if it were itself a Swiss migration-law category with one uniform
consequence. Identify the operative regime first: domestic AIG/FNIA, AFMP/FZA, foster/adoption
routing, provisional admission, or post-separation family-life analysis.
Do not say that stepchildren automatically qualify under the ordinary Swiss-sponsor child route.
For Swiss sponsors, SEM expressly reads article 42 paragraph 1 as referring to the Swiss
national’s own children.
Where the child is the spouse’s child, distinguish legal status from household reality. The
child’s own parent-child relationship, custody/authority documents and the other parent’s rights

•
remain central; the spouse’s role in Switzerland is then often to prove shared housing and
willingness to receive the child into the household.
Do not present a bare consent letter from the parent abroad as invariably sufficient. Where civil
law requires authority to move the child abroad, the legally relevant document may instead need
to be a court or child-protection decision.
In EU/EFTA drafting, it is safe to say that the spouse’s children may qualify as family members,
but only if you immediately add the controls on authority, child welfare, actual family life,
housing and abuse.
Treat late adolescent and long-separated cases as high-risk. Federal guidance says that the
nearer the child is to adulthood, and the longer the child has lived abroad, the more serious and
better-supported the reasons must be; economic motives are specifically disfavoured.
Keep proof categories separate in drafting: identity/filiation, authority/consent, actual family
life, and future household formation. The SEM’s procedural instruction is built on that
separation.
Avoid over-claiming on DNA. In official practice it is a targeted tool for unresolved doubts about
identity or descent, not a general proof of household life or emotional bonds.
If some minor children remain abroad, flag that the authority may ask why the family is not
being fully recomposed and what the future intentions are. That point appears expressly in
Vaud’s official filing guidance.
