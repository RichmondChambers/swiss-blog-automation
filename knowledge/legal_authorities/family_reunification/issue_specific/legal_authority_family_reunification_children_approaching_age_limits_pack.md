# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Family Reunification for Children Approaching Age Limits

## Executive legal summary

A. Executive legal summary. In Swiss family reunification law, the critical age questions are regimespecific. Under the ordinary rules in the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act for Swiss nationals under
Article 42 paragraph 1, for holders of settlement permits under Article 43, and for holders of residence
permits under Article 44, the relevant child category is generally the unmarried child under 18. Under
Article 47 FNIA, the right to family reunification must generally be exercised within five years, but
children over 12 must be reunified within 12 months. By contrast, where Article 42 paragraph 2 applies
to certain foreign family members of Swiss nationals who already hold a durable residence title from an
EU/EFTA state, the relevant descendant category is under 21 or dependent, and Article 47 paragraph 2
disapplies the ordinary Article 47 deadlines for that route. SEM’s AFMP guidance likewise frames EU/
EFTA family reunification around descendants under 21 or dependants.
For age-threshold cases, the most important chronology point is that the child’s age at the date of
filing the application is decisive, not the age when the authority decides. SEM’s AIG directives state
this expressly and cite Federal Supreme Court case law; the official BGE regeste for BGE 136 II 497 says
the relevant age is the child’s age when the family reunification application is filed. Accordingly, a child
who turns 18 during the administrative process does not automatically lose an otherwise timeous
reunification claim merely because the decision comes later.
Delay is legally significant. Once the statutory deadline has expired, a later application may be
authorised only if there are important family reasons. Article 75 OASA defines important family
reasons for late child reunification as existing where the child’s welfare can be safeguarded only by
reunification in Switzerland. SEM’s directives emphasise that this exception is to be applied
restrictively; for adolescents, the closer the case is to majority and the longer the child has lived
abroad, the more serious and better evidenced the reasons must be. Economic motives, improved life
opportunities, domestic help, sibling care, or a politically difficult home-country situation are not
treated as sufficient reasons in themselves.
Partial family separation cases are not all treated alike. Since BGE 136 II 78, a single parent can rely on
the ordinary family reunification provisions even if the child has been living abroad with the other
parent, provided the ordinary timing rules are met. But this does not remove scrutiny. The authorities
still examine abuse, actual family life, custody and consent arrangements, and the child’s welfare.
Official materials require a formal custody basis; a bare statement from the remaining parent abroad is
not enough where the civil-law position does not support relocation.
For provisionally admitted persons, there is a separate timing structure. Under current Article 85c
FNIA, spouses and unmarried children under 18 may be reunited at the earliest three years after the
order for temporary admission, subject to cohabitation, suitable housing, no dependency on social
assistance, language or enrolment requirements for adults, and no annual supplementary benefits.
SEM’s F7 handbook further explains that cantonal authorities must forward characteristic Article 85c
cases even before the three-year waiting period has expired, because timing questions may themselves
need federal handling. After the waiting period is reached, Article 74 OASA and the SEM directives apply
a five-year / 12-month filing structure to such cases as well.
The drafting implication for any internal content system is straightforward: do not say that a child must
be “approved before turning 18”, and do not say that any late child case is automatically impossible. The
safe formulation is that the application must normally be filed within the applicable legal window,
that late child cases are admitted only on a narrow, fact-sensitive exception, and that courts and the
SEM treat older teenagers, long separation, weak custody evidence, and care arrangements abroad as
especially sensitive features.

## Structural overview of age-sensitive child reunification cases

## Structural overview of age-limit-sensitive child reunification cases

The first analytical step is to identify the governing route. For Swiss nationals under Article 42
paragraph 1 FNIA, and for foreign sponsors holding C or B permits under Articles 43 and 44 FNIA,
the ordinary child category is the unmarried child under 18. The ordinary timing rule is the five-year
period, shortened to 12 months once the child is over 12. The purpose of the shortened period, as SEM
explains, is to support earlier integration, including schooling in Switzerland at a sufficiently young age.
A distinct route exists under Article 42 paragraph 2 FNIA for certain family members of Swiss nationals
who already hold a durable residence title from an EU/EFTA state. There, descendants under 21 or
dependants fall within the core family definition, and Article 47 paragraph 2 disapplies the normal
Article 47 deadline structure. Geneva’s official guidance reflects this distinction by stating that if the
family members reside in an EU/EFTA state with a durable residence permit, the application may be filed
at any time, whereas for family members outside the EU/EFTA the five-year / 12-month structure
applies.
For EU/EFTA sponsors under the AFMP, the official SEM factsheet states that the sponsor may bring
the spouse, children who are under 21 or who receive support, and certain dependent ascendants,
irrespective of the family member’s nationality. The AFMP materials examined for this pack are
relationship- and dependency-based rather than built around the Article 47 timetable. That means
internal drafting must not import the ordinary FNIA 18/12-month rule into EU/EFTA cases without first
checking the correct route.
For provisionally admitted persons, current Article 85c FNIA introduces a separate threshold: family
reunification is possible only from three years after the temporary-admission decision, and only if the
statutory conditions are met. SEM’s F7 handbook shows that timing analysis in those cases is not simply
a copy of ordinary FNIA cases, because there is first a waiting period and only then a filing window.
Finally, there is a separate asylum-law family unity/family asylum regime for recognised refugees
under Article 51 AsylA, which the SEM itself distinguishes from Article 85c FNIA and from ordinary FNIA
family reunification. This pack focuses primarily on the age-sensitive delay and chronology issues
arising under the ordinary FNIA / OASA framework and under temporary admission, not on the full
separate law of refugee family asylum.

## Primary legal texts

The key federal statutory provisions are in the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act. Article 42
governs family members of Swiss nationals, including the special Article 42 paragraph 2 route for
certain family members already holding a durable residence title from an EU/EFTA state; Article 43
covers family members of C-permit holders; Article 44 covers family members of B-permit holders;
Article 47 sets the five-year / 12-month deadline structure and the “important family reasons” exception;
and Article 49 allows justified separate residence without destroying the family-unity requirement.
Current Article 85c FNIA provides the separate three-year waiting-period rule for provisionally admitted
persons. The English FNIA text used here expressly states that it is for information only and has no
legal force, so the legally authoritative texts remain the official language versions on the federal
platform.
The main implementing provisions are in the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment.
Article 73 OASA sets the B-permit family reunification filing deadlines and the late-application rule;
Article 74 OASA sets the filing structure for temporary-admission family reunification once the temporal
conditions are met; Article 75 OASA defines “important family reasons” for late child cases by reference
to the child’s welfare; and Article 76 OASA gives examples of justified exceptions to cohabitation. Article
73a and Article 74a OASA also matter because they codify language-related requirements for adult
spouses, while making clear that the child cases are treated differently.
For EU/EFTA cases, the treaty text was not reliably retrievable in-browser during this research, but the
official SEM AFMP factsheet states the current operational rule as covering the spouse, the sponsor’s
or spouse’s children under 21 or receiving support, and certain dependent ascendants. The official
2026 SEM comparison factsheet further confirms that these are the current AFMP family categories,
while also noting that the “updated AFMP” material published in March 2026 is not yet in force. That is
relevant because age-sensitive drafting should not prematurely adopt the new-treaty language as
current law.
The separate asylum-law family-unity route for recognised refugees remains outside the ordinary
Article 47 FNIA deadline structure. SEM’s materials distinguish ordinary/federal foreign-nationals family
reunification from family asylum / asylum-law family reunification under Article 51 AsylA.

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important guidance source is the SEM AIG Directives, chapter 6 on family reunification.
Those directives expressly say that the child’s age at filing is decisive; that the claim does not lapse
merely because the child reaches the relevant age during proceedings; that turning 12 within the
original five-year window shortens rather than supplements that window; and that late child cases are
admissible only on a narrow, child-welfare-centred exception. They also set out the rules on partial
family reunification, custody, child welfare, abuse, status changes, and the start of deadlines.
SEM’s separate directive on family-reunification procedures for third-country nationals is useful
procedurally. It states that the procedure generally begins with the filing of a national visa application at
the Swiss representation abroad by the family member, with an application simultaneously pending
before the competent cantonal migration authority, and it is designed to harmonise responsibilities
between the FDFA, cantons and SEM. That matters because chronology risk in age-sensitive cases is not
only substantive; it is also procedural.
For provisionally admitted persons, the official SEM Handbook Asylum and Return, Article F7 is
central. It explains the current Article 85c FNIA regime, the three-year waiting period, the roles of the
canton and the SEM, the possibility of forwarding cases before expiry of the waiting period, and the
distinction from asylum-law family reunification for refugees with asylum.
For EU/EFTA cases, the official SEM AFMP family reunification factsheet sets out the current
descendant category and the derivative nature of the family member’s residence right. The official
March 2026 comparative factsheet is useful only as a forward-looking caution: it expressly describes
the updated AFMP as not yet in force.

## Cantonal practice notes

Cantonal guidance is most useful on procedural chronology and evidence, rather than on substantive
law. Zurich’s public page for B- and C-permit sponsors states the five-year / 12-month time limits and
identifies the family-relationship date as the trigger; Zurich’s public page for Swiss sponsors likewise
requires proof of important reasons where the filing is outside the deadline.
Zurich’s internal/publicly accessible migration-office guidance PDF is especially valuable for agesensitive child cases. It states that early family reunification is intended to facilitate children’s
integration and schooling; that timeous applications can still be refused for abuse, custody defects, or
obvious conflict with the child’s welfare; that a late application requires proof of weighty reasons; that
older siblings do not ride on the younger sibling’s compliance with the deadline; and that a delayed
paternity acknowledgement may be treated as abusive if used to evade the deadline structure.
Geneva’s official pages make the route distinction clear in a way that is useful for drafting logic. For
Swiss sponsors, Geneva states that an application based on a family member’s durable EU/EFTA
residence title may be filed at any time, whereas applications for family members outside the EU/EFTA
are subject in principle to the five-year / 12-month timetable. For third-country sponsors, Geneva also
identifies the evidence of parental authority required when the application concerns only children.
Basel-Stadt’s official page and form are useful on filing chronology and evidential architecture. Basel
explains that the person abroad who is visa-required must file at the Swiss representation, while the
sponsor in Basel files in parallel with the cantonal migration office; it also stresses that filing the
application does not authorise entry or stay. The Basel form then requires, among other things, birth
certificates, divorce and custody decisions, and the other parent’s consent certified through the Swiss
representation where relevant.
Ticino’s official page examined for this pack is largely a procedural signpost to federal SEM materials
and forms rather than an independent source of distinct age-threshold practice. That is itself a useful
reminder not to overstate the separateness of cantonal substantive law in this field.

## Practical legal analysis

## Practical legal analysis

The safest way to analyse an age-sensitive child case is to separate hard legal timing rules from
discretionary factual assessment. The hard rules are the applicable child category, the applicable
filing window, and the chronology rules for when that window starts. In ordinary FNIA cases, those are
driven by Articles 42 to 47 FNIA and the OASA provisions; in temporary-admission cases there is the
additional threshold that reunification is possible only after three years; and in AFMP-style cases the
family category can extend to descendants under 21 or dependants. The decisive legal date for age
purposes is ordinarily the date of filing, not the date of decision or the date of entry.
The next step is to analyse the chronology of legal opportunity. SEM states that if the sponsor already
had a legal possibility of family reunification before acquiring a stronger status, that earlier period may
count against the deadline; the directive gives the examples of moving from temporary admission to a
residence permit, or from a residence permit to a settlement permit. By contrast, time spent with only a
short-stay permit does not automatically count in the same way because of the permit’s temporary
character. SEM also states that a timely first application that failed in a non-entitlement situation may
preserve the possibility of a later second application if a genuine entitlement subsequently arises and
the second application is itself filed within the relevant period from that entitlement event.
Cases involving late paternity recognition, adoption, or simultaneous parent-and-child
applications require particular care. SEM says that, as a rule, the child relationship exists from birth for
Article 47 purposes even if paternity is recognised later, unless the child relationship had genuinely
been unknown or disputed; in those exceptional cases, recognition or a final paternity judgment can
mark the start of the deadline. For adoption, the relationship arises on adoption. Zurich adds an
important anti-abuse caution: where recognition was delayed in order to evade the deadline structure,
an earlier starting point can be assumed. SEM also says that there is no new deadline merely because
the other parent is brought at the same time as the child.
Late child cases are decided on a narrow exception, but the exception is still a real legal category, not a
rhetorical flourish. The official framework asks whether the child’s welfare can only be protected by
moving to Switzerland, read consistently with Article 8 ECHR and Article 3 CRC. SEM’s own examples
show how difficult this can be. A minor change in care arrangements, ordinary educational ambition, or
a general wish to live together later are not enough. What materially helps are evidence-backed
changes in the care structure abroad, such as the illness or death of the previous carer, combined with
an individual explanation of why remaining in the country of origin is no longer a realistic welfarepreserving option. The older the child, the stronger the evidential burden.
Schooling, language, dependency and integration plans matter, but not all in the same way. Within
the ordinary deadline, the law does not create a separate hard age-at-entry schooling test for children
under 18; however, SEM expressly says the deadline structure exists to favour early integration and
schooling in Switzerland, especially for children over 12. In late cases, the authorities then revisit
integration prospects more concretely: they assess age, time already spent abroad, educational level,
language abilities, ties to the present environment, and the seriousness of uprooting the child from an
established social world. Accordingly, “age at entry” is not usually the primary threshold question; it
becomes a major factual proportionality factor once lateness, partial separation, or welfare concerns
are engaged.
The identifiable refusal-risk patterns are consistent across the federal and cantonal materials
reviewed. High-risk fact patterns include filing only when a teenager is close to 18; applications that
appear to be driven by labour-market access rather than family life; long periods of non-cohabitation
with weak evidence of an existing parental relationship; poor custody or consent documentation;
availability of family care abroad; inadequate housing or finances; unresolved supplementary-benefit or
social-assistance problems; and attempts to create faits accomplis by having the child enter Switzerland
unlawfully before the case is decided. Zurich and SEM also warn that where siblings are differently
placed in time, the younger child’s in-time application does not automatically rescue the older sibling’s
out-of-time case.
From an evidence perspective, age-sensitive child cases should be built around a chronology bundle
and a care-and-custody bundle. The chronology bundle should identify the sponsor’s immigration
status dates, the child’s date of birth, the family-relationship date, any changes of status, any prior
applications, and the exact filing date. The care-and-custody bundle should include the civil-status
records, birth certificates, custody orders, the other parent’s certified consent where needed, and
concrete evidence explaining who has actually cared for the child and why that arrangement can or
cannot continue. Geneva and Basel both require parental-authority evidence in child-only cases; Basel
also requires the other parent’s consent to be authenticated through the Swiss representation where
relevant.

## Drafting cautions

## Drafting cautions

Internal content should always identify the legal route first. AIG Article 42 paragraph 1, AIG Article 42
paragraph 2, Articles 43 and 44, AFMP cases, and Article 85c temporary-admission cases do not share
the same age threshold or the same filing structure. Any drafting that collapses “child” into a single
Swiss immigration-law category is legally unsafe.
Do not write that the child must “enter Switzerland before 18” or that the authorities must “decide
before 18”. The conservative and source-grounded formula is that the application must ordinarily be
filed while the child is still within the relevant legal child category and within the relevant
deadline window. Decision date and travel date are not the primary age-threshold triggers in the
authorities reviewed.
Do not write that a late child case is always barred. The law contains a real exception for important
family reasons, but official materials present it as narrow and heavily fact-sensitive. Internal content
should therefore say that late child cases are possible only on an exceptional, evidence-heavy basis
centred on child welfare and the actual care situation.
Do not reduce child-welfare analysis to a sponsor’s preference. In official practice, child welfare interacts
with custody, existing carers, the child’s own wishes where relevant, educational and linguistic situation,
actual family life, and the seriousness of uprooting. A parent’s unsupported statement that the child
should move is not enough where civil or factual arrangements point the other way.
Do not present schooling, language, or integration as a universal self-standing legal barrier for children
within time. They are best understood as policy reasons behind the deadlines and as stronger factual
considerations in late, near-majority, or weak-relationship cases. Overstating them as automatic legal
bars would go beyond the sources reviewed.
Do not assume that every status change creates a new deadline. SEM’s guidance is more nuanced:
earlier lawful opportunities can count against the clock; short-stay cases are treated differently; and a
new application after a failed first one depends on why the first case failed and whether a genuine later
entitlement arose.
Do not assert, without qualification, that an incomplete filing always preserves the filing date. The
official materials reviewed for this pack strongly support the importance of filing chronology, but they
did not yield a fully reliable federal rule on whether every incomplete or defective filing is dateprotective. Internal content should therefore avoid promising that a placeholder or incomplete
submission always secures the age position.

## Open questions and limitations

Direct retrieval of some Fedlex treaty/statute pages was unreliable in-browser during this research, so
the pack cross-checks article-level points against official SEM legal-bases pages, official SEM directives
and handbook material, official cantonal pages, and mirrored legislation texts that reproduce the
federal wording. The substantive propositions above are therefore presented conservatively. This pack
also does not attempt a full canton-by-canton comparison, and it does not provide a full treatment of
Article 51 AsylA family asylum beyond identifying it as a separate regime.
