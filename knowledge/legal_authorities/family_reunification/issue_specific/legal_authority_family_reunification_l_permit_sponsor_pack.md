# Swiss Family Reunification with an L Permit Sponsor

Suggested filename: legal_authority_family_reunification_l_permit_sponsor_pack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

## Executive legal summary

For third-country sponsors holding a Swiss L permit (short-stay permit), family reunification is legally
possible but not framed as a statutory entitlement. The core legal basis is Article 45 AIG/FNIA,
under which the foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18 of a person with a short-stay permit
may be granted a short-stay permit if they live with the sponsor, suitable housing is available, they do
not depend on social assistance, and the sponsor is not receiving, or becoming entitled to,
supplementary benefits because of the reunification. The SEM’s current AIG directives state expressly
that, in L-permit cases, “there is no legal claim” and that family members receive a short-stay permit
with the same period of validity as the sponsor.
The sponsor’s own L status is structurally fragile. Article 32 AIG defines the short-stay permit as a permit
for a limited period of stay of up to one year, issued for a specific purpose, extendable by up to two
years, with re-granting only after an appropriate interruption of stay. The SEM’s 2026 directives
restate that short-stay permits are temporary, purpose-bound, and ordinarily linked to a finite stay; they
also note that job changes are only possible for important reasons under Article 32 AIG and Article 55
OASA/VZAE. This does not create a separate statutory “renewal prospects” test in Article 45, but it does
mean that the practical strength of an L-based family reunification case depends heavily on whether the
sponsor’s stay looks genuinely sustainable in the near term. That is an inference from the temporary
and derivative structure of the regime, rather than a separately worded condition.
For third-country L-permit sponsor cases, the conditions broadly mirror the B-permit familyreunification conditions except for language. Article 44 AIG requires the spouse to be able to
communicate in the local national language, but Article 45 does not. The SEM’s AIG directives confirm
that, for family members of short-stay-permit holders, the conditions correspond to those for family
members of B-permit holders “except for language competences”. This is an important drafting point:
one should not automatically import B-permit language requirements into Article 45 cases.
The position is materially different for EU/EFTA sponsors with an L EU/EFTA permit. Under the free
movement regime, SEM guidance states that EU/EFTA nationals with a residence permit or a short-stay
permit may be accompanied by qualifying family members. For workers, the right is principally tested
through adequate housing, not financial means; for self-employed persons and non-active persons,
sufficient means remain necessary. Family members’ status is derivative and normally lasts only as long
as the sponsor’s own free-movement residence right, but the EU/EFTA framework is still significantly
stronger than Article 45 AIG because it operates from a rights-based free-movement structure rather
than a discretionary third-country admission model.
The best internal drafting position is therefore this: for third-country sponsors, family reunification
with an L permit is possible, but it is a discretionary, document-heavy, stability-sensitive route
that should not be described as equivalent to B- or C-permit sponsorship. By contrast, EU/EFTA Lpermit sponsors sit in a different legal framework and may have materially stronger familyreunification rights, subject to the conditions of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and
the OLCP directives.

## Structural overview and legal propositions

## Structural overview of family reunification with an L permit sponsor

There are really two different Swiss L-permit family-reunification regimes, and later drafting should
keep them separate.
The first is the third-country regime under the AIG/FNIA. This is the regime most likely to support the
proposition that family reunification becomes harder when the sponsor holds only an L permit. It is
characterised by temporary status, discretionary wording, derivative short-term status for the family, no
automatic work right for the reunited spouse under Article 46, and a strong practical focus on housing,
financial independence, documentary reliability and the sponsor’s near-term stay stability.
The second is the EU/EFTA free-movement regime. Here, family reunification is not assessed under
Article 45 AIG in the same way. Instead, the SEM’s free-movement materials treat EU/EFTA nationals
with L or B status as capable of sponsoring qualifying family members under the AFMP framework, with
adequate housing as a core condition and a less restrictive approach for workers. Those cases may still
be fact-sensitive, because the family permit remains derivative and housing or abuse issues can defeat
the application, but the legal footing is stronger than for third-country Article 45 cases.

## Primary texts and official guidance

## Primary legal texts

The key federal statute is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. The accessible
English text used here is an informational mirror of the current Fedlex consolidation and expressly
states that English is not an official language of the Swiss Confederation and has no legal force; the
authoritative federal texts remain in German, French and Italian. For present purposes, the critical
provisions are Articles 32, 44, 45, 47, 49, 49a, 50, 51 and 52. Article 32 defines the L permit as temporary
and purpose-specific; Article 45 sets the conditions for family reunification by short-stay permit holders;
Article 47 governs deadlines; Article 49 creates an express cohabitation exception only for Articles 4244; Article 49a creates a language-waiver rule only for Articles 43 and 44; Article 50 governs postbreakdown continuation; and Article 52 extends the chapter mutatis mutandis to registered
partnerships.
For internal drafting, two points from the statute matter especially. First, Article 45 lacks the language
limb that is present in Article 44. Second, Article 49’s express exception to cohabitation is framed
only for Articles 42-44, not Article 45. That does not by itself prove that every L-permit case must fail
where the family is not yet living together, because procedural realities differ and cantonal practice
matters, but it does mean that one should be cautious before asserting that the statutory cohabitation
exception clearly extends to Article 45 cases.
The EU/EFTA side is governed by the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its Swiss
implementation materials. The SEM’s official AFMP family-reunification factsheet states that EU/EFTA
nationals with a residence permit or a short-stay permit may be accompanied by qualifying family
members and sets out the housing and financial-conditions structure. The SEM’s 2026 OLCP directives
then add operational detail, including the treatment of adequate housing, abuse indicators and the
special position of workers compared with self-employed and non-active persons.
A related primary structural point is the rule governing the L permit itself. The SEM’s official page on the
L EU/EFTA permit states that EU/EFTA nationals are entitled to it if they have an employment contract
of three to twelve months; the permit’s validity is tied to the contract; it can be extended for a total
period of less than twelve months; and the OLCP directives further explain how it may then be renewed
and, where the contract becomes indefinite or runs for at least one year, replaced with a B EU/EFTA
permit.

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important federal administrative source for third-country cases is the SEM’s AIG directives,
current at 1 January 2026. These directives do three useful things for an internal authority pack. They
state expressly that Article 45 cases involve no legal claim; they confirm that the Article 45
requirements correspond to Article 44 except for language competences; and they explain that,
where a person later moves from an L permit to a B permit, the Article 47 deadline is not counted from
the earlier L-based family-reunification possibility because of the “clearly temporary character” of shortstay residence. They also explain that, after family breakdown, Article 50 does not give the family
member of an L sponsor a claim to a residence permit and that the protection lapses once the
maximum duration of the short-stay permit is exhausted.
The SEM’s Directive on family-reunification procedures for third-country nationals of 4 December
2025 is especially important for evidential strategy. It says that the Swiss representation abroad should
first check whether the file contains the essential documents and information needed to establish
identity and the family relationship. It also allows further case-specific document requests, preassessment notes flagging possible forged documents, sham marriage, forced marriage or trafficking
concerns, and a recommendation for further document authentication or DNA testing. The directive
expressly permits voluntary document verification and even social or neighbourhood inquiries
where proportionate and consented to, while stating that DNA testing is permitted only where justified
doubts about identity or parentage cannot otherwise be dispelled.
The same 2025 SEM directive also shows that valid national travel documents remain the default
expectation for applicants relying on Articles 42-45 AIG, although the competent authority must
consider cases where producing such a document is objectively impossible or unreasonable. That
matters in L-permit sponsor cases because the evidential burden is often heavier where the sponsor’s
own status is already precarious and the family relationship must be proved through foreign civil-status
records.
On the EU/EFTA side, the clearest federal materials are the SEM factsheet on family reunification
under the AFMP, the OLCP directives dated January 2026, and the SEM page on the L EU/EFTA permit.
Read together, these materials support a more robust sponsor position than under Article 45 AIG: the
right is derived from the sponsor’s underlying free-movement residence right; workers generally are
not required to prove finances for family reunification; and EU/EFTA L permits can be renewed without
the same structural fragility as Article 45 short-stay cases.

## Cantonal practice notes

## Cantonal practice notes

The clearest L-specific cantonal evidential guidance located in public official sources is from Canton
Zurich. Zurich has a dedicated page for family reunification by persons with an L permit in thirdcountry cases. It states that the family must live together in an adequate family dwelling, gives a
practical room-count rule (“number of persons minus one = number of rooms”), requires that there be
no social-assistance dependency and no annual supplementary benefits, and requires the absence of
revocation grounds or indications of abuse. Zurich’s evidential list is particularly valuable for internal
drafting because it asks for the lease, landlord’s written consent, six months of payslips, the
employment contract, and a detailed debt-enforcement register extract from the last three years.
For children of separated parents, Zurich also requires a court or administrative custody record and
states that a simple declaration by the parent remaining abroad is not sufficient.
Geneva provides useful practical contrast. Its page for non-EU/EFTA nationals says that a sponsor with
a short-term, residence or settlement permit may apply for family reunification for a spouse and
unmarried children under 18; that the application must be made before entry and within the ordinary
five-year / twelve-month time limits; that the family should normally share the sponsor’s home; and
that the sponsor must not rely on social assistance or annual federal supplementary benefits. The same
Geneva set of pages also states that a non-European spouse of a non-European L-permit holder who
is admitted by family reunification must obtain separate work authorisation, and that this will be
examined by the foreign labour service.
Geneva’s public guidance also illustrates why future drafting should not rely on simplified cantonal
webpages without checking the federal legal framework. One general Geneva family-reunification page
states that the spouse in non-EU/EFTA cases must be able to communicate in French, but Geneva’s more
specific 2026 language-requirements page lists documentary language-attestation requirements for
third-country family-reunification cases involving sponsors with B, C or F permits, not L permits. That
mismatch is a useful internal warning: public cantonal guidance can simplify across statuses, whereas
Article 45 and the SEM’s AIG directives do not make language competence an Article 45 condition.
The public material found from Ticino is more procedural and generally redirects users to SEM federal
materials for the substantive legal rules, rather than setting out an L-specific cantonal test in the same
detail as Zurich or Geneva. For internal drafting, that is itself a useful reminder that canton-specific
public guidance is uneven and that the forum canton’s current operational checklist should be checked
before making procedural assertions.

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

## Practical legal analysis

The main reason L-permit sponsorship is weaker is not that Article 45 contains a long list of extra
negative conditions. It is weaker because of the combined structure of the regime: the sponsor’s own
permit is temporary and purpose-bound; the family’s permit is equally temporary and derivative; the
statute uses discretionary wording; and post-admission security is limited because Article 46 work
rights do not cover Article 45 families, while Article 50 does not convert them into B-permit holders.
That combination makes L-based family reunification markedly less secure than B- or C-based
sponsorship even where the family technically satisfies the formal conditions.
Permit duration, renewal prospects and employment stability. Official sources do not formulate
“renewal prospects” as a standalone legal criterion in Article 45. Even so, they matter in practice because
the sponsor’s L permit is temporary by design and the family only obtains matched short-term status. In
third-country cases, Zurich’s checklist asks for the employment contract and six months of payslips,
which is a practical way of testing whether the sponsor’s current work and income profile can
realistically sustain the family without public support. By contrast, EU/EFTA L permits can be renewed
more freely under the OLCP framework, and EU/EFTA workers retain a stronger family-reunification
footing because the right is linked primarily to housing rather than financial self-sufficiency.
Housing and household formation. In third-country Article 45 cases, living together and suitable
housing are core statutory requirements. Zurich adds particularly concrete evidential expectations,
including the lease and landlord consent. In EU/EFTA cases, the SEM’s OLCP directives say that the
canton should verify adequate housing at filing and at the family’s arrival, and they list abuse
indicators such as very small accommodation, housing arranged only for the procedure, or a lease
supported by third parties with weak finances. This is useful for internal drafting because it shows that
“adequate housing” is not merely formal: it is a real decision point and a practical ground for refusal or
delay.
Finance and public support. For Article 45 cases, the federal rule is negative in form: no socialassistance dependency and no supplementary-benefit exposure caused by reunification. The SEM’s
Article 44 guidance, which Article 45 otherwise mirrors, explains that the family’s financial means must
be sufficient to ensure that reunification will not result in social-assistance dependence; Zurich’s
documentary practice of asking for payslips and debt-enforcement extracts fits that logic. For EU/EFTA
workers, however, the federal materials are materially more generous, because workers are tested
through housing rather than financial means, whereas self-employed and non-active persons must
show sufficient means and can lose the right if social assistance is or would be needed.
Future residence and later improvement of status. Where a third-country sponsor later obtains a B
permit, the legal footing may improve significantly. Article 47 says the time limit for foreign nationals
begins with the grant of a residence or settlement permit, and the SEM’s AIG directives now state
expressly that earlier family-reunification possibilities under Article 45 are not counted because of the
temporary character of L status. In practical terms, that means an unsuccessful or deferred L-permit
sponsor case may become stronger later if the sponsor regularises into B status, but the earlier L stage
should still be described as a weaker, fact-sensitive route.
Common officially identifiable difficulty points. Official sources identify the following recurring risk
areas: inadequate housing; present or near-future social-assistance dependence; supplementary
benefits; doubts about the authenticity of civil-status documents; absence of a valid national travel
document; suspicion of sham marriage, forced marriage or other abuse; and, for children, applications
brought outside the statutory time limits unless there are important family reasons. In EU/EFTA worker
cases, the OLCP directives also flag very low hours, very low pay, extremely short contracts, or
opportunistic housing arrangements as potential abuse indicators.

## Drafting cautions

Future legal content should keep the following cautions in view.
First, separate third-country L cases from EU/EFTA L cases. The latter sit under the AFMP / OLCP
framework and may involve genuine rights-based family reunification; the former sit under Article 45
AIG and are the stronger basis for saying that L-permit family reunification is more difficult in practice.
Second, do not say that a third-country L-permit sponsor is “entitled” to family reunification. The
conservative formulation is that family reunification may be granted, subject to the statutory
conditions and cantonal assessment.
Third, do not automatically import B/C sponsor rules into Article 45 cases. In particular, be cautious
about: language requirements for the spouse; automatic work rights for reunited family members; and
any suggestion that the family’s later status becomes long-term merely because the sponsor was
admitted for family life. Those features differ materially between Articles 44 and 45.
Fourth, do not state that the Article 47 family-reunification deadline for a foreign national starts from
the beginning of the sponsor’s L-permit residence. The SEM’s current guidance says the opposite for
persons who later receive a B permit.
Fifth, be careful with cohabitation exceptions. Article 49 expressly relaxes cohabitation only for Articles
42-44. Because Article 45 is not named there, later drafting should avoid confident statements that an
L-permit sponsor can rely on the same statutory exception without checking current doctrine or cantonspecific practice.
Sixth, in practical drafting use the formula “possible, but discretionary and fact-sensitive” for thirdcountry L sponsors, and then explain why: short duration of the sponsor’s permit, matched short-term
status for the family, housing and financial scrutiny, lack of automatic work rights, and heavy evidential
demands around identity and family relationship. That captures the official materials more accurately
than either “not possible” or “equivalent to B-permit sponsorship”.
