# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Family Reunification for Same-Sex Couples

Suggested filename: legal_authority_family_reunification_same_sex_couple_pack.pdf

## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law is, at the level of substantive family-reunification entitlement, largely neutral as
to the sex of the couple once the relationship falls within a recognised legal category. Since 1 July 2022,
marriage in Switzerland has been open to same-sex couples; new Swiss registered partnerships can no
longer be created, but existing registered partnerships continue. In the immigration field, Article 52
FNIA expressly provides that the provisions on foreign spouses apply mutatis mutandis to registered
partnerships of same-sex couples, and Article 88a FNIA does the same in the temporary-admission
chapter. Official SEM guidance therefore treats same-sex registered partners, within those provisions, as
legally equivalent to spouses for family-reunification purposes.
The main distinction for same-sex couple cases is therefore usually not a special immigration restriction,
but whether Swiss authorities can classify and record the foreign relationship correctly in Swiss civilstatus terms. Official federal civil-status guidance states that a foreign marriage validly concluded
abroad is in principle recognised in Switzerland whether it is between persons of the same sex or
different sexes. By contrast, foreign registered partnerships are recognised in Switzerland only if they
create a civil-status relationship with effects similar to marriage; the Federal Office of Justice gives
examples of foreign forms that are considered equivalent, such as the Italian unione civile and the
former German Lebenspartnerschaft, and examples that are not, such as the French PACS and Belgian
cohabitation légale.
Unmarried same-sex partners do not enjoy the same straightforward statutory route as spouses and
recognised registered partners. SEM’s 2026 directives confirm that unmarried cohabiting partners may
be admitted on a discretionary hardship basis under Article 30 paragraph 1 letter b FNIA together with
Article 31 OASA where the partnership is stable and intended to last, and that same-sex couples may
rely on Article 8 ECHR on the same conditions that opposite-sex couples rely on Article 8 family-life
protection in analogous cases. The official materials also state that a hardship-based permit may be
granted where same-sex partners have refrained from registration or marriage because of threatened
disadvantage in a partner’s country of origin.
For document-sensitive cases, the most important practical points are official registration of the civilstatus event, the quality and authenticity of foreign documents, and the sequencing between civilstatus recognition and migration processing. Federal and cantonal materials show that foreign civilstatus events commonly need to be reported through the Swiss representation abroad or the
competent cantonal civil-status authority, that original documents are generally required, and that
translation, authentication or later verification may be needed. Official SEM procedure guidance also
records that where documents remain unavailable after a relatively long wait, or where a valid national
travel document cannot reasonably or objectively be obtained, the case is still to be forwarded to the
cantonal migration authority for a decision on how to proceed rather than simply being treated as
impossible.
Structural overview of same-sex couple family reunification
routes
The ordinary FNIA route is the clearest route for same-sex spouses and registered partners. For Swiss
sponsors, Article 42 FNIA covers the foreign spouse living with the Swiss national, and Article 52 FNIA
extends the spouse rules to same-sex registered partnerships. For sponsors holding C, B or L permits,
Articles 43 to 45 set the applicable family-reunification rules, with Article 52 again applying spouse
treatment mutatis mutandis to same-sex registered partnerships. Articles 47, 49 and 50 then govern
deadlines, justified separate living, and post-breakdown residence, again with Article 52 carrying those
spouse rules across to registered partnerships.
Under the Swiss-EU/EFTA free movement framework, official SEM materials describe the core familymember category as the spouse, together with certain descendants and dependent ascendants. Samesex spouses therefore fit straightforwardly within the federal “spouse” category. The federal AFMP
materials are less explicit on registered partnerships than the FNIA materials; however, the SEM’s AFMP
directives expressly refer “other family members”, including concubine partners, back to the domestic
migration guidance, and at cantonal level Ticino’s official EU/EFTA family-reunification page expressly
includes the spouse or partner in a registered union living abroad. The safest internal drafting position
is therefore that same-sex spouses are clearly covered under AFMP family reunification, while
references to registered partners in AFMP-only content should be carefully sourced to the specific Swiss
authority relied upon.
Where the relationship has not been formalised, the route is normally discretionary rather than
entitlement-based. SEM’s directives set out a domestic hardship route for unmarried partners of Swiss
nationals or B/C permit holders where the partnership is settled and intended to last, and Geneva’s
cantonal guidance for non-EU/EFTA cohabiting partners reflects the same structure: there is no right to
the permit, the relationship must be stable and of some duration, and evidence such as a cohabitation
agreement, joint housing, photos, letters, and other proof of a genuine and lasting relationship may be
required.
A marriage-preparation route also exists and is relevant where a same-sex couple wish to regularise
status before or during a move to Switzerland. SEM’s directives allow a limited residence permit to
prepare a marriage where the civil-status office confirms that the preparatory process has started and
the marriage can take place within a reasonable period; Geneva’s official guidance requires the
application to be lodged abroad before entry, the civil-status procedure to be initiated before entry, and
a certificate from the civil registry confirming completion of the preparatory formalities. This route is
particularly relevant where a foreign civil-status form is not equivalent to marriage or to a recognised
registered partnership under Swiss law.
For asylum-linked routes, official SEM materials show that same-sex couple treatment is also broadly
equivalent once the relationship type is recognised. In family asylum, the SEM handbook states that
registered partners are equated with spouses and that durable marriage-like cohabitation is also
recognised. For temporarily admitted persons and temporarily admitted refugees, Article 85 paragraph
7 FNIA grants family reunification to spouses and unmarried children after the waiting period, Article
88a FNIA extends those spouse provisions to registered same-sex partnerships, and the SEM handbook
adds that cohabiting partners are outside the core Article 85c route but may fall within the broader
Article 8 ECHR analysis where a stable partnership exists.

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

The primary statutory base for same-sex couple family reunification is the FNIA. Article 42 covers
spouses of Swiss nationals, with Article 42 paragraph 2 providing a broader route for certain foreign
family members of Swiss nationals who hold a durable residence permit from a state with a freemovement agreement. Articles 43 to 45 govern spouses and children of C-, B- and L-permit holders
respectively; Articles 47 to 50 cover time limits, justified separate residence, and continued residence
after family breakdown; and Article 52 states expressly that the chapter’s provisions on foreign spouses
apply mutatis mutandis to registered partnerships of same-sex couples. In the temporary-admission
chapter, Article 85 paragraph 7 governs reunification of spouses and children of temporarily admitted
persons and refugees, while Article 88a extends those spouse rules to registered same-sex
partnerships.
The most important official administrative source is the SEM’s 2026 AIG directives. These directives
expressly state that, since 1 July 2022, no new registered partnerships can be created in Switzerland,
that existing registered partnerships continue, and that the reunification of foreign registered partners
is equivalent to the reunification of foreign spouses under Article 52 FNIA. The directives also preserve a
hardship-based route under Article 31 OASA where same-sex couples have refrained from registration
or marriage because of threatened disadvantage in a partner’s home country, and they set out the
evidence expected for unmarried stable partnerships. They further state that same-sex couples may
claim residence based on Article 8 ECHR/private life on the same conditions that mixed-sex couples rely
on Article 8 family-life protection in analogous circumstances.
For EU/EFTA sponsors, the relevant official materials are the SEM’s AFMP factsheet and AFMP directives.
The factsheet states that EU/EFTA nationals in Switzerland may bring their spouse, certain children and
dependent ascendants, regardless of those family members’ nationality, and lists the documentary
proof required for the family link. The AFMP directives state that the treaty’s “other family members”
facilitation can cover persons such as cohabiting partners but does not create a direct right for them,
and refer those cases back to the domestic AIG guidance. That is why same-sex spouse cases are
straightforward under AFMP, but registered-partner and unmarried-partner drafting should remain
source-specific and route-specific.
On civil-status recognition, the Federal Office of Justice is the key authority. Its public FAQ on “Marriage
and the effects of Marriage for all” confirms that marriage has been open to same-sex couples since 1
July 2022, that existing registered partnerships remain in place unless converted, and that foreign
same-sex marriages previously entered as registered partnerships may be updated in the Swiss civilstatus register. The same FAQ explains that foreign registered partnerships are recognised only if they
are equivalent to the Swiss registered partnership in their effects, giving positive and negative
examples. The OFJ’s 2025 implementation directive further states that foreign registered partnerships
are recognised if they create a “strong” civil-status relationship similar to marriage but are not called
marriage, and that foreign same-sex marriages are recognised as marriages, not as registered
partnerships, after 1 July 2022.
The most useful procedural federal source for document-sensitive overseas cases is the SEM’s
December 2025 directive on family-reunification procedures for third-country nationals. It states that
the Swiss representation abroad performs a preliminary check of jurisdiction, completeness, valid travel
documents and the quality of the information, then forwards the file to the competent canton. It also
records that when necessary documents remain unobtainable after a relatively long period, or when no
prospect of obtaining them exists, the representation must contact the canton, which decides whether
more documents should be requested, whether authenticity checks are needed, and whether the case
can be decided on the documents available. It further states that where production of a valid national
travel document is objectively impossible or unreasonable, the case is still forwarded to the canton for
assessment, and that persons already recognised as refugees by a Geneva Convention state must not
be told to approach their country-of-origin authorities for a passport.

## Cantonal practice notes and practical legal analysis

Cantonal materials broadly confirm legal equivalence while also showing where cases become
document-heavy in practice. Zurich’s official family-reunification pages for Swiss sponsors and for B/C
sponsors expressly include registered partners alongside spouses and apply the ordinary five-year /
twelve-month deadlines by reference to the creation of the family relationship, including registration of
the partnership. Zurich’s civil-status page on events abroad adds that foreign authorities usually do not
notify Swiss authorities directly and that affected persons should report changes promptly to the Swiss
representation abroad, which may translate, authenticate and forward the documents so that the Swiss
civil-status register can be updated.
Geneva’s materials are especially useful for sequencing and unmarried-partner evidence. Its marriagepreparation guidance requires the foreign fiancé(e) to file from abroad with the Swiss representation, to
begin the civil-registry procedure before entry, and to submit a certificate confirming that the marriage
can be celebrated within a reasonable time. Geneva’s separate page for non-EU/EFTA cohabiting
partners states expressly that there is no right to the permit; the relationship must be stable and of
some duration; the couple must share a household and have sufficient means; and the documentary
pack may include a cohabitation agreement or PACS-type contract, proof of a stable and lasting
relationship, photos, letters from relatives or friends, a joint lease, and a joint bank account. That
evidential logic is not sexuality-specific and is therefore highly relevant to same-sex as well as oppositesex unmarried-partner drafting.
Ticino’s official pages are valuable on both AFMP practice and foreign-event reporting. Its EU/EFTA
family-reunification page expressly includes the spouse or partner in a registered union living abroad.
Its foreign civil-status-events page states that civil-status events or changes abroad, including marriage
and registered partnership, should in principle be reported personally to Swiss authorities; the
representation abroad will say which documents are needed and will send them to the cantonal
supervisory authority for recognition and updating; and, as a rule, the documents must be originals
issued no more than six months earlier. That is a strong official indication that many practical problems
in same-sex partner cases are timing and document-form problems rather than distinct legal
restrictions.
Vaud’s official materials are useful for transitional cases. Its “Marriage for all” flyer, prepared for samesex couples, states that where a foreign marriage had previously been registered in Switzerland as a
registered partnership, a simple request can be made to update civil status, without delay or fee. It also
notes that the civil-status office itself notifies the population registry, old-age insurance and the SEM of
the change in status. That is practically important for content drafting because it shows that some
same-sex cases require a civil-status correction before the immigration narrative is cleanly presented.
The practical legal analysis that follows from these sources is clear. The decisive first question is not
whether the couple is same-sex, but which of four categories the relationship falls into in Swiss law:
marriage; recognised registered partnership; non-equivalent foreign civil union; or factual cohabitation
only. Immigration drafting should always identify and state that classification expressly. The second
question is whether the relevant civil-status event has already been entered in the Swiss register, or
whether a prior update / recognition step is still needed. The third question is whether the case can be
documented with original, recent and verifiable civil-status records and a recognised travel document,
or whether the file must be built around an officially explained inability to obtain them.
A cautious but useful inference from the official materials is that same-sex cases can become especially
document-sensitive where the country of origin does not offer a marriage-equivalent institution,
records the relationship in a stigmatising way, or makes it unsafe to approach authorities for civil-status
or passport purposes. The legal basis for handling those cases is not a special same-sex exception;
rather, it lies in the general hardship / discretion mechanisms and the official procedural rule that
impossibility or unreasonableness in obtaining a national travel document must be assessed case by
case by the canton. That should be made explicit in internal drafting so that documentary sensitivity is
not mistaken for a different substantive rule.

## Drafting cautions

The safest drafting formula is that same-sex spouses are treated as spouses under Swiss familyreunification law, and same-sex registered partners are treated by analogy to spouses where the
FNIA or related asylum provisions expressly say so. That is the core legal proposition. Do not write as
though same-sex spouses face a special statutory immigration test; the official materials do not support
that.
The next caution is terminological. “Registered partner” is not the same thing as any foreign civil
union. Since 1 July 2022 no new registered partnerships can be created in Switzerland, but legacy Swiss
partnerships remain, and foreign partnerships may still be recognised if they are sufficiently marriagelike in civil-status effect. A PACS-type relationship or comparable legal cohabitation should not be
casually described in internal content as a Swiss-equivalent registered partnership unless an official
source for equivalence is identified.
A further caution concerns transitional foreign marriage cases. If the couple married abroad before 1
July 2022 and the Swiss register still shows a registered partnership, immigration content should
state that a civil-status update may be needed or advisable before presenting the case simply as
a spouse case. Official sources say the update is available, but not necessarily automatic.
For unmarried-partner drafting, do not overstate rights. The official material supports a discretionary
or hardship-based route, not a general entitlement. The same-sex dimension matters mainly because
SEM expressly accepts that a hardship may exist where the partners have refrained from formalising
the relationship because of threatened disadvantage in a partner’s home country. The evidential burden
remains real and usually includes proof of duration, exclusivity, mutual support and practical obstacles
to living together elsewhere.
For procedural drafting, separate immigration permission from civil-status recognition. Official
sources repeatedly show that visas are decided through the cantonal migration authority, but civilstatus events abroad may need to be reported, translated, authenticated, verified and entered before
the file is clean. In internal content, phrases such as “apply for family reunification” should therefore be
accompanied, where relevant, by a warning that a prior or parallel civil-status update may be necessary.
For AFMP drafting, be especially careful with registered partners. The federal AFMP materials clearly
use the spouse category, which covers same-sex spouses after marriage-for-all, but they are less explicit
than Article 52 FNIA on registered partnerships. Where an AFMP-only text needs to discuss registered
partners, it is safer to cite the specific cantonal or supplementary national-law source used, or to
describe the point as practice-based and route-specific, rather than to present it as an uncited blanket
treaty proposition.
Open questions and limitations. The clearest express federal pairing of “registered partner” with
“spouse” appears in the FNIA and asylum-linked materials. For AFMP-specific drafting, same-sex
spouses are clear, but the federal wording on registered partnerships is less direct than under Article 52
FNIA. Mission-level document checklists also vary by country of residence; official FDFA guidance
expressly says that required documents and procedure differ by country, so any document list used in
public-facing drafting should be location-qualified unless it comes from the general federal rule rather
than a mission checklist.
