## Executive legal summary
Swiss family reunification law is centrally structured but locally administered. The substantive
entitlement framework is federal: for most non-EU/EFTA cases, the core rules sit in the Foreign
Nationals and Integration Act on family reunification, especially Articles 42 to 52, with implementing
detail in the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment. For EU/EFTA mobility cases, family
reunification is primarily governed by the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its Annex I,
which gives a derived residence right to defined family members and limits the documents that may be
required. The FNIA itself expressly yields where international agreements such as the AFMP apply or
where the FNIA is more favourable.
In practice, the key divide is this: federal law fixes the legal categories, eligibility conditions, deadlines,
and baseline procedural architecture, while cantons and, in some cantons, communes determine the
operational pathway by which an application is lodged, checked, registered, and completed. Cantonal
migration authorities are the principal executing authorities for permit issuance and for family
reunification applications in ordinary L/B/C permit tracks, while the SEM retains reserved approval
powers in some cases and exclusive decision-making power in the temporary-admission family
reunification track under Article 85c FNIA. The SEM’s December 2025 directive for third-country family
reunification was expressly issued to delimit competences, detail the procedural variants, and
standardise practice between representations abroad, cantonal migration authorities, and the SEM.
For third-country family reunification under Articles 42 to 45 FNIA, the ordinary sequence is officially
described as follows: the application normally begins with a national visa D request at the Swiss
representation abroad for the applicant’s consular district; the representation performs a preliminary
examination and forwards the file to the competent cantonal migration authority; the canton examines
the merits and decides, subject where applicable to SEM approval; once entry authorisation is granted,
the representation’s task is in principle limited to identity matching and issuance of the visa D. The 2025
SEM directive also makes clear that AFMP-based family reunification is outside the scope of that
directive.
Federal law also leaves room for controlled evidential and procedural variation. The 2025 SEM directive
states that personal appearance at the Swiss representation is not the default rule for filing or collecting
a long-stay visa, but that the SEM may require it for identification or other checks, and that the SEM and
the FDFA maintain a list of states where personal appearance is required in practice because of
document-reliability or abuse-risk concerns. The directive also contemplates exceptional cases in which
\n\n[page 2]\na procedure may begin directly with the cantonal authority rather than the representation abroad.
Those are procedural-administration points, not changes to the underlying federal entitlement rules.
Commune interaction matters because Swiss immigration administration often runs through local
population-registration channels. At federal level, OASA requires arrival registration within 14 days after
entry for stays without gainful activity exceeding three months where an entry authorisation has been
issued, and the registration is made with the office designated by the canton. Cantonal and local
materials then show different allocation models: Vaud uses communal bureaux des étrangers and
contrôle des habitants as intake and transmission points to the cantonal SPOP; Bern directs applicants
to the Einwohnerkontrolle or the Wohngemeinde for the next steps and states that Bern, Biel/Bienne
and Thun have their own responsible offices; Basel-Stadt requires incoming residents to register within
14 days with the Einwohneramt; Geneva centralises much of the work in the OCPM, which is both the
cantonal authority for foreigner permits and a population-register authority.
The most important drafting implication is therefore methodological: separate federal law from local
administration. Conditions such as who counts as a family member, whether there is a right or merely a
discretionary possibility, time limits, the cohabitation principle, housing, social-assistance, language,
and post-dissolution protection are federal. By contrast, online filing portals, commune-first intake,
landlord forms, passport-photo specifications, documentary verification practice, biometric
appointment logistics, and published processing windows are local operational practice. Those local
practices are highly relevant but should never be phrased as if they were nationwide legal rules.
## Structural overview of cantonal practice and procedure
The federal framework can be mapped in three main tracks. The first is the ordinary FNIA track for
family members of Swiss nationals and of holders of C, B and L permits, governed mainly by Articles 42
to 45 FNIA and the corresponding OASA rules on time limits, language, exceptions to cohabitation and
late child reunification. The second is the AFMP track for EU/EFTA sponsors and their family members,
which is broader in scope and gives a derived right of residence to family members irrespective of
nationality, subject to the agreement’s own housing and documentary rules. The third is the temporaryadmission and asylum-related track, where Article 85c FNIA and Article 51 AsylA produce different
competence structures, with the SEM holding central decision-making functions.
At competence level, cantons are the default permit-granting authorities for the relevant short-stay,
residence and settlement permit categories, while the Confederation remains responsible for quotas,
derogations from admission requirements, and the approval procedure. The SEM’s own contact page
states in current terms that family reunification applications and permit-extension requests fall within
the jurisdiction of the competent cantonal migration authorities and should be addressed directly to
them. That is the clearest federal-administrative statement of the day-to-day division of labour.
The ordinary third-country procedure is best understood as a federal-cantonal pipeline. The
representation abroad is the normal filing point and performs a preliminary examination; the canton is
the normal merits authority; and the SEM may intervene through reserved approval. The 2025 SEM
directive states that, where additional investigative measures are necessary, the cantonal migration
authority can instruct the representation abroad to carry them out and may require an advance on
costs before continuing the examination. That is significant for practice because it confirms that
evidence gathering can move back and forth between the canton and the representation, even though
the legal merits remain federal-law questions.
\n\n[page 3]\nLocal interface points then diverge by canton. Zurich allows full files to be submitted directly to the
cantonal migration office at the counter, online, or by post. Basel-Stadt requires the in-Switzerland
sponsor to file a parallel cantonal family reunification request, by post, e-mail or electronic form, while
the foreign family member waits abroad and has no right of entry or stay merely because an application
has been submitted. Bern channels applicants through the Einwohnerkontrolle of the sponsor’s
municipality and, after arrival, through the Wohngemeinde again for follow-on steps; it also states that
Bern, Biel/Bienne and Thun have their own responsible offices. Vaud describes the bureau des
étrangers as the communal front desk that receives and transmits permit requests for the cantonal
SPOP, while its contrôle des habitants supplies registration, identity and civil-status information to the
administration and must forward the stay-title request to SPOP when a foreign national is registered.
Geneva allows online filing through a personal e-démarches account or postal filing on paper M forms,
while Ticino and Luzern publish dedicated online workflows, and St. Gallen’s official portal states that an
application can be made through the Einwohneramt of the place of residence, with e-filing also
available.
The commune layer is especially visible after entry. Vaud’s biometric-card guidance says that, for a first
permit, the applicant first announces arrival at the contrôle des habitants of the commune of residence,
pays fees, and only then is the file transmitted to SPOP, which later issues the invitation to the biometric
centre. Bern says the commune will explain the next steps after arrival. Basel-Stadt states that a person
moving in from abroad or from another canton must register with the Einwohneramt within 14 days.
These are not variations in the legal right to family reunification; they are variations in the mechanics of
post-entry regularisation and card issuance.
For temporary admission, the structure is materially different. Under Article 74 OASA, the request is
filed with the cantonal migration authority, which forwards it with a reasoned opinion to the SEM; under
the 2025 SEM directive, the decision on family reunification and inclusion in temporary admission is
exclusively the SEM’s, and cantonal authorities are not empowered to dispose of the merits themselves.
After entry, the canton reports the arrival and transmits the family members’ identity documents to the
SEM. This is a genuine competence distinction, not just an intake variation.

## Primary legal texts
The primary federal texts for this topic are the FNIA, especially Articles 42 to 52 and 58a; the OASA,
especially Articles 9 to 11, 73 to 77 and 85 to 86; and, for EU/EFTA cases, the AFMP and Annex I Article 3
on family members. The English FNIA translation explicitly states that it is for information purposes only
and has no legal force; close legal analysis should therefore rest on the official German, French or
Italian texts published in the classified compilation.
## Official guidance and administrative materials
The most useful federal administrative materials are the SEM Directives and Comments I. Foreign
Nationals Domain, in force as at 1 January 2026; the SEM’s dedicated family reunification chapter and
annexes; the SEM/FDFA/ASM directive of 4 December 2025 on the procedure for family reunification for
third-country nationals; the SEM contact pages identifying the competent cantonal migration
authorities; and the SEM AFMP factsheet for EU/EFTA family reunification. These materials are not
legislation, but they are authoritative descriptions of current administrative organisation and expected
procedure.

application is lodged, whether the commune has a front-office role, what documentary packaging is
expected, how biometrics are sequenced, and whether the canton publishes processing-times or

## Practical legal analysis
## Practical legal analysis
The core analytical conclusion is that Swiss family reunification is not substantively “cantonal” in the way
that some other migration topics can be. The decisive legal questions remain federal or treaty-based:
which family members are covered, whether there is a right or discretion, the timing windows, what
counts as adequate accommodation, when language proof is needed, when post-dissolution
continuation is possible, and what the consequences of late filing are. Those propositions come from
the FNIA, OASA and the AFMP, not from cantonal websites.
What genuinely varies by canton is the administrative choreography. Some cantons are canton-first and
digital-first, notably Zurich, Geneva, Basel-Stadt, Luzern and Ticino. Others place the commune clearly in
the front line, notably Bern and Vaud. That affects client handling in concrete ways: where the sponsor
should start; whether a commune arrival report or residents’ office contact is needed before or after
cantonal action; whether landlord forms are standardised locally; and how a biometric appointment is
triggered. These differences are procedurally significant for case management and document planning,
but they do not alter the legal framework that the canton must ultimately apply.
The reviewed canton materials also show a second-order distinction between local evidential
administration and substantive legal thresholds. Zurich’s room-count rule and three-month grace
period are a good example. They plainly matter in practice, but they are a local administrative
expression of the federal requirement for suitable or needs-appropriate housing, not a federal roomcount statute. Likewise, Geneva’s insistence that the spouse be able to communicate in French is a local
expression of the federal rule that the spouse must be able to communicate in the national language
spoken at the place of residence. Vaud’s arrival-report language-check process is a local implementation
mechanism for the federal language framework.
AFMP cases require especially careful separation from ordinary FNIA cases. Treaty family reunification is
broader as to eligible family members and simpler as to documentary demands, but the commune/
canton intake may still look similar on the ground because cantonal and local authorities execute the
residence formalities. That operational resemblance should not obscure the different legal basis. The
SEM’s 2025 third-country procedure directive expressly excludes AFMP family reunification from its
scope, which is a strong sign that internal drafting should treat AFMP as a distinct procedural and legal
stream.
## Drafting cautions
A reliable drafting rule is to separate
law
,
federal administrative guidance
, and
local operational
practice
 in every proposition. If the statement concerns eligibility, time limits, cohabitation, language,
\n\n[page 9]\nsocial-assistance, work rights, or post-separation continuation, lead with the federal text. If the
statement concerns filing channel, list of attachments, biometric sequencing, or whether the commune
or the canton is the first intake point, label it expressly as cantonal or communal practice.
Do not blur
entitlement
 and
administrative convenience
. Articles 42 and 43 FNIA use entitlement
language; Articles 44 and 45 do not. Cantonal forms may nevertheless look similar across all categories.
Internal content should not let a common form or portal flatten that doctrinal distinction.
Do not generalise one canton’s evidence rules nationally. Zurich’s housing formula, Geneva’s AL landlord
form and M paper form, and Vaud’s arrival-report language-check process are useful practice
intelligence, but they are not Switzerland-wide rules. The same caution applies to published processing
times such as Luzern’s “up to around five months”; those are administrative estimates that can change
and are canton-specific.
Keep third-country, AFMP, and F-permit/asylum tracks separate. A sentence that is correct for one
stream may be wrong for another. For example, the 2025 SEM directive’s representation-abroad
procedure is the ordinary third-country rule, but it does not cover AFMP cases; and F-permit
reunification is filed with the canton but decided exclusively by the SEM.
Use English translations with care. The English FNIA text expressly says it is for information purposes
only and has no legal force. For operational drafting in English this is still useful, but any close
proposition or quotation should be anchored to the official German, French or Italian version and to the
SR citation.
