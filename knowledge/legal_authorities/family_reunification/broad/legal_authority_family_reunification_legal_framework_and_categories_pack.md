## Executive legal summary
Swiss family reuni cation is not a single route. It is a structured cluster of routes spread across four
main sources: the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration, the Ordinance on Admission, Stay
and Gainful Employment, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons with the EU and EFTA
together with its implementing practice, and the Asylum Act for refugee and temporary-protection
cases. The principal federal family-reuni cation chapter for ordinary immigration cases sits in AIG
chapter 7, especially Articles 42 to 52. Those provisions distinguish sharply between Swiss sponsors,
settlement-permit sponsors, residence-permit sponsors and short-stay sponsors, and they do not give
the same family circle or the same level of entitlement to each group.
At the ordinary AIG level, the core family circle for third-country style reuni cation is narrow: spouse,
registered partner by analogy, and unmarried children under 18. Swiss sponsors and settlement-permit
sponsors have express statutory entitlements, subject to conditions and extinction rules. Residencepermit and short-stay sponsors do not have the same level of right: Article 44 and Article 45 are framed
as permits that may be granted, and SEM’s 2026 guidance expressly states that Article 44 and Article 45
do not create a legal entitlement.
A di erent and materially broader family circle applies under the free-movement regime. Where an EU/
EFTA national has acquired a residence right in Switzerland under the AFMP, family reuni cation
extends to the spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent descendants, and dependent ascendants;
for students, however, the circle is narrower and excludes ascendants. SEM’s AFMP guidance also makes
clear that this is a derivative right linked to the sponsor’s own AFMP residence right, with no general
AIG-style ling deadlines, but with conditions that di er depending on whether the sponsor is a worker,
a self-employed person, an inactive person or a student.
Protection-based routes form a separate cluster. Recognised refugees with asylum fall under Article 51
AsylA, which recognises spouses and minor children as refugees and grants asylum to them unless
special circumstances speak against it; if separation occurred through ight and the qualifying family
members are abroad, their entry must be authorised on request. Temporary protection under Article 71
AsylA covers spouses and minor children of persons in need of protection, and current Switzerlandspeci c Status S materials for Ukraine also use a broader operational family de nition that includes
partners, minor children and certain other close relatives who were wholly or partly supported at the
time of ight. Provisionally admitted persons use a di erent, more restrictive statutory route now
referred to in current o cial practice as Article 85c AIG: spouse and unmarried children under 18 may
join no earlier than three years after the temporary-admission decision, and only if strict housing,
support, language and supplementary-bene t conditions are met.

For internal drafting purposes, the single most important structural point is this: Swiss content must
always separate ordinary AIG reuni cation, AFMP free-movement reuni cation, refugee family asylum,
temporary-protection family protection, and the provisionally-admitted route. Those routes use
di erent family de nitions, di erent ling timelines, di erent language and maintenance tests, and
di erent levels of enforceability. A generic statement that “Swiss law allows spouses, partners, children
and parents to reunite” is wrong unless a speci c legal route is named.
## Structural overview of the legal categories
The framework divides most cleanly by sponsor status.
For Swiss sponsors, Article 42 paragraph 1 AIG gives the foreign spouse and unmarried children under
18 an entitlement to issue and renewal of a residence permit if they live with the Swiss sponsor. Article
42 paragraph 2 creates a distinct and broader route for foreign family members of Swiss nationals who
already hold a durable residence title from a state with which Switzerland has a free-movement
agreement: that wider circle includes the spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent descendants,
and dependent ascendants of the sponsor or spouse. Article 42 also provides an accelerated settlement
consequence: after ve years the spouse may claim settlement if the integration criteria are met, and
children under 12 have a claim to settlement.
For settlement-permit sponsors, Article 43 AIG is the main route. It covers the foreign spouse and
unmarried children under 18 and is framed as a right, but only where the family lives together, suitable
accommodation exists, the family is not dependent on social assistance, the applicable language
condition is met or language enrolment is proved, and the sponsoring side is not drawing (or likely to
draw because of reuni cation) annual supplementary bene ts. Unmarried children under 18 are
exempt from the language requirement, and the spouse may move to settlement after ve years if the
integration criteria are satis ed.
For residence-permit sponsors, Article 44 AIG covers the foreign spouse and unmarried children under
18, but this is a discretionary statutory route rather than a statutory right. SEM’s 2026 guidance states
expressly that there is no legal entitlement, and that cantons may impose stricter conditions. The
statutory conditions otherwise broadly mirror the C-permit route: cohabitation, suitable
accommodation, no social assistance, oral language ability at A1 or enrolment on a qualifying language
course, and no annual supplementary bene ts triggered or potentially triggered by the reunion.
For short-stay sponsors, Article 45 AIG is narrower still. It covers the foreign spouse and unmarried
children under 18 but remains discretionary and carries no legal entitlement. According to SEM’s 2026
guidance, the family members receive short-stay permits with the same validity duration as the
principal permit, and the conditions correspond to the Article 44 route except that the AIG familyreuni cation language requirement does not apply in the same way.
For EU/EFTA sponsors under the AFMP, the family circle is broader and the logic is di erent. SEM’s
AFMP factsheet and 2026 directives treat family reuni cation as a derivative right available irrespective
of the family member’s nationality. The core circle is the spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent
descendants, and dependent ascendants; for students, only the spouse and dependent children qualify.
Workers are not subjected to the same nancial-maintenance test as inactive or self-employed
sponsors, but adequate housing remains relevant. The derivative permit normally lasts as long as the
sponsor’s own AFMP residence right lasts. SEM’s general FAQ also states that EU/EFTA family
reuni cation is not subject to the ordinary ve-year / twelve-month ling deadlines applicable under the
AIG system.

For recognised refugees with asylum, Article 51 AsylA is the principal route. It covers spouses and
minor children, recognises them as refugees and grants asylum unless special circumstances speak
against it, extends to children born in Switzerland, and requires entry to be approved on request when
separation occurred through ight and the qualifying family members are abroad. This is stronger than
the route for provisionally admitted persons.
For provisionally admitted persons, current o cial practice uses Article 85c AIG. SEM’s current forms,
current general FAQ and the 2025 SEM procedural directive all refer to that article. The route is
restrictive: spouse and unmarried children under 18 only, no earlier than three years after the
temporary-admission decision, and only with cohabitation, suitable housing, no social assistance,
language ability or enrolment, and no annual supplementary bene ts caused or risked by the reunion.
Children are exempt from the language condition. This route should not be described as equivalent to
either AFMP reuni cation or refugee family asylum.
A nal set of limiting rules applies across categories. Article 47 AIG imposes the general deadlines for
ordinary AIG entitlement routes; late reuni cation is allowed only for important family reasons. Article
49 AIG permits exceptions to cohabitation where important reasons justify separate residences and the
family community continues. Article 49a AIG allows exceptions to the spousal language-proof
requirement for important reasons, especially disability, illness or other serious limitations on language
acquisition. Article 51 AIG extinguishes rights in cases of abuse or revocation grounds. Article 52 AIG
applies the chapter mutatis mutandis to registered same-sex partnerships. Article 48 AIG separately
deals with foster children for adoption.

## Primary legal texts
The ordinary foreign-nationals framework is built on AIG Chapter 7. Article 42 governs family
members of Swiss nationals; Article 43 those of settlement-permit holders; Article 44 those of
residence-permit holders; Article 45 those of short-stay holders; Article 47 the deadlines; Article 48
foster children for adoption; Article 49 cohabitation exceptions; Article 49a language-proof exceptions;
Article 50 continuation after dissolution of the family community; Article 51 extinction of rights; and
Article 52 registered partnerships. The statutory language matters. Article 42 and Article 43 are framed
as rights. Article 44 and Article 45 are framed as permits that may be granted.
The implementing-ordinance layer matters because it supplies several operational rules that future
content must not overlook. OASA Article 73 extends deadline rules to the B-permit context; Article 73a
sets the oral A1 language standard for spousal B/C family permits and allows enrolment at the initial
grant stage; Article 73b sets the higher A2 oral / A1 written threshold for later settlement by the spouse
of a Swiss or C-permit sponsor; Article 74 governs the procedure and ling deadlines in the provisionaladmission context; Article 74a sets the oral A1 threshold for the spouse in that context; Article 75 limits
late child reuni cation to situations where the child’s welfare can be safeguarded only by moving to
Switzerland; Article 76 explains separate-residence exceptions; and Article 77, as amended with e ect
from 1 January 2025, links the Article 50 paragraph 1(a) continuation route to oral A1 language
competence.
The free-movement treaty layer is the AFMP family-reuni cation regime, re ected in SEM’s AFMP
directives and o cial factsheet implementing Annex I Article 3. It is legally distinct from the AIG chapter.
Its family circle is wider, its logic is derivative from the sponsor’s primary AFMP residence right, and its
nancial tests depend on the sponsor’s economic status rather than simply importing the AIG structure.
The protection layer relies on the Asylum Act. Article 51 AsylA creates refugee family asylum; Article 71
AsylA creates family protection for persons needing temporary protection. These are not merely
procedural variants of AIG family reuni cation; they are separate legal architectures with di erent
outcomes and di erent qualifying circles.
O cial guidance and cantonal practice notes
O cial guidance / administrative materials. SEM’s 2026 AIG directives are the most useful o cial
interpretive document for building internal content because they translate the statute into route-byroute operational rules. The directives explicitly state that Article 44 AIG creates no legal entitlement
and that cantons may impose stricter conditions; they do the same for Article 45 AIG. They also explain
that ling deadlines apply across Swiss, C-permit, B-permit and provisional-admission routes, that those
deadlines are designed to promote rapid integration, and that late reuni cation is exceptional and must
be read compatibly with family-life guarantees while still being applied restrictively. The same directives
explain the post-1 January 2025 Article 50 regime after dissolution of the family community, including its
extension to the spouses and children of B-permit sponsors and the linked A1 oral-language
requirement for the three-year integration-based continuation route.
SEM also publishes focused guidance for specialised routes. The AFMP factsheet and AFMP directives
are the safest o cial sources for the EU/EFTA family circle and for derived-right analysis. SEM’s asylum
guidance distinguishes clearly between family asylum for recognised refugees with asylum and family
reuni cation / inclusion in temporary admission for provisionally admitted persons, with dedicated
art. 85c AIG forms. SEM’s general public FAQ is also useful for current high-level cross-status drafting

because it states, in one place, that EU/EFTA reuni cation has no AIG-style ling deadlines, while Swiss/
C/B/F routes do, and that provisionally admitted persons must in addition wait three years.
Cantonal practice notes. Cantonal materials are procedurally valuable but should never be mistaken
for the national rule itself. Zurich’s migration o ce pages are particularly useful for how a canton
operationalises the federal tests: Zurich’s public pages break applications down by sponsor type, use a
housing rule of thumb of “number of persons minus one equals number of rooms,” require A1 German
or Swiss-course enrolment for the spouse in B/C third-country cases, and note that naturalisation does
not restart the federal ling deadlines.
Vaud’s o cial pages are especially useful for evidential and edge-case drafting. They explicitly
distinguish the Swiss, C/B/L and AFMP routes; they note that C-permit sponsorship is a right route while
B/L sponsorship is not; they list actual documentary expectations such as criminal-record extracts and
nancial evidence; and they provide a separate “outside the conditions of family reuni cation” page for
ascendants or descendants over 18, explaining that such cases are treated under hardship /
exceptional-admission logic and do not carry a right to a permit.
Ticino’s o cial portals are less analytically detailed but still useful for internal procedural content. They
show that the canton distinguishes EU/EFTA and third-country family-reuni cation requests, requires
third-country family members abroad to le through the competent Swiss representation and normally
to wait abroad for the outcome, and publishes the local fee structure and ling route.
## Practical legal analysis
For internal knowledge-system purposes, the cleanest way to classify Swiss family reuni cation is by
route family, not by the generic label “family reuni cation.” The rst split should be between AIG
ordinary routes, AFMP / EU-EFTA routes, and asylum / protection routes. Within AIG ordinary routes,
the next split should be by sponsor status: Swiss, C, B, L, and provisional admission. That taxonomy
follows the legislation and reduces the risk of importing the wrong family circle or the wrong level of
enforceability from one regime into another.
The second important analytical point is the di erence between express entitlement, quali ed
entitlement, and non-entitlement statutory routes. Swiss and C-permit routes are express
entitlements, but only in a quali ed sense: they remain subject to cohabitation, time limits, abuse and
revocation rules, and—in the C-permit route—housing, maintenance, language and supplementarybene t conditions. AFMP family reuni cation is also best treated as an entitlement route, but of a
di erent legal order: it is derivative from the sponsor’s own AFMP residence right, and the legal family
circle is broader. By contrast, B-permit and L-permit routes are explicit non-entitlement statutory routes.
The provisional-admission route is a restrictive statutory route that current o cial materials do not
present in the same “Anspruch” vocabulary as Articles 42 and 43; it should therefore be handled in
internal drafting as a distinct, more di cult route rather than grouped casually with the clearentitlement routes.
The third point is that parents and adult children do not generally qualify under the ordinary AIG
family-reuni cation chapter. In ordinary AIG drafting, the default family circle is spouse / registered
partner and unmarried children under 18, with a separate adoption route. Ascendants and older
descendants appear only in carefully delimited places: the Swiss Article 42 paragraph 2 route when the
incoming family member already has a durable title from a free-movement partner state, and the AFMP
regime for dependent ascendants and certain dependent descendants. Outside those routes, familybased admissions typically move into exceptional territory such as hardship, dependency or Article 8

ECHR analysis, and o cial cantonal guidance is explicit that such cases carry no right to a permit. That
point is one of the highest-risk areas for web-content overstatement.
The fourth point is temporal. The current o cial materials captured here show a three-year waiting
period for the reunion of family members of provisionally admitted persons and provisionally admitted
refugees. A 2024 federal consultation proposed reducing the general waiting period to two years
following Strasbourg and Swiss administrative-court developments, but the o cial SEM forms, FAQ and
2025 directive still re ect the three-year rule in the current operational framework captured for this
authority pack. Internal content should therefore treat the reduction proposal as background only, not
as current law, unless re-veri ed against a later consolidated o cial source at the time of publication.
The fth point concerns post-arrival continuity. Article 50 AIG is not an initial-reuni cation route; it is a
continuation provision after dissolution of the family community. Since 1 January 2025, SEM’s o cial
materials show that the continuation framework reaches beyond the historic Swiss/C context and now
also covers spouses and children of B-permit sponsors, while short-stay sponsors and provisionally
admitted sponsors remain subject to di erent consequences: L-permit family members may only
continue within the short-stay framework, and provisionally admitted persons retain the provisionaladmission logic rather than acquiring an ordinary residence-permit entitlement by default. This is a
classic point at which website content becomes too simpli ed.
## Drafting cautions
Do not use “family reuni cation in Switzerland” as though it were one homogeneous regime. Internal
content should always name the route family rst: AIG / AFMP / AsylA / temporary admission.
Do not say that parents or adult children generally qualify. That is true only in special free-movement or
dependency settings, or in exceptional hardship-style cases. Ordinary AIG reuni cation is much
narrower.
Do not say that all permit-holding sponsors have a “right” to reunify family members. SEM’s 2026
guidance expressly says there is no legal entitlement under Articles 44 and 45 AIG.
Do not import AFMP concepts into AIG content. Under AFMP, the family circle is broader and the
nancial test di ers according to whether the sponsor is a worker, self-employed, inactive or a student.
Under AIG, the core family circle is narrower and the B/C/L logic is di erent.
Do not treat registered partners as an afterthought. Article 52 AIG applies the family-reuni cation
chapter mutatis mutandis to registered same-sex partnerships, and cantonal pages still operationalise
this alongside the post-2022 marriage-equality framework.
Do not overstate the role of language requirements. In the ordinary AIG family-admission routes, the
spouse of a C- or B-sponsor must show A1 oral competence or enrolment; children are exempt. The Lsponsor route is di erent. Swiss-sponsor cases under Article 42 paragraph 1 do not require an entrystage family-reuni cation language proof in the same way, although a later settlement claim for the
spouse depends on the higher OASA Article 73b threshold.
Do not describe late child reuni cation as merely discretionary in the ordinary sense. O cial SEM
guidance says it is to be used restrictively and, for children, only where the child’s welfare requires the
move.

Do not assume that a 2024 reform proposal is current law. The proposal to shorten the waiting period
for provisionally admitted persons is not re ected as operative law in the current o cial guidance
captured here.
Do not universalise cantonal documentary practices. Zurich’s room-count proxy, Vaud’s criminal-record
extract requirement, and Ticino’s work ow presentation are useful practice notes, but the substantive
categories remain federal.
