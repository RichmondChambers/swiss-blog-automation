## Executive legal summary
## Executive legal summary
Swiss family reunification refusals sit on more than one legal track. The first and most common is the
Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration for Swiss sponsors and many third-country
sponsors; the second is the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons for EU/EFTA situations, for
which the AIG/FNIA is only subsidiary where the treaty or implementing rules do not provide a different
rule or where the AIG is more favourable. The SEM’s current foreign nationals directives also distinguish
a separate family-reunification track for provisionally admitted persons and for recognised refugees
under asylum law. That structure matters because refusal grounds, decision-maker, and litigation route
are not uniform across all “family reunification” cases.
For Swiss sponsors under Article 42 AIG, the official SEM guidance describes a statutory entitlement for
foreign spouses and unmarried children under 18 living together with the Swiss sponsor, subject to the
family relationship being genuine, the cohabitation requirement, the statutory filing deadlines for non-
EU/EFTA situations, and the extinction rules for abuse or revocation grounds. For C-permit sponsors
under Article 43 AIG, the entitlement is more conditional: common household, suitable housing, no
welfare dependence, no annual federal supplementary benefits, and language requirements for
spouses. For B-permit sponsors under Article 44 AIG, the SEM guidance is explicit that there is no legal
entitlement and that cantons may apply stricter requirements. That entitlement/discretion distinction
is one of the most important refusal-analysis fault lines.
The refusal grounds that are most clearly identifiable in official materials are: absence of real
cohabitation or a continuing family relationship; inadequate housing; present or foreseeable reliance
on social assistance; annual supplementary benefits or likely eligibility for them because of the
reunification; missing or insufficient language proof for the spouse; late filing outside the statutory
family-reunification periods; missing “important family reasons” for late child reunification; and
extinction of the claim because of abuse of rights or revocation grounds such as serious public-order or
criminal issues. Public materials from Zürich and Geneva add a practical layer: refusals and delays are
often driven by missing civil-status documents, proof of parental authority, proof of housing, financial
records, and language certificates or course enrolment evidence.
On response routes, official materials are clearest on ordinary appeals, less clear on formal
reconsideration, and quite practical on fresh filings. First-instance family reunification decisions are

usually taken by a cantonal migration authority, so the first challenge is generally cantonal and
governed by cantonal procedure. Zürich’s official pages state that the Security Directorate’s
Rekursabteilung handles appeals against Migration Office decisions and that its decisions can then be
challenged before the Administrative Court; the general Zürich administrative appeal guidance gives a
30-day period and requires a written, signed filing. Geneva’s official materials show that the OCPM is the
competent migration authority, the Tribunal administratif de première instance hears appeals against
OCPM foreign-national-status decisions, and further appeals can go to the Chambre administrative of
the Cour de justice; the TAPI filing page requires postal or counter filing and excludes e-mail filing. For
federal decisions by the SEM, the Federal Administrative Court is the ordinary federal appeal court for
rulings of federal authorities.
A working internal distinction can therefore be drawn as follows. A refusal is usually best appealed
where the applicant had an arguable legal entitlement and the authority arguably misapplied the law,
miscomputed the deadline, ignored existing evidence, or too narrowly assessed “important family
reasons.” A case is often better rebuilt and refiled where the problem is currently factual and curable,
such as missing certified civil-status records, missing parental-authority proof, no spouse language
certificate yet, inadequate housing, or incomplete financial proof. A fresh filing is also often the cleaner
route where the sponsor’s legal position has materially changed — for example, where a previously
non-entitled B-permit situation later becomes an entitlement-based C-permit or Swiss-national track.
The SEM directives expressly note that a person who previously failed because they had no legal
entitlement may file a new application when a circumstance later creates such an entitlement, provided
the timing rules identified by the case law are satisfied.
## Structural overview of refusals and response routes
## Structural overview of refusals and response routes
At the federal-structure level, Swiss official materials show four practically distinct family-reunification
categories relevant to refusal strategy: Swiss sponsor cases, C-permit sponsor cases, B-permit
sponsor cases, and provisionally admitted / refugee cases. The SEM’s December 2025 directive on
third-country family-reunification procedure says that the AIG and Asylum Act family-reunification
procedures differ by legal basis, competence, and decision-making power, and can be grouped into
three family-reunification procedures. Geneva’s current public pages map the same split by sponsor
status, including Swiss, non-EU/EFTA, EU/EFTA, and F-permit pages.
The ordinary AIG route is usually cantonal at first instance. Zürich’s and Geneva’s public materials both
present the cantonal migration office as the authority receiving and examining ordinary familyreunification requests. Geneva states expressly that the OCPM service for foreign nationals is the
cantonal authority competent to examine, grant and renew residence authorisations. Zürich’s practice
paper is framed as the Migration Office’s operative guidance on family reunification. Where the
application is under Article 85c AIG for provisionally admitted persons, Geneva’s page states that the
request is forwarded to the SEM, which decides on it; that is a materially different decision route from
ordinary canton-run AIG cases.
The official materials also support a practical division between eligibility refusals and proof refusals.
An eligibility refusal is one where the law, as currently applied to the facts, does not presently grant the
requested status: examples include missing statutory entitlement under Article 44 AIG, a missed childreunification deadline without “important family reasons,” no longer having a subsisting claim because
abuse/revocation grounds extinguish it, or being blocked by public-order or severe criminal grounds. A
proof refusal is one where the official materials indicate a condition that may be met, but the file is not

yet strong enough: housing, finance, language, civil status, parental authority, translation, or current
address and intent to live together. Zurich and Geneva public guidance are especially useful for
identifying what belongs in that second bucket because they set out required attachments in detail.
The official materials are much less explicit on a general, family-reunification-specific reconsideration
mechanism than they are on ordinary appeal. What they do show is that post-decision changes in
circumstances matter. The SEM directives state that a new application may be filed when a new fact
creates a legal entitlement that did not formerly exist, and that in some public-order cases a familyreunification claim may have to be assessed again after a sufficiently long period of correct conduct
abroad. Conversely, the same directives state that persons whose criminal expulsion has been executed
have no claim to reconsideration of their application. That makes “reconsideration” in this field heavily
fact-sensitive and often subordinate, in practice, to either a fresh appeal against the existing decision or
a materially changed fresh application.
Procedural timing is mostly cantonal for first-instance appeals and therefore should never be
universalised in public content. Zürich’s official guidance gives a 30-day period from notification,
requires a signed written appeal, and states that the applicable route is identified in the remedies notice
of the decision. Geneva official pages identify the competent first-instance court and require filing by
post or at the court counter; TAPI case snippets on OCPM matters confirm that OCPM foreign-nationalstatus decisions go there first. Vaud’s official materials, while less family-reunification-specific in publicfacing guidance, identify the SPOP as the cantonal population authority, the CDAP as the last-instance
cantonal administrative court, and public appeal models under the Vaud administrative procedure law
use a 30-day period.

## Primary legal texts
The current primary texts that the content system should treat as anchor authorities are the AIG/FNIA
(SR 142.20), especially Articles 42–45 (family reunification by sponsor status), 47 (deadlines), 49
(exception to cohabitation), 50–52 (effects of breakdown and equal treatment of registered
partnerships), 51 (extinction of claims), 58a (integration criteria), 85c (family reunification for
provisionally admitted persons), and 96 (proportionality). The main implementing ordinance is the
VZAE/OASA (SR 142.201), especially Articles 58, 73, 73a, 73b, 74, 75, 76, and 85. For EU/EFTA cases, the
FZA/AFMP remains indispensable and can be outcome-determinative. The most usable public official
synthesis of how those texts interact is still the SEM’s January 2026 AIG directives.
## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes
## Official guidance and administrative materials
For federal operational guidance, the most important current source is the SEM’s “Weisungen und
Erläuterungen I. Ausländerbereich” in the version stated to be current as of 1 January 2026. This is
the best official consolidated administrative source for identifying refusal grounds, entitlement versus
discretion, deadline operation, the meaning of “important family reasons,” language-proof mechanics,
the effect of abuse and revocation grounds, and the interaction between family reunification and
public-order considerations. Its chapter structure is highly usable for internal knowledge management
because it mirrors sponsor category by sponsor category.
A second high-value federal source is the SEM’s 4 December 2025 directive on family-reunification
procedures for third-country nationals. Its distinct value is procedural: it distinguishes the applicable
legal bases, competencies and decision-making roles in the ordinary AIG route, the provisionaladmission route under Article 85c AIG, and asylum-law inclusion/refugee-family procedures. Where
content needs to say who decides and where to file, this directive is more useful than general rightsfocused factsheets.
For the free-movement track, the official SEM factsheet on family reunification under the AFMP/FZA
is a necessary check before drafting refusal content. It states, among other things, that suitable housing
is required, that workers’ family-reunification rights are not conditioned on financial means in the same
way as economically inactive persons, and that self-employed or economically inactive persons need
sufficient means. That official distinction is important because a content block drafted from Articles 43
or 44 AIG can be materially wrong if pasted into an AFMP case.

## Cantonal practice notes
The best current cantonal public practice note identified is Zürich’s Migration Office family
reunification guidance PDF. It not only restates federal requirements but adds operational detail that
is useful for internal content design. It explains, for example, that when housing is too small the office
may give a three-month period to secure suitable housing before refusing, provided the other
conditions are met. It also requires evidence of important reasons for late child applications and
organises the document requirements by sponsor category. That makes it highly useful for a “how to
cure the file” knowledge module.
Geneva’s current ge.ch family reunification pages are the most useful cantonal public source for
identifying what a rebuilt case file should contain. For non-EU/EFTA sponsors, Geneva lists the M-form,
ID copies, passport photos, lease or landlord attestation, proof of financial means, marriage and birth
certificates with official translation where needed, proof of parental authority if children are involved,
and a French certificate or course-enrolment proof for the spouse. For F-permit cases, Geneva adds a
motivation letter, lease/room-count proof, six recent salary slips, health-insurance documents, a welfare
attestation, non-prosecution certificate, criminal-record extract, and the spouse’s French proof or course
registration. That is unusually concrete official guidance on how to strengthen a new filing after a
documentary weakness.
Publicly accessible Vaud material is thinner on family-reunification refusal handling itself, but it does
support a procedural baseline: the SPOP is the cantonal population authority; the CDAP is identified as
the canton’s final-instance administrative court; and official Vaud appeal templates under the
administrative procedure law use a 30-day appeal period. Publicly surfaced Ticino materials confirm
that the Ufficio della migrazione / Sezione della popolazione issues, renews and modifies foreignnational permits and provides online services for new permit requests and permit modifications,
including reunification-related services, but the available public material reviewed here was much less
explicit on refusal-specific appeal and reconsideration pathways than Zürich and Geneva.
## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
## Practical legal analysis
The strongest official line between appeal and reapplication is whether the refusal is fundamentally
about law or about proof/current facts. If the sponsor falls within an entitlement-creating provision —
especially Article 42 or Article 43 AIG — and the authority arguably misapplied the law, counted the
deadline wrongly, ignored a qualifying language enrolment, or underweighted “important family
reasons,” the case is naturally suited to appeal. By contrast, where the public materials themselves
show a checklist of documents or factual thresholds that the file did not meet, a rebuilt application is
often the cleaner path, especially if the case sits under Article 44 AIG where there is no entitlement and
cantonal discretion is broader. This is an analytical inference from the official hierarchy of entitlement/
discretion and from the cantonal attachment checklists, rather than an explicit SEM instruction that
“weak-evidence cases should be refiled.”
A particularly important strategy point concerns missed deadlines for children. Official SEM guidance
says that, after expiry of the statutory period, reunification may be allowed only where important family
reasons exist, and for children the exception is to be applied restrictively with child welfare as the core
axis. The directives then explain that a substantial change in the care arrangements in the country of
origin can be decisive, whereas purely economic motives, better prospects in Switzerland, or late filings
made close to working age are not enough. In internal content terms, that means a routine “fix the

paperwork and reapply” script is unsafe for late child reunification. What must be corrected is not just
the evidence format; it is the substantive narrative of necessity, supported by objective changes in
care arrangements and the child’s best interests.
Another clear reapplication scenario is changed sponsor status. The SEM directives expressly say that
someone who first filed unsuccessfully without a legal entitlement may file again when a later event
creates such an entitlement, provided the original and later filings fit the relevant timing logic described
by the Federal Supreme Court case law cited by SEM. This is the most authority-safe basis for content
that distinguishes between “appeal the old refusal” and “rebuild after a legally significant status
change.” It is materially safer than generic language about unlimited repeat applications.
Public-order and serious criminal-history cases sit differently again. The SEM directives explain that
claim-extinction and revocation logic can defeat family-reunification claims and state that, in some
circumstances, a new assessment may be called for only after a substantial period of correct conduct
abroad; they also state that there is no right to reconsideration after execution of a criminal expulsion
order. Internal content should therefore not present such cases as ordinary “document cure” refusals. If
the legal bar currently remains in place, an appeal may be weak unless the underlying decision is legally
flawed; if circumstances improve over time, the better frame may be a later fresh assessment based on
materially changed facts and proportionality.
## Drafting cautions
First, content should never speak of “a right to family reunification” without identifying the sponsor
status and the governing legal track. Swiss sponsor cases, C-permit cases, B-permit cases, AFMP cases,
F-permit cases and refugee-family cases do not share a single refusal logic. Using uniform client-facing
wording across those categories would overstate rights in some cases and understate them in others.
Second, content should not universalise the appeal route or appeal deadline. The reliable general
proposition is that the first-instance decision is ordinarily cantonal for ordinary AIG family reunification
and federal for certain SEM-decided categories such as Article 85c AIG matters. Zürich, Geneva and
Vaud illustrate that the first appeal body and procedural framing vary by canton, even though 30-day
periods are common in public materials reviewed here. Public copy should therefore say that the
written decision’s remedies notice governs the immediate appeal route and timeframe, unless the
content is expressly canton-specific.
Third, refusal content should distinguish between a final refusal decision and a file-completion stage.
Geneva’s and Zürich’s public materials both show that the authority may require additional documents
or proof before deciding. Internal content that tells users to “appeal a refusal” should therefore be
reserved for formal negative decisions, not for interim requests for landlord attestations, translations,
or language-course enrolment proof. This distinction is partly inferential but closely grounded in the
public workflow descriptions.
Fourth, content should avoid promising that a new application automatically overcomes a late-filing
refusal. Official SEM guidance points the other way: once the deadline has run, late child reunification
requires important family reasons and genuine welfare-based necessity. A new filing that simply repeats
the same family configuration without material change is not the same thing as curing a missing
payslip or supplying a proper translation.

Fifth, content on language requirements should be carefully phrased. The official position is not
simply “spouse must already speak the local language” or “no language needed.” For Articles 43 and 44
AIG, the official materials say that A1 oral competency is required, but initial filing can still succeed if
there is proof of enrolment in a qualifying language course, with hard proof due by renewal; important
grounds such as disability, illness or major impairment can justify an exception. Over-simplified wording
would misstate both refusal risk and cure strategy.
Sixth, content touching provisionally admitted persons should include a monitoring note. Geneva’s
current official page still reflects the three-year waiting rule in Article 85c AIG, but the Federal
Administrative Court publicly announced in 2022 that the rule can no longer be applied strictly and
automatically in light of Article 8 ECHR case law, and the SEM has also described a federal legislative
adjustment project. That does not justify rewriting current law in present-tense client content, but it
does justify a drafting caution that this sub-area is under active doctrinal and legislative pressure.
## Open questions and limitations
Public official materials were strong on ordinary appeal routes and on what a stronger new filing
should contain, but much less explicit on a general, family-reunification-specific reconsideration
procedure outside the asylum/provisional-admission sphere. For ordinary cantonal family-reunification
refusals, formal reconsideration is likely governed largely by cantonal administrative procedure and
practice, which is not described uniformly in public-facing materials. In this pack, reconsideration has
therefore been treated cautiously: as a possible path in changed-circumstances cases, but not as a
universally standardised remedy across Switzerland. The statutory article numbers for the primary
federal texts were cross-checked mainly through current SEM directives and cantonal official guidance
rather than through line-by-line extraction from Fedlex consolidated-text pages, because the current
public retrieval path for some Fedlex pages was unstable during research.
