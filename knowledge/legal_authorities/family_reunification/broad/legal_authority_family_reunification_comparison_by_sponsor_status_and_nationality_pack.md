## Executive legal summary
Swiss family reunification is not a single route. The core domestic framework is divided by the sponsor’s
status under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration: Swiss citizens, settlement-permit
holders, residence-permit holders and short-stay-permit holders are treated under different provisions,
and the SEM’s own procedural guidance expressly separates these sponsor-based routes under Articles
42 to 45 AIG. The Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons with the EU, together with the OLCP
framework, runs alongside that domestic structure and is treated by SEM as a distinct regime rather
than a sub-category of the ordinary AIG procedure.
The most important drafting distinction is the
strength of entitlement
. According to the SEM’s current
guidance, Articles 42 and 43 AIG confer a true right to family reunification when the statutory
conditions are met, whereas Articles 44 and 45 AIG are discretionary provisions. In practical terms, that
means Swiss sponsors and C-permit sponsors sit in a materially stronger position than non-EU/EFTA Bpermit and L-permit sponsors. EU/EFTA sponsors are different again: their rights arise from the AFMP/
ALCP and are often broader as to eligible family members, but remain derivative of the sponsor’s own
underlying free-movement residence right.
A second major distinction is between
Swiss-citizen sponsors
 and
EU/EFTA sponsors
. The default AIG
route for Swiss sponsors is narrower than the AFMP route, because Article 42(1) AIG is limited to the
foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18. By contrast, Article 42(2) AIG creates a special wider
route where the foreign family member already holds a durable residence permit issued by an EU/EFTA
state: in that setting, the circle expands to the spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent
descendants, and dependent ascendants. SEM’s OLCP directives add that Swiss citizens generally
cannot rely directly on the AFMP in purely internal situations, which is why future comparative content
must not casually equate the Swiss-citizen route with the EU/EFTA route.
A third major distinction is the
degree of conditionality
. For C-permit and B-permit sponsors, SEM
guidance highlights a package of conditions that goes beyond simple family relationship and
cohabitation: suitable housing, no dependence on social assistance, no claim to annual supplementary
benefits because of the reunification, and language requirements for the incoming spouse, including A1
oral competence or proof of enrolment in a language course. The B-permit route is additionally
discretionary, and SEM notes that cantons may impose stricter practical conditions when exercising that
discretion. By contrast, the ordinary Swiss route under Article 42(1) AIG is structurally stronger and does
not replicate that same statutory package as an entry gateway.
Finally, the
practical position of the family member
 changes significantly by route. Under the
domestic AIG framework, work access differs across sponsor types: family members of Swiss citizens
and C-permit holders may work without separate authorisation; family members of B-permit holders do
\n\n[page 2]\nnot have a stand-alone statutory right to work, but the activity is not itself subject to a separate
authorisation procedure; family members of L-permit holders remain in the weakest position, because
employment is authorisation-based and tied to the short-stay logic. Under the AFMP regime, spouses
and qualifying children admitted through family reunification have a right of access to the labour
market, while ascendants admitted as dependent relatives do not.

The core primary-text set for this topic is: the

 (AIG/

Ordinance on Admission,

## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes
## Official guidance and administrative materials
The most useful federal guidance set is the SEM’s
Directives LEI
 and
Directives OLCP
, both current into
2026, together with the SEM’s December 2025 directive on the
procedure for family reunification for
third-country nationals
. The December 2025 directive is especially useful because it sets out, in one
place, the sponsor-status split between Swiss/C/B/L routes, the division of competence between Swiss
representations abroad, cantonal migration authorities and SEM, and the fact that AFMP family
reunification is outside that particular third-country procedure note.
For public-facing federal explanations, the SEM’s AFMP family-reunification factsheet is particularly
helpful on the EU/EFTA side because it states in simple terms who may be sponsored, which categories
must show funds, which family members may work, and that third-country family members with a valid
residence title from a Schengen state are exempt from the visa requirement. The official portal ch.ch is
also useful as a high-level federal explainer, but it is less precise than the SEM directives for comparative
legal drafting.
## Cantonal practice notes
Cantonal materials are important not because they change the federal law, but because they show how
the federal categories are operationalised in evidence and process. Geneva’s official OCPM page for
\n\n[page 8]\nSwiss-national sponsors sharply distinguishes between the Swiss route for family members with
durable EU/EFTA residence permits and the ordinary Swiss route for family outside the EU/EFTA; it also
requires practical evidence such as lease documents, proof of parental authority and proof of financial
means.
Zurich’s official pages illustrate the evidential intensity of cantonal practice. For B/C sponsor cases,
Zurich asks for items such as twelve months of payslips, the employment contract, a debt-register
extract, health-insurance pricing and policy evidence, and proof of important reasons if the application
is late; for Swiss sponsor cases Zurich still requests financial documentation and late-application
justification, even though the underlying federal legal route is stronger. Those pages are useful
reminders not to confuse
cantonal proof requirements
 with
federal legal elements
, but also not to
understate the practical burden of proof in live cases.
Lucerne’s official migration site is unusually useful for comparative drafting because it publishes
separate route-specific information sheets for family reunification by
Swiss sponsors
,
C-permit
sponsors
,
EU/EFTA sponsors
,
non-EU B-permit sponsors
 and
non-EU L-permit sponsors
, and gives a
processing-time indication of about five months. Its route-specific leaflets also make clear how cantonal
practice maps onto federal law, including Lucerne’s room-count formula for adequate housing and its
route-by-route evidential demands. Bern’s official family-reunification page is likewise useful for
deadline language, especially the explicit statement that late child reunification is granted only for
important family reasons.
## Practical legal analysis
The dominant analytical axis is
not nationality alone
, but the combination of nationality and sponsor
status. A Swiss sponsor is not the same as a C-permit sponsor; a non-EU B-permit sponsor is not the
same as an EU/EFTA B-permit sponsor; and an EU/EFTA L-permit sponsor with a genuine AFMP
residence right sits in a different legal universe from a non-EU L-permit sponsor under Article 45 AIG.
Future drafting should therefore avoid short formulas such as “family reunification with a B permit”
unless they immediately specify
which B permit
, under
which regime
, and
for which sponsor
nationality
.
For
Swiss sponsors
, the strongest internal drafting point is that the ordinary domestic route is both
stronger and narrower. It is stronger because it is a right-based route. It is narrower because its default
beneficiary circle is limited to spouse and unmarried minor children, unlike the AFMP circle. The special
Article 42(2) AIG route widens the position, but only where the foreign family member already has the
qualifying durable EU/EFTA residence permit. That is why content comparing “Swiss” and “EU citizens”
should be drafted with particular care: the Swiss route is not simply the EU/EFTA route in domestic
dress.
For
C-permit sponsors
, the legal position is robust but conditional. The family member has a genuine
right once the statutory conditions are met, yet those conditions are more demanding than under the
ordinary Swiss route because SEM guidance expressly foregrounds housing, financial self-sufficiency,
supplementary-benefit control and spouse-language competence. For internal content, this is best
described as a
strong rights-based route with structured admissibility filters
.
For
non-EU/EFTA B-permit sponsors
, the most accurate characterisation is a
conditional and
discretionary route
. The statutory inputs look similar to the C-permit route, but the absence of
entitlement matters. SEM expressly states that no right exists and that cantons may apply stricter
\n\n[page 9]\nconditions in practice. Zurich and Lucerne practice materials illustrate the real-world effect of that
discretion by demanding extensive financial and debt documentation.
For
L-permit sponsors
, the route is weaker still. The sponsor is by definition in a short-stay category,
the family-reunification rule is discretionary, and work access for the incoming family member is not
free-standing. This route should therefore be described in internal content as
possible but structurally
fragile
, not as an ordinary parallel to the Swiss, C or AFMP routes.
For
EU/EFTA sponsors
, the key practical divide is the sponsor’s own AFMP status. Workers benefit from
the most straightforward family-reunification position, because suitable housing is the operative
gateway and proof of funds is not required for the reunification analysis itself. Self-employed and
economically inactive sponsors must show sufficient means. Students are narrower again, because they
may only reunify with spouse and dependent children. All of these routes remain tied to the sponsor’s
original right of residence, so future content should describe them as
derivative
 rather than
autonomous.
The implications for
spouses, children, partners and dependency-based applicants
 are not uniform.
Spouses are central beneficiaries across all principal routes. Registered partners are also treated in
official cantonal route materials as equivalent to spouses, with those materials expressly citing Article
52 AIG in their legal-basis sections. By contrast, unmarried cohabiting partners are not presented as
core family-reunification beneficiaries in the principal route materials; Geneva’s official site places the
“non-EU/EFTA cohabiting partner” page alongside, but separate from, the family-reunification route
pages, which is a sensible reminder not to treat “partner” as a synonym for “spouse” in comparative
copy.
Children require especially careful drafting. Under the Swiss and C-permit routes, children under 12 can
receive an immediate C permit, which is a materially more favourable position than for older children.
Filing deadlines matter sharply for children above 12. Mixed-family or stepchild cases also need care:
under the AFMP, stepchildren can fall within the family definition, but custody rules and the child’s best
interests remain important; under the AIG framework, the child of the foreign spouse of a Swiss citizen
may be analysed by reference to that foreign spouse’s own status rather than automatically under
Article 42(1).
Dependency-based applicants are where the AFMP and Article 42(2) Swiss route matter most.
Dependent parents and grandparents are part of the AFMP family circle and, in the Swiss context, part
of the Article 42(2) widened circle, but they are not part of the ordinary Article 42(1), Article 43, Article 44
or Article 45 sponsor groups. Official AFMP materials also emphasise documentary proof of dependency
from the country of origin or provenance. This makes “can I bring my parents?” a question that should
never be answered generically without first identifying the sponsor’s exact route.
Procedure matters too. The December 2025 SEM directive states that ordinary AIG family reunification
under Articles 42 to 45 normally begins with a D-visa application at the Swiss representation abroad,
which forwards the file to the competent cantonal migration authority. The canton decides on
admission, with SEM approval reserved in certain cases. Geneva and Lucerne practice materials add an
important practical point: third-country family members who already hold certain valid long-stay or
Schengen residence documents may be exempt from filing a separate visa application.
\n\n[page 10]\nDrafting cautions
Do not collapse the AIG and AFMP frameworks into one comparison line.
 “B permit” and “L
permit” are incomplete descriptors unless the content also says whether the sponsor is using the
domestic AIG route or the AFMP route.
Do not say that all B-permit sponsors have a right to family reunification.
 Non-EU/EFTA Bpermit sponsors fall under Article 44 AIG, which SEM describes as discretionary; EU/EFTA Bpermit sponsors may instead rely on AFMP rights if they hold an original treaty-based residence
right.
Do not say that Swiss citizens can generally sponsor parents or adult children in the same
way as EU/EFTA sponsors.
 That broader beneficiary circle appears only in the Article 42(2) AIG
carve-out for family members with durable EU/EFTA residence permits, or in distinct crossborder AFMP settings; the ordinary domestic Swiss route is narrower.
Do not treat all deadlines as identical.
 Swiss and C routes use Article 47 AIG; B routes use
Article 73 OASA; Article 42(2) Swiss cases are not subject to the general filing deadlines; and the
SEM directives expressly note that later B-permit deadlines do not back-run into an earlier Lpermit short-stay period.
Do not use “partner” loosely.
 Principal statutory routes centre on spouse, children and certain
dependency-based ascendants. Registered partners should be treated as the spouse-equivalent
category. Unmarried cohabiting partners should be described separately and only where there is
a distinct legal basis for them.
Do not convert cantonal evidence checklists into federal legal elements.
 Zurich’s requests
for debt-register extracts, insurance premiums and extensive payroll evidence are highly
relevant to practice, but they are not themselves the statutory wording of all sponsor routes.
They are best described as cantonal evidential implementation of federal conditions and risk
assessment.
Do not ignore adjacent but different regimes.
 Provisionally admitted persons and asylumfamily routes have their own structures, decision-maker rules and conditions. SEM’s December
2025 procedure directive treats them separately from Articles 42 to 45 AIG and separately again
from the AFMP route.
