## Executive legal summary
## Executive legal summary
Swiss family-reunification residence is, as a starting point, derivative: it depends on the continuing legal
and factual family relationship that justified admission in the first place. During a period of separation,
that derivative position may continue only if there are objectively important reasons for separate
residences and the family community genuinely continues; once the family community is actually
dissolved, the analysis moves to the post-breakdown continuation rules centred on Article 50 FNIA/AIG/
LEI/LStrI. The SEM’s current foreign-national directives treat the distinction between a justified
temporary separation and a genuine family breakdown as fundamental.
The core federal continuation rule is now two-limbed. After dissolution of the marriage or family
community, residence may continue if the marriage/community lasted at least three years in
Switzerland and the integration criteria are met, or if important personal reasons make continued stay
necessary. As of 1 January 2025, the federal legislature expanded and clarified this framework in order
to improve protection for victims of domestic violence, and the SEM’s current materials confirm that the
post-breakdown regime now reaches a wider set of sponsors and family members than under older
practice.
That wider scope matters. Current SEM materials state that Article 50 applies not only to spouses and
children of Swiss citizens and C-permit holders, but also to those of B-permit holders; the 2025 reform
materials further confirm coverage for spouses and children linked to L-permit holders and
provisionally admitted persons, as well as certain already-admitted cohabiting partners under Article
50(4), while registered partnerships are treated analogously under Article 52. At the same time, the
scope of continuity differs by underlying status: for families of L-permit holders, Article 50 does not
convert the case into a B-permit entitlement, but supports continuation only within the limits of the
short-stay regime.

“Important personal reasons” are expressly non-exhaustive. SEM guidance identifies domestic violence,
forced marriage, strongly endangered social reintegration in the country of origin, death of the spouse,
and certain child-related constellations as recognised bases. The list is open-ended, and the directives
emphasise that the assessment remains fact-sensitive and discretionary, albeit within a structured legal
framework.
Domestic violence is a particularly important sub-category after the 2025 reform. Official federal
materials now stress that the listed indicators of domestic violence are not exhaustive and that
competent authorities must consider, among other things, recognition under victim-support law,
confirmation from specialist shelters or services, police or judicial protection measures, medical reports,
police reports, criminal complaints, and criminal convictions. They also state that integration deficits
directly caused by domestic violence must not prejudice the victim and may justify flexibility under
Article 77f OASA/VZAE.
Children are central, but not in a simplistic way. SEM guidance states that a continuing parental
relationship with a child who has a right to remain in Switzerland can itself amount to an important
personal reason. For a non-custodial parent who already held a Swiss residence permit through the
dissolved marriage, the “special intensity” requirement for the emotional relationship is already met
where ordinary contact is effectively exercised under today’s normal visiting arrangements; the
authorities must, however, verify actual contact and financial contribution in practice. By contrast,
custodial minors generally share the migration fate of the custodial parent unless there is an
independent basis, such as the special position of a Swiss child or other unusually strong
circumstances.
EU/EFTA cases must be kept analytically separate. Under the AFMP/ALCP/FZA framework, the spouse’s
right is ordinarily derivative and ends on divorce or death unless the person can establish an original
free-movement right of their own; the SEM’s free-movement directives further state that third-country
ex-spouses of EU/EFTA sponsors are then assessed under the FNIA/LEI post-breakdown regime,
provided the EU/EFTA sponsor still has a Swiss free-movement residence right. Separate AFMP “right to
remain” rules also exist, especially in certain death cases.
## Structural overview and legal propositions
## Structural overview of post-breakdown residence issues
In practice, Swiss post-breakdown analysis works in layers. The first question is the legal route: FNIA
family reunification, AFMP family reunification, or a hybrid case involving an EU/EFTA sponsor and a
third-country family member. The second question is factual timing: is the couple merely living apart for
objectively important reasons while the family community still exists, or has the family community
already been dissolved? The third question is whether continuity can be shown under the “three years in
Switzerland plus integration” limb. If not, the fourth question is whether an “important personal
reasons” case exists, often through domestic violence, child-centred family life, death, forced marriage,
or seriously compromised reintegration abroad. The fifth question is downstream planning: whether
the person is close to settlement, whether a child-based route is stronger than a breakdown route, and
whether any AFMP original or right-to-remain basis can now replace the former derivative status.

## Primary legal texts and official guidance
## Primary legal texts
The controlling federal statutory framework is the FNIA/AIG/LEI/LStrI family-reunification scheme. In
current SEM guidance, the key provisions are Articles 42 to 45 on family-reunification admission by
sponsor category; Article 49 on justified separate residences; Article 50 on post-dissolution continuity;
Article 51 on extinction through abuse or revocation grounds; Article 52 on the analogous treatment of
registered partnerships; Article 58a on integration criteria; Article 90 on the duty to cooperate; and
Article 96 on proportionality. The SEM’s current directives and the 2025 reform materials treat these
provisions as the decisive legal backbone for breakup cases.
The main implementing provisions are in OASA/VZAE/OASA. Current official materials identify Article 76
as the rule governing important reasons for separate residences; Article 77 as the language rule for
claims under the “three years plus integration” limb; Article 77f(c)(4) as the provision requiring flexibility
where integration deficits flow from domestic violence or forced marriage; and Article 31 as a broader
hardship reference point that may still inform the assessment even where it does not independently
control the outcome.
For EU/EFTA family members, the primary suprastatutory framework is the Agreement on the Free
Movement of Persons and its Annex I principles on family reunification and right to remain. The SEM’s
OLCP directives and English AFMP fact sheet confirm that family members’ rights are ordinarily derived
from the original EU/EFTA holder’s status, that divorce usually ends that derivative status unless there is
an independent AFMP basis, and that limited right-to-remain rules remain relevant, particularly after
death.
## Official guidance and administrative materials
The most important federal administrative authority is the SEM’s Weisungen und Erläuterungen I.
Ausländerbereich, current to 1 January 2026. For this topic, the crucial sections are the exception to
cohabitation (section 6.9), extinction and abuse (sections 6.12 to 6.14), and post-dissolution continuity
(section 6.15), including children, death, domestic violence and reintegration abroad. Those directives
are the clearest official synthesis of how the administration currently organises breakup analysis.
The 1 January 2025 SEM directive amendment is separately important because it records the practical
consequences of the 2024 federal reform. It confirms the broadened scope of Article 50, the role of
Article 50(4) for certain cohabiting partners, the continued application of Article 52 to registered
partnerships, the limits of the route for L-permit families, the preservation of provisional admission in F
cases, and the new non-exhaustive list of indicators for domestic violence.

The Federal Council’s 27 November 2024 notice and the SEM’s legislative project page are the clearest
official materials on entry into force and legislative purpose. Together they show that the reform
entered into force on 1 January 2025 and was expressly intended to improve the migration-law position
of domestic-violence victims by expanding and clarifying the post-dissolution regime.
For AFMP cases, the SEM’s OLCP directives and AFMP family-reunification fact sheet are the key official
administrative materials. The directives explain the breakup logic for EU/EFTA sponsor cases, including
the role of abuse, the loss of derivative status after divorce, the possibility of an original AFMP basis,
and the referral of third-country post-divorce cases into Article 50 FNIA analysis. The fact sheet gives the
same structure in short operational form and adds a concise statement of the death-based right-toremain conditions.
## Cantonal practice notes
## Cantonal practice notes
The clearest current cantonal immigration guidance located in this research is Zürich’s Migrationsamt
instruction of 28 March 2025 on residence after dissolution of the marital community. It is particularly
useful because it operationalises federal law into evidential and procedural practice: it requires, among
other things, work-contract and payslip evidence, proof regarding how the person supports themself if
not working, social-assistance confirmation from the last three years, a detailed debt-register extract for
the last three years, and a language certificate. It also states that Zürich, as a matter of canton practice,
expects both oral and written German at A1 level after breakup, even though the federal ordinance text
highlighted in SEM materials centres on oral knowledge. That written-language expectation should
therefore be treated as canton-specific, not automatically national.
The same Zürich instruction is also useful on timing and route-preservation issues. It states that the
hardship rule in Article 50 is tied to the marriage and the residence derived from it, and that where the
underlying family-reunification claim has already lapsed because the spouses no longer lived together
and there were no legitimate reasons for separate residences, Article 50 will generally not revive that
lost claim later. For internal drafting, this is a strong warning against treating Article 50 as a universal
repair mechanism after long unmanaged separation.
Zürich’s 2025 instruction also gives rare canton-level detail on child-centred cases. It explains that, for a
non-custodial parent already resident through the dissolved marriage, ordinary visiting arrangements
can suffice if they are actually exercised; it even describes, illustratively, what counts as “usual” contact
in preschool and school-age cases. Again, that is valuable operational guidance, but it should be
described as Zürich practice based on case law, not as a rigid nationwide schedule.
A second useful canton source is Zürich’s public page on police protection measures in domesticviolence cases. That page states that protection measures by themselves do not affect residence status
and that counselling centres can connect affected persons to specialist migration services or lawyers.
For advisory drafting, this is a helpful practical reminder that protective action and immigration
continuity must be analysed together, but are not legally identical questions.
Bern’s official domestic-violence legal information page provides a concise current summary for victims
whose status depends on family reunification. It states that a person who separates after more than
three years of common residence in Switzerland and is integrated may obtain an independent permit,
and that a hardship route is available where domestic violence is proven through medical
documentation, police reports, or reports from a women’s shelter or victim-advice service. That is

simplified guidance, but it aligns closely with the federal framework and is useful as a public-facing
canton summary.
Ticino’s official migration-and-domestic-violence pages are also useful, especially for evidential
handling. They tell victims that the cantonal migration office may require medical certificates, police
reports, criminal complaints, civil protection measures and convictions, and they emphasise the duty to
cooperate by supplying the necessary documentation. Because those pages still use some legacy
statutory labels and framing, they should be used as supportive canton practice only, with current
federal law taking priority where wording differs.
## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
## Practical legal analysis
The first operational question is always route identification. A breakup involving a Swiss sponsor, a Cpermit sponsor, a B-permit sponsor, an L-permit sponsor, a provisionally admitted sponsor, or an EU/
EFTA sponsor does not run on a single legal script. Current federal materials treat these as related but
distinct frameworks, and the consequences after breakdown vary materially, especially for L-permit
families, provisionally admitted families, and EU/EFTA-derived cases. Internal content should therefore
always identify the sponsor’s status before stating the consequences of separation or divorce.
The second operational question is chronology. The decisive timeline is not merely the marriage
certificate and the divorce decree, but the factual history of cohabitation in Switzerland. SEM guidance
makes clear that the three-year rule turns on actual household life in Switzerland, not the formal
existence of the marriage, and Zürich’s 2025 guidance reinforces that a claim under Article 50 generally
will not revive if the underlying cohabitation-based right had already lapsed before the person tried to
rely on breakup hardship. In practice, that means every file should build a dated cohabitation timeline
using registrations, tenancy records, commune entries, correspondence, and any evidence explaining
temporary absences.
The third operational question is the evidence architecture for the chosen limb. For the “three years plus
integration” limb, the evidence should focus on Swiss cohabitation dates, lawful status continuity, work
and income, language, debt and welfare history, and public-order compliance. Zürich’s current checklist
is especially valuable because it shows the sort of evidence a canton may require in routine postbreakdown practice: current work contract, recent payslips, social-assistance certificates, debt-register
extracts, and a language certificate. Even where another canton’s checklist differs, that package is a
useful benchmark for internal system drafting.
For the “important personal reasons” limb, the evidence needs to be themed rather than generic.
Domestic-violence cases should gather protection orders, police records, medical evidence, victimsupport recognition, shelter or specialist-service confirmations, criminal complaints and any judicial
outcomes. Reintegration arguments should be fact-specific, not merely comparative claims that life
would be worse abroad. Child-based cases should include court orders, school and residence
information, calendars of actual contact, travel records, communication logs, maintenance-payment
proof and, where relevant, evidence about the real feasibility of preserving the relationship from
abroad. SEM guidance repeatedly warns that abstract assertions are insufficient.
Children are often the decisive “downstream family-status” issue, but only when presented accurately.
Federal guidance shows a comparatively favourable approach where a non-custodial parent already had
residence through the dissolved marriage and continues to exercise ordinary visiting contact in practice.

At the same time, the same guidance stresses financial contribution, actual continuity of contact,
geographical feasibility and the parent’s conduct. A weak or merely formal family connection will not be
rescued by simply invoking the child’s existence.
Longer-term planning should also consider settlement timing. Zürich’s current settlement instruction
states that a spouse of a Swiss citizen or a C-permit holder has no settlement claim if the marriage had
already definitively failed before the relevant five-year threshold; however, if the five-year threshold was
achieved during genuine married life and the person remains otherwise lawfully regulated afterwards,
Zürich states that the settlement claim may still be derived from the dissolved marriage. That is a
powerful internal planning point for cases close to the five-year line. It also means that breakup analysis
should not end with renewal: it should ask whether a settlement application is already open, nearly
open, or lost.
Future re-partnering must also be handled carefully. For Article 50, the SEM’s current guidance states
that shorter periods of marriage with different partners cannot be stacked to make three years. Zürich’s
settlement instruction similarly says the five-year settlement clock restarts with a later marriage rather
than carrying over from the first. Internal content should therefore resist any suggestion that familystatus time can generally be “added up” across successive relationships.
Finally, AFMP cases require a conscious “pivot” analysis after breakdown. Under official SEM materials,
the ex-spouse may continue in Switzerland if they now have their own original AFMP category, such as
work or sufficient resources, and certain death scenarios may activate a right to remain. For thirdcountry ex-spouses of EU/EFTA sponsors, however, the question often shifts back into the FNIA Article
50 framework, provided the EU/EFTA sponsor still has Swiss residence under the AFMP. Internal drafting
should therefore ask not only “did the marriage end?” but also “what independent residence basis now
exists, if any?”
## Drafting cautions
Do not write that divorce or separation automatically cancels a Swiss family-reunification permit.
The law distinguishes between justified temporary separation, true dissolution, Article 50
continuation, AFMP original rights, and general revocation grounds.
Do not collapse Article 49 and Article 50 into one idea. Article 49 is about continued family life
despite separate residences; Article 50 is about residence after the family community has
dissolved. The evidential burden and legal consequences are different.
Do not generalise canton-specific practice as national law. Zürich’s requirement of written A1
German after breakup is a local practice note; the federal ordinance materials speak in terms of
oral language ability for the Article 50(1)(a) route.
Do not state that domestic violence requires a criminal conviction, or that only physical violence
counts. Official federal materials accept psychological violence and list non-exhaustive indicators
ranging from victim-support recognition and shelter confirmation to police and medical
evidence.
Do not describe child-based continuity in abstract terms such as “has a child in Switzerland”. The
official test is fact-sensitive and centres on actual emotional ties, real contact, financial
responsibility, conduct, and the feasibility of preserving family life from abroad.
