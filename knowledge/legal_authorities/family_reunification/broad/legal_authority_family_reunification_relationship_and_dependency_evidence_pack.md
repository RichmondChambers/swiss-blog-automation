## Executive legal summary
Swiss family-reunification evidence is built on two separate layers that should never be collapsed in
later drafting. The first layer is legal category: whether the applicant is a spouse, registered partner,
child, or—on the relevant routes—a dependent ascendant or older dependent descendant. The second
layer is proof and credibility: whether the claimed relationship, identity, civil status, parental authority,
or dependency is sufficiently established. The federal statute makes the core categories in Articles 42 to
45 and 52 AIG, while Article 90 AIG imposes a positive duty to cooperate by giving complete and correct
information and submitting evidence promptly. The current SEM directive on third-country family
reunification repeats that the burden lies on the persons invoking family ties to prove them.
For the ordinary AIG route, the recognised family nucleus is narrow: spouses, registered partners
treated as spouses, and unmarried children under 18. Current SEM guidance is explicit that same-sex
registered partners are treated like spouses; since 1 July 2022, marriage is open to same-sex couples
and old registered partnerships continue. The same guidance also shows that children of unmarried
parents require attention to custody and actual household arrangements, and that unmarried partners
do not sit comfortably inside the ordinary Articles 42 to 45 AIG framework; if they are considered at all,
the analysis moves into discretionary hardship and “durable relationship” logic rather than ordinary
statutory family reunification.
Dependency matters most on the broader Swiss-with-EU/EFTA-durable-residence route in Article 42(2)
AIG and, even more importantly, under the EU/EFTA free-movement route in Article 3 Annex I AFMP/
FZA as explained in the current SEM VFP directives. Those routes cover descendants under 21 and
descendants or ascendants who are actually maintained by the sponsor. The current VFP guidance adds
that the need must truly exist and be proven, and that the authorities may ask for a certificate from the
home or origin state confirming the kinship and, where necessary, the maintenance relationship.
The official materials do not reduce proof to one fixed federal checklist. Instead, they show a graduated
system: civil-status documents first; then, where necessary, custody or parental-authority evidence,
travel/identity documents, financial support evidence, and—if doubts remain—document verification,
embassy checks, proportional social or neighbourhood inquiries, and in narrowly justified cases DNA
testing. Swiss civil-status practice and canton or mission instructions also explain why applicants are so
often asked for recent originals, legalisations or apostilles, and official translations. The important
drafting point is therefore that Swiss law does not make “a translated marriage certificate” the whole
test; it treats relationship proof as a structured evidential exercise that becomes more demanding when
the record is weak, contradictory, or procedurally sensitive.

## Structural overview of relationship and dependency evidence
The first structural distinction is between the domestic AIG system and the EU/EFTA free-movement
system. Under Articles 42 to 45 AIG, the recognised family members are, in substance, the spouse or
registered partner and unmarried children under 18, with different entitlement levels depending on
whether the sponsor is Swiss, settled, resident, or only short-term resident. Article 42(2) AIG adds a
special Swiss route for foreign family members who already hold a durable EU/EFTA residence status,
and that route adopts the broader family circle of spouse, descendants under 21 or dependent, and
dependent ascendants. Under the AFMP/FZA route, the current SEM directives state that family
members of an EU/EFTA sponsor include the spouse, descendants under 21 or maintained by the
sponsor, and dependent ascendants; “other family members” may benefit from facilitated admission,
but without a direct claim.
The second distinction is between status documents and factual family-life evidence. Civil-status
records prove formal kinship or marriage: marriage certificates, registered partnership records, birth
certificates showing filiation, and—where relevant—divorce or dissolution documents. But Swiss
guidance also expects proof of the operative family situation: who has custody, which parent the child
lives with, whether the family relationship is actually lived, whether the sponsor has in fact maintained
an adult child or ascendant, and why some children are being left abroad while others are brought to
Switzerland. Current SEM guidance on unmarried parents, current SEM guidance on “actually lived”
family relationships, and current cantonal instructions for children of previous relationships all point in
that direction.
The third distinction is between eligibility and credibility control. A person may fall inside a legally
recognised category and still fail because the authorities conclude that the relationship is abusive,
sham, legally invalid, unsupported by reliable records, or insufficiently evidenced. The federal statute
allows claims to lapse when they are asserted abusively to evade admission rules, and spousal cases
can be suspended if there are indications that the marriage may be civilly invalid. The SEM’s 2025
directive also shows that representations may flag signs of falsification, sham marriage, forced
marriage, underage marriage, human trafficking, or other abuse, and may recommend further
authentication or DNA testing.
A useful content-system model is therefore: route selection → legal family category → mandatory
relationship documents → scenario-specific supplements → credibility review triggers. On that
model, a marriage certificate is necessary in a spousal case, but it is not sufficient if the file raises shammarriage or invalidity concerns; a birth certificate is necessary in a child case, but it does not answer
custody and consent questions where a child comes from a previous relationship; and kinship alone
does not prove dependency for an adult child or ascendant.

## Primary legal texts
The core federal statute is the AIG. Article 42 gives Swiss sponsors an entitlement for foreign spouses
and unmarried children under 18 who live with them; Article 42(2) then expands the eligible family circle
for a distinct Swiss route linked to durable EU/EFTA residence, covering the spouse, descendants under
21 or maintained by the sponsor, and dependent ascendants. Articles 43 to 45 regulate family
reunification for sponsors holding settlement, residence, or short-stay permits. Article 52 extends the
spouse rules to same-sex registered partnerships.
For relationship evidence specifically, three statutory provisions matter especially. Article 45a AIG
requires the authorities to report and suspend a spousal-reunification application if there are
indications of a civil-law invalidity ground in the marriage. Article 51 AIG provides that claims lapse
where they are asserted abusively, particularly to circumvent admission and stay rules. Article 90 AIG
imposes the formal duty of cooperation, including truthful and complete information and the prompt
filing of required evidence.
For the EU/EFTA free-movement system, the primary norm is Article 3 Annex I AFMP/FZA, as
summarised in the current official SEM VFP directives. The directive states that family members include
the spouse, descendants under 21 or maintained by the sponsor, and dependent ascendants. It also
states that “other family members” may be admitted on a favoured basis even though they do not enjoy
a direct claim. That broader family circle is expressly said to be wider than the AIG model.
The implementing ordinance matters in two evidential ways. First, as reflected in the Fedlex ordinance
text surfaced through official search results, the permit procedure requires that the documents
presented make out a legal or public-international-law claim, while merely starting family-law
proceedings, renting housing, or taking similar preparatory steps does not itself generate a residence
claim. Second, the current SEM procedure directive ties the applicant’s cooperation duty to the
requirement to present a valid recognised national travel document under Articles 13 and 89 AIG
together with Article 8 VZAE.
A final statutory point relevant to “proof of family life” is Article 49 AIG. It recognises that family
reunification is not defeated automatically by separate addresses if important reasons justify separate
residences and the family community continues. That does not soften proof requirements; it changes
what must be proved. In those cases, the evidential question becomes whether the family relationship
continues in substance even without one shared daily address.
## Official guidance and administrative materials
The current SEM AIG directives frame family reunification as a mechanism to enable and secure family
life in Switzerland. Before approval, the authorities must clarify where the centre of family life will in
future be located; if that centre remains abroad, family reunification conditions are not met. For Swiss

sponsors under Article 42(1) AIG, the same directives say that the right of residence presupposes an
actually lived family relationship, not a merely formal one. Those propositions are important because
they show that “proof of family life” in Swiss practice is not confined to the existence of civil-status
records.
The same AIG directives deal directly with children of unmarried parents. They explain that where the
child lives with both parents, the child’s permit status follows the mother if she has sole custody; where
the parents do not share a household, the child’s status normally follows the parent with whom the
child lives and who holds custody; and where there is joint custody and a shared household, or
alternating care, the status follows the more favourable parent. In practical terms, this means Swiss
official guidance treats filiation, custody, and actual care arrangements as distinct questions that may
each need proof.
The AIG directives also now deal expressly with same-sex and unmarried relationships. They state
that the reunification of foreign registered partners is equivalent to that of spouses, that no new
registered partnerships can be created in Switzerland after 1 July 2022 because marriage is open to
same-sex couples, and that a hardship-based residence permit may be considered where the persons
forgo registration or marriage because of threatened disadvantage in a partner’s home country. That is
official support for the proposition that same-sex spouses and recognised registered partners sit
inside the ordinary relationship-evidence framework, while unmarried partners usually require
discretionary and more fact-intensive proof of a durable relationship.
The SEM directive of 4 December 2025 on third-country family-reunification procedures is the most
important current operational source on proof. It states that applicants relying on family ties must
prove them and cooperate. It allows representations to flag signs of forgery, sham marriage, forced
marriage, underage marriage, trafficking, or other abuse; to recommend further document
authentication or DNA testing; and, with written consent and subject to proportionality, to obtain social
or neighbourhood investigations. On DNA, the directive explains that the authority may make a permit
or benefit in an administrative procedure dependent on a DNA profile where there are justified doubts
about filiation or identity that cannot be removed in another way; the test must be proportionate and
conducted with written consent.
That 2025 directive is also important because it shows how Swiss practice handles weak civil-record
systems. It explains that doubts about family relations arise especially where the state of origin has a
poorly developed, unreliable, or non-existent civil-status register. It further notes that a representation
may conduct voluntary document verification in appropriate situations in order to check authenticity,
correctness of content, and whether the records were properly entered in local registers. This is official
confirmation that “credibility” becomes more fact-sensitive when the registry environment is weak.
The current SEM VFP directives perform the same function for dependency and EU/EFTA routes. They
say that the AFMP/FZA family circle covers spouses, descendants under 21 or maintained by the
sponsor, and dependent ascendants; that students may only be joined by the spouse and dependent
children, not ascendants; that the neediness of the supported person must actually exist and be proved;
and that Swiss authorities may require a certificate from the home or origin authorities confirming
kinship and, if necessary, the fact of maintenance. They also make clear that prior actual support is a
significant evidential element, while a merely abstract civil-law maintenance duty is not enough on its
own.
The VFP directives also show how credibility control works in the free-movement context. They require
a legally existing and genuinely intended marriage; they say the sham-marriage practice explained in
the AIG guidance also applies under the AFMP/FZA; and they give official examples of abusive child

applications, including an older third-country child with no durable relationship to the sponsoring
parent or an 18-to-21-year-old child seeking entry only to continue studies in Switzerland. Those
examples are useful because they show that even within a broader treaty route, Switzerland still insists
on a real family relationship and a non-abusive purpose.
As background on foreign civil-status records more generally, the Federal Office of Justice explains in
current civil-status guidance that where foreign nationals’ data are not already current in the Swiss civilstatus register, the required documents must generally be original, not older than six months, usually
legalised, and translated if not in a Swiss official language. That is not a family-reunification directive as
such, but it is a useful official explanation for why migration files often require recent legalised and
translated civil-status records before the relationship can be accepted administratively.
## Cantonal practice notes
Zurich publishes the most detailed public-facing checklist among the cantonal materials reviewed. For
third-country sponsors with B or C permits, Zurich requires a marriage certificate certified by the Swiss
representation abroad or bearing a Hague apostille, a translated foreign criminal-record extract unless
already in German, English, French, or Italian, and extensive supporting material on the sponsor’s
personal and financial situation. For third-country children, the canton asks for an apostilled birth
certificate, judicial or administrative custody proof for children of separated or divorced parents, and
says expressly that a simple declaration from the parent remaining abroad is not enough. Zurich further
warns that the migration office may have civil-status documents checked by the Swiss representation
abroad at the applicants’ expense.
Zurich’s checklist for Swiss sponsors is similar but slightly different in emphasis. It asks for a birth
certificate with an officially certified German translation unless the document is already in French,
Italian, or English; court or administrative custody evidence for children of separated parents; and a
written relocation consent from the parent abroad where there is joint custody. Again, the canton states
that it may arrange verification of civil-status documents through the Swiss representation abroad. For
internal drafting, Zurich is a strong official example of a canton treating filiation, custody, translation,
and document authenticity as separate evidential steps.
Geneva publishes leaner but still useful evidence instructions. On the OCPM page for EU/EFTA
sponsors, the canton requires identity documents, a lease or host’s attestation, a marriage certificate
and children’s birth certificates with official translation if needed, parental-authority evidence where
only the children are being reunited, and proof of means. Geneva’s public-facing guidance therefore
confirms two operational points: translation requirements are canton-facing and practical rather than
abstract, and parent-child cases often require a separate parental-authority document in addition to the
birth certificate itself.
Vaud publishes particularly clear instructions for blended-family and child-relocation scenarios. Its
pages require copies of marriage, partnership, or birth records with filiation, and, for children born of a
previous marriage or outside marriage, an official document—legalised and, if necessary, translated—
showing custody or parental authority, together with a legalised and, if necessary, translated written
consent from the parent remaining abroad or proof of that parent’s disappearance or death. Vaud also
asks for an explanatory letter where not all minor children are included in the regroupement, which is a
strong official sign that the canton examines the coherence of the family-composition narrative, not just
the existence of a certificate. Its Swiss-sponsor page also notes that for children over 21 relying on the
AIG Article 42(2)-type EU/EFTA-durable-residence route, proof that maintenance is guaranteed is
required.

Ticino appears, on the public page reviewed, to signpost applicants mainly toward federal SEM and EDA
materials rather than publishing a detailed canton-specific evidence list on that page. That does not
mean Ticino has no internal practice; it means the public-facing material is less granular. For the content
system, Ticino is therefore best treated as an example of a canton where users may be directed back to
federal rules and mission-specific instructions rather than to a detailed local checklist published online.
At the Swiss representation level, documentary handling can become even more post-specific. The
Swiss Consulate General in Istanbul’s current family-reunification visa-D checklist for a child states that
all documents must carry an apostille and that the detailed family register extract must be translated
into a Swiss national language by a sworn translator. That is not a universal federal rule for every post,
but it is an official federal example of how consular practice can turn civil-status proof into a more
demanding package of originals, apostilles, and language-specific translations.
## Practical legal analysis
The most important analytical distinction for future drafting is between formal civil status and
credible family reality. In a straightforward spousal case with reliable registers, a marriage or
registered-partnership document, identity documents, and core sponsor documents may be enough.
But where the file contains indicators of sham marriage, forced marriage, underage marriage, or forged
records, the legal issue shifts from “is there a certificate?” to “is this legal relationship genuine, valid,
and reliably evidenced?” Swiss sources expressly authorise that shift.
In parent-child cases, a birth certificate often does not finish the analysis. If the child is from a previous
relationship, or only one parent is sponsoring, the authorities commonly want to know who has
custody, who exercises parental responsibility, whether the other parent consents, and why the family is
being reconstituted only in part. Zurich rejects a mere simple declaration from the parent staying
abroad as insufficient in some cases; Vaud requires official legalised custody or parental-authority
evidence and legalised consent; Geneva asks for parental-authority proof or a dedicated form. That
official material shows that “proof of family life” in child cases is frequently about lawful ability to
relocate the child, not just biological descent.
For same-sex couples, the legal analysis should now be comparatively straightforward at the level of
legal status. Same-sex spouses belong in the same evidential category as any other spouses;
recognised registered partnerships are equated to marriage for family-reunification purposes; and
foreign partnerships capable of recognition in Switzerland remain relevant. The harder cases are
unmarried partners, whether same-sex or opposite-sex. There, the statutory family status is absent, so
the case typically depends on hardship or facilitated-admission logic and much heavier factual evidence
of a durable, serious relationship.
For dependency-based routes, kinship is only the beginning. The AFMP/FZA guidance requires actual
and proven need; it notes that Swiss authorities may ask for a public certificate from the home or origin
authorities; it treats prior actual support as significant; and it links recognition of dependency to the
sponsor’s ability to support the family without social assistance. In practice, a viable dependency file
should therefore usually contain: reliable civil-status evidence of kinship, evidence of the dependent
person’s financial or practical need, evidence of support already provided, and evidence that the
sponsor can continue maintaining the person after arrival. Mere statements of moral obligation or
future intention are unlikely to carry much weight.

Where the record is weak or inconsistent, the official materials point to a phased escalation. First, the
authorities test the file against the duty of cooperation and against the reliability of the registry
environment. Second, they may seek document verification or other consular checks. Third, if justified
doubts remain and no less intrusive way removes them, DNA may be considered, but only
proportionately and with written consent. The sources therefore support an internal drafting rule that
difficult-document cases should be presented as corroboration problems, not as purely discretionary
“trust me” cases. The file should be built outwards from the best available primary records to secondary
official records, to factual corroboration such as family photos and proof of long-term support, and only
then to more intrusive tools.
A further point of practice is consistency across the whole family narrative. This is an inference from
the official materials rather than a single quoted rule, but it is a strong one. The SEM’s focus on the
future centre of family life, Zurich’s demand for the identities and addresses of all children, Vaud’s
request for an explanation where some children remain abroad, and the VFP examples of abusive adultchild applications all indicate that the authorities cross-check chronology, family composition, parental
roles, and motive. A file can therefore be weakened by unexplained omissions, not only by forged
documents.
## Drafting cautions and source list
## Drafting cautions
Do not write Swiss family-reunification content as if there were a single universal route. At least four
branches must be distinguished in later content: ordinary AIG family reunification; the broader Swiss
Article 42(2) route linked to durable EU/EFTA residence; AFMP/FZA family reunification for EU/EFTA
sponsors; and discretionary or hardship-based cases for unmarried partners or non-core relatives. The
evidential demands differ materially across those branches.
Do not treat civil-status proof and credibility proof as interchangeable. A marriage certificate can
prove that a marriage was recorded, but not necessarily that it is genuine, valid for migration purposes,
or free from forced-marriage or sham-marriage concerns. A birth certificate can prove filiation, but not
custody, parental authority, or relocation consent. Swiss official materials insist on those distinctions.
Do not state that apostille, legalisation, or translation is always demanded by a single federal familyreunification rule. The sources support a narrower and more accurate formulation: foreign civil-status
documents in Swiss administrative practice are commonly expected to be recent originals and may
need legalisation and translation; the exact documentary package varies by canton, civil-status context,
and Swiss mission abroad.
Do not say that DNA is routine. The current SEM directive limits it to situations with justified unresolved
doubts, especially where reliable civil registers are weak or missing, and it requires proportionality and
written consent. Later content should position DNA as an exceptional corroborative mechanism, not as
a standard first-line requirement.
Do not describe an unmarried partner as having the same ordinary right as a spouse or recognised
registered partner. Official materials support a claim-based route for spouses and registered partners,
but move unmarried partners into facilitated-admission or hardship logic, where the evidence focus is
on the existence of a stable, durable, serious relationship.

Do not reduce dependency to blood relationship or to a formal maintenance duty. Official SEM
guidance requires a real and provable support relationship and allows the authorities to insist on official
confirmation from the home state. Later content should therefore ask for evidence of actual need and
actual support, not just kinship.
