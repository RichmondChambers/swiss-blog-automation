## Executive legal summary
Swiss family reunification law does not use a single, uniform accommodation rule across all sponsor
categories. For Swiss sponsors under Article 42 AIG/LEI/LStrI, the statute is built primarily around the
requirement that the family live together in Switzerland; for sponsors holding C, B, L or F status,
suitable or appropriate accommodation is an express statutory condition. Official SEM guidance
nevertheless treats housing sufficiency as relevant even in Swiss-sponsor cases because the
cohabitation requirement must be real in practice, not merely formal.
Official federal guidance describes suitable housing functionally rather than by a hard statutory
formula. The key questions are whether the dwelling can actually accommodate the whole family
and whether the arrangement would lead to overcrowding. SEM guidance and several cantonal
materials commonly use a benchmark of number of family members minus one = minimum number
of rooms, but that benchmark appears as administrative practice or a rule of thumb, not as a federal
statutory formula enacted in the AIG or OASA.
The Swiss authorities’ documentary focus is practical and tenancy-facing. Official materials ask for lease
agreements, purchase contracts, room-count evidence, the number of persons sharing the dwelling,
host attestations in subletting or hosted arrangements, and sometimes landlord consent where
occupancy would exceed what the tenancy documents allow. The same materials show that Swiss
procedure often assesses future household formation after arrival, not only the sponsor’s current
domestic situation.
Two cautions are especially important for downstream drafting. First, an independent self-contained
apartment is not always mandatory: SEM states that accommodation shared with a spouse’s parents
can still be adequate if it is not overcrowded, so content should not overstate a “separate flat” rule.
Second, the cohabitation requirement can be relaxed only exceptionally under Article 49 AIG and Article
76 OASA/VZAE, where there are weighty, objective reasons for separate residences and the family
community genuinely continues.
For provisionally admitted sponsors, Article 85c AIG/LEI/LStrI adds an especially important householdformation dimension. SEM’s asylum manual states that the housing and common-household conditions
in Article 85c are materially aligned with the Article 44 route, while also stressing that this route can
apply even where family members are already in Switzerland and may result in new family
communities rather than only the reconstitution of households that previously existed abroad.

Structural overview of housing and household formation
requirements
Swiss sponsors. Article 42(1) AIG requires the foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18 of a
Swiss national to live together with the sponsor. Official SEM guidance adds that the accommodation in
Switzerland must in reality be able to house the whole family, and Zurich’s public guidance translates
this into a requirement that the family will live together in an “appropriate family dwelling”, supported
by the family lease.
Settlement-permit sponsors. Article 43 AIG expressly requires both cohabitation and the existence of
suitable accommodation. SEM’s AIG directives define suitable housing here as accommodation that
can house the entire family and does not lead to over-occupation.
Residence-permit sponsors. Article 44 AIG also requires cohabitation and suitable accommodation,
but this route is discretionary rather than entitlement-based. SEM states that, unlike Article 43 cases,
Article 44 cases do not confer a legal right and that cantons may apply stricter conditions within the
bounds of their powers.
Short-stay sponsors. Article 45 AIG requires cohabitation and suitable accommodation for family
reunification with L-permit holders. Zurich’s internal instruction treats the housing assessment for L/B/C
cases in a common way, using room-count, tenancy limits and landlord-consent logic.
Provisionally admitted sponsors. Article 85c AIG governs the family reunification of provisionally
admitted persons. It requires common household, suitable accommodation, no social-assistance
dependency, and additional conditions; OASA Article 74 channels the application to the cantonal
migration authority, which forwards the file and its view to the SEM, and the SEM determines whether
the legal conditions are met.
Boundary points. Swiss practice must keep three neighbouring regimes conceptually separate:
standard AIG family reunification under Articles 42 to 45; the FZA/ALCP route, which has its own logic
and its own official housing description; and family asylum / asylum-family unity, which follows a
different legal framework again. SEM’s own materials expressly separate those tracks.

## Primary legal texts
The core federal texts are the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (AIG/LEI/LStrI; SR
142.20) and the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment (VZAE/OASA; SR 142.201), both
published in the official federal collection on Fedlex. For this topic, the key statutory provisions are
Articles 42, 43, 44, 45, 49 and 85c AIG, and the key implementing provisions are Articles 74 and 76
OASA/VZAE.
Taken together, those provisions establish the following legal structure. Article 42 is the Swiss-sponsor
rule centred on cohabitation; Article 43 is the C-permit route with an entitlement if cumulative
conditions are met, including suitable accommodation; Article 44 is the B-permit route, similarly
structured but discretionary; Article 45 is the L-permit route; Article 49 creates the statutory exception
to cohabitation where important reasons justify separate residences and the family community
continues; and Article 85c governs reunification with provisionally admitted sponsors. OASA Article 74
specifies the cantonal filing and SEM review mechanism for Article 85c cases, while Article 76 gives
examples of the “important reasons” that can justify separate residences.
Because official Fedlex pages are legally controlling and multilingual, content writers should treat the
authoritative formulations as the German, French and Italian texts of those provisions. Convenience
reproductions may help with article-by-article access, but they do not replace the binding federal
publication.

## Official guidance and administrative materials
SEM’s AIG directives are the most important federal interpretive material for this topic. In the family
reunification chapter, SEM states that family members must live together in Switzerland, that the
dwelling must in reality be able to accommodate the whole family, and that for Articles 43 and 44 a
dwelling is suitable when it allows the whole family to be housed without over-occupation. The
directives also record that some cantonal authorities use room count as a benchmark.
SEM’s separate directive on family reunification procedure for third-country nationals, dated 4
December 2025, is procedurally important. It states that the purpose of family reunification is to make
family life possible in Switzerland, and that the persons relying on family ties must prove the relevant
facts and cooperate in establishing the case, including by giving complete information and providing
the necessary evidence without delay. It also confirms that, in Articles 42 to 45 cases, the competent
cantonal authority examines the merits and grants entry authorisation if the conditions are met.
For provisional-admission cases, SEM’s asylum manual article F7 is especially valuable. It explains that
Article 85c LEI/AIG applies whether the family members are still abroad or already in Switzerland; that
the application is filed through the cantonal migration authority and decided by the SEM; that the
Article 85c material conditions are the same as those in Article 44 LEI except for the special waitingperiod architecture; and that, unlike family asylum, Article 85c can result in new family communities
rather than only the restoration of a pre-existing common household.
As a comparator, SEM’s FZA/ALCP family-reunification factsheet defines an “appropriate” dwelling as one
that corresponds to the local conditions applicable to Swiss citizens. That definition belongs to the
free-movement framework rather than the standard AIG route, but it is still useful as an official federal
description of what “appropriate housing” can mean operationally in Swiss migration administration.
A narrower but practically revealing SEM factsheet is the adoption-entry consent note. In an adoption
context tied back to Articles 42 ff. AIG, SEM states that it concretely checks the number of rooms,
generally on the basis that the dwelling should provide rooms according to the “persons minus one”
benchmark, and that it assesses the file using a lease, employment contract, salary slips, debtenforcement extract, insurance documents and other financial records. Because that note is adoptionspecific, it should not be treated as a general rule of law; it is best used as evidence of the kind of
housing and household-formation proof that SEM considers operationally relevant.
## Cantonal practice notes
Cantonal practice is materially important because the federal statutes and SEM guidance leave room for
procedural detail, documentary demands and local housing assessment. The clearest documented
differences concern what housing evidence must be filed, how room-count is operationalised, and
how future household formation is documented.
Zurich is the most explicit. Its public guidance for Swiss sponsors says the family must in future live
together in an appropriate family dwelling and requires a copy of the family lease. Zurich’s internal
migration instruction then gives more granular rules: for L/B/C reunification the housing must comply
with cantonal building, fire and health rules; room size is calculated according to the “family members
minus one” benchmark; if the intended occupancy would exceed the lease’s maximum occupancy or the
minimum size, landlord consent must be produced; and if consent is refused, the applicants may
receive three months to secure suitable accommodation if the other conditions are already met. Zurich

also treats initial cohabitation as mandatory and applies Article 49 / Article 76 logic once cohabitation
later becomes interrupted.
Geneva requires housing proof in more than one procedural format. For standard non-EU/AELE family
reunification, the OCPM asks for a copy of the lease or, in hosted/sublet situations, a written host
attestation on the cantonal AL form, plus the main lease and identity documents. For F-permit cases,
Geneva’s official page asks for a motivation letter stating the address of the persons to be included and
the intention to live together, plus a lease or another document showing the number of rooms in the
dwelling.
Vaud expressly states that C-permit family reunification is available where the family lives in a common
household, while B- and L-permit reunification requires common household, suitable housing and no
social-assistance dependency. Its document list is particularly useful for household-composition logic: it
asks for the lease showing the number of persons sharing the accommodation, and where minor
children are left outside the application it asks for a letter explaining why the family unit is not being
fully recomposed and what the future intentions are for those children.
Nidwalden provides one of the clearest cantonal definitions. Its 2025 family-reunification factsheet
states that accommodation is suitable when it corresponds to the local conditions for Swiss citizens
at the place of residence, that the dwelling must actually house the whole family, and that the general
guide is “family members minus one = minimum number of rooms”. On evidence, Nidwalden expressly
accepts either a rent contract or a purchase contract for suitable accommodation in the canton.
## Practical legal analysis
The housing requirement in Swiss family reunification is best understood as a household-formation
control rather than as an isolated property-law test. What matters is not ownership or luxury, but
whether the family can establish and maintain a real common household in Switzerland without
overcrowding and without disqualifying welfare dependence. That logic is visible across the statute, the
SEM directives and the cantonal forms.
On current housing versus planned post-arrival housing, the official materials support a careful
inference: Swiss authorities frequently assess whether there is (or will be) accommodation available for
the family’s common household at the time of arrival, rather than insisting that the family already
physically cohabit before the entry decision. Zurich phrases the condition in the future tense, Geneva Fpermit practice asks for an intention-to-live-together letter, and SEM’s adoption factsheet requires proof
that the future adoptive parents will live with the child. Zurich’s three-month cure period is further
evidence that documented future housing arrangements can matter, not just the sponsor’s current flat
on the filing date. This is an inference drawn from the official materials rather than an express general
codified rule.
On room counts and occupancy, the safest internal position is that room count is highly relevant but
not exhaustive. Federal and cantonal materials repeatedly use the persons-minus-one formula, yet they
also look at whether the home is actually over-occupied, whether the tenancy contract permits the
intended occupancy, and whether the landlord objects. Content should therefore avoid reducing the
whole analysis to a crude numerical room test.
On shared housing, federal guidance is significantly more flexible than some oversimplified public
summaries suggest. SEM expressly says that a dwelling can remain adequate even where a married
couple live together with one spouse’s parents, provided the accommodation is not overcrowded. That

makes it unsafe to draft a blanket rule that an applicant must already hold, or must always move into, a
self-contained apartment occupied only by the nuclear family. The legally safer statement is that the
family must have lawful, demonstrable accommodation capable of housing them without overoccupation, and some cantons will then demand stronger tenancy evidence or landlord consent
depending on the arrangement.
On household composition, Vaud’s materials are especially revealing: they require an explanation
where the family unit is not fully recomposed. That shows that migration authorities are not checking
housing in a vacuum; they are asking whether the proposed accommodation matches the actual
family structure being advanced in the application and, where children remain abroad, whether the
future household plan is coherent.
On cohabitation exceptions, Article 49 AIG and Article 76 OASA should be treated as narrow
exceptions rather than routine flexibility. SEM and the Federal Supreme Court make clear that not every
separation counts: the reason must be weighty and objective, and the family community must really
continue. Professional obligations and temporary separation due to serious family problems are
recognised examples, but the exception does not neutralise the general rule that family reunification
aims at actual living together in Switzerland.
For provisionally admitted sponsors, household formation needs especially careful drafting. SEM’s F7
manual confirms that Article 85c can apply even where the family member is already in Switzerland and
that it can lead to new family communities. Internal content should therefore not repeat asylum-family
formulations requiring prior co-residence before flight when dealing with Article 85c immigration-family
cases.
## Drafting cautions and source list
Internal drafting should therefore avoid six common errors. It should not say that all Swiss familyreunification routes have the same accommodation rule; it should not call the persons-minus-one
benchmark a statutory formula; it should not say that a separate self-contained apartment is always
mandatory; it should not collapse AIG, FZA and asylum-family regimes into a single “family
reunification” rule; it should distinguish present housing from documented future post-arrival
housing; and it should remember that English versions of Swiss federal legislation are informational
only, with the German, French and Italian texts carrying legal force.
