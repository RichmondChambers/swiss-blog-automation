## Executive legal summary
Swiss family reunification initiated from outside Switzerland is usually a two-part process: a residencepermit decision under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and a linked entry process
under the visa/entry rules. In the core third-country family reunification route under Articles 42–45 AIG,
SEM’s instruction of 4 December 2025 states expressly that the procedure has two components: the
application for the residence permit and the application for entry or visa. For long stays, the Ordinance
on Entry and Visa Issuance requires compliance with the long-stay entry conditions and, where
necessary, a national visa D.
For those ordinary AIG routes, the default starting point is the Swiss representation abroad for the
applicant’s lawful place of residence. The representation performs a preliminary check, ensures that the
essential documents and information are present, and then forwards the file to the competent cantonal
migration authority. The canton carries out the substantive examination, may request further enquiries
or document checks, and, if the legal requirements are met, issues the entry authorisation that is then
sent back to the representation for issuance of the visa D. SEM approval remains reserved for specific
consent cases.
The general rule is that the foreign family member must wait abroad for the decision. Article 17 AIG
states that persons who entered lawfully for a temporary stay and then seek a permit for a permanent
stay must await the decision abroad, although the competent canton may allow stay during the
proceedings where the admission conditions are obviously met. Personal appearance is not a general
filing requirement, but SEM or the representation may require in-person attendance for identification
or further enquiries, and the 2025 SEM instruction also allows special handling where travel to the
mission is disproportionate or dangerous.
The route changes materially with the sponsor’s legal status. For Swiss citizens and holders of C, B or L
permits, the main statutory bases are Articles 42–45 AIG. For provisionally admitted persons (permit
F), the route is different: the application is filed with the canton, the canton forwards it with a position
statement to SEM, and SEM decides the case under Article 85c AIG; the 4 December 2025 SEM
instruction also says cantons must accept such applications even before the waiting period has expired
and that an initial personal appearance abroad or initial D-visa filing is not required for the cantonal
preliminary stage. For recognised refugees with asylum, the application goes directly to SEM under
the Asylum Act. For EU/EFTA sponsors, the family member’s right is derived from the sponsor’s AFMP
status, suitable accommodation is required, and non-EU/EFTA family members still remain subject to
the general travel-document and visa rules unless an applicable exemption applies.

Cantonal practice matters in a practical sense even though the legal framework is federal. Zurich
publicly allows sponsor-side filing of entry-authorisation applications online, by post or at the counter,
and publishes a room-count rule and extensive documentary checklists. Geneva uses online or postal
permit filing, but still states that visa-required family members must apply for the visa at the Swiss
representation abroad and wait there for the decision. Vaud and Ticino openly state, for important
categories, that an entry request must first be lodged at the Swiss representation abroad. The result is
that later drafting should present the federal sequence as the baseline but should also flag cantonsensitive intake mechanics and documentary handling.
## Structural overview of from-abroad procedure and sequencing
Default third-country procedure under Articles 42–45 AIG
The default structure is: sponsor and family prepare the file; the family member abroad files, in
principle, a visa D application at the competent Swiss representation; the representation checks
jurisdiction, travel document quality, identity indicators and whether the file contains the essential
documents; the file is then transmitted to the competent canton; the canton performs the substantive
family-reunification assessment; if the case is approved, the canton issues the entry authorisation and
sends it back to the mission; the mission performs the final identification step and issues the visa D.
SEM retains an approval role in reserved consent cases.
The SEM instruction is important because it clarifies that the mission’s role is not merely clerical: it
undertakes a summary but meaningful pre-check. It verifies whether the information and documents
are sufficient to establish identity and family relationship, whether the travel document is valid, and
whether local circumstances justify asking for more documents. It may prepare a preliminary
assessment pointing out signs of document fraud, sham marriage, forced marriage, minor marriage,
trafficking or other abuse, and it may recommend document legalisation checks or a DNA test. The
canton remains the substantive decision-maker for Articles 42–45 AIG cases and may commission
additional enquiries through the mission.
The pre-entry sequence is therefore evidence-dependent. The essential documents are those needed
to establish the applicant’s identity and family relationship, such as civil-status material; the mission
may also require more where local documentary reliability is poor or the case raises red flags. If the
applicant cannot obtain requested documents, or if obtaining them is objectively impossible or
unreasonable, the mission should still forward the file to the canton so that the canton can decide the
next procedural step rather than leaving the matter blocked indefinitely at mission level.
Route variations by sponsor status
For sponsors with Swiss nationality, Article 42 AIG gives the core statutory basis for spouses and
unmarried children under 18, provided they live together. For C-permit sponsors, Article 43 AIG creates
an entitlement if the statutory conditions are met. For B-permit and L-permit sponsors, Articles 44 and
45 AIG are discretionary rather than entitlement provisions. In each of those routes, living together
remains central, and for the non-Swiss sponsor routes appropriate housing and social-assistance
independence are express statutory conditions.
For permit F sponsors, the structure is different. Under SEM’s 2025 instruction and current cantonal
guidance, the application is filed first with the canton, the canton runs the preliminary procedure and
then transmits the file with its position to SEM, and SEM alone decides whether to authorise entry and
include the family in the provisional admission. The instruction also says that cantons must accept such

applications even before the waiting period has expired and must not insist at that stage on an initial
personal appearance at the Swiss representation or an initial D-visa application.
For recognised refugees with asylum, the 2025 instruction draws a further distinction: applications
under Article 51 AsylG are submitted directly to SEM, whereas AIG-based applications that are also
available must go via the cantonal authorities. That distinction should be kept separate in any later
drafting because it changes who receives the first filing and who makes the first substantive decision.
For EU/EFTA sponsors, the procedural logic is lighter on admission conditions but still not identical to a
purely internal registration exercise. SEM’s official factsheet says the family member must present a
passport or identity card, a visa if required, and official proof of family relationship; suitable
accommodation is required, and the family member’s right remains a derived right dependent on the
sponsor’s AFMP residence right. SEM’s 2026 VFP directives add that non-EU/EFTA family members
remain subject to the general visa and travel-document rules of the visa ordinance, though certain
Schengen long-stay visas or residence titles can remove the need for a separate short-stay visa in some
contexts.
Role of canton, commune, representation and SEM
The representation abroad is the default intake point for ordinary AIG family reunification from
abroad, subject to limited exceptions. The canton is the main substantive decision-maker for Articles
42–45 AIG cases and the competent authority for visa issuance where the stay itself falls to the canton
to authorise. SEM enters the process either through the federal consent procedure or as the primary
decision-maker in F-permit and asylum-family routes. The commune generally appears later, mainly at
the registration stage after arrival, though some cantonal instructions refer to commune-level arrival
formalities.

## Primary legal texts
The central federal statute is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration. For ordinary
family reunification, Articles 42–45 AIG identify the covered sponsor statuses and family members.
Article 42 gives the basic family-reunification right for spouses and unmarried children under 18 of
Swiss citizens, conditioned on living together. Article 43 does the same for C-permit sponsors, adding
appropriate housing, no social-assistance dependency, language and complementary-benefit
conditions. Articles 44 and 45 govern B- and L-permit sponsors on a discretionary basis, again requiring
cohabitation and, for B and L cases, appropriate housing and financial independence. Article 47 sets the
general family-reunification deadlines; Article 49 allows an exception to cohabitation where important
reasons justify separate residences and the family community continues; and Article 51 provides for

extinction of the right in cases of abuse or revocation grounds. Article 17 is the core “wait abroad” rule.
The relevant implementing ordinance is the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment. Article 5
provides that, if a permit application is approved while the person is still abroad, the competent
authority authorises the Swiss representation to issue the visa; if no visa is required, the authority may
issue an assurance of the permit instead. Article 6 helps with sequencing: where Article 17(2) AIG is
invoked, conditions are “obviously fulfilled” particularly where the submitted documents show a legal or
treaty-based entitlement, there are no revocation grounds, and the person has complied with the duty
to cooperate; but Article 6(2) warns that steps such as renting accommodation, enrolling children in
school, or signing a contract do not by themselves create a claim. Article 8 regulates foreign identity
papers in the registration/permit context. Article 73 sets deadlines for B-permit family reunification and
provides for interviews of children over 14, usually at the Swiss representation at the place of residence,
in late-child cases. Article 74 governs permit-F family reunification through the canton and SEM. Article
75 defines “important family reasons” for late child reunification by reference to the child’s welfare.
Article 76 gives examples of acceptable reasons for derogation from cohabitation.
The entry framework sits in the Ordinance on Entry and Visa Issuance. Article 4 requires, for a stay
over 90 days, both compliance with core entry conditions and—where required—a long-stay visa under
Article 9. Article 21 governs issuance of long-stay visas. Article 22 fixes territorial jurisdiction at the Swiss
representation responsible for the foreign national’s place of residence, subject to exceptions approved
by the canton or accepted by the representation. Article 23 makes personal appearance non-mandatory
as a rule, but allows SEM to require it. Article 24 says SEM determines the list of documentary evidence
to be produced for a long-stay visa. Article 39 places visa-issuing competence with the cantonal
migration authorities where the stay itself falls to the canton to authorise.
The AFMP / EU-EFTA regime is separate. SEM’s official AFMP factsheet states that EU/EFTA sponsors
may extend their derived residence right to certain family members, that suitable accommodation is
required, that the family member’s right remains dependent on the sponsor’s own AFMP right, and that
non-EU/EFTA family members must still present identity, visa and family-relationship evidence where
applicable. SEM’s 2026 VFP directives further state that non-EU/EFTA family members remain subject to
the general visa and travel-document rules. SEM’s same directives also underline that, save for limited
exceptions, family members of Swiss nationals cannot rely directly on the AFMP and instead fall back on
the AIG regime addressed in the AIG directives.
## Official guidance and administrative materials
The most important federal procedural source for this topic is SEM’s instruction of 4 December 2025
on family reunification procedures for third-country nationals. It is addressed jointly to Swiss
foreign representations and cantonal migration authorities, was prepared with the Federal Department
of Foreign Affairs and the association of cantonal migration authorities, and is explicitly designed to
harmonise responsibilities and process flow. It is currently the clearest official source on who does
what, when the file moves from the mission to the canton, how mission-side pre-checking works, how
extra enquiries are triggered, and how Articles 42 ff. AIG, Article 85c AIG and Asylum Act family routes
are to be kept apart.
The SEM AIG directives, updated to 1 January 2026, remain the central interpretive source for the
foreign-nationals regime generally. For this topic, they are especially useful for: the distinction between
entitlement and discretionary family-reunification provisions; the treatment of delayed applications; the
meaning of ongoing family life and cohabitation; Swiss-citizen cases under Article 42 AIG; and the

relationship between AIG family reunification and the AFMP route. They also preserve important
drafting cautions drawn from case law, in particular on cohabitation and late child reunification.
The SEM VFP directives and the official AFMP factsheet on family reunification are the main federal
materials for EU/EFTA sponsor cases. Together they confirm the derivative nature of the family
member’s status, the requirement for suitable accommodation, the continuing relevance of visa rules
for non-EU/EFTA relatives, and the post-entry registration obligations in Switzerland. They are the
correct federal materials to cite where later content covers EU/EFTA sponsors or non-EU/EFTA relatives
of EU/EFTA sponsors.
SEM’s current Entry pages are also useful administrative sources, though less family-specific. SEM
states that Swiss representations abroad are responsible for issuing and refusing visas, not SEM itself,
and it points users to the visa application form and the Swiss visa portal. That is useful for procedural
drafting, especially where operational content needs to explain who physically receives and issues the
visa application.
## Cantonal practice notes
Zurich publishes one of the most operationally detailed official canton-level sets of pages. For Swiss
sponsors, Zurich says the family must live together in an appropriate dwelling and applies a public rule
of thumb that the number of rooms should equal the number of persons minus one. Its sponsor-side
checklist requires, among other things, the entry-authorisation application, civil-status records, the
lease, wage slips, and in some cases criminal-record extracts with certified German translations. For B/C
sponsor cases, Zurich also asks for landlord consent to the move-in, insurance and debt information,
language proof where relevant, and warns that it may have civil-status documents checked through the
Swiss representation abroad at the applicants’ expense. Zurich expressly allows filing at the counter,
online or by post, which makes it a strong example of canton-side sponsor filing in practice even
though the federal baseline is mission-first.
Geneva also presents a mature canton-level practice model. For Swiss and third-country sponsors,
Geneva’s official pages say that visa-required persons must apply for the visa through the Swiss
representation in their place of residence and must wait abroad, while the permit request itself can be
filed online or by post using Geneva’s own process. Geneva’s third-country page also requires common
household, appropriate housing, no social assistance, no annual federal supplementary benefits, and
French-language ability for the spouse, and it specifies a documentary package including identification
documents, housing proof, means-of-support evidence, civil-status records with official translations
where needed, and language proof. For F-permit cases, Geneva states that the application is forwarded
to SEM, which decides it, and gives a canton-specific filing address. Geneva also publishes indicative
processing times of six to eight months for family-reunification applications; that is practical evidence,
but it should be treated as a local administrative estimate, not a legal deadline.
Vaud is useful because it clearly distinguishes between several routes. For third-country family
reunification to B/C/L sponsors, Vaud requires a passport copy including any visa copy, durable financial
means, an original foreign criminal-record extract in the relevant cases, and the usual civil-status, birth,
custody and foreign-parent-consent documents, with legalisation and translation mentioned where
necessary. Vaud also requires the lease and asks, for child cases, how many people already share the
dwelling. On its EU/EFTA pages, Vaud is especially explicit that where a third-country family member is
joining a holder of a UE/AELE permit, an entry request must first be filed with the Swiss consular
representation nearest the person’s domicile. Vaud also illustrates the post-entry role of the

commune: in one AFMP page it asks for an arrival notice to be completed at the commune’s inhabitants
control and foreigners’ bureau.
Ticino provides a clear Italian-language statement of the standard from-abroad rule for third-country
cases: persons residing abroad must lodge the application for entry authorisation for family
reunification with the Swiss representation responsible for their place of domicile and must await the
outcome abroad. Ticino also states an exception for a person already in Switzerland with a valid Swiss
visa or staying visa-free as a tourist. For EU/AELS family reunification, Ticino’s guidance instead frames
the process as a request sent to the migration office within 14 days after entry. Those pages are
valuable because they show, in a different language region, the same basic split between third-country
sponsor routes and AFMP routes.
The surveyed cantonal materials therefore support a cautious conclusion: federal law determines the
legal route, but the practical intake point, document packaging, translation/legalisation
expectations, room-count metrics, online portals, and filing mechanics can vary materially by
canton. Any later public-facing or internal content should reflect that.
## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions
The most important analytical point is that “family reunification from abroad” in Switzerland is
procedurally layered. The residence-permit entitlement or discretion is one question; the ability to
enter Switzerland for the purpose of taking up that residence is another. In ordinary AIG cases, the
canton substantively authorises the stay and, where the stay is cantonally authorised, also drives the
visa issuance; the mission does the pre-check and the final visa mechanics. Drafting that treats the visa
step as a mere formality is inaccurate, but so is drafting that treats the mission as the ultimate
substantive family-reunification authority in ordinary AIG cases.
A second point is that planned post-arrival household formation is enough to make the file
assessable, but it must be evidenced. The law and guidance repeatedly speak in forward-looking terms:
the family must intend to live together, an appropriate dwelling must be available, and the reunited
family must not depend on social assistance. Cantonal checklists therefore ask for current leases,
landlord consent, room counts, wage slips, debt and insurance information, and in some F cases even
health-insurance offers for the incoming family members. But VZAE Article 6(2) is a useful
counterweight: preparatory acts such as renting a home or taking other readiness steps do not
themselves generate the legal claim. They are evidence of readiness, not a substitute for statutory
entitlement.
A third point is that document difficulty is officially built into the process rather than ignored by it.
The 2025 SEM instruction allows the mission to escalate civil-status reliability concerns, recommend
legalisation checks or DNA testing, undertake voluntary document verification and, in proportionate
cases, arrange local enquiries with the applicant’s consent. Equally important, it does not require the
process to stop altogether where documents cannot objectively be obtained; the file must still move to
the canton for a procedural decision on how to proceed. Later drafting should therefore distinguish
carefully between: core eligibility; ordinary proof; and enhanced credibility or authenticity enquiries.
A fourth point is that sponsor status must be separated rigorously in later content. Swiss sponsors,
C sponsors, B/L sponsors, EU/EFTA sponsors, permit-F sponsors and refugees with asylum do not share
a single procedural map. The differences affect the starting authority, the necessity and timing of visa
filing, the presence or absence of an entitlement, the role of SEM, and the documentary burden. This is

especially important because some cantonal websites present user-facing workflows that look similar
on the surface while still resting on different legal bases.
## Drafting cautions
Do not say that all from-abroad family-reunification cases must start at the canton. For ordinary
Articles 42–45 AIG cases, the federal baseline is mission-first, even though some cantons operationally
allow sponsor-side filing or intake. The safest wording is that the case normally starts at the Swiss
representation abroad, subject to canton-specific intake arrangements and exceptional cantonfirst handling.
Do not say that every non-EU/EFTA family member always needs a fresh visa D. Long-stay visa
requirements depend on nationality, and official federal guidance also recognises circumstances in
which an applicable Schengen D visa or valid Schengen residence title affects entry formalities. The
correct formulation is that a visa D is required where the person is subject to the long-stay visa
requirement, and that visa and travel-document status must be checked individually.
Do not write that personal attendance is always mandatory or always dispensable. The legal rule
is non-mandatory filing attendance, but SEM or the mission can require attendance for identification or
additional checks, and the 2025 instruction recognises special handling in hardship situations.
Do not treat the commune as the substantive pre-entry authority. The commune’s role in the
surveyed material is mainly post-entry registration or local administrative handling. The substantive
pre-entry actors are the Swiss representation, the canton, and, in the relevant routes, SEM.
Do not treat processing times as legal deadlines. Geneva’s published six-to-eight-month timeframe is
useful for expectation management, but it is not a federal deadline and should not be generalised
across Switzerland.
Do not collapse civil-status proof into a single universal checklist. Official material shows that
translation, apostille/legalisation, local document-reliability concerns, custody documentation, foreignparent consent, and possible authenticity checks vary by country of origin, by the type of family
relationship, and by canton.
## Open questions and limitations
This pack is strongly grounded in federal and cantonal official sources, but two limitations should be
kept in mind. First, the web-exposed consolidated Fedlex texts surfaced older official PDFs for some
ordinances, so the procedural discussion above relies where appropriate on those ordinance texts
together with current SEM guidance from late 2025 and early 2026 confirming the current article
numbering and operational use. Second, the cantonal practice notes are deliberately selective, not
exhaustive; they show real canton sensitivity, but they should not be treated as a full survey of all 26
cantons.
