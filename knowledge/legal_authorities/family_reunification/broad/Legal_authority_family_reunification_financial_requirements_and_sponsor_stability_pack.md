# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Financial Requirements and Sponsor Stability in Family Reunification

Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Financial
Requirements and Sponsor Stability in Family
Reunification
## Executive legal summary
This pack concerns the financial dimension of Swiss family reunification across the main sponsor
categories under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act: Swiss citizens under Article 42, settlementpermit holders under Article 43, residence-permit holders under Article 44, short-stay permit holders
under Article 45, and provisionally admitted persons under Article 85c. EU/EFTA cases follow the
separate AFMP/OLCP framework, as explained by SEM’s OLCP directives and factsheets.
There is no single universal Swiss income threshold for family reunification. The governing concepts
are instead category-specific: suitable housing, avoidance of dependence on social assistance, nonreceipt or non-entitlement to specified annual federal supplementary benefits, and a fact-sensitive
assessment of whether the sponsor and household can realistically support the family over time. SEM’s
foreign-nationals directives anchor financial sufficiency for Articles 43 and 44 at least to the CSIAS/SKOS
social-assistance standards, while the OLCP framework uses those standards as the reference point
for economically inactive EU/EFTA persons and, for pensioners, also compares means to the threshold
for Swiss supplementary benefits.
For Swiss sponsors, the point most likely to be misstated is this: Article 42 itself does not contain an
express statutory “no social assistance / minimum income” condition. Financial issues nevertheless
remain relevant because the right may expire where Article 51 links the case to Article 63 revocation
grounds, and SEM’s 2026 directives say that in Swiss-family cases the relevant threshold is the concrete
risk of durable and substantial social-assistance dependence, not merely ordinary “financial
worries.”
For C-permit sponsors, the statutory position is strongest in formal legal terms because Article 43
creates an entitlement, but it is still conditioned on cohabitation, suitable accommodation, no socialassistance dependence, language, and no annual federal supplementary benefits. SEM instructs
cantons to assess past and present finances and the probable long-term evolution of the household’s
means, taking into account the financial possibilities of all family members; earning capacity or income
must be concretely proven and likely to remain available over the medium or long term.
For B-permit sponsors, Article 44 is materially stricter in practice because it is discretionary rather than
rights-based. SEM says the family’s means must at least reach CSIAS/SKOS standards, but future
income should in principle not be counted; it may be considered only exceptionally where the future
job is real, secure and sustainably exercisable in light of the family situation. SEM also states expressly
that cantons may impose stricter conditions in Article 44 cases.
For F-permit cases, Article 85c now contains an express social-assistance and supplementary-benefits
screen after the three-year waiting period. For EU/EFTA sponsors, the standard differs sharply by status:
workers generally do not have to prove financial means for family reunification beyond suitable

accommodation, whereas self-employed and economically inactive persons must show sufficient
means; economically inactive persons must avoid becoming a burden on Swiss welfare, and SEM’s OLCP
guidance uses CSIAS/SKOS and, for pensioners, supplementary-benefits thresholds as practical
benchmarks.
For internal drafting purposes, the key distinction is between formal file completeness and
persuasive economic credibility. Formal completeness is about filing the required identity,
relationship and baseline financial documents. Persuasive credibility is about whether the material
actually demonstrates a sustainable household budget once rent, health insurance, debts, alimony and
household composition are taken into account. That distinction is strongly supported by SEM’s 2025
procedural directive, under which the Swiss representation mainly checks whether “essential
documents” are present, while the canton decides the substantive family-reunification conditions and
may require additional evidence.
Structural overview of financial requirements
Swiss citizen sponsor. Article 42 grants the foreign spouse and unmarried children under 18 of a Swiss
national a residence entitlement if they live with the Swiss sponsor. The article does not itself add a
social-assistance or minimum-income clause. However, Article 51 provides that Article 42 rights expire if
there are grounds for revocation under Article 63, and SEM says that in this setting the relevant financial
question is whether there is a concrete risk of durable and substantial welfare dependence.
Settlement-permit sponsor. Article 43 provides an entitlement to family reunification, but only if the
family lives together, the accommodation is suitable, the family does not depend on social assistance,
the spouse satisfies the language condition, and the sponsor is not receiving, and would not become
entitled to receive, annual federal supplementary benefits because of reunification. SEM instructs
decision-makers to look at the household’s past and present finances and the likely long-term
trajectory, not merely a single payslip or a snapshot.
Residence-permit sponsor. Article 44 uses “may be granted”, not entitlement language. The statute
requires cohabitation, suitable housing, absence of social-assistance dependence, language, and no
annual federal supplementary benefits. SEM then adds a practice rule of major importance: the
household must at least meet CSIAS/SKOS levels; cantons may require more; and future income is
generally excluded unless a real, secure and sustainably exercisable job makes the projected income
genuinely credible.
Short-stay permit sponsor. Article 45 is also discretionary. It requires cohabitation, suitable housing,
no social-assistance dependence, and no annual federal supplementary benefits. The SEM directives
state that, apart from the language requirement, the Article 45 financial conditions are the same as for
Article 44.
Provisionally admitted sponsor. Article 85c allows reunification only after three years and only if there
is cohabitation, suitable housing, no social-assistance dependence, language compliance, and no
annual federal supplementary benefits. Geneva’s official guidance reflects those elements and
operationalises them through salary slips, a social-assistance attestation, proof of housing and healthinsurance documentation.
EU/EFTA sponsor. Under the AFMP/OLCP framework, suitable accommodation is always relevant, but
the financial standard depends on the sponsor’s status. SEM’s family-reunification factsheet says that if
the main sponsor is an employee, family reunification is not contingent on the sponsor’s financial

situation, whereas self-employed or economically inactive persons must have the necessary funds to
support family members. SEM’s self-sufficiency materials add that economically inactive persons must
have enough means to avoid Swiss welfare and that sufficiency is judged in principle by whether a
similarly situated Swiss citizen could claim social assistance, using CSIAS/SKOS as the core benchmark.

The core primary texts are the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and the Ordinance on
Admission, Residence and Employment. For present purposes, the most important statutory
provisions are Articles 42 to 45, 47, 51, 62, 63 and 85c AIG, together with OASA provisions on familyreunification timing, language and reporting duties. The English AIG text used for readability is an
official translation mirrored by Refworld; Refworld itself notes that the authentic legal texts are the
German, French and Italian versions.
On the ordinance side, OASA Article 73 governs filing deadlines for B-permit family reunification,
Articles 73a and 73b address language, Article 74 addresses provisional-admission family reunification,
Article 75 defines important family reasons for late child reunification, and Articles 82b and 82d require
reporting of social assistance and supplementary benefits to cantonal migration authorities.
For EU/EFTA cases, the practical legal basis is the AFMP/Annex I together with the OLCP and SEM’s 2026
OLCP directives. SEM’s official materials explicitly locate family reunification in Article 3 Annex I AFMP,
and economically inactive residence in Article 24 Annex I AFMP together with Article 16 OLCP.
## Official guidance and cantonal practice notes
SEM’s 2026 AIG directives provide the most important federal administrative guidance on financial
sufficiency. For Article 43 cases they say authorities must evaluate social-assistance risk by looking at
the household’s past and current position and its probable long-term financial development, taking
into account the means of all family members; earning opportunities and related income must be
concretely proven and likely to remain available at least over the medium or long term; and the financial
base must reach at least the CSIAS/SKOS standards. The same directives say that supplementarybenefits risk is assessed analogically, and that the size of the anticipated deficit is legally relevant.
For Article 44 cases, SEM’s directives are even more operational. They state that family means must at
least correspond to the CSIAS/SKOS standards, that cantons may require more, and that future income
should in principle not be counted. The only stated exception is where the projected income can, with
sufficient probability, be generated over the long term because the job is secure and real and can
actually be exercised in light of the family situation.
SEM also gives a crucial clarification for Swiss-sponsor cases. Its 2026 directives state that in Article 42
cases the relevant revocation grounds are those of Article 63 AIG, and that financal concerns alone are
insufficient: the risk of welfare dependence must be concrete, durable and on a large scale. The
directives further clarify that while supplementary benefits and health-insurance premium reductions
are not migration-law “social assistance” as such, family reunification under Articles 43 to 45 is

separately conditioned on the sponsor not receiving, and not becoming entitled to receive, annual
federal supplementary benefits.
SEM’s 2026 OLCP directives and factsheets draw the main category distinction for EU/EFTA sponsors.
Workers need suitable accommodation but generally do not have to prove financial means for family
reunification; self-employed and economically inactive sponsors do. For economically inactive persons,
sufficient means are judged by whether a similarly situated Swiss citizen would qualify for social
assistance, using CSIAS/SKOS as the benchmark; for pensioners, the means must exceed the Swiss
supplementary-benefits threshold. SEM adds that permits may be limited, revoked or not renewed if the
person later no longer has sufficient means.
Cantonal practice gives the best official picture of presentation. In Zurich, the official familyreunification pages for B/C and L sponsors require the lease, landlord consent, salary slips, work
contract, debt-register extracts, health-insurance premium evidence, health-insurance policies for all
household members, and details of debts or maintenance obligations. Zurich also operationalises
adequate housing through a simple room formula: persons minus one equals rooms. It requires
twelve months’ payslips for B/C cases and six months’ payslips for L cases.
In Bern, the official migration service page adopts the same practical room formula and makes the
documentary financial file explicit. For EU/EFTA sponsors who are self-employed or inactive, the canton
requires proof of the necessary funds. For third-country spouse cases, Bern asks for the lease or
purchase agreement, a Swiss health-insurance premium quote, the policies of household members,
debt/alimony information, the work contract and recent payslips, a debt-register extract, and a
certificate from the social service of the current residence and the residences of the last five years.
In Geneva, the official family-reunification page for a non-EU/EFTA sponsor states the key legal finance
points in plain terms: the sponsor must not depend on social assistance and must not currently receive,
or by reason of the reunification become entitled to receive, annual federal top-up benefits; housing
must be adequate; and the application must include proof of financial means and the lease. Geneva’s
official page for F-permit cases is especially useful for documentary practice because it specifies six
recent salary slips, a social-assistance attestation from Hospice général, a certificate of non-prosecution,
the lease, health-insurance documentation and language evidence.
In Ticino, the public material located in this research was less detailed, but the canton’s official online
family-reunification form states that applicants should be ready with information about their stay in
Switzerland, including any employment activity or financial situation. That is enough to support a
modest practice point: even where public guidance is terse, cantonal implementation can still require
structured financial disclosure.
## Practical legal analysis
The official materials support a clear internal distinction between legal sufficiency and economic
credibility. Legal sufficiency asks whether the correct statutory test is met for the sponsor category: no
express income clause for Article 42, explicit no-social-assistance and no-supplementary-benefits
conditions for Articles 43 to 45 and 85c, and status-dependent self-sufficiency under the AFMP/OLCP
regime. Economic credibility asks whether the evidence makes that conclusion believable in a forwardlooking way. SEM’s directives repeatedly require decision-makers to assess not only current income but
the probable long-term development of the household’s finances.

That means internal content should not describe the Swiss test as a fixed “income threshold” test. In
Article 43 cases, the position is better framed as a social-assistance-risk and supplementary-benefitsrisk assessment anchored to CSIAS/SKOS and household-level probability. In Article 44 cases, it is better
framed as a discretionary sustainability assessment in which future earnings are usually discounted
unless exceptionally secure. In EU/EFTA economically inactive cases, it is best framed as a selfsufficiency assessment against Swiss welfare and, for pensioners, supplementary-benefits thresholds.
Official Swiss materials do not prescribe one federal “budget template”, but they do describe the
building blocks of the budget. SEM uses CSIAS/SKOS as the baseline; Zurich and Bern then
operationalise the budget by asking for rent, health-insurance premiums, debts, alimony and
household-member income records; Geneva requires proof of means and, in F-permit cases, recent
salary slips plus social-assistance and debt-enforcement evidence. The practical implication is that
“budget” in this field should be understood as a household sustainability calculation, not merely a
wage threshold.
The distinction between technical completeness and persuasive credibility is also well grounded in
official procedure. SEM’s December 2025 family-reunification procedural directive says that Swiss
representations abroad mainly verify whether the file contains the essential documents proving
identity and family relationship, while the competent canton then examines the substantive conditions
and may ask for other useful documents. From an internal drafting perspective, that means a file can be
procedurally complete yet still financially weak if the numbers do not convincingly show sustainable
self-support. This is an inference drawn from the SEM procedure directive together with the SEM
financial-assessment directives and cantonal document lists.
In practice, the most persuasive financial file will usually do four things at once. It will show stable
current income; it will show that housing and health-insurance costs are already identified and
affordable; it will address debt, alimony and enforcement history rather than ignore them; and it will
explain any periods of under-employment, recent social-assistance reliance or projected post-arrival
changes in a way consistent with the statutory category. That synthesis goes beyond any one
document, but it follows directly from the official requirement to examine social-assistance risk,
supplementary-benefits risk, the probability of future income, and the structure of the household’s
ongoing costs.
## Drafting cautions
Do not write as if Switzerland has one federal minimum income figure for all family
reunification cases. The law is category-based and uses social-assistance risk, supplementarybenefits rules, and AFMP/OLCP self-sufficiency tests instead of one universal threshold.
Do not say that Article 42 imposes the same express no-social-assistance condition as
Articles 43 to 45. That is inaccurate. The better formulation is that Article 42 lacks an express
income clause, but financial risk can matter through Article 51 and Article 63 revocation logic,
and only where the welfare risk is concrete, durable and substantial.
Do not collapse social assistance and annual federal supplementary benefits into one
concept. SEM expressly distinguishes them. Supplementary benefits are not the same thing as
migration-law social assistance, but they are separately disqualifying or reportable in several
family-reunification contexts.

Do not present cantonal document lists as if they were the substantive national law. They
are implementation evidence and are very useful for documentary strategy, but the legal basis
remains the AIG, OASA, AFMP/OLCP and SEM directives.
Do not overstate projected future earnings, especially in Article 44 cases. SEM says future
income is generally not counted there, except where the job and the ability to perform it are
genuinely secure and realistic.
Do not say that EU/EFTA family reunification always turns on proof of means. For workers,
SEM says it generally does not; for self-employed and economically inactive sponsors, it does.
