## Executive legal summary
For ordinary Swiss family reunification under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and
its implementing ordinance, the core timing rule is that family reunification must generally be claimed
within five years, but applications concerning children over 12 must be made within 12 months. The
SEM’s 2026 AIG directives treat those rules as applying to both spouses and children, and explain that
the policy aim is early integration in Switzerland. The same directives also state that, for ordinary AIG
routes, the child’s age is assessed at the date the application is filed, not at the date of decision.
The start of the clock depends on the sponsor’s legal status. For Swiss sponsors, the period runs from
the sponsor’s entry into Switzerland or the creation of the family relationship; for settlement-permit
holders, from grant of the settlement permit or creation of the family relationship; for residence-permit
holders, from grant of the residence permit or creation of the family relationship. The SEM also explains
two important refinements: a later acquisition of a stronger legal entitlement can, in some
circumstances, trigger a fresh deadline if an earlier application was timely but failed for lack of
entitlement; but time spent in statuses that already allowed family reunification can count against later
deadlines, especially when moving from provisional admission to a residence permit or from a
residence permit to settlement.
If the five-year or 12-month deadline has been missed, a late child reunification application is not simply
lost, but it moves into the stricter “important family reasons” framework. SEM guidance says that, in
child cases, the late application should only be allowed where the child’s welfare requires reunification,
and that stronger reasons are needed the older the child is and the closer the child is to majority.
Children over 14 may also be heard, usually through the Swiss representation abroad, which makes late
teenage cases especially time-sensitive in practice.
Provisionally admitted sponsors are a distinct and especially sensitive category. The SEM’s asylum
handbook states that the statutory wording of Article 85c AIG still sets a three-year waiting period
before family reunification, but—following the European Court of Human Rights in M.A. v Denmark and
the Federal Administrative Court’s leading decision BVGE 2022 VII/6—the SEM must already conduct an
individual, in-depth proportionality review from 18 months after provisional admission. If insisting on
the balance of the statutory waiting period would be disproportionate, family reunification can be
granted before the full three years have elapsed. At the same time, the SEM continues to describe a live
legislative project to reduce the statutory waiting period from three years to two years, so this area
remains legally fluid.
The EU/EFTA regime under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons has a different timing
architecture. Federal materials define the eligible relatives and conditions—such as adequate

accommodation and, for non-employed sponsors, sufficient resources—but do not reproduce the AIG’s
five-year/12-month deadline structure. Geneva’s official implementation page reflects that difference by
stating that EU/EFTA family reunification may be filed at any time, except that non-dependent
descendants must apply before their 21st birthday; the SEM’s VFP directives likewise state that nondependent descendants lose the family-reunification entitlement when they turn 21.
Procedure matters as much as substantive law in age-sensitive cases. The SEM’s 2025 directive on thirdcountry family-reunification procedure says the case normally begins with a D-visa application at the
competent Swiss representation abroad. The representation forwards the file to the canton once the
essential papers are in place and the preliminary review is complete; if important documents are
missing, the applicant is given time to supply them, and if obtaining them is objectively impossible or
unreasonable, the file can still be sent to the canton so the canton can decide whether to continue on
the material available. That procedural design supports a strategy of filing before a decisive birthday or
deadline and then curing evidential gaps, but it does not amount to an express rule that any thin or
defective filing automatically stops the clock. Zurich’s official filing page separately warns that the
examination of the application can take several months, which reinforces the practical need to file early
in age-sensitive matters.
## Structural overview of timing and age-limit issues
Ordinary AIG routes
The ordinary deadline regime sits principally in Article 47 AIG and is operationalised for residencepermit holders and provisionally admitted persons through Articles 73 to 75 VZAE, as summarised in
the SEM’s AIG directives and F7 handbook. The basic structure is straightforward but unforgiving: five
years for most family members, compressed to 12 months for children over 12, with the filing date
controlling the age question in ordinary AIG cases.
Provisionally admitted sponsors
Provisionally admitted sponsors face a two-layer timing structure. First there is the statutory waiting
period in Article 85c AIG. Second, once the temporal gateway is open, the ordinary five-year or 12-
month filing periods under Article 74 paragraph 3 VZAE apply. The critical complication is that the SEM’s
current asylum handbook overlays the statutory three-year wait with an obligatory proportionality
review from month 18 onward. That means “three years” is no longer a complete statement of the
operational law.
EU and EFTA free movement cases
EU/EFTA family reunification is not built around Article 47 AIG. Federal VFP guidance grounds the
entitlement in Article 3 Annex I AFMP/FZA, gives a broader family definition than the AIG, and ties the
family member’s right to the sponsor’s underlying free-movement status. In practice, the age-sensitive
cliff is usually 21 for non-dependent descendants, not 12, and cantonal implementation reflects that.
Refugee and asylum-related routes
Recognised refugees with asylum must not be collapsed into the ordinary AIG framework. The SEM’s F3
handbook treats family asylum and asylum-based family reunification under Article 51 AsylG as a
separate regime. Zurich’s guidance is useful on sequencing: where an AIG claim later becomes relevant
after an asylum-based route has been pursued, the earlier asylum family-reunification filing may be

relevant when assessing whether Article 47 AIG deadlines were respected. That is a niche but important
internal drafting point.

## Primary legal texts
The core statutory framework is the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration (AIG, SR
142.20). For timing purposes, the most important provisions are Articles 42 to 45 on sponsor-categorybased family reunification, Article 47 on filing periods, Article 49 on exceptions to the cohabitation
requirement, and Article 85c on family reunification for provisionally admitted persons. The SEM’s 2026
AIG directives and the F7 asylum handbook are the main official interpretive materials used by the
administration to operationalise those provisions.
The implementing framework is the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment (VZAE, SR
142.201). For this topic, the critical provisions are Articles 73 to 76, especially Article 73 for residencepermit sponsors, Article 74 for provisionally admitted persons, Article 75 on child-welfare-based late
reunification, and Article 76 on justified separation despite the normal rule of living together. The SEM’s
AIG directives and F7 handbook repeatedly use those provisions to define when clocks begin, how
waiting periods interact with filing periods, and how late cases are assessed.
The Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons regime, specifically Article 3 Annex I AFMP/FZA,
governs EU/EFTA family reunification. The SEM’s VFP directives explain that this is a distinct regime with
its own broader family definition, derived rights for family members, and age structure under which
descendants under 21 or dependent descendants qualify. The same federal materials state that AIG
family-reunification guidance is only applied in this area insofar as it offers a more favourable rule or
addresses compatible procedural questions.
The Asylum Act (AsylG, SR 142.31), especially Article 51, creates a separate family-asylum and asylumfamily-reunification framework for recognised refugees with asylum. The SEM’s F3 handbook makes
clear that this is distinct from ordinary foreign-nationals law. For internal drafting, that separation
matters because not every “family reunification” timing question is really an Article 47 AIG question.

## Official guidance and administrative materials
The single most important operational source for ordinary timing questions is the SEM’s Weisungen
AIG, updated to 1 January 2026. It explains the five-year and 12-month rules, the filing-date age rule,
the special mechanics when a child turns 12 during the five-year period, the differential trigger dates
for Swiss/C/B/F sponsors, the consequences of missed deadlines, and the treatment of later status
upgrades or renewed applications. When drafting internal content for the main third-country regime,
this should be the default federal administrative source.
For third-country procedure, the key source is the SEM’s Directive on family-reunification procedure
for third-country nationals dated 4 December 2025. It states that the process generally begins with a
D-visa application at the competent Swiss representation abroad, and it gives detailed guidance on how
incomplete files are handled: the representation identifies missing essential documents, grants time to
provide them, and can still transmit the file to the canton where obtaining documents is objectively
impossible or where the case is otherwise blocked. It also confirms that the canton decides whether the
existing evidence is enough or whether further investigation will be commissioned.
For provisionally admitted sponsors, the most important source is the SEM’s F7 asylum handbook
article. It is the clearest current official statement of the tension between the still-existing statutory
three-year waiting period and the post-M.A. v Denmark / BVGE 2022 VII/6 requirement for an individual
proportionality review already from month 18. It also explains how the later five-year/12-month filing
periods under Article 74 paragraph 3 VZAE fit around that waiting period.
For EU/EFTA cases, the SEM’s VFP directives and EU/EFTA family-reunification factsheet are the core
federal materials. They define the AFMP/FZA categories, the dependency rules, the requirement for
adequate accommodation, the limited role of financial means depending on the sponsor’s economic
status, and the sponsor-dependent nature of the derived family member’s status.
The public ch.ch guidance is useful as a high-level cross-government source, especially for basic
deadlines and public-facing summaries. However, it simplifies some issues that matter in internal legal
drafting. In particular, it says that for F-permit holders the SEM can examine family-reunification
applications after 18 months, whereas the more legally precise SEM F7 handbook explains that the
statutory rule is still three years but that proportionality review must begin from month 18. Internal
content should therefore treat ch.ch as a public summary, not as the last word on nuanced timing
doctrine.
## Cantonal practice notes
Zurich is useful for two reasons: it gives a clear local filing checklist and it openly signals processing
reality. Zurich’s public filing page for B- and C-permit sponsors says the file should include, among other
things, the entry-authorisation form, lease, landlord consent, 12 months of salary slips, employment
contract, debt-enforcement extract, health-insurance quotations, and—if the deadline has already been
missed—evidence of the important reasons for late filing. The same page states that review can take
several months. Zurich also publishes a more detailed practice note stating a room-count rule of
thumb (“number of persons minus one equals the minimum number of rooms”).
Geneva structures its guidance by sponsor category and therefore makes regime differences unusually
visible. Its pages for Swiss and non-EU/EFTA sponsors repeat the ordinary five-year/12-month timing
rules, require the application to be made before entry in ordinary third-country cases, and request lease
evidence or a landlord attestation on the canton’s form, plus financial evidence and, where applicable,

language evidence for the spouse. Geneva’s page for F-permit holders still says “no sooner than three
years”, lists category-specific attachments such as motivation letter, six salary slips and proof of
relationship, and identifies the SEM as the deciding authority. That means Geneva’s public material still
expresses the statutory waiting period in its classical form, even though the SEM’s own F7 handbook
now overlays it with the 18-month proportionality-review obligation.
Ticino is useful for local procedural timing. Its official page for third-country family reunification says
that relatives living abroad must lodge the request for entry authorisation with the competent Swiss
representation abroad and wait abroad for the outcome, subject only to a limited exception for certain
persons already present in Switzerland with a valid Swiss visa or in visa-free tourist status. Its EU/EFTA
page, by contrast, states that the request must be transmitted to the migration office within 14 days of
entry. Those are not federal family-reunification eligibility deadlines, but they are operational cantonlevel timing rules that content should not ignore.
## Practical legal analysis
Hard legal deadlines and softer procedural bottlenecks
The hardest legal deadlines are the ordinary five-year/12-month limits and, in AFMP/FZA cases, the 21stbirthday cut-off for non-dependent descendants. Those are the dates that can extinguish an ordinary
entitlement. But in practice, the real risk often appears earlier: visa-stage filing abroad, document
verification, possible requests for supplementary evidence, and cantonal processing times that can run
for months. The SEM’s 2025 procedure directive and Zurich’s public filing page make that practical
reality unusually clear.
Age-sensitive cases
A case involving a child nearing 12, 18, or 21 should be treated as urgent even where the statute offers
a little more time on paper. For ordinary AIG routes, filing before 12 is often the decisive move because
it avoids collapsing the case into the one-year window. For child cases nearing 18, filing before majority
is critical because the ordinary entitlement concerns minor children; the SEM’s ordinary AIG guidance
and F7 handbook both anchor minority at filing, while the AFMP/FZA regime anchors non-dependent
descendant status at 21. Where a child is over 14 and an interview may be required, extra lead time
should be built in.
Sponsor readiness and evidence sequencing
A frequent strategic mistake is to treat timing as if it were independent of substantive readiness. That is
wrong, especially in Article 44 AIG cases, where there is no legal entitlement and the canton may apply
stricter conditions. The SEM’s AIG directives say future income is generally not counted, save where a
durable, concrete earning prospect exists; Zurich and Geneva both ask for present-tense evidence such
as lease documentation, salary slips, employment contracts, and health-insurance costing. In other
words, the “best filing date” is normally the earliest date on which the application can be lodged before
the legal deadline with real documentary proof of sponsor readiness, not merely a plan to become
ready later.
The SEM’s 2025 procedural directive nevertheless shows that Swiss practice does not insist on a perfect
file before the application can begin. Missing documents can trigger a request for completion, and
where documents are unobtainable the matter can still be referred to the canton for a decision on how
to proceed. For internal strategy writing, the safe formulation is that a timely filing with the essential

identity/family-relationship material may be better than waiting for a perfect file in a case close to an
age cutoff, but applicants should be warned that an incomplete file can still lead to delay, further
investigation, or refusal.
Status changes and second applications
The SEM’s AIG directives give two briefing points that should be preserved in internal content. First, a
sponsor who later acquires a stronger status or a true entitlement may, in some circumstances, obtain
a fresh deadline if an earlier application had already been made in time and failed because there was
no entitlement yet. Second, not every status change resets the clock: the SEM says prior time can count
where the sponsor already had a legal possibility of reunification, but it does not count time spent on a
short-term permit before grant of a residence permit because the short-term stay is regarded as too
temporary to force a final reunification decision.
A further sequencing point arises where refugee-related routes and ordinary AIG routes overlap.
Zurich’s guidance, relying on Federal Supreme Court authority, states that where a person first pursued
family reunification under asylum law and later had to proceed under the AIG, the earlier asylum filing
should be taken into account when checking whether Article 47 AIG deadlines were met. That is not the
mainstream Article 47 scenario, but it is a valuable internal safeguard against oversimplified deadline
drafting.
## Drafting cautions
Internal content on this topic should be written with exceptional care:
Do not state that provisionally admitted sponsors must simply wait three years. The current,
more accurate formulation is that Article 85c AIG still states a three-year waiting period, but SEM
practice must include an individual proportionality review from 18 months onward, and earlier
approval is possible if continued waiting would be disproportionate.
Do not convert the public ch.ch summary into a statutory proposition. ch.ch is helpful, but on Fpermit timing it compresses a more nuanced SEM position.
Do not generalise the filing-date age rule across every route. For ordinary AIG and F-permit
cases, the filing date controls; Zurich’s guidance indicates that direct Article 8 ECHR child claims
are different and use the date of decision.
Do not import Article 47 AIG’s five-year/12-month logic into AFMP/FZA cases. The EU/EFTA route
has its own structure, including the under-21 framework for non-dependent descendants.
Do not say that third-country relatives can simply enter first and apply later. Federal guidance
and the cantonal pages for Geneva and Ticino make clear that the normal rule is application
from abroad and waiting abroad for the decision, subject to limited exceptions.
Do not describe sponsor readiness in purely abstract terms. Official practice is document-heavy:
rent, room count, landlord consent or attestation, earnings, debt, insurance and, where relevant,
language enrolment are all timing-relevant because they affect whether a case is actually ready
to be decided.
Do use current article numbering for provisional-admission family reunification. The dedicated
SEM F7 handbook and Geneva’s legal-basis note use Article 85c AIG/LEI; some older passages in
broader guidance still refer to the older numbering. Internal content should standardise on the
current provision and, where necessary, note the administrative cross-reference.
