## Executive legal summary

Swiss work-permit law does not recognise "commercial necessity" or "business criticality" as standalone legal tests. For third-country national hires, the employer must satisfy the cumulative admission framework under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and implementing rules: overall economic interest, quotas, labour-market precedence, vacancy-notification compliance where applicable, local/ sectoral salary and employment conditions, personal qualifications, and suitable accommodation. Official SEM materials summarise those criteria expressly and treat them as the controlling structure for work-permit analysis. 1

Accordingly, commercial need matters only to the extent that it is translated into **recognised** **legal** **categories** **and** **recognised** **evidence.** The most useful official formulations are not "mission-critical" or "urgent", but rather: why the recruitment is necessary; what duties the post entails; how the role fits the company; what search efforts were made in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area; whether there is concrete project work supported by order documentation; and, for start-ups, whether there is acredible organisational, staffing, financing, and market plan. SEM's application-document page and cantonal guidance are particularly important on this evidential architecture. 2

For senior and specialist hires, role design is legally relevant because Swiss authorities do not assess the candidate in isolation. They test whether the **role itself** is properly characterised as managerial, specialist, or otherwise highly qualified; whether the candidate's qualifications fit that role;whether the salary is coherent for the claimed level; and whether the employer's recruitment evidence shows a genuine search for suitable precedence candidates. Official guidance is clear that admissions are limited to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, and that authorities expect job descriptions, company information, and evidence of qualifications and references. 3

Business urgency, client deadlines, revenue protection, technical risk, regulatory deadlines, and operational continuity may all be relevant facts, but they are not legal shortcuts. They do not waive labour-market testing, salary compliance, quotas, or qualification requirements, save in narrowly defined exceptions such as certain operational transfers within international companiesor limited evidential relaxations in shortage occupations. Any future content should therefore distinguish sharply between **commercial importance** and **legal eligibility.** 4

Official materials are comparatively concrete on documents and proof, but thinner on some drafting questions that lawyers often care about, including the precise role of organisational charts, the

treatment of "new" as opposed to replacement roles, and the exact line between a legitimate specialist profile and an over-tailored one. On those points, the safest approach is to classify conclusions as practice points or cautious inferences rather than clear rules. 5

## Structural overview of commercial necessity and role design

The legal structure is best understood as a sequence. First, the employer must show that the proposed hire serves the **overall economic** **interest** of Switzerland. SEM's public materials state that the applicant must explain and document why admission is in that interest; SEM's directives add that the assessment takes account, in particular, of labour-market conditions, sustainable economic development, and the foreign national's ability to integrate. The law is therefore macro-and system-oriented, not merely employer-convenience oriented. 6

Secondly, the employer must confront the **labour-market** **priority** rules. Federal guidance requires proof of a genuine search, with appropriate timing, geographic breadth, and suitable advertising content, and states that contact with third-country nationals should only occur after those efforts fail. SEM's application-document guidance then converts that principle into a documentary checklist: RAV and EURES evidence, copies of advertisements, other search efforts, and information on candidates and reasons for rejection. This is where role design becomes legally sensitive, because the job description used in the search must be defensible and objectively connected to the role rather than crafted around the preferred candidate. 7

Thirdly, the post must satisfy **salary** **and** **employment-conditions** scrutiny. Federal guidance requires compliance with local, professional and sectoral norms, and SEM expects a signed employment contract. That means a role described as "senior", "specialist", or "business-critical" should carry duties, contractual terms, and remuneration that are credible at that level. A senior title with generic duties or a specialist label with a salary inconsistent with comparable roles is a drafting risk. 8

Fourthly,the role must fit the **personal-qualifications** gateway. Admissions are limited to managers, specialists and other qualified workers; qualifications are checked through CVs, diplomas and employment references. Cantonal Zurich guidance makes the same point in more direct operational language: the professional qualifications must correspond to the new activity. This role-profile fit is one of the main legal points at which exaggerated commercial-necessity narratives fail. 9

Finally, the evidence package must link the role to the employer's real organisation. SEM's application-document page expressly requires the reason why the vacancy must be filled,the job description or duties, and information on the company. For project hires, SEM requires proof of the order, a project description, and an implementation plan. For new businesses, it requires foundation documents and a three-year business plan showing the intended organisation, staff development, financing, and market and competition analysis. Swiss practice therefore treats organisational need as something that must be shown through concrete corporate and project evidence, not asserted in slogans. 10

## Sources and authorities

**Primary** **legal texts.** The core federal instruments are the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, usually cited in Switzerland as AIG / LEI / LStrI (RS 142.20), and the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment or OASA/VZAE (RS 142.201). SEM's official legal-bases pages identify those texts as the governing admission instruments for non-EU/EFTA labour-market access and cross-refer to the detailed FNIA/LEI directives and sector-specific rules in chapter 4.7 onwards. 21

<!-- 5 -->

**Official guidance** **and** **administrative** **materials.** The most useful federal materials for this topic are SEM's public pages on the basis for admission, procedure, and application documents; SEM's directives chapter on gainful employment; the SEM annex on local/sectoral wage and work conditions for posted workers; and SECO's labour-shortage indicator system and wage-comparison material. Together, those sources explain both the legal tests and the evidence expected by authorities. 22

**Cantonal practice** **notes.** Zurich's official January 2024 guidance is particularly useful because it translates the federal criteria into concrete evidence requirements, including specific reasoning, business-plan material, balance-sheet and profit-and-loss material, recruitment evidence and candidate lists. Geneva's official page is valuable because it requires a letter describing the emnployer's situation, projects and reasons for the engagement, as well as staffing information and recruitment proof, and notes that renewals may be conditioned on announced objectives. Bern's guidance is simpler but confirms the documentary core: justification letter, CV, diplomas, contract, passport copy and proof of personnel search in Switzerland and the EU-27/EFTA. 23

**German, French, Italian and English cross-checking.** The most detailed directive material is in French and German. English SEMA pages are useful summaries and are conservative enough for cross-checking the structure of the law. Italian official material reviewed during this research largely restated the federal criteria and added little on role-design evidence, so it was used mainly for consistency checking rather than as a primary analytical source. 24

## Practical legal analysis

**Commercial necessity must be translated into the statutory framework.** The safest legal formulation is not that the role is commercially important, but that the recruitment serves the overall economic interest, that the position could not be filled after a genuine prior search in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area, that the salary and conditions are compliant, and that the role properly falls within the manager/specialist/qualified-worker categories. A commercially urgent role that fails those tests remains legally weak; a well-evidenced role that satisfies them may succeed even if the employer avoids emotive language such as "mission-critical". 25

**Role design is an evidential issue, not merely HR drafting.** Federal application guidance requires the employer to justify why the vacancy must be filled and to provide the job description or duties together with company information. Geneva additionally asks for information on the company and staffing situation, including employee numbers, nationalities and permit types. That makes organisational context legally relevant. However, no reviewed federal source expressly says that an employer must submit an organisational chart by that name. The prudent conclusion is therefore that reporting lines, span of control, strategic responsibility and business function are best proved through a coherent job description and, where useful, an organigramme, but the organigramme is an evidential aid rather than an express federal legal requirement. 26

**Seniority and specialist status should be shown objectively.** **A** post presented as senior should ordinarily show real decision-making, accountability, or strategic responsibility; a post presented as specialist should show technically or professionally specific duties that fit the candidate's education and experience. SEM's public guidance checks qualifications by CV, diplomas and references, while Zurich states that professional qualifications must correspond to the new activity. Official materials are

<!-- 6 -->

therefore hostile to inflated titles attached to generic duties, and to specialist labels that are unsupported by the actual role profile. 13

**Client obligations, project urgency, technical delivery and operational continuity can be relevant,** **but only if evidenced in the forms Swiss authorities actually use.** SEM's application-document guidance for project work requires proof of the order, a project description and an implementation plan. Geneva requires the employer's letter to describe its situation, projects and reasons for the engagement. Zurich's cantonal guidance similarly asks for a specific application explanation and, where relevant, business-plan and financial material. That means references to a delivery deadline, regulatory milestone, technical failure risk, or threatened revenue stream should be backed by mandate documents, project plans, implementation timelines, or financial and staffing evidence;otherwise they remain advocacy rhetoric rather than authority-facing proof. 5

**Regulatory compliance can strengthen necessity, but it does not replace immigration eligibility.** In regulated sectors, SEM's directives state that employers must investigate the underlying professional-authorisation requirements before filing the immigration application. The health-professions guidance is explicit that separate federal or cantonal professional regulation may apply and that the employer must check this in advance. This is important for compliance-driven roles: the fact that a function is needed to satisfy sector regulation or licence obligations may support the necessity narrative, but the case must still satisfy the ordinary AIG/LEI/FNIA admission criteria. 27

**New roles and replacement roles are not subject to clearly different federal legal tests on the** **reviewed** **materials.** SEM's vacancy-notification guiddance covers posts to be filled or re-filled, but no reviewed federal source creates a different substantive doctrine for newly created roles. The practical difference is evidential. A replacement role can be supported by continuity oof function, prior headcount and business dependency; a newly created role usually needs more robust evidence of why the function is needed now and how it fits the organisation. In both cases, however, the employer still has to handle recruitment proof, salary compliance and qualification fit. 28

**The interaction between role design and labour-market testing is one of the highest-risk drafting** **areas.** SEM requires a genuine search and adequate ad content; SEM's document checklist requires disclosure of why applicants were rejected; and official guidance warns that third-country contact should come only after the Swiss/EU/EFTA search has failed. In a related SEM practice note for UK third-country cases, native English is expressly said not to be a qualification marker on its own, and priority candidates must not be excluded on professionally irrelevant criteria. The safest inference is that over-tailored job descriptions, language requirements, and candidate-specific niche criteria arevulnerable unless the employer can show that they arise objectively from the role, project, client environment or regulated function. 29

**Shortage occupations help, but only within limits.** SEM's directives accept evidential relaxations for certain occupations with pronounced structural shortage, identified with the help of SECO indicators and SEM practice, and Zurich mirrors that approach. Yet SEM also says cantonal authorities may still seek special evidence in critical situations, and the vacancy-notification obligation continues to apply unless an exception exists. Shortage operates as a facilitative factor, not as a substitute for a properly designed, properly remunerated and properly evidennced role. 30

**Start-ups and scale-ups should be handled cautiously and document-richly.** SEM's federal application guidance requires business-creation cases to include foundation documents and a three-year plan covering projected organisation, staff development, finances, market and competition. SEM's directives further state that durable economic benefit may exist where the new business diversifies the regional economy, creates or secures local jobs, makes substantial investment and generates new

<!-- 7 -->

mandates, and that innovation can also be considered for start-ups. Official materials do not contain an express category for "scale-ups", so any scale-up analysis should be framed as a cautious extension of start-up logic: staffing trajectory, financing, customer demand, organisational development, and durable economic contribution matter more than generic claims of growth potential. 16

**Multinational employers have some targeted advantages, but not a blanket exemption.** SEM's guidance states that precedence does not apply to operational transfers of senior managers and essential specialists within international companies, and vacancy-notification rules also recognise a limited exception for certain operational transfers where the person has been employed for at least six months in the same company. But that is narrower than a general "global business necessity" exception. Other requirements, including economic-interest reasoning, salary compliance and role-qualification fit, remain relevant unless a specific rule says otherwise. Reporting lines, prior group employment, proprietary know-how and function in the group structure are therefore especially important evidence in multinational cases. 31

## Drafting cautions

Do not describe "commercial necessity" as if it were itself the legal threshold. The legally safer formula is that the commercial facts help demonstrate overall economic interest, the necessity of filling the vacancy, the genuineness of the role, and the fit between role, salary and candidate. 32

Do **not** imply that high revenue impact, client sensitivity, or operational urgency guarantees approval. Official material requires those assertions to be converted into recognised proof such as order confirmations, project descriptions, implementation plans, business plans, staffing evidence and recruitment evidence. 5

Do **not** let the role description become candidate-led. If the advert, job description, rejection reasons, salary level and CV do not match, the application starts to look engineered around a named individual. That risk is especially acute where niche language requirements, brand-specific tools or culturally loaded criteria are used without objective justification. 29

**Do** **not** use "senior", "head", "director", "lead expert", or "specialist" labels unless the duties, reporting context and remuneration support them. Swiss authorities test substance, not the employer's preferred title. An organisational chart is often useful, but future content should say that it is prudent evidence rather than an express federal requirement unless a canton specifically asks for it. 33

Do **not** assume that a shortage occupation removes the need for disciplined labour-market drafting. The official position is softer: some proof requirements may be eased, but vacancy-notification duties and the possibility of extra evidence remain. 34

Do not overstate projected growth in start-up or scale-up cases. Geneva expressly notes that renewal or extension may be linked to the announced objectives, and SEM's federal material focuses on durable economic contribution supported by organisation, staffing, financing annd market evidence. Unsupported forecasts can therefore become renewal-stage vulnerabilities. 35

Do **not** present multinational transfers as exempt from the general framework. The official exception is narrow and role-specific: operational transfers of senior managers and essential specialists. Future content should describe the exception conservatively, and only where the prior group employment, group structure and essentiality evidence are properly documented. 18

Do not state more than the authorities do on points where the official material is thin. In particular, there is no clear reviewed federal authority creating separate substantive tests for new versus replacement roles, or making organisational charts mandatory. Those points should be framed as practice and evidence, not as black-letter rules. 19
