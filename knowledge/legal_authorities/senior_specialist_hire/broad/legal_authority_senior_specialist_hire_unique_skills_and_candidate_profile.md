# Swiss legal authority pack on unique skills and candidate profile evidence in senior or specialist Swiss work permit hires

Suggested filename:

legalauthorityseniorspecialisthireuniqueskillsandcandidateprofilepack.pdf

Jurisdiction: Switzerland. Scope: third-country national admission to the Swiss labour market under the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration and the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, with limited reference to intra-group specialist transfers where the official materials address them. Swiss official guidance applies a binary system: EU/EFTA nationals benefit from facilitated access, whereas for third-country nationals only qualified workers may be admitted, and only within a restricted framework. 1

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does **not** create a freestanding immigration category for a merely "strong" or "unique" candidate profile. The operative legal categories are those in Article 23 FNIA: managers, specialists and other qualified workers, plus a set of narrow derogation-type situations in Article 23(3), including persons with special professional knowledge or skills where a demonstrable need exists, executive transferees,and persons whose Swiss activity is indispensable for economically significant international business relationships. That structure matters: "unique skills" is evidential language, not the legal test itself. 2

In ordinary third-country hiring, the candidate's profile is assessed within a **cumulative** framework. Salaried admission requires that the hire serve the interests of the economy as a whole, that an employer application be made, and that the requirements in Articles 20 to 25 FNIA be met. In practice that means the case stands or falls not only on the candidate's qualifications, but also on labour-market priority, salary and working connditions, quota availability, and the overall credibility of the role and business need. A persuasive CV alone is therefore legally insufficient. 3

Official SEM guidance treats formal education and multi-year experience as the primary route into "qualified worker" status. It says qualified workers are principally persons with a university or university of applied sciences degree and several years of professional experience, while some professions or specialisms may also accept persons with particular specialist training and several years of experience. The SEMA directives go further and say personal qualifications may arise at different levels, including higher education, special vocational training with several years' experience, a professional diploma supplemented by further training, or, in specific specialised fields, exceptional and indispensable language skills. 4

The candidate profile must also be tied to the **Swiss** **role** **itself.** Zurich's official explanatory notes state that the professional qualifications must correspond to the new job to be performed. The federal SEM checklist and cantonal filing guidance then require documents that connect person, role and business need: a CV, diplomas, employment certificates, a motivation letter explaining the company, project and foreign worker, the employment or assignment contract, and, depending on context, a business plan, commercial-register evidence, or aproject description and implementation plan. 5

Scarcity in the resident labour market is a separate and indispensable part of the analysis. In shortage occupations the SEM directives allow some flexibility in how labour-market priority is evidenced, and Zurich recognises that dated adverts and candidate lists may sometimes be dispensed with if a pronounced skilled-labour shortage is properly justified. But that is not a general waiver: SEM states that the vacancy-notification obligation remains in force without restriction, and Geneva's official guidance still insists on prooof of Swiss and EU/EFTA recruitment efforts and asks whether a locally available worker could be trained within a reasonable time. 6

The most important drafting caution is that official Swiss materials are **hostile** **to** **generic** **specialist** **claims and to candidate-tailored exclusion criteria.** In non-shortage cases, the SEM directives say employers must search in a timely and appropriate manner, must not contact third-country nationals before those efforts have genuinely failed, and must avoid excluding priority workers through non-relevant criteria such as foreign stays or language or technical abilities that are not indispensable for the post. The same directives also say employers are expected to make efforts to provide specific continuing training to workers available onn the Swiss labour market. That is the clearest official basis for rejecting blog-style claims that an employer may simply define a highly particular wish-list around a preferred candidate and rely on that preference for approval. 7

## Structural overview of unique skills and candidate profile

The legal architecture is best understood as four linked proof blocks rather than one general "talent" assessment. First comes **candidate** **qualification** **evidence**: degrees, diplomas, employment certificates, CV, job references, and, where relevant, additional material explaining foreign education systems, course duration, syllabuses, examinations and results. SEM expressly says authorities assess qualifications through the CV, diplomas and employment certificates, and that extra explanatory documents are useful where the foreign educational system differs materially from the Swiss one. 8

Second comes **role-necessity** **evidence.** The official material repeatedly asks for a specific justification from the employer, not just generic praise of the candidate. The SEM checklist requires a motivation explaining the company, project and foreign worker. Geneva requires a letter from the employer describing its situation, projects and reasons for the hire. For project cases,the SEM checklist adds the project description and implementationplan; for company-creation cases, it adds a business plan and commercial-register material. This shows that the candidate's qualities must be mapped to an identified Swiss operational need. 9

Third comes **labour-market** **scarcity** **evidence.** Article 21 FNIA reqquires proof that no suitable priority worker can be found; SEM's detailed guidance explains when that proof may be lightened in occupations with a strong structural shortage and when it must be produced case by case. In the non-shortage setting, the recruitment file must show timely, geographically broad and professionally appropriate searches, and the Swiss/EU/EFTA candidate pool must not be narrowed by non-indispensable requirements. The candidate profile therefore interacts directly with recruitment evidence: the more unusual the qualifications relied upon, the more carefully the employer needs to show that those qualifications were truly necessary rather than artificially chosen. 10

Fourth comes **compliance** **and** **market-conformity** **evidence.** Salary and working conditions must be customary for the locality, profession and sector, and the authorities expect a current, complete, signed contract. In regulated professions, recognition or licensing may be necessary, but immigration

These elements do not prove "uniqueness" by themselves, but they helpo test whether the claimed seniority or specialism is credible in the Swiss labour-market context. 11

### Primary legal texts

The main statutory stack is compact but demanding. Article 18 FNIA governs salaried employment and requires economy-wide interest, an employer application, and fulfilment of Articles 20 to 25. Article 21FNIA then imposes labour-market priority. Article 22 FNIA requires local and sector-customary salary and working conditions. Article 23 FNIA supplies the personal-requirements test: managers, specialists and other qualified workers only, with additional residence-permit integration factors and a narrow set of special cases, including need-based admissions for persons with special professional knowledge or skills. 23

<!-- 5 -->

For present purposes, Article 23 FNIA is the pivotal text. Paragraph 1 defines the ordinary class of admissible workers. Paragraph 2 adds integration indicators when residence permits are being considered. Paragraph 3 matters for "unique skills" rhetoric because it is the place where Swiss law actually speaks of persons with special professional knowledge or skills, but it does so only where a need for admission is shown; it also separately recognises executive transfers and activity indispensable for economically significant international business relationships in Switzerland. Those are narrower legal hooks than a free-floating claim that a candidate is exceptional. 2

At ordinance level, OASA Article 7 is particularly important for specialist or senior hires in regulated sectors. It states that trade-police, health-police and similar authorisations enabling a foreign national to exercise a profession do **not** replace the immigration-law authorisation to work. In other words, sector permission and migration permission run in parallel. 24

The federal legal texts therefore support a conservative internal position: the law requires a role-linked, labour-market-aware and compliance-heavy case, not a generic celebration of a candidate's strengths. Where content needs to refer to "unique skills", it should tie the phrase back to Article 23(3)(c) or the broader Article 23 framework, rather than presenting it as an autonomous approval standard. 25

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most useful federal starting point is the SEM page on the basis for admission to the Swiss labour market. It summarises the third-country national framework, explains that third-country workers are admitted only if they serve the economic interests of the country, confirms the labour-market priority test, explains the vacancy-notification obligation, sets outthe salary-and-conditions rule, and states that qualified workers are principally those with higher education and several years' experience, though some specialised vocational profiles can also qualify. It also says that authorities verify qualifications by CV, diplomas and work certificates and may require translations and extra educational information where the foreign training system differs materially from the Swiss one. 8

The SEM directives are the most detailed official source on "unique skills" and candidate-profile evidence. They state that personal qualifications may stem from higher education, specialised vocational training with several years' experience, a professional diploma supplemented by further training, or exceptional and indispensable language skills in specific specialised domains. They also say that, from a labour-market perspective, the required personal qualifications may sometimes be inferred from the role itself, for example where the foreign worker is expected to create or direct enterprises important to the labour market. 26

On labour-market scarcity, the same directives are exacting. In occupations with a strong structural skilled-labour shortage, cantonal authorities may dispense with demanding full proof of recruitment efforts and accept a plausibole showing that the role falls within a shortage occupation. SEM then lists occupational families for which this eased approach may apply, based on SECO indicators and empirical admission practice. But SEM expressly preserves both vacancy-notification obligations and the possibility that cantonal authorities may call for special ad hoc proof where they consider it useful. 27

Outside objectively shortage-affected fields, the directives require a case-by-case examination and give particularly important anti-abuse guidance. They say recruitment measures must not be undertaken

<!-- 6 -->

merely to satisfy a formality; they must start early enough before the planned signing date. They also say it is necessary to avoid excluding priority workers through non-relevant professional criteria such as foreign stays, or language or technical aptitudes that are not indispensable. The directives further say employers are expected to make efforts to offer specific continuing training to workers who are available on the Swiss labour market. The directives cite Federal Administrative Court decisions in support of those statements, including casesC-2638/2010 and C-1123/2013. 28

The SEM checklist of annexes is especially useful for internal content controls because it shows what the administration actually expects in specialist and qualified-worker files. For specialist recruitment it calls for the passport copy, cantonal application form, motivation, employment or assignment document, tabular CV, proof of qualifications by diplomas and work certificates with translations where needed, and-where relevant-recruitment evidence. For project cases it adds the business contract, project description and implementation plan. For company-creation cases it adds the business plan and commercial-register material. 29

SECO's own material is relevant, but mainly as **administrative** **support** rather than as an autonomous legal test. The 2023 indicator system is the publication explicitly referenced by the SEM directives when discussing shortage occupations. Separately, SECO publishes salary-comparison guidance for international salary comparisons, and both SEM and cantonal guidance point employers to salary tools and salary-comparison methods when checking compliance with Article 22 FNIA. 30

On regulated professions, SBFI confirms that recognition is required for certain professions in Switzerland and that the competent recognition body depends on the qualification and profession concerned. That complements, rather than displaces, OASA Article 7. The combined effect is that a regulated-profession hire may need both professional recognition and migration permission. 31

Official federal guidance is relatively detailed on formal qualifications, role correlation, labour-market priority, shortage occupations, language and regulated professions. It is notably thinner on patents, publications, market knowledge, client relationships and similar "star candidate" factors. In the reviewed official materials, those features are not framed as standalone legal criteria; at most, they are potentially useful **evidence** if they prove a recognised statutory proposition such as special professional knowledge, indispensability for international business relations, or genuine managerial seniority. That is the safest internal reading of the source set. 32

## Cantonal practice notes

Zurich's official explanatory notes are a particularly useful practice source because they translate the federal rules into filing expectations. They say applications are individually examined; there is no legal right to a permit; documents must be in German or English; the labour-market file may include RAV and EURES postings, dated adverts and a detailed applicant list; and in professions with a pronounced shortage of skilled labour these items may sometimes be dispensed with if the shortage occupation is properly justified. Zurich also says temporary cyclical fluctuations are not enough to justify a shortage claim. On candidate evidence, Zurich states that third-country nationals may be admitted only if they are managers, specialists or other qualified workers, that their professional qualifications must correspond to the new job, and that in shortage cases below degree level the file must show at least a vocational diploma or at least five years' work experience. 33

Geneva's official guidance is more explicit about what the employer must prove as part of the labour-market case. It requires proof that the role was notified to the cantonal employment office as quickly as possible, proof of in-depth searches in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area, and proof that the employer

<!-- 7 -->

cannot train or have trained within a reasonable time a worker available on the Swiss labour market. It also says only managers, specialists and qualified workers may be admitted, requires evidence of the candidate's CV, diplomas and work certificates, requires an employer letter explaining the business situation, projects and reasons for hire, and states as a cantonal practice point that the job is a full-time job. Geneva also notes that the authority may attach conditions and may make renewal depend on the objectives announced by the employer in the application. 34

The official Ticino material reviewed is much thinner on standalone candidate-profile analysis. The relevant permit pages and "detail" pages mainly channel users back to SEM's federal bases for third-country labour-market admission and highlight, in a practical way, that regulated professions may carry separate recognition requirements. That absence is itself informative for internal drafting: detailed propositions about candidate-profile evidence should be grounded primarily in federal SEM guidance and in the more developed cantonal practice notes such as Zurich and Geneva, rather than attributed to a comprehensive Swiss-wide cantonal consensus. 35

The cantonal materials therefore support two conservative conclusions. First, canton-specific implementation matters a great deal, especially on how deeply the labour-market file must be evidenced. Secondly, any public-facing content that speaks broadly about "Swiss practice" should avoid presenting one canton's more flexible or more demanding evidential posture as though it were a uniform national rule. 36

## Practical legal analysis

**There is no legally sufficient claim of "exceptional candidate" in the abstract.** The best synthesis of the sources is that Swiss authorities do not approve a third-country senior or specialist hire because the person is impressive in a vacuum. They approve, if at all, because the employer proves the statutory package: admissible worker category,economic interest, labour-market priority or a recognised exception, compliant salary and conditions, and a credible role/profile match. That is why the official forms and checklists ask for the candidate's evidence **and** the employer's justification, recruitment file and contract. 37

**Formal education, qualifications and experience remain the core evidential spine.** The official materials privilege higher education plus several years' experience, while leaving room for vocationally trained specialists where the profession or shortage context warrants it. Where authorities may not readily understand the foreign qualification, SEM says it is useful to provide supporting documents on the training institution and the duration and content of the course. For internal evidential planning, that means a candidate-profile pack should usually contain not just degree certificates, but also transcripts, training descriptions, evidence of specialisation, and employment certificates showing the progressive build-up of relevant responsibility. That last sentence is a conservative evidential inference from the official document list and explanatory notes. 38

**Rare technical skills, unusual market knowledge, client relationships, patents and publications** **can** **matter, but only** **as** **role-linked** **evidence.** The legal hooks are narrow. For special professional knowledge or skills, Article 23(3)(c) requires a demonstrated need. For international commercial roles, Article 23(3)(e) requires that the activity in Switzerland be indispensable for economically significant international business relationships. For science- or culture-adjacent profiles, Article 23(3)(b) may sometimes be relevant. The official materials reviewed do not offer a general checklist saying that patents, publications, market knowledge or client books independentlysatisfy Swiss immigration law. The internally safe way to use those matters is therefore evidential: they may help show that the person possesses special professional knowledge, that the Swiss role really requires that knowledge, or that the person is genuinely indispensable to the relevant business relationship. 39

<!-- 8 -->

**Language is treated with more nuance than blog content often suggests.** SEM states that, in a globalised labour market and in highly specialised or international companies, very good English can be enough for durable integration of qualified workers; accordingly, authorities may dispense with checking whether managers and specialists in such an international environment know a Swiss national language. But SEM also states that, in sectors where contacts with the social environment are important for durable labour-market integration-giving health care and hospitality/restaurants as examples-knowledge of the relevant national language can become an additional decisive criterion. At the same time, for the labour-market-priority test, non-indispensable language skills must not be used to screen out Swiss or EU/EFTA candidates. The practicaI consequence is that language requirements need to be both **sector-specific** and **indispensability-based.** 40

**Scarcity evidence and candidate evidence must be consistent with each other.** In manifest shortage occupations, the employer may sometimes rely on the shortage framework rather than a full documentary recruitment dossier. But SEM's shortage approach still depends on occupation-level evidence, not on saying that the preferred candidate happens to be unusually good. Zurich makes the applicant responsible for justifying the shortage occupation, and SEM keeps vacancy notification in force. In non-shortage cases, the recruitment file must show timely and appropriate market testing, and the authorities expect employers to consider training available workers. These materials together imply that an employer cannot lawfully transform a candidate-specific wish into a labour-market-necessity claim merely by drafting a narrow role description around that person. That final proposition is an inference from the official texts, but it is a strong one. 41

**Role** **design** **is** **therefore** **a** **legal** risk **area.** Zurich says the professional qualifications must correspond to the new job to be performed. Geneva insists on an employer letter explaining the business situation, projects and reasons for the engagement. The SEM checklist requires a motivation tied to the company, project and foreign worker. Read together, those materials suggest that the role must be designed around a genuine Swiss business need and then matched to a suitably qualified non-resident candidate -not retrospectively engineered around the candidate's biography. Role descriptions that contain candidate-specific but non-essential requirements are exposed twice over: under Article 23, because the claimed qualification may not correspond to the true role, and under Article 21, because the search may have screened out priority workers using irrelevant criteria. 42

**Salary is legally necessary, but not legally sufficient.** The hard rule is Article 22 FNIA**:** salary and working conditions must be customary for the locality, profession and sector. Geneva and Zurich both operationalise that rule through a complete and signed employment contract, and Geneva also treats full-time employment as part of its practice picture for these cases. A generous salary may support the credibility of a claimed senior or specialist profile, but the official materials do not treat salary as an alternative to proving qualifications, need or labour-market scarcity. Internally, salary should be used as a corroborative indicator only. That last point is an inference, but it follows closely from the way the official materials separate Article 22 from Articles 21 and 23. 43

**Regulated professions require a dual-track analysis.** If the role is in a regulated profession, the case may fail either because the migration criteria are not met or because the professional-recognition or licensing requirements are not met. SBFI confirms that recognition is required for certain professions, while OASA Article 7 makes clear that professional authorisations do not replace immigration permission. For internal drafting, that means certifications and licences should be described as part of the candidate's qualification evidence, but never as a substitute for the work-permittest. 31

## Drafting cautions and source list

### Drafting cautions

·Do not write that Switzerland has a general "unique skills" or "exceptional CV" work-permit route for third-country nationals. The legal categories are managers, specialists and other qualified workers, plus narrow need-based special cases uunder Article 23(3). 2

·Do not imply that employer preference, business convenience, or a strong candidate profile alone is enough for approval. Ordinary salaried admission is cumulative and also requires economy-wide interest, labour-market priority, compliant salary/conditions and a credible role/ profile match. 44

·Do not say that shortage occupations eliminate recruitment duties. The official position is only that documentary proof may be lightened in manifest structural shortage occupations; vacancy-notification obligations remain, sector rules remain reserved, and cantons may still request ad hoc evidence. 45

·Do not endorse candidate-tailored exclusion criteria. SEM expressly warns against excluding Swiss or EU/EFTA candidates on the basis of non-indispensable foreign stays or language or technical abilities. 17

·Do not present English-language ability as automatically sufficient. SEM allows English to suffice in highly specialised and international settings, but says national-language ability can be decisive in sectors such as health care and hospitality. 46

·Do not say that patents, publications, market knowledge or client relationships are standalone approval criteria. In the reviewed official materials they are, at most, supporting evidence of statutory concepts such as special professionnal knowledge, genuine managerial seniority or indispensability for significant international business relationships. Official guidance is otherwise thin and should be described as such. 47

·Do not suggest that a professional licence or diploma-recognition decision replaces immigration approval. In regulated professions the two regimes are cumulative. 48

·Do not flatten cantonal practice. Geneva, Zurich and Ticino do not present the same operational detail, and content should not present one canton's filing posture as a universal Swiss rule. 49
