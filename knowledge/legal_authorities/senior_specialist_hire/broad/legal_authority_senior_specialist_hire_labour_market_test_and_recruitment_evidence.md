# Swiss legal authority pack on labour market testing and recruitment evidence in senior and specialist hire work permit cases

# Executive legal summary

Swiss law does **not** create a general labour-market-testing exemption merely because a role is senior, executive, niche, or highly specialised. For third-country local hires, resident-worker priority remains part of the ordinary admission structure alongside overall economic interest, quotas, salary and employment conditions, and personal qualifications. SEM's official summary states that third-country nationals may be admitted only if no person with priority can be recruited from Switzerland or the EU/ EFTA labour market, while the same official SEM material limits admission on personal-qualification grounds to managers, specialists and other qualified workers. In other words, **seniority** **and** **specialism are primarily Article 23 qualification issues; they do not by themselves displace Article** **21 priority requirements.** 1

The main federal softening identified in this research concerns **professions** **with** **a** **pronounced** **structural skilled-labour shortage.** The SEM directives say that, in such professions, cantonal labour-market authorities **may** ease the way in which the priority requirement is evidenced and may in some cases refrain from demanding concrete search efforts if the application plausibly demonstrates that the occupation is a shortage profession. But the same directives also state that the vacancy-notification requirement under Article 21a AIG remains fully applicable, and that cantonal authorities may still require special proof depending on local labour-market conditions, regional priorities, or wider economic interests. This is therefore an evidential relaxation, not a blanket statutory exemption. 2

Official SEM materials are comparatively clear about the **types** of **recruitment** **evidence** expected. The SEM's application-documents page requires confirmation of vacancy advertising via the regional employment office and the EURES system, copies of advertisements in specialist journals, Swiss national weekly or daily press, specialist online portals and social media such as LinkedIn, information on other recruitment efforts customary in the sector, confirmation from an appointed employment agent,and information on applications received together with reasons why candidates could not be considered. The SEM's English guidance similarly recognises RAV, EURES, specialist journals, recruitment agencies, online portals, social media and private referrals as relevant search channels, and notes that candidate lists with reasons for rejection are often helpful. 3

**For confidential and** **executive** **search,** official sources are thinner. No federal source found in this run says that confidentiality, seniority, or commercial sensitivity removes the need to prove an unsuccessful Swiss and EU/EFTA search. What the official sources do show is that evidence through **appointed** **employment agents and sector-customary channels** is acceptable. The safer legal view is therefore that headhunters and targeted search are **modes** **of proof,** not independent waivers. By inference from the accepted categories of evidence, an employer can rely on an executive-search firm or specialist recruiter, but should retain a disclosure-ready pack showing the mandate, search geography, timing, channels used, candidate pipeline and objective reasons why priority candidates were unsuitable.That inference is supported by the official evidence categories, but the precise handling of confidential redactions is not clearly spelled out in the public federal guidance reviewed here. 3

The strongest compliance risks are familiar and recurring. The SEM directives say search efforts must be genuine rather than tokenistic, must occur in a reasonable period before the contract with the third-country candidate is signed, and must not exclude priority candidates by irrelevant criteria such as unnecessary language demands, foreign-stay requirements, or specialised knowledge with only a slight connection to the role. The directives also expect employers to use public employment services early and to make efforts through specialist media, electronic media and private recruitment channels, and they note that employers are expected to consider specific training or upskilling of workers already available on the labour market. These points are especially important in senior and technical roles, where there is a practical temptation to define the brief around the candidate rather than around the business need. 4

The clearest **true** **exceptions** identified were not "senior roles" as such, but distinct statutory or ordinance pathways, especially **intra-group** **or** **company-internal transfers** of managers, highly qualified specialists and indispensable personnel. The SEM's official English summary says the precedence rules do not apply to management or specialist transfers in international companies, and the SEM directives explain that Article 46 VZAE enables simplified recruitment for executives, highly qualified specialists and indispensable specialists in international firms and research institutes, without applying resident-worker priority. Those are specific categories and should not be collapsed into ordinary local-hire cases. 5

## Structural overview of labour market testing in senior and specialist cases

The federal structure starts with the admission criteria that SEM summarises as arising from the FNIA and the VZAE: overall economic interest, quotas, precedence, vacancy-notification obligations, salary and employment conditions, and personal requirements. SEM's English guidance presents these as cumulative components of the admission analysis for non-EU/EFTA workers. 6

**For** **ordinary** **local employment** of a third-country national, the labour-market-testing element is built around Article 21 AIG/FNIA and, where applicable, Article 21a AIG/FNIA. SEM's official guidance says that employers must prove that, despite genuine efforts over an appropriate period, across the relevant geographic scope and through suitable channels, no suitable priority worker from Switzerland or the EU/EFTA area could be recruited. The same guidance adds that contact with third-country nationals should only takeplace once that search has failed.7

**For** **senior and** **specialist** roles, Article 23 operates in parallel rather than as a substitute.SEM's guidance restricts admmission to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, and the directives explain that the necessary qualification may arise from academic education, specialist training with several years' experience, additional training, or indispensable specialist knowledge. The directives also say that qualification can sometimes be inferred from the function itself, for example where the person is a company founder or a business leader of a labour-market-significant undertaking. But that is stilla qualification analysis, not a priority waiver. 1

**For pronounced** **shortage** **occupations,** the SEM directivesallow cantonal authorities to treat the priority proof more flexibly. The directives name examples including certain management posts in research and development, healIth, education, ICT, consulting, finance and insurance, machinery/ electrical/metal industries, as well as management-analysis specialists, engineers, ICT specialists, certain health professionals and university teachers. Yet the same directives reserve case-specific review, say that special proof can still be requested, and emphasise that the vacancy-notification duty remains in force. The Zurich cantonal explanatory notes similarly say that in professions with a

**For temporary internal transfers or assignments,** different rules may apply. The SEM directives treat company-internal transfer in international undertakings under Article 46 VZAE as a facilitated path in which the resident-worker-priority rule does not apply, and Zurich's explanatory notes similarly state that precedence is waived for assigned employees in cross-border services because they are only temporarily in Switzerland and then return to the home country. Those pathways should be treated as category-specific exceptions, not as evidence that "seniority" generally removes the need for labour-market testing in local-hire cases. 9

### Primary legal texts and official guidance

The controlling federal instruments are the **Federal Act** on **Foreign** **Nationals** **and** **Integration** and the **VZAE ordinance on admission, stay and employment.** SEM's legal-framework pages expressly identify those as the relevant legislation and link to the official consolidated texts on the federal platform. SEM's English labour-market page then organises the admission criteria under Articles 18, 20, 21, 21a, 22, 23, 24 and 30 FNIA, while the directives add operational guidance on provisions such as Article 46 VZAE for internal transfers. 19

For this topic, the most relevant provisions are as follows. Article **18** addresses overall economic interest; **Article** **20** quotas; **Article** **21** precedence; **Article** 21anotification of vacancies in covered occupations; **Article** **22** salary and employment conditions; **Article** **23** personal requirements; **Article** **30**exceptions; and **Article** **46** **VZAE** company-internal transfer in international undertakings. The SEM materials reviewed here consistently treat Articles 21 and 23 as separate enquiries: the employer must prove unsuccessful recruitment of priority workers, and the foreign national must separately satisfy the qualification threshold for managers, specialists or other qualified workers. 20

The most important official administrative source is the SEM directive **"Weisungen** **AIG,** **Kapitel** 4**Aufenthalt** **mit** **Erwerbstätigkeit",** current at 1 January 2026 in the version retrieved here. That directive isparticularly valuable because it translates broad statutory concepts into practical adjudicative standards. It states that the priority principle is, in principle, to be followed in every case; explains how to prove priority compliance; identifies shortage occupations where proof may be eased; warns against formulaic or over-tailored recruitment exercises; and explains specific exception pathways, especially internal transfers. 21

SEM's **application-documents** page is also important because it is one of the clearest official sources on the expected evidence bundle. It specifies the minimum recruitment-evidence items that should accompany a non-EU/EFTA work-permit application, including RAV/EURES confirmation, copies of

<!-- 6 -->

adverts, information on other sector-usual search efforts, confirmation from an appointed employment agent,and details of applications received with reasons for rejection. For practical drafting, that page is one of the best official anchors for what a recruitment-evidence pack should contain. 22

SECO's official **Stellenmeldepflicht** page is relevant because it confirms the policy rationale and current administrative monitoring of the vacancy-notification system, while SEM's labour-market page explains that this notification duty is an additional condition in occupations with unemployment at or above the statutory threshold. For senior and specialist cases, the key drafting point is that Article 21a is **not** the whole labour-market test: it sits alongside Article 21 rather than replacing it. 23

## Cantonal practice notes

**Canton** **of Zurich.** Zurich's official explanatory notes for third-country nationals state that relevant precedence evidence can include RAV and EURES postings, dated job advertisements from print or online media, and a list of applicants with detailed information, including nationality, qualifications, application date and reason for rejection. For professions with a pronounced shortage of skilled labour, Zurich says job posting, detailed advertisements and candidate lists may be dispensed with if the applicant justifies the shortage, while making clear that temporary cyclical fluctuations do not suffice. Zurich also warns that the competent authority may require additional documentation beyond the minimum legal documentation listed. 24

**Canton** **of** **Geneva.** Geneva's current guidance is comparatively demanding and particularly useful for internal drafting. It states that an employer may apply for a third-country permit only if it has first explored the Swiss and European labour markets unsuccessfully. Geneva requires proof that the vacancy was notified to the cantonal employment office as quickly as possible **regardless** of **the** **unemployment** **rate** **of the** **branch** **concerned,** that a timely and thorough search was carried out in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA area using websites and recruitment agencies, and that an available worker on the Swiss labour market could not be trained within a reasonable time. For full work-permit applications Geneva also requires evidence of the research conducted and its detailed results, together with information on the employer's workforce composition by nationality and permit type; it expressly carves out intra-group management transfers from the search-evidence requirement. 25

**Canton** **of** **St** **Gallen.** St Gallen's public guidance is brief but pointed. Its 2023 fact sheet for third-country employed residence requires proof of unsuccessful efforts to recruit a highly qualified worker in Switzerland and in the EU/EFTA states, states that the vacancy must be notified to the RAV, and asks for the employer's justification for needing a foreign worker. It therefore alignswith the federal structure and is useful as a concise cantonal practice indicator that the failure reasons and business rationale should be spelled out. 26

**Canton** **of Vaud.** Publicly accessible Vaud material retrieved in this run was thinner on the content of recruitment evidence than Geneva or Zurich. Vaud's official work-permit form is nonetheless useful because it distinguishes ordinary work permits from **internal transfer** and **service-provider** cases and asks the employer to identify whether the role is director, manager, qualified employee or otherwise. That helps confirm, at least administratively, that cantonal procedure distinguishes transfer-type cases from ordinary local hires. Public Vaud materials retrieved here did not yield a detailed published checklist equivalent to Geneva's or Zurich's for senior/specialist recruitment evidence, so caution is warranted before attributing a specific Vaud practice beyond the federal baseline. 27

## Practical legal analysis and drafting cautions

The safest legal analysis is that **seniority and specialism do not reduce the existence of the labour-market test; they mostly affect how the employer proves it.** In an ordinary local-hire case, the employer still needs to show an unsuccessful Swiss and EU/EFTA search. What changes in a ggenuinely specialist case is often the evidential narrative: the role may legitimately require rare technical competences, there may be sector-specific search channels, and there may be structural shortage material. But the core burden remains. That is why official sources repeatedly separate precedence from qualification. 1

**On recruitment for executive and niche specialist roles,** the federal materials support a multi-channel evidence pack rather than a single decisive document. The most defensible pack will normally include: proof of RAV/EURES or equivalent public-employment involvement where required; dated adverts or search records from sector-appropriate channels; headhunter or specialist-agency confirmation; a candidate schedule showing who applied or was approached; and candidate-specific reasons why priority candidates were unsuitable. For project- or urgency-driven hires, project contracts, implementation plans and a short chronology can help explain why the vacancy matters economically and why the employer recruited in the way it ddid, but those materials should be framed as additional support rather than as substitutes for the Article 21 exercise. 3

**For** **confidential recruitment,** the official sources reviewed here are notably underdevelopoed.They support use of recruiters and sector-customary channels, but do not expressly say how much anonymity or redaction will be tolerated. The cautious internal position should therefore be: if confidentiality is relied upon, preserve a version of the file that still allows the authority to understand the role definition, the search geography, the channels used, the search period, the fact pattern of candidates considered, and the objective reasons for non-selection. Where disclosure risk exists,redact judiciously rather than replacing substance with conclusory assertions. That recommendation is an inference from the accepted categories of evidence and the requirement to explain why priority candidates could not be considered. 3

**On** **scarcity** **evidence,** the line to hold is that a structural shortage can ease the proof burden, but only where the occupation itself is convincingly shortage-affected and only subject to cantonal scrutiny. The SEM directives support reliance on SECO indicators and SEM experience, and Zurich says cyclical fluctuations are not enough. So "we struggled to find people" is weak; "the role falls within a recognised shortage field, with supporting labour-market indicators and a documented sector search" is much stronger. Even then, the pack should not imply a full exemption unless the case falls inside a true exception path such as an internal transfer. 8

**On** **why** **resident** **candidates** **were** **unsuitable,** official materials favour specificity. Candidate-rejection reasons should connect to the actual role and should be demonstrably relevant: absence of a required regulated qualification, lack of experience in a critical system or process, inability to perform the required scope of leadership, or unsuccessful assessment against objectively necessary criteria. The SEM directives make clear that criteria with weak job relevance are dangerous. Shortlists should therefore be written as an evidence matrix, not as a set of impressionistic HR comments. 28

**On** **candidate** **uniqueness,** the conservative legal view is that uniqueness helps most under the headings of qualification, indispensability and economic interest, but does **not** itself replace priority proof unless the application falls within an express exception pathway. The SEM directives say that qualification can sometimes be inferred from the role or function,including company leaders of labour-market-significant businesses, and Article 23 can accommodate indispensable specialist knowledge. But nothing in the official materials reviewed here says that a claim that "this person is uniquely good" removes the need to show unsuccessful recruitment of priority workers in an ordinary local-hire case. 29

<!-- 8 -->

**On** **commercial urgency,** the sources retrieved here do not support any proposition that urgency independently waives labour-market testing. If anything, they cut the other way: SEM expects early use of RAV and other channels, and Geneva expects a timely, thorough search and asks whether available Swiss workers could be trained within a reasonable time. The better legal framing is therefore that urgency may support the commercial-need narrative and may explain why the role could not be left vacant, but a last-minute process that compresses or bypasses the Swiss/EU/EFTA search is risky. 30

The most important **drafting** **cautions** **for** **future** **blog** **content** are these. Do not say that executive, senior or highly specialised roles are "exempt" from labour-market testing. Do not say that a shortage occupation automatically removes the need for recruitment evidence. Do not equate Article 21a vacancy notification with full Article 21 compliance. Do not imply that a headhunter letter or confidentiality rationale, on its own, is sufficient. Do not present an internal-transfer rule as if it applied to ordinary local hires. Do not use loose phrases such as "no suitable resident candidate existed" without supporting facts showing how the search was run and why actual candidates were unsuitable. Each of those shortcuts is broader than the official materials support. 31

The authority pack is grounded primarily in the following official materials:

**·SEM official labour-market guidance for non-EU/EFTA nationals,** especially the sections on precedence, vacancy notification, personal requirements and exceptions. ⁶

**·SEM** **application-documents** **guidance,** which is the clearest official checklist for recruitment-evidence contents. 22

**·SEM** **directives, Chapter** 4, current at 1 January 2026 in the retrieved version, especially sections 4.3.2,4.3.3,4.3.5 and 4.4.9. 33

**·SECO's** **official vacancy-notification** **page,** used to distinguish Article 21a from the broader Article 21 search requirement. 34

**·Cantonal official materials** from Zurich, Geneva, St Gallen and Vaud, used for practice notes and administrative expectations. 35

Open questions and limitations should be noted explicitly for internal use. First, while the controlling legal texts are the official Fedlex consolidated texts, Fedlex's JavaScript rendering did not expose the article text cleanly in this environment;the pack therefore relies on official SEM/SECO pages and SEM directives that cite and operationalise the governing provisions. Secondly, the SEM directives cite Federal Administrative Court case numbers on priority and recruitment efforts, but direct official judgment texts were not successfully retrieved in this run; case-law points in thispack are therefore drawn conservatively from the SEM's own summaries rather than from fresh court-text analysis. Thirdly, published official guidance on **confidential** **executive** **recruitment** is thin: the conclusions in that part of the pack are necessarily cautious and, where indicated,partly inferential rather than express. 36
