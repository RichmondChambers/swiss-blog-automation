# Swiss legal authority pack on salary, seniority and market alignment for senior and specialist work permit hires

Internal use only. Jurisdiction: Switzerland. Scope: senior and specialist work-permit hires under the Swiss admission regime, principally for third-state nationals, plus posted, seconded and transfer situations where Swiss salary and local-conditions review is engaged. Suggested filename:

## Executive summary and framework

### A. Executive legal summary

Switzerland applies a dual admission system. EU and EFTA workers benefit from easier labour-market access under free movement rules, while third-state nationals are admitted only wvithin a complementary and more restrictive work-permit system. For the latter group, the relevant admission criteria are cumulative: overall economic interest, quotas, priority of wvorkers already entitled to the Swiss or EU/EFTA labour market, vacancy-notification duties, salary and employment conditions customary in the region and sector, personal qualifications, and accommodation where required.Salary compliance is therefore one necessary element of the file, not the whole file. 1

The core salary rule is Article 22 FNIA/AIG/LEI/LStrI: salary, social-security contributions and employment conditions must match local, occupational and sectoral conditions. SEM explains that this review is not only for the protection of the foreign worker but also to prevent wage dumping and protect workers already in Switzerland. In the 2026 SEM directives, cantons are told to compare first against legal requirements and the pay and conditions for comparable work in the same company and the same branch, and also to use current statistical wage surveys. SEM expressly notes that collective-agreement minimum wages may sit below what resident workers are in fact paid, so a permit review is not exhausted by showing bare compliance with a minimum floor. 2

Seniority and specialist status matter through Article 23 FNIA. Admission is limited to managers, specialists and other qualified workers. The authorities look at the role and the person together: CV, diplomas, certificates, employment references, and the match between the candidate's qualifications and the proposed activity. Salary is not itself the statutory qualification test, but it is obviously read alongside job title, duties, reporting line and candidate profile. A file that claims "senior manager" or "essential specialist" while offering pay and conditions more typical of a junior or generalist post is therefore vulnerable on both the salary/local-conditions axis and the qualifications/role-match axis. That last point is a practice inference from the official materials, not a separate statutoryrule. 3

Variable pay is treated cautiously. SEM states that, in principle, only salary components carrying a contractual entitlement can be taken into account. It adds that employee participation schemes typical in founder or start-up settings may be counted, but only if the worker still has the direct financial means needed for subsistence and the participation remains proportionate to the basic salary. By contrast, in

the posted-worker and international wage-comparison context, SECO distinguishes between genuine wage items and reimbursements of expenses: travel, accommodation and meal expenses are not wages, while certain posted-worker allowances with wage character can be counted under specific rules. The safest ordinary permit presentation is therefore fixed contractual Swiss pay first,variable upside second, and expenses separately. 4

The practical consequence is that compensation cannot be analysed in isolation. A sound Swiss senior/ specialist salary narrative must fit the route being used, the canton, the sector, the working time,any applicable collective or normal labour contract, the recruitment history, the candidate's experience, and the documentary form of the contract. High pay alone does not secure approval; under-market pay can undermine the application directly; and unclear structuring of allowances, bonuses,equity or secondment expenses makes the file harder to defend. 5

### B.Structural overview of salary, seniority and market alignment

The safest analytical sequence is to identify the admission regime first, then the canton and sector, then any binding floor from a generally binding collective agreement, normal labour contract or cantonal minimum-wage regime, then the comparable Swiss role and working time, and only then the compensation structure. That compensation structure should be translated into a Swiss-review format: gross monthly or hourly fixed pay, weekly hours, contract duration, 13th salary if any, and any deductions or allowances stated transparently. This is how the federal and cantonal materialIs are organised. 6

For local hires, the benchmark question is whether the proposed Swiss package matches local, occupational and sectoral norms for the actual role. For intra-group transfers and other secondments, the same local-conditions rule still bites, but the file must also separate home-country base salary, Swiss top-up or posting allowance, and reimbursement of expenses. For posted-worker wage-comparison exercises, SECO goes further and converts remuneration into comparable Swiss hourly wage terms, taking account of working hours, holiday pay, 13th month and mandatory surcharges where applicable. 7

Market alignment is not assessed by a single federal number. SEMM instead points to layered evidence: legal rules, company and branch comparators, current statistical wage data, and official tools such as Salarium and the SECO NJational Wage Calculator. Both federal calculators are framed as informational tools rather than wage recommendations. For drafting purposes, they are useful corroborative evidence, especially where they are triangulated with applicable collective agreements, cantonal labour-market materials and employer-specific comparators. 8

### D. Primary legal texts

The primary federal framework is the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, SR 142.20, identified by SEM as the governing statute for admission to employment together with the Ordinance on Admission, Period of Stay and Employment, SR 142.201. For compensation analysis, the operative statutory articles are the admission provisions on overall economic interest, quotas, priority, vacancy notification, salary and employment conditions, personal qualifications and accommodation; SEM's official English summary maps those directly to Articles 18, 20, 21, 21a, 22, 23 and 24 FNIA. For cross-border services and transfers, SEM and cantonal guidance also work with Article 26 FNIA. 22

On the implementing side, SEM guidance explains that the ordinance requires a written and binding employment contract, at least signed by the employer, and that the contract must expose the essential terms relevant to salary review, including function, place of work, duration, working time, salary,social benefits and deductions. The same SEM materials explain the ordinance rule on the duration of expense reimbursement in long postings and the exception for sectors protected by generally binding collective agreements or normal labour contracts guaranteeing a minimum wage. 23

For posted workers and international wage-comparison questions, the relevant primary legislation is the Posted Workers Act, SR 823.20, and the Posted Workers Ordinance, SR 823.201. SECO's official pages describe these instruments as imposing compliance with Swiss minimum wage and employment conditions on foreign employers posting workers into Switzerland. They are not the ordinary local-hire regime, but they are legally important where a "specialist hire" is structured as a secondment or service posting rather than a direct Swiss employment contract. 24

Collective-agreement law also matters. SECO explains that collective agreements regulate employment conditions and that their normative terms commonly include wage, 13th month, compensation, continued salary, holidays and working time. Where a collective agreement has been declared generally binding, its scope is extended to all employers and workers within the relevant sector or territorial field. Current SECO materials confirm that such scopes can be national, cantonal or regional, which is why sector and canton must be identified before any senior/specialist salary conclusion is drawn. 25

## Official guidance and administrative materials

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

The most operational federal guidance is SEM's 2026 chapter on "residence with gainful employment". It says cantons must review salary and working conditions systematically, starting with legal provisions and comparable work in the same business and the same sector, and then considering current wage

<!-- 6 -->

surveys. SEM adds a useful but often overlooked point: the average wages in those surveys should, in urban conditions, usually be treated as a minimum, whereas in other regions they may occasionally be slightly undercut. For innternal drafting, that means a "statistical median only" argument is not automatically enough in cities such as Zurich or Geneva. 11

SEM also gives the most precise federal guidance on what can count as remuneration. The rule of thumb is contractual entitlement. This is the strongest federal basis for treating fixed salary, a fixed 13th month and other guaranteed salary items as the core benchmark, while treating discretionary performance pay with caution. The same guidance is the best official basis for including stock or other employee participation only in limitedcases where there remains enough direct cash income for living costs and the participation is proportionate to base salary. 13

The federal benchmarking tools are formal aids, not binding wage tariffs. BFS describes Salarium as a statistical calculator for gross monthly salary based on region, economic sector, occupational group and individual features such as age and education. SECO describes the National Wage Calculator as a tool that produces a gross monthly wage median and an interquartile range for a specific profile, and expressly says the results do not represent wage recommendations. Those two points matter for drafting: one should use the calculators as evidence of plausible market range, not as if they themselves fixed the legally correct salary. 26

Federal labour-market materials also matter beyond pure salary. SEM's official basics page states that employers must register workers with the social-security authorities and handle deduction of tax at source where applicable. It also refers users to SECO's collective-agreement materials, the FSIO social-security guide and the Federal Tax Administration's withholding-tax materials. These are not peripheral. They show why "compensation compliance" in Swiss permit work is broader than salary amount alone. 27

Foor international compensation structures, SECO's instruction on international wage comparison is highly relevant. It requires comparison of the wage effectively paid with the wage owed under Swiss minimum conditions, usually by converting to gross hourly wage. It excludes pure expenses from wage, includes certain posted-worker allowances with wage character, explains how 13th and 14th month pay, holiday pay, Christmas pay and mandatory surcharges are treated, and excludes social-security contributions as non-wage items. That instruction arises in the posted-worker and wage-control context, but it is the clearest official Swiss source on how to dissect a foreign compensation package into wage, allowance and expense elements. 28

## Cantonal practice notes

### F. Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich.** Zurich's official third-state guidance restates the cumulative federal criteria and emphasises that only managers, specialists and otherwise qualifiedworkers can be admitted and that employers must comply with local, occupational and sectoral salary and working conditions. Its detailed explanatory note adds that the contract must be current and complete, and that, in shortage occupations, the canton may dispense with detailed recruitment evidence where the shortage is properly substantiated. For intra-group transfers, Zurich requires the file to separate the home-country basic salary, the posting allowance, and assumption of all posting expenses, together with insurance and social-contribution arrangements. That is particularly useful cantonal authority for secondment drafting. 29

<!-- 7 -->

Geneva.Geneva's OCIRT expressly states that it validates the engagement ofworkers from outside the EU/EFTA and oversees compliance with salary and work conditions in the canton. Geneva's published third-state permit page says the employer must show prior search efforts in Switzerland and the EU/ EFTA, must respect branch salary and work conditions, and that the job is full-time; it also points employers to the canton's online wage-calculator page, which in turn explains that the federal calculator estimates gross monthly wage ranges for a specific profile in the chosen canton and across Switzerland. Geneva's permnit form separately asks for gross monthly salary, 13th salary and hours per week, which shows the canton's preference for a transparent fixed-pay presentation. These are Geneva-specific practice points and should be labelled as such when used. 30

**Vaud.** Vaud's work-permit form classifies the proposed role as director, cadre, qualified employee or unqualified employee, and separately asks for weekly hours, gross salary excluding 13th salary, deductions for food or lodging, whether a 13th salary is granted, and the employment contract as an annex. The form also records a prior decision by the labour-market surveillance authority. That structure makes clear that Vaud reviews pay together with role classification and working-time reality. In a sectoral example, Vaud's official agricultural circular states that third-state permissions require prior control of qualifications, labour-market priority and compliance with branch salary and working conditions. 31

**Lucerne.** Lucerne's official note for third-state hires states that the employer must provide the labour-market authority with information on the duration of employment, employment conditions and remuneration, that the contract must be complete and comply with local and sectoral conditions, and that the federal wage calculator Salarium may serve as the calculation basis. Lucerne also repeats that only managers, specialists and other qualified workers qualify, and that the qualifications must fit the proposed activity. 32

Basel-Stadt.Basel-Stadt's labour-relations authority states that it grants work permits and controls compliance both with the cantonal minimum wage and with local and sector-customary wages.The canton also publishes a specific minimum-wage regime. For immigration drafting, the important point is that a Basel file may need both a permit-law analysis and a local labour-law floor analysis,and those are not always the same question. 33

**Ticino.** Ticino's labour-market surveillance office states that it monitors salary and working conditions in domestic employers, verifies sector-specific minimum wages and oversees service providers from abroad. In its labour-market FAQ for uncovered sectors, Ticino says that where there is no direct reference minimum wage, it uses a reference wage model based on Swiss wage-structure data and variables concerning the worker, employer and job. Ticino's public permit information also says that work permits remain subject to labour-market examination including quotas, worker priority, salary and working conditions, and qualifications. Those materials are particularly useful where the case concerns Ticino and the sector is not cleanly covered by a generally binding collective agreement. 34

A final cantonal point is that some cantons now also have statutory minimum-wage regimes, including Geneva, Basel-Stadt and Ticino. Those are legally relevant floors where applicable, but they should not be confused with proof that a senior or specialist package is market-aligned for permit purposes. For senior/specialist hires, the market-alignment question is usually substantially above the floor and requires a role-and sector-specific analysis. The existence of the floor is official; the need to go beyond it in senior/specialist cases is a conservative inference from the federal framework. 35

## Practical legal analysis

### G. Practical legal analysis

**Salary is corroborative evidence, not a standalone gateway.** In Swiss third-state work permit law, salary is a compliance condition under Article 22 FNIA, but it sits beside other cumulative conditions such as economic interest, quotas, priority rules, vacancy notification and qualifications. For that reason, a generous package can be helpful evidence that a role is genuinely senior or specialist, but the file will still fail if the employer cannot show the role serves Swiss economic interests,the labour-market search was genuine where required, or the candidate actually meets the qualification standard. Thisis the correct internal logic for content drafting. 20

**The benchmark is the Swiss role, not the employer's global package design.** For local hires, the central question is what is normal in the relevant Swiss labour market for that role in that location and sector, at that working time and level of responsibility. SEM's own methodology moves from binding labour-law sources and same-employer or same-branch comparators to statistical tools. That means a global total-compensation number is not enough by itself. The file should translate the package into Swiss review terms: fixed gross cash salary, weekly hours, contract length, 13th salary, any cash allowances, and any deductions for board or lodging. 36

**Fixed pay should carry the ordinary permit file.** The reviewed official materials do not provide **a** general federal formula for discretionary annual bonus, commission or carried-interest style upside in ordinary Article 22 salary benchmarking. What they do provide is a consistent direction of travel: SEM focuses on contractually due components; cantonal forms focus on monthly gross salary, 13th salary and weekly hours; and the posted-worker comparison rules include only specific identified items under detailed logic. The conservative drafting conclusion is that discretionary bonus and commission should be treated as supplementary support unless they are formula-based and contractually guaranteed. Equity should only be advanced where it can be explained under SEM's specific conditions for employee participation. 37

**Expenses and benefits should be separated from wages.** This is clearest in transfer and posting cases. SEM says travel, accommodation and meal expenses linked to a posting are not salary and cannot be deducted from gross pay. SECO's international wage-comparison instruction makes the same separation and excludes social-security contributions as non-wage. The broader permit file still needs to show that social-security and insurance obligations are met, and that tax-at-source obligations are handled where applicable, but those are compliance features, not substitutes for adequate Swiss salary. 38

**Secondments and** **transfers** **require** **unbundling.** Zurich's transfer guidance is especially concrete: the file should identify the home employer and host employer, the function, tasks, place of assignment, start date, duration, home-country base salary, posting wage supplement, who pays travel/ accommodation/meals, insurance, social contributions, and the Swiss-law treatment of holidays and working time where needed. For internal drafting, that is the right model. If the worker is in truth being locally hired into Switzerland, the Swisslocal-hire benchmark should be used; if the case is a genuine temporary secondment or international service provision, the transfer or posted-worker logic should be used and labelled accordingly. 39

**Under-market salary creates two kinds of problem.** First, it can directly breach the Article 22 salary-and-conditions rule. Second, it can weaken the credibility of the employer's labour-market-testing story: if the employer searched at a materially uncompetitive salary, a canton may doubt whether the search

<!-- 9 -->

truly tested the relevant market. That proposition is not stated in those exact words in thesources,so it should be presented as an inference, but it follows naturally from SEM's insistence on genuine recruitment efforts and on local/sectoral salary compliance. Zurich's shortage-occupation guidance points the same way: relaxed recruitment evidence depends on a real shortage, not on a poorly pitched offer. 40

**Title** **and** **salary** **must** **tell the** **same** **story.** Vaud's form asks the employer whether the person is a director, cadre, qualified employee or unqualified employee, then asks for working time, gross pay and 13th salary.Geneva asks for the job category, gross monthly pay, 13th salary and hours per week; Geneva's public guidance also says that the job for a third-state hire is full-time. These official materials show that cantons do not read salary in the abstract. Theyread it against role classification and working time. Drafting should therefore avoid inflated titles paired with thin fixed pay, weak duties or junior reporting lines. 41

**Candidate** **profile** **and** **commpensation** **must align.** SEM says qualifications are checked on the basis of CV,education certificates and employment references, with translations where needed. The authorities may also need contextual information on foreign education and training. This means compensation works best when it corroborates an already coherent technical or managerial story: scarce expertise, responsibility level, relevant experience, and a salary that looks plausible for that profile in Switzerland. Where the CV is thin, the references weak, or the education only loosely connected to the role, very high salary will not rescue the file. 42

## Drafting cautions and source list

## H.Drafting cautions

State scope explicitly. Unless the case is genuinely about an EU/EFTA free-movement hire, write as if the file is being judged under the third-state admissions framework and say so. If the facts concern a secondment, intra-group transfer or service posting, label that route expressly instead of mixing local-hire and posting concepts. 17

Do not write or imply a universal Swiss salary threshold for senior or specialist permits. The legal test is local, occupational and sectoral alignment, filtered through any applicable collective or normal labour contract and any cantonal minimum-wage floor. If a number is mentioned, identify its source and legal status. 43

Present remuneration in the format the authorities expect: gross monthly or hourly salary,weekly hours,contract duration, 13th salary, and any deductions or fixed allowances. Do not hide the fixed-pay level inside global "total compensation" language. For senior/specialist files, the base salary should usually carry the market-alignment argument. 44

Distinguish sharply between wages, allowances and expenses. Reimbursements for travel, meals and accommodation should be shown as expenses, not as salary substitutes. Treat discretionary bonus or commission conservatively, and explain any equity or employee-participation plan only if the file can satisfy SEM's conditions for counting it. 45

Do not imply that seniority or specialist status can be proved by pay alone. Tie the compensation narrative to the job description, duties, reporting line, candidate CV, certificates, references and, where required, recruitment evidence. If the file relies onn a shortage occupation, say so and support it, but do not let shortage language become a substitute for an adequate salary explanation. 46

Flag canton-specific practice carefully. Geneva's full-time point, Lucerne's use of Salarium as a calculation basis, Zurich's detailed transfer-contract requirements, Vaud's classification form, Basel-Stadt's dual control of local customary wages and the cantonal minimum wage, and Ticino's reference-wage model are all useful, but they should not be presented as universal Swiss rules. 47
