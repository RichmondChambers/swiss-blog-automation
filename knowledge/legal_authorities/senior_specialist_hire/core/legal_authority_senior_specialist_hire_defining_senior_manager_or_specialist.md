# Legal authority pack on senior managers and specialists in Swiss work permit cases

## A Executive legal summary

Swiss work-permit law does **not** turn on whether an employer labels a role "senior", "managerial", "specialist", "key personnel", or "commercially critical". For third-country admissions, the legal structure is cumulative: the role must be in the overall economic interest, an employer must apply, quotas must be available where applicable, domestic/EU/EFTA priority must be satisfied unless a recognised exception applies, salary and working conditions must be market-conform, personal requirements must be met, and suitable accommodation must be available. Withinthat framework, the core statutory category for ordinary non-EU/EFTA labour admission is the one in Article 23: managers, specialists, and other qualified workers. "Seniority" in an internal hierarchy is therefore not a free-standing admission ground. 1

Swiss official materials use several overlapping expressions, but they are not all legal categories of equal weight. The statute itself speaks, in the official language versions, of managers/executives, specialists, and other qualified workers. SEM's public English pages use "highly qualified workers" as an explanatory umbrella for those statutory categories, while SEM's Directive chapter on intra-company exchange uses additional language such as higher management, highly qualified workers and indispensable specialists for the specific Article 46 OASA route. The safest internal drafting position is therefore that "highly qualified", "senior", "key personnel" and similar phrasing are **descriptive** **or** **source-dependent** **labels,** not autonomous nationwide tests unless tied back to a specific statutory or directive provision. 2

The official materials place the analytical weight on the **substance** of the role and candidate profile: actual duties, decision-making power, organisational importance, qualifications, professional experience, fit between qualifications and the proposed work, recruitment history, salary alignment, and documentary evidence. SEM guidance expressly states that qualifications may arise at different levels, including university or university of applied sciences degrees, specialised vocational training with several years' experience, a professional diploma with further training, or, in certain specialised contexts, exceptional and indispensable language skills. In shortage occupations, the guidance allows more flexibility and may also permit non-academic roles where there is at least completed vocational training or generally at least five years' professional experience. 3

Managerial characteristics matter, but Swiss law does not provide a single exhaustive nationwide checklist of headcount, budget, or board-level authority. The clearest official functional description appears in SEM's guidance on Article 46 OASA, which covers executive functions and persons with important responsibilities and decision-making power within the enterprise, as well as indispensable transfers within a transnational group. Outside that specific route, those same features are best treated as **evidence** that a role genuinely falls within the statutory manager/specialist/qualified-worker framework; they are not, on the reviewed sources, a statutory checklist that automatically answers the case. 4

<!-- 1 -->

Specialist status also requires care. Swiss law distinguishes between ordinary professional competence and stronger categories such as special professional knowledge or skills, indispensable specialists in international-company transfers, and persons whose activity in Switzerland is indispensable for economically significant international business relationships. The law therefore supports an argument based on rarity, necessity, and business relevance **only** **where** **those** **points** **are** **evidenced** **and** **tied** **to** **a** **recognised** **legal route.** It does not support a generic proposition that any hard-to-find employee or niche job title is automatically permit-worthy. 5

Salary is relevant, but not in the way automated content often assumes. The federal rule is that salary and working conditions must be customary for the locality, profession, and sector. SEM guidance treats salary as an anti-undercutting and market-alignment control, assessed against legal rules, in-company comparators, sector practice, collective agreements, and statistical tools. The guidance also says the statistical toolIs reflect average wages and may in practice function as minima in cities while permitting some regional variation. That makes salary an evidential factor, not a universal legal threshold for "seniority".6

Labour-market testing interacts directly with claims of seniority and specialism. In ordinary cases, the employer must show timely and genuine recruitment efforts in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA and must not exclude priority candidates through irrelevant criteria. In shortage occupations, SEM allows cantonal authorities to relax the proof of exhausted local/EU labouur supply, but the vacancy-notification duty remains, and cantons may still ask for ad hoc evidence depending on local labour-market circumstances. This means that "senior specialist" cannot be treated as a simple bypass of labour-market testing.7

## B Structural overview of senior and specialist hire concepts

The federal admission architecture is best understood as four connected layers. First, Articles 18 to 24FNIA/LStrI set the ordinary employee-admission structure: overall economic interest, employer application, quotas, priority, salary and working conditions, personal requirements, and accommodation. Secondly, Article 23 then narrows the ordinary labour-admission pool to managers, specialists, and other qualified workers, with additional long-term integration factors for residence permits. Thirdly, Article 23 paragraph 3 creates specific derogation-style gateways for investors and entrepreneurs, recognised persons in science/culture/sport, persons with special professional knowledge or skills where admission is needed, persons in executive transfers between internationally active companies, and poersons whose activity in Switzerland is indispensable for economically significant international business relationships. Fourthly, Article 46 OASA creates a specific implementing route for internal transfers in international companies, simplifying the position of higher management and indispensable specialists. 8

Within that structure, the reviewed official materials support the following taxonomy. **Manager** is a statutory category in Article 23 and, in the transfer context, an operational category linked to executive functions and important responsibilities with decision-making power. **Specialist** is both a statutory category and, in some exception routes, a stronger one, such as the indispensable specialist under Article 46 **OASA. Other** **qualified** **wvorker** is the residual statutory class for people who are not properly described as managers or specialists but whose education, training, and experience still meet the legal threshold. **Highly** **qualified** **worker** is uused by SEM's public English and by some guidance languagge as an umbrella or explanatory expression rather than as a separate statutory class. **Key** **personnel** is best treated as a practical shorthand, not as a single federal legal term of art for general admission cases.

This source structure matters for drafting. If a blog says that Switzerland "only admits senior managers and key personnel", that wording is too loose. It compresses several different legjal pathways into a single supposed rule and risks obscuring the actual statutory categories. It is safer to say that, for ordinary third-country work permits, the law limits admission mainly to managers, specialists, and other qualified workers, while certain special provisions also exist for indispensable specialists, executive transfers, and other narrowly framed cases. 10

The same caution applies to "commercially critical hires". That phrase is not itself the test.The nearest statutory hooks are the overall economic-interest criterion in Article 18, the Article 23 paragraph 3 route for persons whose activity is indispensable to economically significant international business relationships, and the Article 46 transfer framework for higher management and indispensable specialists in international groups. In other words, business criticality can be legally relevant, but only where it is translated into the language of the statute and supported by evidence. 11

## D Primary legal texts

The central federal text is the **FNIA/LStrI.** For employee admission, Article 18 makes the whole test cumulative by requiring overall economic interest, an employer application, and fulfilment of Articles 20to 25. Article 21 contains the domestic/EU/EFTA priority rule. Article 22 requires salary and working conditions customary for the locality, profession and sector. Article 23 narrows ordinary admission to managers, specialists and other qualified workers, adds long-term integration factors for residence permits, and creates special gateways for people with special professional knowledge or skills, executive transferees, and persons indispensable to economically significant international business relations. Article 24 adds suitable accommodation. Article 30 is the general statutory gateway for limnited derogations from the admission requirements. 27

The most important implementing ordinance text for "senior" or "specialist" discourse is **Article** **46OASA/VZAE,** on operational transfer in international companies. Its wording is narrower than generic business language suggests: it is aimed at facilitating transfers of higher management and indispensable specialists in internationally active companies, while still requiring overall economic interest, an employer application, quotas, salary/work-condition compliance, and suitable accommodation. In other words, it is an exception route with defined inputs, not a general corporate convenience provision. 28

For internal drafting, the safest legal map is therefore:

**·Ordinary** **local** hire: Articles 18, 21,22,23 and 24 FNIA/LStrI.

<!-- 6 -->

**·Specific exception / indispensability analysis:** Article 23 paragraph 3 FNIA/LStrI.

**·International intra-group** **transfer**: Article 46 OASA/VZAE plus the SEM ch. 4.4.9 guidance. 29

## E Official guidance and administrative materials

The most important administrative source is theSEM **Directive** **chapter** on **gainful employment,** current to 1 January 2026. It is where the flexible terminology is operationalised. Three points matter most.First, the Directive explains that personal qualifications maybe established through several education-and-experience pathways and notes that the required qualifications may sometimes be inferred from the worker's function, for example where theperson is called to establish or manage an enterprise important to the labour market. Secondly, it develops the shortage-occupation route under Article 23 paragraph 3 letter c by allowing more flexibility on proof and by listing the kinds of occupations for which that flexibility may be available. Thirdly, it gives the clearest administrative explanation of the Article 46 OASA transfer route, describing executive functions, important responsibilities, and decision-making power. 30

The SEM public overview pages in **English,** **French** **and** **Italian** are also useful because they show how terminology shifts with language and audience. The English pages say non-EU/EFTA nationals must be "highly qualified", meaning managers, specialists or other skilled professionals, and they stress that complete documentation remains in German, French or Italian. The French and Italian versions stay closer to the statutory categories and emphasise qualifications, labour-market priority, salary compliance and integration. For drafting purposes, this supports a conservative rule: English explanatory phrasing should never be allowed to outrun the official-language legal categories. 31

On salary, SEM's Directive chapter 4.3.4 is more useful than general public summaries. It says the purpose of Article 22 is both to protect foreign workers from abusive conditions and to prevent cheaper foreign labour undercutting domestic workers. It directs authorities first to legal norms and to pay and conditions for comparable work in the same business and sector, then to current statistical surveys and tools such as Salarium, the SECO national wage calculator and other aids. It also requires written information on the essential remuneration elements and a binding written employment contract. 32

SECO materials matter chiefly in two places. First, SEM's Directive expressly relies on **SECO labour-availability** **indicators** when identifying shortage occupations. Secondly, the SECO **national wage** **calculator** is described as a tool for estimating median wages and wage ranges, not as a source of wage recommendations. That makes it a useful evidential aid, but not a statutory salary floor or a proxy for legal seniority. 33

## F Cantonal practice notes

The reviewed cantonal materials broadly align with federal law but are useful because they show what authorities actually ask for. **Zurich** states that third-country nationals may be admitted only as managers, specialists or other qualified workers, that the professional qualifications must match the proposed activity, and that in occupations with marked skills shortages permits may also be granted for roles that do not necessarily require a university degree if there is at least vocational training or at least five years' experience. Zurich's practical checklists for intra-company transfers and postings require a specific case justification, details of the company and assignment, function, task area, project documentation, salary components, expense coverage, CV, diplomas and identity documents. Those documents demonstrate that the canton tests the factual profile of the role rather than the employer's title alone. 34

<!-- 7 -->

**Geneva** takes a similarly evidence-heavy approach. Its guidance requires the emnployer to file around six to eight weeks before entry and says that priority means the employer must first explore the Swiss and EU/EFTA labour markets unsuccessfully. Geneva specifically asks for proof that the vacancy was notified promptly to the cantonal employment office, that broad searches were conducted in Switzerland and the EU/EFTA through online advertising and agencies, and that no available local worker could be trained within a reasonable time. The same page says that only managers, specialists and qualified workers may be admitted, that quotas must be available, that wage and working conditions must be customary in the sector, and that the job is full-time. 35

**Basel-Stadt** is less interpretively detailed in the reviewed material, but that is itself instructive. Its page for third-country hires mainly routes employers back to the federal legal bases, federal SEM admission criteria and Salarium. That suggests Basel's practice presentation is more federal-framework-driven and less inclined to publish its own qquasi-definitional gloss on "manager" or "specialist". 36

For internal use, the safest weighting is therefore: cantonal materials are valuable for **evidence** **expectations** **and** **procedural practice,** but they should not be elevated into nationwide legal definitions unless they clearly mirror federal texts. 37

## G Practical legal analysis

The first practical point is that **immigration-law seniority differs from corporate seniority.** Swiss law is interested in whether a person truly falls within the legally relevant categories and whether admission satisfies the cumulative federal conditions. A role called "Senior Manager", "Principal Specialist" or "Regional Director" may still fail if the underlying duties are ordinary, if the skills are available locally, if the salary is misaligned, or if the employer cannot evidence the business need and recruitment steps. Conversely, a non-academic specialist or technically indispensable worker may still qualify where the legal criteria are met, especially in shortage occupations or a recognised exception route. 38

The second is that **managerial relevance** is **functional.** The nationally clearest administrative indicators are concentrated in the Article 46 OASA setting: executive functions, important responsibilities, and decision-making power within the enterprise. Outside that setting, evidence such as leading an important business unit, responsibility for establishing or directing a labour-market-significant enterprise, or a central organisational role can help demonstrate that the applicant is genuinely within Article 23 rather than merely experienced. But the reviewed federal materials do not create a universal checklist of direct reports, budget size or board membership. Those are evidential features, not statutory bright lines. 39

The third is that **specialism must normally carry an element of rarity, necessity or legally relevant** **distinctiveness.** Ordinary professional competence does not become specialist status merely because the employer prefers a particular worker. Swiss law offers stronger legal traction where there is special professional knowledge or skill and a need for admission, where thecandidate is an indispensable specialist in an international group transfer, or where the Swiss activity is indispensable for economically significant international business relationships. Those concepts are far closer to "key personnel" than generic C-suite or seniority language. 5

The fourth is that **labour-market testing and role justification must cohere.** **If** **the** employer claims a highly specialised senior role, the recruitment narrative should show why the role genuinely required those capabilities and why suitable Swiss or EU/EFTA candidates could not be found or trained within a reasonable time. SEM's guidance requires timely and appropriate recruitment efforts and warns against contrived exclusion criteria. Geneva's practice note reinforces that the employer should also explain why local training is not feasible within a reasonable time. In shortage occupations, the proof burden may be lighter, but even there the vacancy-notification duty remains and cantons may still ask questions. 40

<!-- 8 -->

The fifth is that **salary evidence** **plays** **a** **corroborative role.** A salary well below market is an obvious problem because Article 22 is directly engaged. A salary broadly aligned with market data helps support role credibility. But the legal question is still whether the salary and conditions are customary for the locality,profession and sector, not whether they exceed a universal "senior salary" threshold. SEM's own guidance treats statistical tools as aids and notes that averages may function differently across regions. That is another reason not to hard-code salary bands into general explanatory content unless a specific source and canton support them. 41

The sixth is that **the evidential bundle should be built around substance.** The reviewed official materials support requesting: an accurate job description; an organisational explanation of where the role sits; the actual decision-making and specialist responsibilities; project or transfer details wvhere relevant; the written employment or assignment contract; salary breakdown; expense arrangements for postings; CV; diplomas; references; translations; and evidence of labour-market testing. Where the role is said to be business-critical, the documents should show why it is critical in statutory terms, not merely assert that it is. 42

The seventh is that **Article** 46 **OASA** **should** **be** **used** **carefully.** It is highly relevant to international intra-group transfers, but it is not a general solution for local recruitment. It still requires economic interest, employer sponsorship, quotas, salary compliance and housing, even though it disapplies the domestic-priority rule. Blog content that generalises Article 46 into a wider principle that all senior or specialist hires are exempt from labour-market testing would be legally wrong. 23

## H Drafting cautions and I Source list

Drafting should consistently separate **internal** HR **labels** from **legal categories.** Avoid saying that Switzerland admits "senior hires", "key personnel" or "commercially critical employees" as if those were universal statutory classes. Instead say that, for ordinary third-country permits, Swiss law mainly admits managers, specialists and other qualified workers, subject to cumulative statutory conditions, and that special routes exist for certain indispensable or transfer-based cases. 8

Do not imply that a high corporate rankautomatically proves immigration-law seniority. Where managerial status is relevant, describe the actual function: executive responsibility, important internal decision-making, organisational scope, and why the role matters in labour-market or economic terms. Where specialist status is relevant, describe the specific knowledge, why it is unusual or indispensable, and how the evidence supports that. 43

Do not write as though niche skills eliminate labour-market testing. In ordinary cases they do not.The correct position is that the employer must demonstrate genuine and timely recruitment efforts, although the evidential burden may be relaxed in recognised shortage occupations and specific exception routes. 44

Do not turn salary into a headline eligibility threshold unless a source specifically does so. The legally safe formulation is that salary and working conditions must be customary for the locality,profession and sector, and that salary level can support or undermine the credibility of the application but is not itself a free-standing statutory seniority test. 6

Do not overstate the precision of official terminology where the sources are flexible. The reviewed materials do not provide a single exhaustive Swiss-law definition of "senior manager" or "key personnel". Where the pack above says terminology is flexible or source-dependent, that is because the statute uses open-textured categories and the guidance develops them through examples and evidential indicators rather than closed definitions. 45
