# Swiss Legal Authority Pack on Short-Term

# Assignment and Project-Based Work

## Executive summary and structural overview

## A. Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration law treats the concept of work broadly. The decisive question is not simply whether the individual is paid in Switzerland, whether the stay is short, or whether the activity is called a "business trip". Under SEMM's work-permit instructions, an activity is treated as gainful employment whenever it is ordinarily remunerated and affects the Swiss labour market; the test is objective, and even unpaid or nominally paid activity can still qualify as work. Doubtful cases are decided by the cantonal labour-market authority, with unresolved cases referable to SEM. 1

SEM's official annex on the definition of gainful activity draws a clear practical line between non-working business presence and productive work. Short business meetings, contract negotiations, representative visits by senior executives, strategy meetings involving information exchange only, preparatory visits before a future transfer, passive attendance at conferences, and theoretical or technical classroom-style training without integration into the work process are treated as non-gainful in principle. By contrast, on-the-job training integrated into production or operations, repair or maintenance work, implementation of software or reorganisation programmes, and regular managerial performance-setting or feedback activity in Switzerland are treated as gainful activity. This makes project implementation, installation, operational consulting and client delivery high-risk categories even where the stayis brief. 2

For EU/EFTA free-movement cases, Switzerland operates a notification regime for qualifying short-term work. SEM states that cross-border service provision by posted workers or self-employed service providers is liberalised for up to 90 effective working days per calendar year, with prior notification rather than a residence permit. The same electronic system is also used for EU/EFTA nationals taking employment with a Swiss employer for up to three months. But notification is not universal: it applies only to listed categories, and service provision beyond 90 days or employment with a Swiss employer beyond three months requires a permit application before work begins. Outside specific EU/EFTA or equivalent routes, permit rules remain decisive. 3

Nationality and employer location matter materially. Notificationn is available to EU/EFTA nationals taking Swiss short-term employment; to posted workers from EU/EFTA employers regardless of citizenship if third-country nationals were already admitted to the regular labour market of the EU/EFTA state for at least 12 months; and to EU/EFTA self-employed service providers. SEM also confirms a separate UK route under the Services Mobility Agreement for certain UK-based service provision up to 90 working days, while UK nationals taking employment with a Swiss employer remain under the FNIA permit regime. For all other persons, notification does not apply merely because the project is short. 4

The eight-day rule is also narrower than many business teamns assume. For posted workers and self-employed service providers, some general sectors do not require notification until work exceeds eight days in the calendar year, but sensitive sectors such as construction, hospitality, cleaninng,security,

itinerant trade, sex work, and landscaping require notification from day one. SEM's user guide and SEM's public page also make clear that each assignment must be filed in advance, usually eight calendar days before workstarts; the emergency exception is narrow and limited to genuinely unforeseeable damage situations requiring immediate action. Commercial urgency or client pressure is not itself an exception. ⁵

Client-site work and project deployments carry special risk where the facts look less like genuine service provision and more like labour leasing or local working. SEM's instructions on sham self-employment and labour leasing emphasise subordination, organisational integration and host-company instruction rights. Where the Swiss recipient effectively directs the worker's day-to-day work, authorities may treat the arrangement as staff leasing rather than a genuine services contract. That matters because foreign labour leasing into Switzerland is prohibited, and labour-market consequences become significantly more severe. 6

Document quality is central in permit-required project cases. Zurich's official document lists for postings require a specific case explanation, project description, Swiss client contract or works contract, a posting confirmation or assignment agreement, and supporting personal documents such as CVs, diplomas and passport copies. SEM's wider instructions likewise stress verification of the actual purpose and duration of the stay, and expressly warn that 120-day type short assignments must not be used for rotation or to evade quotas, labour-market controls, registration duties, tax or social-security obligations. Weak project evidence, rolling assignments and fragmented travel patterns therefore create real refusal and compliance risk. 7

Misclassification and timing defects have practical consequences. The SEM user guide says work may begin only after the relevant waiting period or after the permit route has been completed, and subsequent changes must be reported immediately. The posted-worker sanctions page states that breaches can lead to warnings, administrative fines, and market exclusion; non-compliance with the eight-day waiting period is expressly treated as a minor infraction that can still trigger sanctions, while serious wage or working-condition breaches can trigger fines up to CHF 30,000 and service bans, with repeat bans lasting up to five years. The posting company remains responsible for correct notification even if a client or other representative files on its behalf. 8

## B. Structural overview of short-term assignment and project-based work

Swiss authorities effectively work through four questions. First, is the person merely attending as a genuine business visitor, or are they carrying out gainful activity in Switzerland? Second, if there is gainful activity, is it a Swiss employment relationship, cross-border service provision, self-employment, or labour leasing? Third, does a limited notification route exist for that person, employer and activity, or is a permit required from the outset? Fourth, are the factual documents and timelines strong enough to support the chosen classification? 9

A short-term assignment can therefore fall into very different legal buckets even when commercially described in the same way. A strategy meeting, contract negotiation or passive conference attendance may remain outside gainfulactivity. A short implementation sprint, software go-live, machine repair or reorganisation roll-out is usually work. A worker hired directly by a Swiss company for up to three months may use the short-term notification route if they are EU/EFTA. A worker sent by an EU/EFTA company to perform a client contract in Switzerland may use service notification if the route is otherwise available. A worker sent by a non-EU/EFTA employer often falls outside notification and into permit law. A worker who is effectively supplied to a Swiss host under Swiss direction may be treated as labour leasing instead of genuine project delivery. 10

Duration is relevant, but it is not the only determinant and must never be treated as a safe harbour. SEM and SECO materials repeatedly show that some activities require notification or a permit from the first day, while others may remain non-gainful or notification-free for a short period. Likewise, staying under 90 days does not itself guarantee that notification is available; nationality, employer base, activity type and sector all remain critical. 11

## Primary and administrative authorities

### D. Primary legal texts

The core legal architecture is stable and official. For this topic, the primary texts are the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons with the EU, especially Article 5 AFMP and Articles 17-23 of Annex I; the Ordinance introducing free movement; the Federal Act on Foreign Nationals and Integration; the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Gainful Employment; the Posted Workers Act and Posted Workers Ordinance; and the Recruitment Act for labour-leasing issues. SECO's and the posting portal's official "labour law" pages identify these as the governing sources for posted workers and service provision. 26

Within that framework, the most operationally important provisions for short assignments are those identified in SEM's instructions: AIG Article 11 and VZAE Articles 1-4 for the concept of gainful activity; VZAE Article 12 for certain short-term work settings; VZAE Article 14 for work "without taking up a

<!-- 5 -->

position" and the eight-day threshold; VZAE Article 19(4)(a) and Article 56 for the four-month or 120-day permit logic; AIG Articles 22 and 23 for wage, working-condition and qualification checks; and AIG Article 26 for service-provider admission. SEM's instructions use those provisions directly in analysing postings, project work and temporary service provision. 27

For labour leasing, the critical texts are the Recruitment Act and, for AFMP cases, Article 22(3) of Annex I AFMP as cited in SEM's user guide. The guide expressly states that the leasing of staff from abroad to work in Switzerland is not permitted. SEM's AIG instructions separately explain that where the host enterprise has instruction rights over the worker, the arrangement is treated as labour leasing rather than a mere services contract. 21

### E. Official guidance and administrative materials

SEM's public notification page is the best starting point for the legal availability of notification. It sets out the covered categories, the 90-day structure, the eight-day filing rule, the "day one" sectors, the emergency exception and the fact that the day counts apply both to the posting company and to the individual worker. 28

SEM's 2026 user guide is the strongest operational source for internal content drafting. It confirms that each assignment must be entered separately, that employers may notify only their own employees, that changes must be reported immediately, that new notifications are required for replacement workers, additional workers and follow-up or warranty work, and that foreign labour leasing is prohibited. It also explains how multiple worksites and multiple engagements should be handled, which is directly relevant to rolling projects and fragmented filings. 29

SEM's annex on the concept of gainful activity is the most useful source for the business-visitor versus productive-work distinction. It gives a conservative and practical taxonomy of exempt and non-exempt activities and should be treated as the key administrative authority fordrafting examples about meetings, negotiations, conferences, theoretical training, maintenance, implementation and project deployment. 2

SEM's wider Chapter 4 instructions areessential where notification is unavailable or where a permnit is needed. They explain the broad work test, the distinction between work with and without taking up a position in Switzerland, the permit-free or permit-triggering effect of the eight-day threshold for foreign-employer cases, the obligation to wait for the decision abroad where a permit is required, the short-term four-month route, anti-rotation controls, and theauthority's approach to sham self-employment and labour leasing. 30

SECO's pages complement SEM by separating Swiss hiring from cross-border posting, setting out the rules for employers based outside the EU/EFTA, and pointing out thestronger sectoral controls for third-country postings. The posting portal then adds the practical layer on wages, conditions, recurring work and sanctions. 31

## Cantonal practice notes

### F. Cantonal practice notes

Zurich provides particularly useful evidence on the documents that make a project case look real and admissible. For third-party client-site postings, Zurich requires a specific statement of reasons, a project description, the Swiss client contract or works contract, a posting confirmation or posting agreement

<!-- 6 -->

depending on duration, and personal documents such as CVs, diplomas and passport copies. For intra-group postings without a third-party client, Zurich still expects a tailored explanation, the posting confirmation or assignment agreement, and the same personal evidence. This is a strong official indication that weak or generic project files are vulnerable. 32

Zurich also states, in general terms, that non-EU/EFTA nationals require a work permit in every case. That must be read carefully and in context: it is a useful cantonal practice note for standard Swiss employment and permit routes, but it does not override the separate federal rule allowing some third-country workers posted by EU/EFTA employers to use the AFMMP notificationroute if the 12-month labour-market condition is met. Internal drafting should therefore avoid using Zurich's statement as a universal federal rule without qualification. 33

Geneva's official pages are particularly helpful on timing and admission standards. For EU/EFTA companies or self-employed service providers, Geneva says that assignments of up to 90 days per year or three consecutive months must be notified, with only effective working days counted; assignments above that threshold require cantonal authorisation, usually filed six to eight weeks in advance, and work may not start before approval. For longer cases, Geneva also highlights the Swiss economic-interest test, the focus on managers, specialists and qualified workers, quota availability, and compliance with customary wage and working conditions. 34

For employers or independent providers based outside the EU/EFTA, Geneva states more starkly that cantonal authorisation is required, the file should be filed six to eight weeks before the mission, and work usually cannot start before authorisation is granted. Geneva's employer portal also shows a dedicated filing channel for posted workers outside intra-group transfer cases, which is useful for internal process mapping. 35

Ticino's official pages confirmn the same federal structure from the Italian-language side: EU/EFTA citizens and posted workers of EU/EFTA companies working for fewer than 90 days do not need a permit but must notify, while broader permit rights above 90 days are limited and special service agreements remain a separate category. These pages are useful corroborative multilingual sources rather than the main authority. 36

## Practical legal analysis

### G. Practical legal analysis

The safest analytical distinction is between presence for discussion and presence for delivery.If the visitor is in Switzerland to discuss, negotiate, observe, prepare or attend passively, SEM's materials support non-gainful treatment more readily. If the person is in Switzerland to install, repair, implement, train on live systems, supervise performance operationally, carry out project tasks on site or otherwise produce an output for a Swiss business or site, Swiss authorities are much more likely to treat the activity as work. Internal content should therefore focus less on labels such as "assignment", "deployment" or "business trip", and more on what the person will physically do in Switzerland. 37

Project deployments are not protected merely because they are finite or commercially defined as projects. SEM's own examples treat software implementation and reorganisation roll-out as gainful activity. Zurich's requirement for a project description and Swiss client contract points in the same direction: the authorities expect a genuine, evidenced project with defined activities, location and timing, not a broad narrative that a specialist is "assisting" or "supporting" a Swiss affiliate or client. Where the file does not show a genuine temporary project, authorities may suspect local work or labour leasing instead. 38

<!-- 7 -->

Client-site work is a major fault line. Genuine cross-border service provision is still possible at a Swiss client site, but only where the foreign company remains the real service provider and the worker acts on behalf of and at the expense of that foreign company. Once the Swiss client or Swiss affiliate gives operative instructions, controls the work process, or integrates the worker into its daily organisation, SEM's guidance moves towvards labour leasing or sham self-employment analysis. That is especially risky where the person fills a functional gap in the Swiss business rather than delivering a self-contained external service. 39

Repeat assignments and fragmented filings are another recurrent risk. SEM's user guide says each assignment and place of work should normally be reported, while the recurring-work materials require later work on the same project toreference the earlier notification. SEM's permit guidance also prohibits rotation through 120-day or four-month permits to avoid quotas or other controls. A pattern of monthly "few days on site" visits for the same implementation, reorganisation or operational support case is therefore not low risk simply because each trip is short. Authorities will look at cumulative reality, not just isolated travel dates. 40

Timing is legally material. The user guide gives a strict calendar rule: eight calendar days, including Sundays and public holidays, before work begins, unless the particular route is a Swiss short-term hire which is filed by the day before. It also says changes must be reported immediately and that certain changes require a new notification. Geneva and SEM both make clear that permit-requiring work must not start before approval. Internal content should therefore assume that "we have filed" is not enough if the waiting period has not yet run or the permit decision is still pending. 41

Salary and working conditions matter because they are bound up with immigration and posting compliance. SEM's instructions state that posted workers and permit-required service providers must meet Swiss customary wage and working conditions, and that assignment expenses such as travel, accommodation and meals are not to be netted off against salary. The posting portal and sanctions pages make clear that wage and condition breaches can lead to substantial sanctions. This should be flagged in immigration content only as an admission and compliance condition, not as broader employment-law advice. 42

A narrow but important exception exists for some specialist cross-border services over 90 days. SEM's FAQ and user guide say that special Switzerland-EU service agreements, notably in public procurement and air and land transport, can create an entitlement to authorisation for the full duration of the service. Internal content should therefore avoid saying flatly that there is "no entitlement" to any permit above 90 days; the correct formulation is that general services above 90 days fall outside the short-service right and are handled under national law, usually without entitlement, except where a special service agreement apoplies. 43

Finally, a small but potentially material nationality nuance has to be preserved. The main short-service notification route is built around EU/EFTA law, but the UK has a separate short-service mobility arrangement for qualifying UK-based service providers. Internal content should therefore not present "non-EU/EFTA" as a single undifferentiated class for all short-term services, even though the general rule remains that non-EU/EFTA national and employer cases are substantially more restricted than EU/EFTA cases. 44

## Drafting cautions and sources

### H. Drafting cautions

Future content on Swiss short-term assignment work should never state or imply that short duration by itself removes authorisation requirements. The legally safer formulation is that short durationis only one variable, alongside nationality, employer location, activity type, sector, day counts and the availability of a specific notification route. 45

Content should distinguish clearly between a genuine business visit and productive work. "Meetings", "training" and "consulting" are not safe umbrella terms. The guidance supports different outcomes for theoretical training without integration into the work process and on-the-job training in live operations; likewise for passive conference attendance as compared with speaking or delivering services. 46

Content should also distinguish between Swiss employment, cross-border service provision, intra-group assignment, local hire, self-employed service provision and labour leasing. Where the Swiss host gives instructions, controls the workflow, or uses the assignee as if they were part of the Swiss workforce, the facts may move away from service provision and towards labour leasing or local working.Foreign labour leasing should be treated as a major red-flag concept, not a routine staffing option. 47

Notification should never be described as "simple" without qualification. Even when notification is legally available, it is assignment-specific, time-sensitive, sector-sensitive, and dependent on correct day counting and correct classification. The posting company remains responsible for correctness even if the client or another representative files the submission. 48

Commercial urgency should not be presented as a cure for defects. The emergency exception is framed around repairs, accidents, natural disasters and comparable unforeseeable damage situations,and even then the work must begin immediately, generally within three calendar days of the damage. This is much narrower than ordinary project urgency, deal deadlines or client escalation. 28

Where the route is permit-based, content should emphasise that work cannot start until the permit or authorisation pathway has been completed. Geneva states this expressly for both EU/EFTA over-90-day cases and third-country cases, and SEM's instructions say the decision must be awaited abroad where a permit is required. 49

Where the case involves regulated professions, add a narrow compliance flag rather than full professional-regulation advice: SEM states that EU/EFTA service providers in regulated professions for up to 90 days may also need a separate professional-qualification declaration through SERI. 50
