# Swiss legal authority pack on repeat assignments, cumulative presence and fragmentation risk in short-term assignment cases

# Executive legal summary

This pack addresses how Swiss immigration and labour-market authorisation rules treat repeated short-term assignments, rolling projects, split deployments, rotating workers and attempts to keep activity below notification or permit thresholds. The clearest official rule is that, for the EU/EFTA short-term service regime, counting is cumulative at least at **person** **level and** **sending** **undertaking** **level.** SEM states that the 90 working-day annual limit and the eight-day notification threshold apply both to the posting undertaking and to the posted worker; for the undertaking, what matters is the dates on which its staff are posted to Switzerland, not the number of staff simultaneously present. A worker's own 90-day balance is also cumulative and is not reset merely because a different employer posts that worker later in the same calendar year. 1

That means Swiss law and official guidance do not permit analysis of each visit in isolation where the visits are productive, work-related activities within the notification regime. Repeated five-day or ten-day project visits can add up to notification or permit consequences even if each trip is short. Official SEM guidance also treats split activity across several periods as a normal cumulative scenario: the ZEMIS examples expressly show multiple staggered reporting periods within one year being aggregated to exhaust the 90-day allowance. 2

The materials reviewed do **not** identify a separate 90-day bucket by Swiss client, project, recipient or site. Those concepts matter in a different way: they determine how work must be described and filed in the notification system. As a rule, each assignment and worksite must be notified; however, one notification can sometimes cover repeated deployments for the same order, same client and same site, and in a narrower exception one maintenance or service relationship for the same client across different sites. Resumed work, warranty work and follow-on work are not always treated as wholly fresh matters; SEM guidance requires linked follow-up filing and, in certain cases, no new eight-day lead time. 3

The principal anti-fragmentation points in the official materials are practical rather than doctrinal. They appear through cumulative person/company counting, the continuing treatment of phased and interrupted project work, rules on multiple sites and same-day deployments, the prohibition on combining the notification route with a 120-day/four-month service permit in a way that circumvents legislative limits, and scrutiny of Swiss "letterbox" or sham establishments created to disguise what is really foreign service provision or to bypass the 90-day ceiling. 4

Once activity clearly exceeds, or is extended beyond, 90 effective workdays or three months in a calendar year, the case moves into permnit territory. Outside the special bilateral service sectors identified by SEM, that longer service provision falls under the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act and implementing rules, is subject to labour-market review and quota logic, and carries **no** **legal** **entitlement** to approval. SEM's instructions also direct cantons to examine whether the work is in the overall economic interest of Switzerland and not readily satisfiable by labour already available in the region, especially in sectors such as construction and hospitality. 5

# Structural overview of cumulative presence and repeat assignments

## Legal architecture of short-term assignment control

Swiss law starts from a general principle that gainful activity in Switzerland is subject to authorisation. For EU/EFTA-linked short-term activity, however, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and its Swiss implementing framework create a notification route rather than a full permit route for certain categories of work carried out for up to three months or 90 days within a calendar year. SEM's principal categories are: EU/EFTA nationals taking up Swiss employment for up to three months; EU/EFTA self-employed service providers established in an EU/EFTA state; and posted workers employed by an undertaking based in an EU/EFTA state, irrespective of nationality, provided non-EU/EFTA posted workers were already durably admitted to the regular laboour market of an EU/EFTA state for at least 12months before posting. 6

The legal instruments repeatedly cited by SEM and cantonal authorities for this framework are the AFMP/FZA and Annex I, the Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons, the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment, the Federal Act on Posted Workers and its ordinance, and-where staff leasing is attempted-the Employment Services Act. Official cantonal materials also identify the relevant SR/RS numbers for these texts.7

For employers based **outside** the EU/EFTA, the notification route does not generally apply. SECO's official guidance states that postings by third-country employers are permit-required from the first day in the first-day sectors and otherwise if the work lasts more than eight days in the calendar year. It likewise states that, in the non-first-day sectors, what matters is the effective duration of the work in Switzerland and it is irrelevant whether the work is continuous or carried out day by day. 8

## Counting dimensions that official materials clearly recognise

The clearest counting rule is the **double** **count** for posted work: the annual 90-day ceiling and the eight notification-free days are counted both for the posting undertaking and for the posted worker. SEM's online guidance, FAQ and user handbook all say this expressly. The practical effect is that a company can exhaust its own annual allowance even where some individual workers still have personal days left, and a worker can exhaust personal days even where a new company still has undertaking-level days available. 9

SEM is equally clear that simultaneous headcount does not multiply the undertaking's daily count. If a construction company posts three workers at the same time for five working days, only five days are deducted from the undertaking's balance. But if those three workers are deployed sequentially on different days, 15 company-days are used. That is one of the official reasons why worker rotation does not, by itself, eliminate cumulative presence risk. It may preserve one worker's personal balance; it does **not** create new undertaking-level days. 10

Swiss authorities also treat the worker's personal balance as portable across employers. The official FAQ on entsendung.admin.ch states that if a worker has already been posted to Switzerland for 90 days by

one company, no other company may post that worker again in the same calendar year. SEM's annexed examples likewise state that a person who has exhausted the 90-day allowance cannot then rely on the notification regime later that year as a Swiss employee, a posted worker of another undertaking, or a self-employed provider. 11

For Swiss employment with a Swiss employer, SEM's annexes indicate that the notification route is still personal. A Swiss employer may notify multiple EU/EFTA workers if each contract is limited to three months in the calendar year, but the individual employee's own 90-day ceiling remains relevant. Cantonal Bern's guidance summarises that position by stating that a Swiss company may engage as many EU/EFTA workers as it wishes under the notification regime, but each worker's Swiss work is limited to 90 days per calendar year. 12

## Client, project and worksite concepts in the official material

The reviewed official materials do not set out a standalone annual quota by Swiss client, Swiss recipient, project or worksite. Instead, those concepts appear mostly in the filing mechanics. SEM's 2026 user handbook states that, as a rule, **each** **individual order** **and** **each** **worksite** must be notified,and all planned workdays must be specified. One filing is enough if the same order is carried out for the same client at the same site over multiple deployments, or where there is uninterrupted work at the same site. In a narrower exception, one filing may also cover maintenance or service work performed in several deployments for one client at different sites, provided the dates for the individual orders and the first site are listed. 13

The same handbook also recognisesthat split or phased assignments remain part of the same regulated work pattern. If there is an interruption, a duration change, or cancellation, the cantonal authority must be informed. If work resumes after interruption, or follow-up work is carried out on the same project, or warranty work is to be performed, a new notification is required that references the earlier notification; but SEM says this linked new filing does **not** trigger a new eight-day waiting period in those cases. The official "recurring orders" material on entsendung.admin.ch goes further and states that where work on a project resumes within three months after the end of the last work, or maintenance or warranty work is carried out within three months, the new filing must refer back to the first project notification and no new eight-day period is triggered. 3

That is an important practical anti-fragmentation rule. Split billing periods, breaks between phases, or the use of "warranty", "service" or "follow-on" labels do not automatically reset the short-term analysis. The filing system itself preserves continuity where the underlying project relationship remains linked. 3

The worksite rules also matter. SEM's examples say the same worker may not be notified on two sites simultaneously on the same calendar day. Consecutive work at different sites on the same day is allowed, with approximate times entered in the comments field, and still counts as only one workday. This is a narrow operational rule, but it shows that Swiss authorities focus on **effective** **workdays** **actually** **worked,** not merely the existence of multiple projects or multiple Swiss locations. 10

## Productive work versus non-productive visits

Not every repeat visit belongs in the cumulative authorisation analysis. SEM's Annex 5 distinguishes non-notifiable and notifiable activity. Purely representative visits, non-binding customer relationship meetings, internal strategy meetings, workshop attendance without productive contribution, and pure goods delivery including unloading are listed as non-notifiable. By contrast, customer advice on a concrete Swiss project, project-planning meetings, acceptance of subcontractor work on a Swiss

<!-- 3 -->

construction site, measurements taken for a Swiss project, implementation of a programme at a Swiss headquarters, repair, maintenance and warranty work, installation work, and project work spread over several months for a few days each month are all given as **notifiable** **productive** **activity.** 14

This distinction is central for repeat-assignment analysis. Swiss authorities do not appear to aggregate mere travel or ordinary non-productive business meetings into a work-day quota; but they do aggregate productive project activity even where that activity is broken into brief and intermittent visits. The official example of a specialist who comes on site for a few days each month over several months to implement a reorganisation programme shows exactly that. 15

# Primary legal texts and official administrative materials

## Primary legal texts

The primary legal framework identified in the official materials reviewed is as follows. The treaty base is the **Agreement** on **the** **Free** **Movement** **of Persons,** SR 0.142.112.681, especially Art. 5 AFMP/FZA and Annex I provisions relied on by SEM for short-term service provision and notification. SEM's VFP instructions cite Art. 5(1) AFMP/FZA and Annex I Arts 6, 20, 21 and 22 as the key provisions for the 90-day service regime, the notification route and the non-liberalised sectors. 27

The Swiss implementing texts expressly identified by SEM and cantonal authorities are the **Foreign** **Nationals and Integration Act,** AIG/FNIA, SR 142.20; the **Ordinance on Admission, Stay and** **Employment,** VZAE/OASA, SR 142.201; the **Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons,** VFP/FMPO, SR 142.203; the **Federal Act on Posted Workers,** EntsG, SR 823.20; the **Ordinance on Posted Workers,** EntsV, SR 823.201; and the **Employment** **Services** **Act,** AVG/RecA, SR 823.11. 28

For the non-EU/EFTA and over-90-day parts of the analysis, the key AIG/VZAE provisions highlighted by SEM concern the overall economic interest of Switzerland, labour-market access conditions, wage and employment conditions, qualifications and quota treatment. For short cross-border activity outside the AFMP route, older official VZAE materials indexed by SEM also indicate that foreign nationals performing cross-border services or temporary work for a foreign employer require a permit if the activity exceeds eight days in the calendar year. 29

An ancillary but useful official source for the service-versus-establishment distinction in regulated professions is the **Federal Act on the Declaration Requirement and Verification of Service Provider** **Qualifications** **in** **Regulated** **Professions,** DRPA, SR 935.01, and the related ordinance, cited by SERI in its service-provider guidance. That guidance is not the primary immigration text for ordinary posting cases, but it is an official statement of how Switzerland distinguishes temporary service provision from establishment. 30

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most operationally useful federal materials for this topic are SEM's current pages on the notification procedure, its FAQ, its 2026user handbook for the notification platform, and the 2026 VFP instructions with the 2024 annexes. Those documents contain the clearest official statements on cumulative counting, staggered periods, worker/company balances, multiple staff, change handling,recurring orders and the relationship between notification and permits. 31

SEM's 2023 fact sheet on cross-border service provision remains useful for twNo points: first, the AFMP liberalises only a **partial** cross-border service freedom; and second, outside special service agreements, services lasting more than 90 days fall under AIG rather than AFMP and are labour-market tested and quota-managed. 32

SECO's materials are important on the labour and posted-worker side. They reproduce the first-day sectors, explain when non-EU/EFTA postings need permits instead of notification, and emphasise that in ordinary sectors daywise and intermittent work still counts once it exceeds eight days in the year. SECO's and cantonal materials also point to sanctions and published service bans under the posted-workers framework. 33

Some useful operational points are found on **entsendung.admin.ch,** notably that a worker who has already used the 90-day annual allowance cannot be reposted by another undertaking in the samne year, and that resumed project work, maintenance and warranty work within three months should be linked back to the original notification without a new eight-day waiting period. In this research session, those points were retrievable through indexed official search snippets rather than a full page open, so they are best treated as strong **practice** **points** that sit alongside-rather than above-the SEM handbook and SEM instructions. 34

## Cantonal practice notes

The cantonal materials reviewed do not materially depart from the federal scheme, but they are useful for how authorities at filing level present the rules. **Geneva** states that missions or service provision of up to 90 days a yyear or three consecutive months must be announced to the Confederation and that **only** **effective** **working** **days** should be notified; it gives the example of a two-month posting where non-working Thursdays and weekends are not announced. 35

**Ticino** repeats that, outside the first-day sectors, notification is required only if the work in Switzerland exceeds eight days in the calendar year, **regardless of whether it is performed continuously or by** **individual days.** It also states that activity beyond 90 working ddays or three months per calendar year remains permit-required. 36

**Zurich** confirms the 90-day annual notification route for EU/EFTA workers and refers users back to SEM Annex 5 for distinguishing notifiable from non-notifiable activity. It also emphasises that work may not start before expiry of the eight-day notice period save for genuine emergency cases, and that short-notice project planning or staffing problems are not accepted as emergencies.37

**Aargau** **and** **Bern** are useful mainly because their official pages restate the company-based cap: an EU/ EFTA undertaking may post workers for a maximum of 90 days per calendar year, after which an authorisation route is required. Aargau's pages also list the principal legal texts and stress that the foreign undertaking may only notify its own employees. 38

## Practical legal analysis

### How Swiss authorities are likely to view repeat assignments, rolling projects and fragmentation risk

In a standard EU/EFTA service case, the safest legal view isthat repeated short project visits form part of one annual cumulative analysis at company and person level. A reorganisation expert coming to Switzerland for a few days each month over several mnonths is not treated in the official material as a succession of isolated "fresh starts"; SEM lists that fact pattern as a notifiable project deployment.The same is true of implementation work, installation work, repair, maintenance and warranty activity. 15

That has direct consequences for rolling projects and split assignments. If the work is productive and within the scope of the notification regime, the relevant question is not simply "how long is this trip0?" but also "how many days has this person used this year?" and "how many dates has this undertaking already used this year?" If the same order is being performed for the same client at the same site across several deployments, SEM allows a single filing that contains all relevant dates. If the work stops and restarts,or warranty work follows, the later filing must be linked to the earlier filing rather than being presented as unrelated activity. 3

<!-- 10 -->

Rotating workers is only a partial solution, and often not a solution at all.It may preserve the original worker's personal balance; but it does not create a fresh undertaking balance. The company's annual allowance is consumed by the dates on which its staff work in Switzerland. Official examples make the contrast explicit: three workers on the same five days use five company days; three workers on different days use 15. Once the company's 90 annual days are exhausted, even a worker who has never before been posted by that company cannot be sent under notification. 39

Changing Swiss clients or worksites should likewise not be presented as a reset mechanism. The official material does not create separate annual day pools by client, recipient or site. Instead, those elements affect what must be filed. A new client or new worksite may require a new filing. It does **not** obviously restore an exhausted person-level or undertaking-level annual balance. Officially, what resets the notification count is the next **calendar** **year,** not a change of Swiss end-customer, worksite or project phase. 40

Where the case begins to look less like a temporary cross-border service and more like an ongoing Swiss operation, the legal risk shifts. SEM instructs cantons to verify whether a purported Swiss employer actually carries on effective and durable activity in Switzerland, or whether the Swiss entity is a letterbox arrangement used to bypass the 90-day service ceiling by creating the appearance of a Swiss local hiring. The authority may verify management, office infrastructure, payroll evidence and operational autonomy. In parallel, SERI's official guidance for regulated professions states that duration is only one criterion: even a sub-90-day stay can be establishment rather than service provision if the person comes with the intention of moving to Switzerland and entering the Swiss labour market. That SERI point must be used carefully outside regulated professions, but it is a useful warning signal. 41

Longer or repeatedly renewed project work also triggers labour-market risk. Once the work clearly exceeds 90 days or is obviously going to do so, the file moves out of the simplified AFMP notification framework and into discretionary permit territory. SEM says the cantonal authority must then assess the case under AIG/VZAE; it must consider overall economic interest, labour-market access conditions and wage safeguards, and it should generally not approve foreign service labour where suitable regional labour is available, save in cases of genuine specialised know-how or project necessity. Official guidance specifically mentions that exceptions may be justified where the posted employee's involvement is absolutely necessary because otherwise the whole project could be jeopardised. 42

The relationship between notification and four-month/120-day permit practice is a further anti-fragmentation mechanism. SEM's annexes allow later permits in the same calendar year, but only in tightly delimited ways: used notification days are offset against the 120-day total, and a person already holding a 120-day/four-month service permit cannot simply fall back onto notification in thesame year for the same service-provider activity. SEM calls the contrary an impermissible circumvention of the legislator's restriction and instructs cantons to refuse the notification. 24

A final practical nuance matters for intermittent assignments: in Swiss practice, only **effective** **working** **days** should be counted and declared, not entire spans that include idle weekends. Geneva says so expressly; SEM's ZEMIS examples show that if a filer wrongly reports the entire calendar stretch including weekends, the system counts those days and can reject the filing for exceeding the maximum. That means accurate day-by-day scheduling matters. It is a compliance point, not a loophole. 43

## Drafting cautions

Internal content on this topic should state the counting rules conservatively. It should **not** say that each visit is assessed separately wherever productive work in Switzerland is being carried out. It should instead say that the official Swiss materials aggregate notification time at least by person and by

<!-- 11 -->

posting undertaking, and that repeated short phases can be part of one cumulative annual authorisation analysis. 44

It should not imply that rotating workers necessarily avoids risk.A worker rotation may preserve one individual's remaining balance, but it does not stop the company from using up its own annual days, and simultaneous versus sequential deployment changes the undertaking's count. It shoulId also **not** imply that changing employer always resolves the issue, because a worker's own 90-day annual balance followvs that worker across employers. 45

It should **not** imply that splitting a project into phases, pausing the work, or relabelling later work as service, follow-up or warranty automatically removes notification or permit risk. The SEM handbook and recurring-order guidance treat resumed and follow-on work as linked back to the earlier project notification in specified circumstances. 3

It should distinguish **company/person counting** from **client/site/project filing mechanics.** The reviewed official sources do not establish a separate annual 90-day pool by Swiss client or site. They do, however, require separate or specially structured notifications by orderand site. Any internal content suggesting a formal recipient-level or project-level annual counting bucket would therefore be too confident unless a route-specific canton or sector guidance expressly says so. 46

It should distinguish **productive** **project** **work** from ordinary business travel. SEM's Annex 5 makes that distinction concrete. Non-binding meetings and internal strategy sessions may fall outside notification analysis; project planning, technical advice, implementation, acceptance work, and monthly on-site project activity do not. 47

It should also avoid treating the SERI "service versus establishment" guidance as if it were a universal immigration counting code. It is formally aimed at regulated professionsand the declaration procedure for professional qualifications. It nevertheless offers a useful conservative warning: duration alone is not the only criterion, and an apparently short presence may still be analysed as establishment if the factual pattern shows integration into the Swiss labour market. 30

## Open questions and limitations

The official sources reviewed are strong on **person-level and** **undertaking-level** counting,on notification mechanics, and on the move from notification to permit. They are much thinner on any standalone aggregation by Swiss recipient, project or site. For internal drafting, that means recipient/ project/site should be presented as **filing** **and** **continuity** **concepts,** not clearly established annual counting buckets, unless future canton- or sector-specific material supports afirmer proposition. 46

The strongest anti-fragmentation content is administrative rather than judicial. The pack therefore relies mainly on legislation, SEM/SECO guidance, SERI guidance and cantonal practice pages, not on reported case law. That is appropriate for operational compliance drafting, but it means the safest formulations are those that track the official guidance closely rather than extending it into broad anti-abuse doctrines not expressly stated. 48
