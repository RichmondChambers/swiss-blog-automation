# Swiss legal authority pack on short-termassignment refusals and compliance failures

**Suggested internal filename:**

legalauthorityshorttermassignmentrefusalsandcompliancefailurespack.pdf

This pack is drafted for internal use in an immigration law content system. It focuses on **Swiss** **short-term assignment, posting, cross-border service provision, and project-based work** where the principal risks are refusal, misclassification, non-compliance, or later adverse treatment. It is written conservatively and distinguishes between **clear legal rules, evidential or administrative practice** **risks, and areas where the official materials are thin.** Unless stated otherwise, references to "work authorisation" include both **permit** and **notification** regimes where Swiss law treats either as a required immigration step. 1

## Executive legal summary and structural overview

This section covers items A and B.

Swiss law starts from a broad concept of "gainful employment". Under the FNIA, foreign nationals who wish to work in Switzerland require authorisation regardless of the length of stay, and "gainful employment" includes any salaried or self-employed activity normally carried out for payment, whether or not payment is actually made. SEM's 2026 directives emphasise that the concept must be interpreted **widely,** using **objective** rather than subjective criteria, and that activity affecting the Swiss labour market is generally treated as gainful employment. In doubtful cases, the competent cantonal labour-market authority decides, and must refer doubtful cases to SEM. 2

That broad starting point explains why many short Swiss assignments fail or attract compliance risk. The core risk pattern is not only "missing a permit", but **misdescribing** **productive** **work** **as** **a** **visit,** **training, supervision,** or **client** **meeting,** and then using the wrong route or no route at all. SEM's own annex on the notion of gainful activity treats passive conference attendance, short business meetings, negotiations, and purely theoretical training as non-gainful in principle, but treats on-the-job training integrated into work or production, repairs, maintenance, project implementation, and regular personnel-management activity in Switzerland as gainful employment. 3

A second structural divide is between the **EUJ/EFTA** **notification** **regime** and the **ordinary** **permit** **regime.** For EU/EFTA-based service provision within the scope of free movement, notification can cover certain work up to **90** **working** **days per calendar** **year,** but each assignment must still be notified in time, and the work usually may not start until the notification rules are satisfied. Beyond 90 days, a permit is required and there is **no** **legal entitlement** to that permit merely because the first 90 days were notification-eligible. By contrast, for FNIA/OASA cases outside the notification regime, cross-border work for a foreign employer generally requires a permit once activity exceeds **eight** **days** **in** **a** **calendar year,** and in certain sectors a permit is required **from** **day** **one.** 4

A third structural risk concerns **who is really employing the worker and where the work is really** **anchored.** Swiss immigration law uses a factual concept of the employer. SEM states that the de facto employer is the person or business that uses the worker under its authority, with its tools or on its premises; in staff-leasing situations, the user undertaking is treated as the de facto employer. In parallel, SECO and SEM materials make clear that cross-border **staff leasing/** **labour leasing** **from** **abroad** is not permitted, including indirect leasing through a foreign service provider that itself uses borrowed labour. This matters because assignments dressed up as "project support" can in practice be treated as local hire, staff augmentation, or unlawful service/leasing structures. 5

A fourth recurring risk is **weak** **evidence.** Swiss statutes do not usually say "refuse because the project evidence is weak", but official materials do require enough information to classify the activity correctly and test the admission criteria. SEM's business-visit/training annex tells authorities to verify from the documents that training is genuinely theoretical and to check the duration, content, and location. Zurich's official permit checklist for posted employees sent to a third-party Swiss undertaking requires a specific application justification, project description, Swiss contract/work contract, assignment confirmation or assignment contract, and detailed data on the home employer, host company, function, tasks, location, duration, salary, expenses, insurance, and social contributions. Weak, vague, inconsistent, or missing documents therefore create a real refusal or reclassification risk even where there is no standalone statutory "weak evidence" refusal ground. 6

The main legal consequences described in official material are also differentiated. Under the FNIA, employing unauthorised foreign workers or procuring unauthorised cross-border services can trigger criminal liability; breaches of notification obligations can also be fined; and repeated employer misconduct can lead the authorities to **refuse or only partially authorise future applications** for foreign workers. Under the Posted Workers Act, infringements relating to minimum Swiss salary and working conditions, notification, or due diligence in subcontracting can lead to administrative fines, service bans, and publication on a public sanctions list. These are overlapping but not identical regimes. A compliant salary position under posted-worker law does **not** cure an immigration defect, and a formally valid notification does not immunise a case from labour inspection or sanction.7

## Primary legal texts and official materials

This section covers items **D** and E.

The **federal statutory core** is the FNIA (LEI/AIG), especially articles **11, 18, 22, 26, 91, 117, 120,** and **122.** Article 11 creates the universal work-authorisation rule and defines gainful activity. Article 18 sets the baseline admission conditions for salaried employment by requiring, among other things, an employer application and compliance with the conditions in articles 20-25. Article 22 requires compliance with salary and employment conditions customary for the place, profession, and sector, and also regulates compensation of posted employees' expenses. Article 26 governs temporary cross-border services under the FNIA. Article 91 imposes due-diligence duties on employers and recipients of services. Articles 117 and 120 create offence regimes, while article 122 expressly allows the authority to refuse or only partially authorise future employer applications after repeated violations. 35

The principal **implementing** **ordinance** is the OASA/VZAE. Articles 1a-4 are especially important because they widen and operationalise the concept of gainful employment: work for a Swiss or foreign employer counts, even if paid abroad, and even if performed only by the hour, by the day,or temporarily; cross-border services are expressly included; and the canton decides in the first instance whether an activity is gainful, with SEM deciding doubtful cases. Article 12 deals with short-term work entry permissions and distinguishes cases that do or do not require declaration of arrival. Article **14** is central for non-notification cases: more than eight days of cross-border woork in a calendar year requires a permit, and listed sectors require a permit whatever the duration. 36

<!-- 7 -->

For the **EU/EFTA** **notification** **regimne,** the key official authority is the OLCP/VEP notification structure as explained by SEM and SECO. OLCP article **9** applies the posted-worker notification rules by analogy to short Swiss employment of up to three months and to self-employed service provision up to 90 working days; for Swiss employment of up to three months, the notification is due no later than the day before work starts. SEM's current guidance states that each assignment must be entered in the system in time; the eight-day period and the 90-day limit are counted both for the undertaking and for the worker; and for services beyond 90 days a permit is required with no legal entitlement. The same materials also stress that the notification route does **not** apply outside the defined categories. 37

**The Posted Workers Act** and **Posted Workers Ordinance** are the main official sources for salary-condition compliance and for the timing/content of notifications for posted workers. PWA article 2requires the employer to guarantee the Swiss minimum working and salary conditions in the protected statutory domains. EntsV article 6 makes notification mandatory for all work exceeding eight days per calendar year, from day one in specified sectors, and allows work within the eight-day period only in narrow emergencies. It also specifies the content of the notification, including worker identity, start date, expected duration, type of work, activity and function in Switzerland, precise place of work, and a contact person; for non-EU/EFTA nationals posted from an EU/EFTA employer, thenotification must also include their residence status in the posting state. 38

The sanctions side is split. Under the PWA, article 9 allows administrative sanctions including fines of up to **CHF** **5,000** for certain breaches such as notification-related violations, up to **CHF** **30,000** for article 2breaches, service bans for one to five years, recovery of control costs, and a public list of sanctioned businesses maintained by SECO. SECO's posted-workers pages confirm the monthly public list and the public nature of service-ban sanctions. For notification offences under the OLCPitself, the Federal Council report cited in SEM search results states that article **32a** **OLCP** punishes intentional or negligent breach oof the notification duties with a fine of up to **CHF** **5,000.** 39

The most useful **SEM administrative** **guidance** for classification issues is the 2026 LEI chapter on gainful activity and the separate annex on the "notion of gainful activity". Those materials give the clearest official examnples of where Switzerland itself distinguishes a visit from **productive** work.They also confirm that the immigration characterisation does not depend on how other authorities, such as tax or social-security bodies, classify the relationship; for immigration law, the OASA definition is decisive. 40

The most useful **SECO** **administrative** **guidance** for structural compliance risk concerns labour leasing and posted-worker enforcement. SECO's Recruitment Act guidance states that direct labour leasing from abroad to Switzerland is not permitted and that indirect leasing by way of a foreign service provider using borrowed staff is equally impermissible. SEM's English notification guidance mirrors that position and cites both article 22(3) Annex I AFMP and article 12 of the Recruitment Act. This is stronger and more direct than many private summaries, and it should be treated as primary administrative authority on "assignment" structures that in truth look like staff augmentation. 41

## Cantonal practice notes

This section covers item F.

<!-- 8 -->

Official cantonal materials generally do not publish long catalogues of "refusal grounds". What they do publish is still valuable because it shows how the abstract federal rules are operationalised and where canton-level case handling becomes strict.

**Zurich** is especially useful. Its notification guidance states that posted workers and self-employed service providers must notify at least eight days before work, and that starting work before expiry of that period is **expressly** **not allowed.** It also says that, where a posted worker from an EU/EFTA employer is not an EU/EFTA national, a permit is mandatory before the Swiss work starts unless the worker has already been admitted permanently to the regular labour market of an EU/EFTA state for at least twelve months. Zurich further notes that Swiss, not foreign, law determines whether a person is genuinely self-employed for Swiss purposes. The page also lists the three core self-employment documents expected on inspection-notification confirmation or work-permit evidence, A1 social-security evidence, and the Swiss client contract or written client confirmation-and warns that failure to produce them can lead to a fine of up to **CHF** **5,000** and a stop-work order; where there is doubt, further proof of self-employment may be required. 42

Zurich's separate work-permit checklist for the **posting** **of employees** **to** **a** **third-party** **Swiss** **undertaking** is one of the best official evidential sources located for project-based permit cases. It requires a specific application justification, project description, Swiss contract/work contract, and assignment confirmation or assignment contract. The latter must identify the posted worker,the sending employer, the Swiss host or mission undertaking, function, duties, place of work, start date, duration, base salary, posting allowance, assumption of expenses, insurance, social-security arrangements, and Swiss-law clauses or equivalent regulation of working terms. In practice, this is powerful authority for saying that weak, generic, or internally inconsistent project evidence can materially undermine a permit case even where the statute itself is framed more generally. 43

**Ticino** also publishes practical compliance points. Its labour-market office states that posted workers and self-employed service providers must notify online at least eight days before the activity starts,that incomplete notifications will be **refused,** and that for posted workers the employer's declaration on Swiss minimum working and salary conditions must be attached. Ticino further states that work beyond **90 working days or three months per calendar year** remains permit-subject, and that-except in limited special-agreement contexts such as certain public-procurement or transport arrangements-there is no **right** to a work permit. It also states that third-country nationals do not benefit from the notification procedure unless they satisfy the EU/EFTA labour-market admission condition for posted third-country nationals. 44

**Geneva's** public-facing pages are briefer, but they confirm useful route distinctions. For EU/AELE undertakings or independents offering services or posting staff in Geneva, the route depends on the **duration** **of the** **mission**: the case must either be notified to the Confederation or authorised by Geneva. Geneva's labour-market office also states that EU/AELE businesses wanting to post staff or provide services in the canton for **more** **than** **90** **days** fall within the cantonal authorisation function. For unddertakings or independents from **outside** the EU/AELE, Geneva states that a canton permit is required and that the complete file should be sent **six** **to** **eight** **weeks** before the mission starts. 45

These cantonal materials should be used carefully. They are strong **practice** **notes,** not substitutes for the federal statutes and ordinances. But they are exactly the kind of official soources that support risk statements about what authorities want to see in real files, how late or incomplete notifications are handled, and how quickly an "assignment" case can become a permit or enforcement problem. 46

## Practical legal analysis

### This section covers item G.

The clearest legal risk is **misclassification of productive work as a business visit.** Official SEM materials do not treat "business visitor" as a free-standing statutory category that can absorb real project delivery. Instead, they draw a factual line between short, non-productive commercial presence and productive labour supplied into the Swiss market. Activities such as negotiations, market analysis, passive attendance, or purely theoretical training can sit on the non-gainful side; activities such as project implementation, reorganisation work, maintenance, repairs, productive on-the-job training, and recurring staff interviews or performance management in Switzerland sit on the gainful side. Where a file describes "meetings" but the evidence shows implementation, supervision with operational impact, hands-on troubleshooting, or delivery work at the client site, the official materials support reclassification to gainful employment. 47

A second risk is **incorrect use** **of the** **notification** **procedure.** The notification route is not a substitute for the permit regime whenever the parties prefer speed. It is limited by personal scope, by activity type,and by cumulative day-counting. SEM's current guidance makes clear that the 90-day rule is calculated for both the company and the worker, and that once the thresholds are known to be exceeded, the case belongs in the permit regime. For non-notification cases under the FNIA/OASA, article 14 OASA creates its own eight-day structure and day-one permit sectors. The practical lesson is that repeated short assignments can cease to be "low risk" surprisingly quickly, especially where several workers rotate through the same Swiss project or where historical use of Swiss assignments has already consumed the available threshold. 48

A third risk is **late** **filing** **and** **premature** **work** **start.** The official materials do not support any general proposition that late or defective filings can simply be regularised after the fact. SECO states plainly that work may start only after notification or permit issuance, and Zurich says wwork before expiry of the prior-notification period is expressly prohibited. SEM's FAQ allows some changes to a notification after filing, but those changes must be reported **immediately** **and** **before** **work** **begins** or before the notified period changes, and some changes require a new notification rather than a simple amendment. The single statutory continuation rule located in OASA article 14(2) is narrowv:it only addresses the case where a cross-border FNIA activity unexpectedly exceeds the original eight-day planning and the declaration is made before the eight days expire. It should not be used as a general "cure" proposition. 49

A fourth risk is **weak** **project evidence.** The official materials are strongest here at the administrative-practice level rather than as black-letter refusal grounds. SEM's annex requires the documentary record to show that training is really theoretical and to set out duration, content, and place. Zurich's checklist shows what a robust Swiss assignment file looks like in practice: specific business justification,defined project, defined Swiss contract, and assignment terms that identify the worker, home employer, Swiss recipient, function, duties, site, dates, remuneration, expenses, insurance, and social-security arrangements. Where the record is vague on the Swiss customer, the contractual deliverable, the practical tasks, the location, or the worker's exact role, the authorities have official support to doubt the route, the duration, the classification, or even whether the structure is a real cross-border service rather than local labour supply. 32

A fifth risk is that the actual arrangement looks like **local hire, staff augmentation, or labour leasing.** Swiss immigration law separates activity "with taking up employment" from activity "without taking up employment". SEM also treats the employer concept factually, not formalistically, and says the de facto employer is the user undertaking where the work is actually performed under that undertaking's direction. From that official starting point, a case becomes dangerous if the Swiss recipient controls the day-to-day work, sets the practical tasks, integrates the worker into its teams, provides the tools and premises, or expects the individual merely to fill labour gaps. Where the assignment additionally involves a foreign intermediary or borrowed staff, SECO's prohibition on direct and indirect labour leasing from abroad becomes highly relevant. The stronger the factual resemblance to staffing rather than a genuine deliverable-based service, the weaker any "assignment" framing becomes. This conclusion is partly an inference from the official materials, but it is a strong one. 50

<!-- 10 -->

A sixth risk is **salary** **and** **working-condition** **non-compliance.** This risk is often described too loosely in immigration content. The better view is that salary/conditions issues sit partly in labour-market admission and partly in posted-worker enforcement. For ordinary FNIA admission, article 22 FNIA requires the salary and employment conditions customary for the place, profession, and sector. For posted workers, article 2 PWA imposes Swiss minimum conditions in the protected domains, and article 9 PWA provides the sanctions framework. Some cantonal materials, including Zurich and Ticino, mnake this operational by insisting on salary-condition declarations or by warning that Swiss collective or standard terms, not home-state terms, govern the Swiss posting. A notificationcan therefore be formally valid while the assignment still fails labour-market control or produces a posted-worker sanction. 51

A seventh risk concerns **responsibility** **allocation.** Swiss law does not leave compliance solely to the foreign employer. The Swiss employer must check work entitlement before work begins, and recipients of cross-border services carry an equivalent duty in relation to the foreign service provider. SEM also recommends checking in broader outsourced-work situations to avoid problems during inspections. For subcontracted posted-worker chains, the due-diligence materials under article 8b EntsV show the kind of records a Swiss contractor mnay seek from a subcontractor to verify compliance with minimum Swiss salary and working conditions. This means that responsibility can attach to the foreign employer, the Swiss host or end-user, and the Swiss principal/contracting undertaking in different ways. 52

Finally, on **future** **applications** **and** **renewals,** the most explicit official basis collected is FNIA article 122: repeated employer violations can lead the competent authority to refuse or only partially authorise the employer's future applications for foreign workers. Beyond that, SECO's public sanctionns list under the PWA and public service-ban decisions plainly create reputational and practical adverse history, but the materials gathered do not justify saying that every posted-worker sanction automatically produces a later immigration refusal. The safer formulation is that official sources expressly support future-admission consequences for repeated FNIA employer misconduct, and separately describe sanction histories and public lists under the PWA which may be relevant to later assessments wvithout operating as an automatic statutory refusal ground. 53

## Drafting cautions

This section covers item H.

Content on this topic should avoid saying that Switzerland has a single codified list of "refusal grounds" for short-term assignments. The materials are broader and more structural. Many problems arise because the activity is legally classified as gainful work, because the chosen route is unavailable, because timing rules were not met, or because the factual record does not support the claimed route. That is different from a narrow list of discretionary refusal grounds. 54

<!-- 11 -->

Do not use "business visit" as a blanket label for short Swiss presence. The official SEM annex contains concrete counter-examples showing that project work, maintenance, reorganisation work, productive training, and recurring personnel-management tasks are gainful activity even where the stay is short. Content should therefore describe business-visitor treatment as **limited** **and** **fact-sensitive,** not as a broad carve-out. 12

Do **not** imply that late or deficient notification can always be corrected retrospectively. The official guidance supports pre-start changes in some circumstances, and OASA article 14(2) contains a narrow continuation rule for a specific type of FNIA cross-border activity. Neither source supports a general "regularise later" approach once work has started unlawfully or the wrong route has been used. 55

**Do** **not** collapse immigration authorisation risk into posted-worker or employment-law enforcement.A file may be notification-eligible yet still breach minimum Swiss salary or working conditions; equally, a labour-law compliant structure may still fail because the wrong immigration route was used or because the arrangement looks like unlawful labour leasing. Content should distinguish at least four separate axes**: immigration permission, posted-worker salary/conditions, staff-leasing / employment-service rules, and tax/social-security/regulated-profession issues.** The official sources support the first three directly; separate tax and social-security issues are flagged only incidentally in the materials gathered. 56

Do **not** overstate "refusal because evidence is weak" as though it were a named statutory ground. The stronger and more accurate statement is that official materials require sufficient documents to prove that the activity is genuinely non-gainfulI where that is claimed, or to support the claimed project, roole, route, duration, and remuneration where a permit or posting case is pursued. Weak evidence is therefore best treated as a **serious evidential and administrative refusal risk** rather than a codified label. 32

Do not say that any Swiss client using a foreign contractor becomes the legal employer. The official materials support a **factual employer /** de **facto** **employer** analysis and make labour-leasing scenarios particularly sensitive, but ordinary service contracts remain legally distinct from staffing if they are genuine deliverable-based contracts. Content should therefore describe "local hire / labour leasing / staff augmentation" as a **risk** **factor triggered** **by** **the** **facts,** not an automatic outcome of every client-site assignment. 57

Where article-level extracts below come from **Refworld** **or** **Lawbrary** reproductions rather than directly parsed Fedlex text, that should be noted internally. In this research environment, some Fedlex pages were not reliably machine-readable, but the non-official mirrors used here reproduce Swiss instruments and in several places link back to Fedlex references. For internal drafting, the underlying legal authority remains the Swiss enactment itself. 58
