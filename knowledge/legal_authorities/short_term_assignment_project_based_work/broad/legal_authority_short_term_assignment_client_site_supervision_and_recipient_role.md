# Swiss legal authority pack on client-site work, supervision and recipient role in short-term assignment and project-based work

## Executive legal summary

Swiss immigration and labour-market classification is substance-led, not label-led. The key legal divide is between a genuine cross-border service or posting, in which the foreign employer remains the real employer and delivers a defined service or work result in Switzerland, and an arrangement in which the Swiss recipient effectively receives labour under its own day-to-day control. The latter can move the case towards local hire, personnel supply or prohibited foreign labour leasing, depending on the facts. The core legal tools are the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, the Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment, thePosted Workers Act, the Employment Service and Hiring of Services Act, the Employment Service Ordinance, and the current SEM and SECO directives that explain how these texts are applied in practice. 1

For immigration purposes, the decisive questions are usually: who holds the effective right to instruct and control the worker; who bears responsibility for the work result; whether the worker is integrated into the Swiss recipient's organisation in personal, organisational and temporal terms; whether the engagement is tied to a specific documented project or instead looks like staff augmentation; and whether the worker remains under the foreign employment contract or is in reality taking up a Swiss role. SEM's directives expressly state that posted employees continue to work under the foreign employer's authority and that the foreign employer remains responsible for the work result,whereas labour leasing exists where the Swiss client holds the effective instruction right and the supplier no longer controls the result. SECO's AVG guidance adds further indicators, including integration into the host's organisation, use of the host's tools and material, hourly charging rather than a result-based price, and the host bearing the risk of defective performance. 2

The Swiss recipient does, however, have a real legal role even in a genuine project model. SEM states that service-provider permit applications are usually filed by the Swiss ordering party in the canton of assignment and that the permit is also issued to that Swiss ordering party or site operator. That procedural role does not make the Swiss recipient the employer by itself, but itdoes mean Swiss authorities will expect the Swiss side to explain the project, the site, the host function and the interface with the foreign contractor. The Swiss recipient therefore becomes a central evidential actor even where the foreign company remains the true employer and service provider. 3

The strongest indicators of a genuine project posting are a specific documented project; a service or works contract with defined scope, timing and outputs; a project plan showing why the worker is needed in Switzerland; continuity of the foreign employment contract; an assignment confirmation showing task, function, place, duration, salary and expense arrangements; and facts showing that the foreign employer, not the Swiss client, manages the worker and stands behind the result. SEM's project annex is especially important here: it insists that permits are granted for a specific documented project, that project changes require fresh approval, that posted workers remain subject to the foreign employment contract, and that project staff must be genuinely specialist rather than performing ordinary general tasks that compete with local labour. 4

The highest-risk indicators are the converse: reporting to a Swiss line manager for daily work allocation; integration into Swiss shifts, teams and management structures; ordinary role fulfilment rather than defined deliverables; open-ended or repeatedly extended deployment; time-and-materials charging without a real service result; use of Swiss premises, systems and equipment as part of ordinary operations without a clear project-based explanation; and several workers being supplied in a way that looks like headcount supplementation. None of those factors is always decisive in isolation, but together they map closely onto the criteria that SEM and SECO use to identify labour leasing or employer-like control by the Swiss host. 5

A separate but critical rule is that foreign labour leasing into Switzerland is generally prohibited. AVG expressly states that labour leasing from abroad into Switzerland is not permitted. SEM's directives add that third-country nationals may in principle not enter Switzerland for personnel leasing, save for narrow special-project exceptions with additional proof. SECO's posting guidance and FAQ also make clear that the posted-worker regime does not legalise foreign labour leasing, and SEM's EU/EFTA service-provider factsheet states that staff leasing services by EU/EFTA legal entities are not liberalised. This means that the 90-day EU/EFTA notification route cannot be used to sanitise a staff-supply model that is in substance labour leasing. 6

Official cantonal material is useful but comparatively thin on fine-grained supervision analysis. Zurich provides the most concrete checklist for posted employees deployed into a third-party company and requires a specific justification, project description,service or works contract, and assignment documentation identifying the home company, host company, function, tasks, place, duration, salary, allowances, expenses, insurance and social charges. Geneva similarly requires a signed service contract, signed employment contract and a motivation letter explaining duration, place and the reasons why a third-country service provider is needed. Basel-Stadt's official pages reinforce the same structure and point applicants to example assignment confirmations. 7

## Structural overview of client-site and supervision analysis

In practice, Swiss immigration and labour-market analysis tends to run through the following sequence.

1. Is **the** **activity** **"gainful employment"** at all? FNIA defines gainful employment very broadly, and ASEO states that dependent work includes any activityfor an employer in Switzerland or abroad, regardless of where pay is made and even if the work is only temporary or part-time. The competent cantonal labour-market authority decides doubtful cases and must refer uncertain cases to SEM. 8

**2. Is the case framed as a time-limited cross-border service or posting?** ASEO defines a cross-border service as a time-limited service rendered in Switzerland under a contractual relationship by a person or company based abroad. EntsG defines a posting as work carried out in Switzerland for a limited period on the foreign employer's account and under its direction for a Swiss service recipient or within a group company. 9

<!-- 2 -->

3. **Who** is **the real operational controller?** SEM and SECO both treat the locus of instruction and control as central. In a genuine posting, the foreign employer retains authority and responsibility for the work result, while the Swiss site exercises only limited site-owner powers such as access and safety. In labour leasing, the host exercises the essential instruction and control power over the worker. 10

**4.How integrated is the worker into Swiss operations?** AVV and SECO identify integration into the host in personal, organisational and temporal terms, use of host tools and material, and host assumption of the risk of defective performance as markers of labour leasing rather than pure service delivery. 11

**5.Is the engagement tied to a documented project and deliverable, or is it effectively staff** **augmentation?** SEM's project guidance expects a specific project, a project description, an implementation plan and project-specific know-how. It warns against project staff performing general IT tasks that compete with local labour. That is the clearest federal administrative signal against body-shopping models dressed up as "projects". 12

**6. What evidential package supports the asserted structure?** Federal and cantonal materials repeatedly ask for the service contract or works contract, project description, reasoned justification, implementation plan, foreign employment contract continuity, and an assignment confirmation or secondment agreement containing detailed information on role, location, duration, pay and expenses. Zurich and Geneva show that Swiss authorities expect both contractual and practical evidence, not just labels.13

7. **Does the model drift into local hire or employer substitution?** SEM's project annex states that if there is a new direct fixed-term hire by the Swiss branch, the case is treated as a change of purpose requiring a new admission procedure. That is the clearest official bridge between a genuine posting and a Swiss employment relationship. 14

### Primary legal texts and official guidance

**Primary** **legal texts.** FNIA supplies the immigration framework: Art. 11 makes gainful employment permit-based and places permit applications in salaried cases with the employer; Arts. 18 to 24 set the labour-market conditions for salaried and self-employed admission, including overall economic interest, precedence, local wages and conditions, personal qualifications and accommodation. ASEO then adds the operational definitions and filing rules that matter most for client-site cases: dependent work is broad; cross-border service must be time-limited; the cantonal labour-market authority decides doubtful classification questions; certain short cross-border assignments are exempt only up to specific limits; and for cross-border services the filing package must include both the service contract and the assignment confirmation. EntsG supplies the posted-worker concept by requiring work on the foreign employer's account and under its direction. AVG and AVV then provide the decisive labour-leasing boundary: foreign labour leasing into Switzerland is prohibited, and labour leasing exists where essential instruction powers are ceded to the Swiss host, especially where the worker is integrated into the host's organisation and uses the host's tools and material. 31

<!-- 6 -->

**Official** **federal** **guidance.** The most useful immigration authority is SEM chapter 4 of the FNIA directives, especially section 4.7.1 on project staff, sections 4.8.2.10 and 4.8.2.11 on distinguishing postings from labour leasing and on application handling, and the SEM annex on "Projektmitarbeitende / Dienstleistungserbringer". Together, these materials define a project, emphasise project-specific know-how, tie permission to a documented project and project plan, require foreign employment continuity in genuine postings, and list the evidence expected for service-provider cases. The SEM assignment-confirmation template is also important because it shows, in a structured official form, which details of the posting theauthorities expect to see. 32

SECO's AVG guidance is the key authority on the operational distinction between performance of a service contract and labour leasing. It gives the most granular official analysis of instruction rights, integration, host equipment, charging by hours and risk allocation, and it expressly contrasts posted workers with leased workers. SECO's posting pages and FAQ then reinforce that the posted-worker regime is for the employer's own employees, not for borrowed workers, and that labour leasing from abroad remains impermissible. SEM's public labour-market summary page is also useful because it updates the mainstream federal explanation of overall economic interest, precedence, wages and qualifications, even though it is not written specifically for client-site cases. 33

For EU/EFTA short-term work, the current notification page confirms the 90-day framework, while the SEM EU/EFTA factsheet records that staff leasing services are not liberalised. Those two sources matter whenever a client-site service model is presented under the 90-day route rather than under ordinary third-country admission rules. 30

## Cantonal practice notes

The clearest official cantonal evidence on host-site postings was located in Zurich. Zurich's January 2024checklist for "Entsandte/r, unselbständige/r Dienstleistungserbringer/in mit Einsatz in Drittfirma" expressly treats the Swiss third party as the "Einsatzbetrieb" or ordering party and requires: a specific justification; a project description; the Swiss client-foreign contractor service or works contract; and either the SEM assignment confirmation or an assignment contract. Zurich also insists that the posting documents identify both the home company and the host company, the worker's function and task set during the posting, the place of work, the duration, home salary, posting allowance, expense cover, insurance and social contributions, and treatment of holidays and working time. That is unusually concrete cantonal evidence of what Swiss authorities consider probative in client-site service models. 23

Zurich's broader explanatory note on third-country employment takes the same approach. It repeats the need for the assignment confirmation or assignment contract, requires the same core content, and distinguishes internal and external postings by reference to the home company and host company.It also states, as a matter of Zurich practice, that precedence is not applied to temporary cross-border service providers or posted workers because they are only temporarily in Switzerland and then return home. That point should be treated as canton-specific practice, not as a freestanding federal rule for all service-provider cases. 34

<!-- 7 -->

Geneva's current page for non-EU/EFTA companies and independents posting staff or offering services is less detailed on supervision, but still useful. It requires the employer to filethe application in posted-worker cases, states that the worker cannot begin before authorisation is granted, and lists the core dossier items: a completed form, colour ID copy, the signed service contract, the signed employment contract, and a motivation letter stating the duration, place of performance and the reasons that make recourse to the third-country service provider necessary. Geneva also frames the admission criteria in labour-market terms: economic interest, protection of workers already integrated into the internal labour market, avoidance of wage and social dumping, and limitation to managers, specialists and qualified workers. 35

Basel-Stadt's current posting pages do not add much extra analysis on supervision, but they do confirm the same federal structure, distinguish the main time bands, and make official example assignment confirmations available for both third-country and EU/EFTA postings. They are therefore useful corroborative evidence that cantonal practice expects structured assignment documentation and treats the posting route as a legally distinct route from ordinary Swiss hiring. 36

Taken together, the cantonal material located for this pack is helpful on documents and procedural handling, but thin on deeper discussion of reporting lines and client-side operational control. On those points, the federal SEM and SECO guidance still carries most of the practical weight. 37

## Practical legal analysis

The Swiss recipient, client, host or project owner is legally important, but not automatically the employer. The authorities are concerned less with the commercial title of the Swiss entity than with its functional role in the arrangement. In a genuine service-delivery model, the Swiss recipient may define the business need, contract for a result, provide site access, impose site safety rules, coordinate interfaces, and accept the deliverable. SEM expressly recognises thatthe permit will often be applied for by, and issued to, the Swiss orderingparty. But those procedural and operational interfaces do not displace the foreign employer if the foreign employer still directs the worker and remains answerable for the result. 38

**Supervision** **and** **reporting** **lines.** Swiss law does not contain a stand-alone statutory test framed in terms of "reporting lines". The operative test is who holds the effective Weisungsrecht and who bears responsibility for the work product. That means a reporting line to a Swiss client manager for daily task allocation, prioritisation, leave approval, houurs, conduct management or performance appraisal is legally risky, because those facts line up with the host-side instruction and control criteria used by SEM, SECO and AVV. Conversely, a reporting line to a foreign home-company manager, combined with Swiss-side coordination limited to access, safety, interface management and acceptance, is much more consistent with a posted-service model. The second sentence is an inference from the official criteria, but it is a close one. 2

**Integration into** **Swiss** **operations.** Integration is a major practical risk marker. AWV and SECO both treat integration into the host in personal, organisational and temporal terms as a sign of labour leasing; SECO adds that use of host tools, material and devices, and work mainly at the host's premises and within the host's time structure, point in the same direction. This does not mean that any use of Swiss systems or premises is fatal. Many real projects necessarily require access to the Swiss client's environment. The legal point is narrower: if Swiss systems, premises and routines are used in a way that shows the worker has become part of the Swiss organisation, rather than merely present to carry out a defined external project, the host-control risk increases materially. That is an analytical inference from the express legal indicators and should be presented conservatively. 39

<!-- 8 -->

**Deliverables** **versus** **staff** **augmentation.** Swiss materials do not use the commercial term "staff augmentation", but the legal distinction is clear. A genuine service model is tied to a specific documented project, a project plan, defined tasks and outputs, and specialist know-how. A staff-augmentation model looks like the supply of labour into the Swiss business, especially where the work is billed by hours, the role is interchangeable, the worker fills a capacity gap in the Swiss team, and the host bears the operational risk. SEM's project annex is unusually explicit in the IT context: project staff must be genuine specialists with specific project know-how, and the authorities must prevent them from taking on general IT tasks that compete with local and European workers. That language should not be mechanically extrapolated to every industry, but it is an important official warning against presenting ordinary operational headcount as "project work". 40

**Local** hire **versus** **cross-border** **service.** The cleanest official dividing line is continuity of the foreign employment contract and the absence of a new direct Swiss employment relationship. SEM states that in all poosting cases the workers remain subject to the foreign employment contract; if there is a new fixed-term direct hire by the Swiss branch, Swiss authorities should treat that as a change of stay purpose requiring a new admission procedure. In practical file assessment, the risk of local-hire characterisation rises where the Swiss entity recruits the individual into an ordinary role, places them on Swiss reporting and HR structures, or uses the person to fill a job that could naturally be held by a Swiss employee. Those examples are analytical inferences, but they follow closely from the federal guidance and from the local-hire rules summarised on SEM'slabour-market basis page. 41

**Foreign employer control and result responsibility.** The core evidential question is not whether the foreign employer still pays salary, although that matters; it is whether the foreign employer still organises, instructs and stands behind performance. The most useful evidence therefore goes beyond the employment contract itself. Authorities are likely to attach weight to a documented service or works contract with deliverables, milestones and acceptance structure; a project description and implementation plan; an assignment letter or SEM assignment confirmation identifying the foreign employer, host, function, tasks, place and duration; and a liability structure showing that the foreign contractor, not the Swiss client, remains responsible for re-performance or defects. This is partly an inference from the federal and cantonal document lists and partly a direct application of SEM's and SECO's control-and-result tests. 42

**Evidence** **package.** For internal content purposes, the safest evidential bundle for a genuine client-site project model is usually the following: a signed service or works agreement between the Swiss recipient and foreign provider; a project description and implementation plan showing what is being delivered and why Swiss-site presence is required; the foreign employment contract; the assignment confirmation or assignment letter; CVs and qualification documents proving specialist know-how; evidence of who manages the worker and who accepts the output; remuneration and expense evidence satisfying Swiss local standards; and a short explanatory note, if needed, dealing expressly with any potentially adverse facts such as use of Swiss systems, timesheets or onsite supervision. Zurich's checklist and Geneva's document list strongly support that approach. 43

**Several workers** **supplied.** Swiss official texts do not create a numerical threshold at which a service provider case automatically becomes labour leasing. Even so, the legal risk rises if several workers are supplied to the same Swiss recipient in a pattern that looks like hnosted capacity rather than a defined outsourced deliverable. The reason is structural: multiple workers deployed under host-side direction, on host tools, within host rosters, and charged by time, look more like labour supply. SEM's specific warning about project staff taking on general tasks rather than specialist project work increases the importance of explaining team composition, division of responsibility and deliverable ownership whenever more than one worker is posted. 44

<!-- 9 -->

**Permit duration** **and** **project** drift. SEM's directives say that for services, short-stay permits should be used in principle even for longer projects, while residence permits are reserved for rarer exceptions. The necessary period of presence must follow the project plan, and project changes are treated seriously: even moving to another project at the same host can amount to a fresh permission issue. That means future client content should avoid language suggesting free redeployment "as needed" across multiple Swiss workstreams or locations. 45

## Drafting cautions

Future client-site pproject content should be drafted with the Swiss classification tests in mind.

·Do not state or imply thatthe Swiss client will "supervise", "manage", "direct", "allocate daily tasks to", "approve time off for", or "evaluate" the worker in the ordinary managerial sense if the intended structure is a genuine posting or project service. That language maps too closely onto the host-side instruction criteria used for labour leasing analysis. 46

· Do not rely on labels such as "service agreement", "statement of work", "host company" or "contractor" as if they were dispositive. Swiss authorities look at the totality of circumstances, and the competent labour-market authority decides doubtful classification issues. 28

·Avoid body-shopping language such as "resource augmentation", "backfill", "fills a Swiss role", "embedded in the client team", "extension subject to business needs", or "hours as required". If time-based charging cannot be avoided commercially, the file should still explain the defined project scope, the foreign employer's control and the supplier's responsibility for the result. Hour-based charging is an express labour-leasing indicator in SECO guidance. 19

· If use of Swiss systems, equipment or premises is unavoidable, explain why access is necessary for the project and how managerial control remains with the foreign employer. The official criteria treat deep integration and use of host tools as risk factors, but they do not say that every site-access or system-access case is automatically labour leasing. 39

·Frame the Swiss recipient's role carefully: it may be the customer, project owner, site operator, procedural applicant or permit addressee, but in a genuine service model it should not be described as the substantive employer. Where a host letter is used, it should confirm site, project, interface and access arrangements, not recreate a Swiss employment relationship. 47

·Tie the engagement to a specific documented project and, where possible, to deliverables, milestones, acceptance events and specialist know-how. SEM's project annex is especially unhelpful to cases that look like ordinary operational labour dressed up as a "project", particularly in IT. 48

<!-- 10 -->

·Where there is any realistic possibility of conversion into a Swiss direct hire, say so separately and treat it as a different legal route. SEM expressly treats Swiss direct hiring by the Swiss branch as a change of purpose that requires a new admission analysis. 14

## Case law
