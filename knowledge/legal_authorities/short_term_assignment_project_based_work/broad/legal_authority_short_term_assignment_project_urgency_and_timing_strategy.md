# Swiss legal authority pack on project urgency and timing in short-term assignments and project-based work

Jurisdiction: Switzerland. Research use: internal immigration-law authority pack for short-term assignments and project-based work. The analysis below is grounded primarily in SEM, SECO and cantonal official materials current to 24 May 2026. Timing points are often found in administrative guidance and canton practice pages rather than in statute alone; where that is so, it is stated expressly. 1

## Executive legal summary

Swiss law treats timing as a compliance issue, not as a commercial convenience issue. The decisive questions are: which route applies; what must happen before work starts; and whether those pre-start conditions have actually been met. In notification cases, work may begin only once a valid notification has been made and the applicable lead time has run, unless a narrowly defined emergency exception applies. In permit cases, work may not begin merely because an application has been filed; the relevant authorities must have authorised employment, and where entry formalities apply,visa/entry-authorisation / residence steps must also be in place. Official sources do **not** support any general proposition that client pressure, project urgency, or commercial loss can override filing order or authorisation requirements. 2

The key distinction is between short-term **Swiss** **employment** and short-term **cross-border** **service** **provision.** For EU/EFTA nationals taking up employment in Switzerland for up to three months in a calendar year, the notification must be filed no later than theday before work starts. By contrast, posted workers and self-employed service providers using the notification procedure must, as a rule, be notified at least eight calendar days before work begins in Switzerland. If the service will exceed 90working days, a permit is required; SEM's English notification page expressly states that there is no legal entitlement to that permit. 3

Commercial urgency matters mainly as **evidence,** not as an override. Official project-assignment guidance accepts that Swiss businesses may need foreign specialists for documented projects, especially where specific project know-how cannot be sourced locally; but the same guidance also stresses that admissions must be confined to genuinely specialised workers and must not permit foreign assignees to compete with local or European labour by performing general tasks. In other words, a pressing project can help explain why this project exists and why this specific specialist is needed, but it does not itself create legal entitlement to immediate deployment. 4

Premature deployment creates two distinct risks. First, the worker may be working unlawfully. Second, the employer and the Swiss recipient / host may expose themselves to sanctions. SEM's VFP guidance is especially clear for late notifications: the system may still issue confirmation of receipt, but that confirmation is **not** approval of non-compoliance with the lead time; the filer is told to contact the canton immediately, postpone the assignment, and accept that sanctions may follow. In permit cases, official materials require the employer and Swiss recipient to verify work authorisation before allowing work to start, and SEM's AIG guidance points to sanctions uunder the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act, the Federal Act on Illegal Employment, and the Posted Workers Act. 5

The practical consequence for internal content is straightforward. Timing-strategy material must never imply that urgency "gets around" notification or permit sequencing. The legally safe message is that urgency may justify **early filing, fuller evidence, and careful route selection;** **it** does not justify work before a valid notification or before the relevant permit / entry /arrival steps are complete. 6

## Structural overview of urgency and timing analysis

Swiss urgency analysis should be approached in four layers. The first layer is **route** **identification**: is this a Swiss employment relationship, a cross-border posting / service, or a permit-based third-country assignment? Official materials draw those categories sharply, and the timing rules differ accordingly. For example, Zurich distinguishes "Stellenantritt Schweiz" from "Entsendung", while SEM and SECO distinguish Swiss employment, notification-based service provision, and permit-based service provision over the relevant thresholds.7

The second layer is the **pre-start** **legal** act. In some cases that act is a notification; in others it is a permit decision. For notification-based postings and self-employed services, the usual rule is prior notification at least eight days before work. For EU/EFTA short-termn employment in Switzerland, the notification deadline is later: no later than the day before work starts. For permit cases, the pre-start act is actual authorisation, not mere filing. SEM's AIG guidance says the start of work may take place only when the relevant immigration approval exists; Geneva's official pages make the same point in French, with route-specific nuance depending on whether the individual already holds an entry authorisation or assurance of residence authorisation. 8

The third layer is **sequencing** **after** **filing.** Zurich sets out a classic third-country sequence: employer filing with the labour-market authority; cantonal labour-market pre-decision; SEM consent where required; then migration-authority issuance of the residence / work permission. Geneva similarly states that third-country applications must be filed well before entry or mission start, and that, in most cases, work cannot begin until authorisation has been granted. This is why "we have filed already" is not a legally meaningful start-date defence. 9

The fourth layer is **urgency** **evidence.** Urgency may still matter, but only within the legal framework. Official materials show three legitimate uses: explaining a genuine emergency for the notification exception; proving why a documented project needs a specific foreign specialist; and explaining the actual necessary duration of presence in Switzerland, including in truly exceptional delay scenarios. Those uses support the file factually; none displace the legal threshold. 1⁰

A final structural point is that public materials on **processing** **times** are mostly administrative,not statutory. Geneva publishes a 6-8 week lead-time expectation for certain third-country work cases, and Lucerne publishes a rough "up to about six weeks" processing time for work-permit categories. These are useful operational indicators, but they are not legal guarantees and should not be presented as binding deadlines on the authorities.11

### Primary legal texts and official guidance

**Primary legal texts.** The core federal instruments for this topic are the Foreign Nationals and Integration Act AIG (SR 142.20), the Ordinance on Admission, Stay and Employment **VZAE** (SR 142.201), the Ordinance on Free Movement of Persons VFP (SR 142.203), the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons **AFMP,** the Posted Workers Act **EntsG** (SR 823.20), and the Posted Workers Ordinance **EntsV** (SR 823.201). Official canton pages in Basel-Stadt and SEM legal-basis pages identify these instruments as the governing legal framework. 23

The provisions doing the real timing work, as shown by current SEM and SECO guidance, are these. First, **AIG** **article** 11 and related implementing provisions supply the general principle that gainful employment is regulated and normally requires prior immigration compliance; SEM's AIG chapter 4repeats that all gainful activity is in principle permit-relevant and distinguishes activity with and without "Stellenantritt" in Switzerland. Secondly, **VZAE** **articles** **14** **and** **17,** as cited in SEM's guidance, govern permit-required work without "Stellennantritt", including the rule that the decision on work authorisation must be awaited abroad. Thirdly, **VFP** **article** **9** and the posted-worker rules underpin the eight-day prior-notification regime used by posted workers and self-employed service providers. Finally, **AIG** **article** **91** and the sanction provisions in **AIG, BGSA** and **EntsG** anchor employer/recipient responsibility and enforcement. 24

**Official guidance** **and** **administrative** **materials.** Because the statutes and ordinances establish the framework but do not themselves answer every practical timing question, the most useful operational authorities are the current **SEM** **online** **notification** **pages,** the **SEM** **VFP** **directives** for the notification route, the **SEM AIG chapter 4 directives** for permit-based work, the **SEM annex on project workers** **and service providers,** the **SECO guidance on foreign-law reporting and permit duties,** and selected **cantonal practice** **materials.** Those documents spell out the lead times, the late-notification consequences, the narrow scope of emergency exceptions, the documentary expectations for project files, and the labour-market sequence in third-country cases. 25

One important methodological caution follows from the sources. Published **processing** **times** are usually administrative estimates or canton-level service information, not rights conferred by federal law. By contrast, the **pre-start** **prohibition** is treated by official materials as a hard legal rule. Internal content should therefore give more weight to "may not start before..." than to "processing usually takes...". 26

### Cantonal practice notes

**Zurich** is particularly useful on permit sequencing and project evidence. The canton states that third-country nationals require a labour-market pre-decision from the Office for Economy, after which the positive pre-decision goes to SEM, and only then does the migration office examine and issue the residence / work permission from the immigration-law side. Zurich also publishes document checklists for project postings: specific application reasoning, project description, the Swiss contract / works contract, the assignee's CV and diplomas, and details of start date and duration. For local hires it also expects labour-market search evidence; for temporary service providers it says local priority is waived, but economic interest, salary and qqualifications still matter. 27

**Geneva** is the clearest canton on timing lead times and start-date consequences. For third-country service providers and postings, it requires the complete file to be sent at least **6-8** **weeks** before mission start and warns that SEM approval may extend that timetable. It also states that work is allowed after arrival declaration to the OCPM only for persons already holding an entry authorisation with employment or an assurance of residence authorisation; in all other cases, work cannot begin before authorisation is granted. Geneva's file requirements are also substantive: signed service contract, signed employment contract, and a motivation letter showing duration, place of performance, and the reasons why a third-country service provider is needed. For third-country local hires, Geneva again uses **a** **6-8** **week** pre-entry filing expectation, insists on prior Swiss / EU recruitment attempts, and states that employing a foreignnational without a work permit is a criminal offence. 28

**Basel-Stadt** is especially useful for time-sensitive notification practice. It requires eight days' advance notice for EUJ/EFTA company postings, but only day-before notice for Swviss short-term employment. It says each site must be notified separately; changes must be requested by e-mail before effective work start and normal lead times still apply. For the same works on the same project at the same address,if continued within three months, Basel-Stadt allows notification only one day before the next deployment. It also describes emergency filing in practical terms: at imnmediate risk, a short-notice filing is possible, but the filer must expressly identify the case as an emergency and submit confirmation and explanation of the Swiss company's damage. 29

**Lucerne** publishes a useful but modest processing benchmark: work-permit applications are processed in about up **to** **six** **weeks** for both EU/EFTA and non-EU/EFTA categories on the migration-office forms page. That is an administrative expectation, not a statutory deadline, but it is a helpful reference when drafting conservative internal lead-time assumptions. 30

Taken together, the cantonal materials reviewed here support a conservative internal conclusion: cantons may publish practical accommodations for same-project follow-on notifications or genuine emergencies, but the public-facing materials reviewed do not advertise a general "urgent commercial project" mechanism that permits work before the ordinary legal trigger. 31

### Practical legal analysis

The first legal question in any short-term assignment is **whether** **the** **case** is **notification-capable** **at** all. If the case falls within the EU/EFTA short-term regime, notification may replace a permit. But notification is not a softer form of authorisation in the sense of optional timing; it is the required legal precondition for that route. SEM and SECO both present it as a mandatory compliance step before work begins, and SEM's VFP directives state that the filing must in every case occur before the start of gainful activity. 32

Within notification cases, filing **sequence** **depends** **on** **the sub-route.** If the worker is an EU/EFTA national taking up short employment with a Swiss employer for up to three months, the filing may be made as late as the day before the first working day. If instead the case is a posting or a self-employed service assignment, the filing must usually be made eight calendar days before the work starts. That is why internal content should never speak generically of "the notification route" without saying whether it means **short Swiss employment or cross-border service provision.** Those two routes use different lead times and different start triggers. 3

**The** **emergency** **/ repair** / **maintenance** category is real, but narrow. Official materials allow start before expiry of the eight-day period only in emergencies such as repairs, accidents, natural disasters or other unforeseeable events, and even then work may begin no earlier than the **day** of **notification.** SEM's VFP directives require cumulative conditions: the work must address a suddenly occurring damage event, it must prevent further damage, and it must be carried out immediately, generally within three calendar days. The examples given are machinery essential to business continuity, public or private energy/heat/water / transport disruption, and indispensable urgent measures for life, health or the environment; SEM adds that some IT services may qualify if those conditions are met. None of this resembles ordinary client urgency, budget pressure or a late project timeline. 15

**For permit** **cases,** the filing sequence is stricter and more layered. Zurich's official sequence is employer filing to the cantonal labour-market authority, cantonal labour-market pre-decision, SEM consent where required, then migration-authority issuance. Geneva likewise insists that the employer files first and that work generally cannot start before authorisation. SEM guidance adds that for entry to take up work in Switzerland, a visa or an assurance oof residence authorisation must exist, and that mere employer filing does not suffice. Put differently, a permit case has at least five separate milestones that internal content should keep apart: application; labour-market approval; federal approval where needed; immigration / residence authorisation; and the final lawful work-start trigger after entry and local arrival formalities. 33

This is where **commercial urgency** is **most** **often** **overstated** in practice. The official authorities accept that a project may be commercially important and time-sensitive, but they still require the ordinary legal thresholdds. Zurich explicitly continues to require economic interest, salary compliance and qualifications for third-country service providers, even though it waives local priority in temporary service cases. SEM's project-workers annex goes further: admissions are to be limnited to specialists whose project-specific know-how is not available locally, and authorities must prevent assignments that effectively place general tasks into competition with local and European labour. For local hires, Geneva requires actual proof of prior Swiss / EU recruitment efforts. Therefore, "the client needs someone now" is not enough; the file must instead show **why this project exists, why this exact personis needed, why the** **duration requested is the real duration, and wwhy the route chosen is legally available.** 34

The documentary expectations confirm that point. Zurich asks for a project description, the Swiss contract / work contract, specific application reasoning about the business, project and assignee, CV and diplomas, and in SEM's own checklist the **implementation** **plan** appears as a specific item for project deployments. Geneva asks for a signed service contract, signed employment contract, CV, diplomas and a letter explaining the necessity, duration and location of the mission. These are not mere formalities; they are the official channels through which timing need and business need are evidenced. But evidence is not the same thing as entitlement: a convincing project narrative can support a compliant file, not legalise a premature start. 35

<!-- 8 -->

The **risk** **of** **retrospective** **regularisation** should be treated with particular caution. For the notification route, SEM is explicit: a notification filed late, or even after work has already begun, may still generate a receipt confirmation, but that confirmation does not validate the breach and the filer is told to postpone the assignment and contact the canton, on pain of sanctions. For permit cases, I did not locate any official published material saying that a premature start can safely be regularised afterwards; instead, the materials repeatedly say the opposite - work must wait until authorisation exists, and unauthorised employment can trigger administrative or criminal consequences. The conservative internal position should therefore be that retrospective regularisation is **not** a **safe** **strategy** **and** **should** **not** be **assumed** **to** **exist,** except where a competent authority expressly says so in the individual file. That is partly a legal conclusion and partly a cautious inference from the published official material. 36

There is also a **recipient-side** **risk.** SEM's AIG guidance says the employer has the duty to ensure that foreign workers hold the required permissions, and that persons who receive cross-border services must also verify that the foreign persons actually delivering the work are authorised to do so in Switzerland. SEM describes a broad "factual employer" concept, and refers to Federal Supreme Court case law for that proposition. The same guidance also notes that foreign staff leasing into Switzerland is prohibited, and SEM's notification page repeats that prohibition for the AFMP context. A Swiss client that lets a worker start because "the vendor is handling immigration" therefore has no safe harbour in the public guidance reviewed. 37

Finally, timing can become more complex when the project has **multiple** **sites, multiple** **cantons,** or **post-installation** **follow-on** **work.** SEM's VFP directives say each assignment and site must in principle be notified separately, and if the place of work changes, a new eight-day period runs. Basel-Stadt then adds a useful practice detail: for the same works on the same project at the same address,continued within three months, one-day prior reporting may suffice. That is helpful for true follow-on maintenance, but it is stil a procedural rule within the notification architecture; it is not a general licence to compress initial filing lead time for a new project. 20

### Drafting cautions

·Do not write that project urgency, customer pressure, contractual penalties or go-live risk can override notification lead times or permit approval requirements. The official sources support only narrow emergency exceptions and otherwise insist on compliance before work starts. 38

·Do **not** collapse "notification filed", "notification compliant", "notification acknowledged" and "notification approved" into one concept. SEM expressly says a notification receipt is not approval of deviations from legal deadlines. 14

·Do not collapse "permit application", "cantonal labour-market pre-decision", "SEM approval", "visa / entry authorisation", "arrival registration / announcement", and "lawful first working day" into one concept. Zurich, Geneva and SEM all treat these as distinct steps. 33

<!-- 9 -->

·Do not present business-need evidence as if it creates an automatic approval right. Official materials use project need, project-specific know-how and actual duration to assess the file;they do not make those factors a substitute for the legal criteria. 39

·Do not suggest that late filing can usually be corrected after deployment. The published materials support the opposite approach: stop, contact the canton, and assume sanctions risk if work began prematurely. 40

·Do not ignore host-company / recipient obligations. Official guidance places verification duties not only on the signatory employer, but also on Swiss businesses taking the benefit of cross-border services, and it separately prohibits foreign staff leasing into Switzerland. 37

·When writing about "urgent repairs" or "maintenance", define them narrowly and against the official criteria: sudden damage, imnmediate action, no later than about three calendar days, and only from the day of notification. Avoid language that would let ordinary project acceleration masquerade as an emergency. 20

·When discussing timing windows, label them as **published** **administrative** **expectations** unless a statuteor ordinance sets the deadline. Geneva's 6-8 week guidance and Lucerne's ca six-week guidance are useful, but they are not statutory guarantees. 11
