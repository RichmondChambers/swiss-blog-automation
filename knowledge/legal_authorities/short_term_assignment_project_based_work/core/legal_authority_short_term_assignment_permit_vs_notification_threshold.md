## Executive legal summary

Swiss law does not treat the notification procedure as a general substitute for a work permit. The notification route is a limited immigration mechanism for specific short-term situations, principally: EU/ EFTA nationals taking up short-term employment with a Swiss employer; posted workers sent by an employer established in an EU/EFTA state; and EU/EFTA self-employed service providers established in an EU/EFTA state. Outside those categories, the notification route is unavailable unless a separate special regime applies, such as the UK services mobility framework for UK-based service provision. For all other cases, the formal permit regime remains the default. 1

For cross-border service provision, Swiss official materials distinguish sharply between work that remains within the limited 90-day service framework and work that leaves it. Where the planned service exceeds the relevant ceiling from the outset, a permit must be obtained **before** work starts. Once the activity falls outside the 90-day framework, Switzerland does not recognise any general entitlement to a permit; official guidance states that labour-market review, wage and working-condition review, qualifications, and quotas become relevant. 2

Classification depends on more than duration. The authorities look at the **structure** of the activity: whether there is a Swiss employment contract; wvhether the worker remains employed by the foreign undertaking; who gives employment instructions; whether the person is economically independent; whether the activity is genuinely temporary; and whether the person is integrating into the Swiss labour market rather than merely providing a time-limited service. Official interpretive material also shows that some short visits are not treated as notifiable work at all, while productive on-site project work usually is. 3

Day counting is also strict. For posted workers and self-employed service providers, the eight-day notification-free allowance and the 90-day ceiling apply by **calendar** **year** and are counted both at company level and at individual level. Each day with any work counts as one working day; simultaneous deployment of several workers on the same day counts differently from sequential deployment;and follow-up work, maintenance and warranty work may or maynot trigger a fresh eight-day waiting period depending on whether the work resumes within three months and whether it remains the same project. 4

Notification eligibility is also separate from **salary and** **working-condition** **compliance.** Official SEM guidance expressly states that a notification receipt does not confirm that the salary declared is compliant with Swiss minimum conditions. Posted workers remain subject to the Posted Workers Act regime, and sanctions can follow both for misuse of notification rules and for wage and working-condition breaches. 5

## Structural overview of the analysis

A conservative Swiss threshold analysis should proceed in a fixed order. First, identify whether the planned conduct is a gainful activity and, if so, whether it is merely a non-productive visit or a productive on-site contribution to a project. SEM annex material treats seminars, contract negotiations, strategic internal meetings, and simple goods delivery as examples that may fall outside the immigration notification duty, whereas on-the-job training integrated into production, project-planning meetings linked to concrete execution, on-site measurements for an imminent contract, work acceptance on site, and comparable productive project work are treated as notifiable. The annex is expressly illustrative, not exhaustive. 6

Secondly, determine the legal structure of the Swiss activity. A worker with a **Swiss** **employmment** **contract** is not analysed as a cross-border service provider; an employee who remains tied to the foreign employer, is instructed by that employer, and comes only to complete the foreign employer's service obligatioon is analysed as a **posted** **worker;** and a provider who remains established abroad, works on a non-salaried basis, and performs under a service or works contract is analysed as a **self-employed** **service** **provider.** These distinctions are legally significant because different thresholds, filing parties, and exclusions apply.7

Thirdly, check **nationality** **and** **establishment.** Notification is a federal short-term route only for the categories expressly recognised in the SEM materials. A posted third-country national can enter that route only if already durably integrated into the labour market of an EU/EFTA state, generally evidenced by a standard or permanent residence status there for at least twelve months. Otherwvise,the ordinary permit regime applies. 8

Fourthly, count **time** properly. For Swiss short-term employment, the federal materials focus on work contracts of up to three months and a worker-specific 90-day ceiling in the calendar year. For cross-border services, the eight-day rule and the 90-day rule are calculated by calendar year and, for postings, at both company and worker level. The fact that a worker has not previously worked in Switzerland does not help if the company has already exhausted the relevant allowance, and vice versa. 9

Fifthly, check for **sectoral overlays.** Some sectors require notification from the first day. Regulated professions may require a separate SERI declaration and qualification check even where the immigration route is notification rather than permit. Certain financial services remain subject to prior licensing, and staff leasing from abroad is prohibited. 10

Finally, identify the **competent canton** and the missing proof. Official SEM guidance states that notifications are handled by the cantonal authority where the work is carried out, usually on the labour-market side. For over-90-day project authorisations, SEM guidance also says that related projects may in some cases be handled by the canton of the first project if the projects are objectively connected and fully specified in advance. 11

## Primary legal texts and official guidance

## Primary legal texts

**Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons and Annex I.** The treaty basis for the notification framework is the AFMP/ALCP, especially the service-provider provisions referred to by the Swiss authorities as Article 5 AFMP and Articles 17 to 23 of Annex I. Official SEM and SECO materials make clear that this framework liberalises only a **limited** class of cross-border services and does not create a general right to provide any service in Switzerland without authorisation. SEM also uses Annex I to ground the prohibition on staff leasing from abroad and the prior-authorisation rule for certain financial services. 24

**OLCP** **/** **FMIO** / **VEP** / SR **142.203.** The Ordinance on the Free Movement of Persons is the main implementing ordinance for notification and short-term service provision. Current article references identified in official and near-official materials include Article 2 on scope, Article 9 on notification and authorisation procedures, and Article 15 on services exceeding 90 days. The SEM directives use these provisions as the operational legal basis for the notification regime. 25

**FNIA** **/** LEI / **AIG** / **SR** **142.20.** The FNIA supplies the default permit regime and labour-market tests outside the notification route. Current materials identify Article 11 as the general permit requirement for gainful employment, while Articles 18, 21, 22, 23 and 24 govern overall economic interest, precedence, salary and employment conditions, personal requirements, and accommodation for third-country labour-market admission. 26

**OASA** **/** **ASEO** **/** **SR** **142.201.** The OASA contains the permit architecture used when service provision leaves the simple notification track, including the provisions identified in current materials as Articles 19a and 20a on maximum numbers and permit structures for service provision under the AFMP/EFTA framework. SEM annex material uses these provisions when discussing four-month or 120-day authorisations and same-year interaction with previously notified work. 27

**Posted Workers Act and Posted Workers Ordinance / PWA-PWO / LDét-Odét / SR 823.20 and** **823.201.** These instruments set the notification basis for postings and underpin wage and working-condition controls. The SEM directives treat Article 6 of the Act and Article 6 of the Ordinance as the legal foundation for notification, first-day sectors, and salary-notification requirements for posted workers, and Article 9 of the Act as the sanctions provision for notification breaches in postings. 28

**Recruitment** **and** **staff-leasing** **law.** The SEMA notification page expressly links the prohibition on foreign staff leasing into Switzerland to Article 22 paragraph 3 of Annex I AFMP and Article 12 of the Recruitment Act. For short-term project analysis, this is a substantive exclusion, not merely a procedural defect. 29

## Official guidance and administrative materials

The most operationally important threshold material sits in **SEM** **guidance,** not in legislation alone. The core federal sources are the SEM notification page, the SEM user guide, the SEM cross-border service-provision factsheet, the 2026 OLCP directives, and the 2024 annex volume to those directives. Together they explain category eligibility, day counting, project continuity, productive-activity examples, late filing consequences, and the link between notification and permits. 30

For the concept of **service** **provider** and the distinction between temporary service provision and establishment, the most useful official interpretive note is the SERI document on wwho may be regarded as a service provider under Directive 2005/36/EC. Although written in the regulated-professions context, it expressly analyses the ALCP notion of self-employed provider and posted worker, including the indicators of subordination, remuneration structure, intent to remain established abroad, and lack of integration into the Swiss labour market. 31

For salary and working-condition controls, the SECO/official **posting.admin.ch** materials are the key administrative layer. Those materials reinforce that the poosting regime sits alongside immigration classification, not inside it, and they describe sanctions for breaching notification duties and wage-protection rules. 32

## Cantonal practice notes

At federal levelI, the SEM directives state that cantons designate the competent authorities for notification and permit procedures, and that notification handling lies in principle with labour-market authorities. For over-90-day project permmits, the directives add that authorisations are grannted only for the canton of activity and for the announced project duration; if several projects are objectively connected, the first canton will generally handle the authorisation and notify the other cantons concerned. 33

**Bern** is a useful general practice ilustration. Its labour-market pages separate EU/EFTA notifications up to 90 days from permit applications above 90 days. For Swiss employers hiring EU/EFTA nationals on notification, Bern requires personal details, professional qualifications, the planned Swiss activity, and a responsible company contact, and states that the notification confirmation must be available before the employee starts work. For self-employed service providers notified up to 90 days, Bern says that, during labour-market checks, the provider must be able to show the notification confirmation, form A1 and a copy of the service or works contract. For over-90-day self-employed or posted assignments, Bern requires an application at least four weeks in advance and lists documentary itemns such as proof of self-employment, CV, passport copy, diplomas, and the client contract. 34

**Geneva** provides a helpful illustration of cumulative national counting in the regulated-professions field. Its 90-day declaration page for health professionals states expressly that, if part of the 90-day allowance has already been used in another canton, only the remaining balance may be used in Geneva during the same calendar year. It also requires a current CV andd, where there are several employers, asks the applicant to identify the main employer and list the others. That is sector-specific cantonal practice rather than a general immigration rule, but it shows how cantonal authorities operationalise national day limits and evidential scrutiny. 35

**Ticino** states that incomplete notifications are refused and that work exceeding 90 working days or three months per calendar year remains subject to the permit requirement before the activity begins. Ticino also indicates that, for posted workers, an employer declaration on minimum Swiss salary and working conditions must be attached. 36

**Neuchâtel** publicly restates the federal filing deadlines and warns that late filings can lead to administrative fines. Its page distinguishes the one-day lead time for Swiss employers from the eight-day lead time for foreign undertakings and foreign service providers. 37

These cantonal pages are useful operational evidence, but they do not change the federal threshold rules. They should be treated as practice notes and document-check indications rather than as independent legal bases. 38

## Practical legal analysis

## Activity and service-structure analysis

The first threshold question is whether the Swiss-facing activity is of the kind that the immigration notification regime treats as relevant project work. SEM annex material shows that the answer is not simply "any business visit equals work"or "any short stay is exempt". Non-productive participation in seminars, purely theoretical technical courses not integrated into production, contract negotiations, internal strategic meetings, and simple delivery of goods are given as examples outside the notification duty. By contrast, on-the-job training integrated into production, speaking at a conference as a trainer, client meetings about concrete project steps, reception of site wworks, on-site measurements for an imminent contract, and other productive client-facing project contributions are treated as notifiable. Because the annex says its examples are only illustrative, borderline activities must be analysed conservatively and with the project facts in hand. 6

The second threshold question is whether the case is really **service** **provision,** really **Swiss** **employment,** or an impermissible **labour-supply** structure dressed up as a posting. Official SERI guidance says that a self-employed provider is non-salaried, remunerated under a service relationship rather than a Swiss employment contract, and remains established in the EU/EFTA state. A posted worker remains bound by the foreign employment contract, takes employment instructions from the foreign employer, and does not integrate into the Swiss labour market. On the other hand, where a Swiss employment contract is concluded, the case is not analysed as cross-border service provision. This distinction matters directly for who may file, which deadlines apply, and whether the eight-day notification-free rule exists at all. 19

For internal drafting, the role of the Swiss company should therefore be expressed precisely. A **Swiss** **employer** can notify only its own eligible short-term EU/EFTA employee. A **Swiss** **client** or **host** is different: where the foreign undertaking remains the employer and service contractor,the foreign undertaking is the notifying party. The SEM user guide also states that employers may only submit notification forms for their own employees. As a result, a Swiss recipient cannot be described interchangeably as "employer", "host" and "client" unless the contracts and instruction chain genuinely support that characterisation. 39

<!-- 9 -->

Where the Swiss-side structure starts to resemble labour supply rather than service delivery, risk rises sharply. SEM states that foreign staff leasing into Switzerland is prohibited. That means the internal analysis must test whether the foreign entity is providing a result-oriented service under its own responsibility, or is merely placing labour at the disposal oof the Swiss undertaking. If the latter, notification is not the correct route and the structure may be unlawful even before permit questions are reached. 20

## Duration and cumulative counting

Duration in this area is measured by **calendar** **year,** not by a rolling twelve-month period. The SERI service-provider note even gives the example of a service running from October of one year to March of the next: that can amount to 180 consecutive days of activity overall, but it consumes the 90-day capacity for each calendar year separately and does not open a fresh right until the next year begins. 40

For cross-border services, the eight-day threshold and the 90-day ceiling must be handled separately. Many services do not need immigration notification until Swiss work exceeds eight days in the calendar year,but first-day sectors are carved out and must be notified from day one. The 90-day ceiling is a different limit: once it is exceeded, notification is no longer enough and the case moves into the permit regime. This distinction is often blurred in client-facing content and should not be blurred internally. 41

The counting rules are strict and sometimes counter-intuitive. Official guidance states that each day with even one hour of work counts as one day. For postings, the company's account and the worker's account both matter. Three workers working simultaneously for the same five days use only five company days, but three workers working on different days use fifteen company days. The same worker cannot be registered on two building sites simultaneously on the same day, although consecutive same-day work at two sites may still count as only one day if the timings are properly indicated. 42

Project continuity also matters. Under the SEM directives, follow-up work, maintenance work and warranty work on the same project can continue under a fresh notification that refers back to the original filing without generating a new eight-day waiting period, provided the work resumes within three mnonths. If the work resumes after more than three months, concerns a new project, or involves a changed place of work, a new eight-day notice period is triggered. For project-based content, that means "same project" and "new project" must be factually evidenced, not assumed. 43

Where it becomes apparent that more than 90 days will be needed, the permit route must be engaged before the notification route is exhausted. SEM annexes also show that, in some same-year situations, days already used through notification may reduce the usable duration of a later four-month or 120-day authorisation for the same worker. That is not the easiest part of the regime, but it is precisely why cumulative same-year presence should be documented at the start of the analysis rather than rebuilt later. 44

## Evidence required

A robust threshold assessment should capture the following evidential package before any claim is made that notification is available:

**·Identity, nationality and** **residence** **status** of each individual, including whether any posted third-country national already holds standard or permanent status in an EU/EFTA labour market and for how long. 45

**·Employer establishment and contractual map,** including the service or wworks contract, the employment contract, and whether the Swiss entity is employer, client, host, or none of those. 46

**·Instruction** **and** **supervision** **chain,** showing whether the foreign employer keeps labour-law control or whether the Swiss side is effectively directing the work as if it were the employer. 47

**·Detailed** **activity** **description,** including whether the work is productive, merely preparatory, purely representational, or part of regulated-profession practice. 48

**·Day** **ledger** **by** **worker** **and** **by** **company,** including prior notifications in other cantons, other Swiss assignments in the same calendar year, and any same-year permits already issued. 49

**·Project continuity** **evidence,** including whether maintenance, warranty or resumed work relates to the same project and whether the interruption is within three months. 50

**·Sector and** **profession** **overlay** **evidence,** including whether the work falls in a first-day sector and whether a separate SERI declaration is required for a regulated profession. 51

**·Compliance** **documents,** especially notification confirmation, form A1, CV, diplomas, prooof of self-employment, and the Swiss client contract where cantonal practice or permit route demands them. Bern's pages are particularly clear on these document sets. 52

## Drafting cautions and source list

## Drafting cautions

·Do not write "work in Switzerland for under 90 days may be carried out by notification" without first stating the relevant category: Swiss-employed EU/EFTA worker, EU/EFTA self-employed service provider, or posted worker of an EU/EFTA undertaking. 12

·Do **not** merge short-term Swiss employment with cross-border service provision. The filing party, deadline, and day-count logic differ. 53

·Do **not** present the eight-day rule as if it were a general exemption. It applies only to postings and self-employed services, and even then not in first-day sectors. 54

·Do **not** discuss the 90-day limit for postings without also mentioning the **company-side** count and the calendar-year method. 55

·Do **not** describe a notification receipt as if it approved wage compliance or cured structural defects. It does neither. 56

·Do **not** describe foreign labour supply as "a notification case". Foreign staff leasing into Switzerland is prohibited. 20

·Do **not** turn the SEM annex examples on productive activity into hard statutory law. They are useful official guidance, but they are expressly illustrative and fact-sensitive. 57

·Where federal materials are fragmented or the point is canton-specific, say so expressly. That is particularly important for same-year notification/permit interaction, connected multi-canton projects, and regulated-profession overlays. 58
